# SEO — hub

Search engine optimization is Neil Patel's flagship domain and the channel he says
built his own audience. His teaching is remarkably consistent across a decade of videos:
rankings come from many small factors adding up (not one silver bullet), the fastest
wins come from long-tail phrases and from expanding pages that *already* rank, backlinks
and brand signals are the heaviest levers, and the whole thing must be built for the user
first — with his own tool Ubersuggest as the recurring workflow spine. This hub
synthesizes the frameworks from his L2 source pages; every claim is dated to the source
that established it and cited to that source page. Self-reported traffic and revenue
figures are marked as such. His views have also *expanded* over time — from "get loved by
Google" (2017–2020) to "get chosen across the entire internet" (2025) — and those dates
matter.

## Core philosophy: SEO as a "24-hour salesman"

The foundational framing, from [[2020-05-04-yt-pIbQfOcsEsE]] (2020-05-04):

- SEO is optimizing a site for a search term so it keeps delivering traffic without
  ongoing ad spend — paraphrase; Neil's metaphor is a salesperson who never clocks out.
  > In other words, it's your 24-hour salesman.
  > If you stop paying money to Google or Whatnot for paid ads, you know what, your
  > traffic dies down. But with SEO, if you don't do anything for a whole week straight,
  > you don't even open up your wallet, you can still continually get traffic.
- There is no silver bullet — rankings are "a lot of little things that add up" (2020-05-04).
  > Well there's not really a silver bullet. It's a lot of little things that add up.
- Five "Fact or Fiction" myths he busts (2020-05-04, paraphrase): a hard industry can't
  rank (false), only big companies win (false — mom-and-pops rank), more pages is always
  better (false — he cites About.com cutting >half its content and gaining traffic), you
  must manually submit your site (false — Google finds it, though a sitemap still helps),
  and you must spend money to win (false — affordability is why he chose SEO).
- Datable 2020 stats he anchors the case on: 93% of online experiences start with a
  search engine; over 31.7% of clicks go to position one (per Advanced Web Ranking), and
  "if you're not in the top 10... you're not going to be seen at all"; every 1-second
  page-speed delay costs ~7% of conversions (per HubSpot) (2020-05-04).
  > Your position is everything! Over 31.7% of people click on position one, according to
  > Advanced Web Ranking.
- Ranking-factor stack he names (2020-05-04, paraphrase): page speed, mobile UX
  (Google's mobile-first index), topical in-depth content, short URLs (per Ahrefs),
  content length *without fluff*, backlinks (per an Ahrefs chart, more impact than
  on-page SEO), brand/user signals, and SSL. Corroborated in
  [[2019-07-04-yt-JiqNIUQNQ7I]] (200+ factors, "do every little thing right").

## The SEO timeline & patience

Neil's most-asked question — "how long does SEO take?" — from [[2020-03-26-yt-wc_uo1prLg8]]
(2020-03-26):

- The honest answer is "it depends," because there are too many factors and industries
  differ (2020-03-26).
  > So how long does it take to rank on Google? Well, are you ready for this? The answer
  > is it depends.
- A ~6-month "traction" rule, with an explicit **traction ≠ top-ranking** distinction: he
  cites Forbes' 4–6 month figure and his own experience of roughly six months to start
  seeing traction on a new site actively doing content, on-page and link building (2020-03-26).
  > it can take roughly six months before you start seeing results. It doesn't mean in six
  > months you're going to rank at the top... but it usually takes six months to start
  > seeing traction.
- Long-tail-first is the fastest path to early wins (2020-03-26): "the best way to start
  seeing the traction faster is going after the long tail phrases which convert better
  than head terms."
- SEO is harder than it used to be because Google now has "prime pickings" among 1.7B+
  websites (as of 2020-01-01) and can afford to be selective; keyword strategy shifted
  from 5–10 generic terms to lists of 50–100+, with 3–4 keywords per page (2020-03-26,
  paraphrase).
- Fresh vs. regurgitated content: he self-reports 47.6% more traffic on genuinely new
  topics than on old/regurgitated content (2020-03-26, self-reported).
  > Every time I blog on something new we're seeing on average 47.6% more traffic than
  > when we blog on something that's been regurgitated and is old.
- Note on word count: he explicitly rejects the "Google wants a 2,000-word article" belief
  — "It's not about word count. It's about providing what the users are looking for as
  quick as possible" (2020-03-26). This reconciles with his depth advice below: depth means
  answering the query completely, not padding for length.

## Domain authority as a leading indicator

Because rankings and traffic *lag*, Neil optimizes toward a signal that moves *first*.
From [[2017-01-18-yt-KsZIVpkpte0]] (2017-01-18) and [[2017-05-26-yt-BkR7L41SroU]] (2017-05-26):

- Google PageRank (0–10, created by Larry Page) is a poor progress signal because it
  refreshes too rarely — "potentially only every six months to a year" — so it can't tell
  you whether recent changes are helping (2017-01-18, paraphrase).
  > are you really going to wait six months to a year for Google to update that page rank
  > number to figure out if you're doing better or worse?
- Rankings/traffic are the true measure but also slow — he says it took him "six months to
  a year and a half" to rank page-one for terms like "online marketing" and "SEO" on
  NeilPatel.com (2017-01-18, self-reported). Domain authority is the **leading indicator**
  that moves before them:
  > But the leading indicator that showed me that I was doing well is that my domain
  > authority continually started to increase.
- Domain authority is a 0–100 score (Google/YouTube/Facebook near 100); it behaves on a
  logarithmic scale — "way easier to go from 1 to 10, than it is to go from 10 to 20"
  ([[2017-05-26-yt-BkR7L41SroU]], 2017-05-26).
  > And it works like on a Richter scale, right? A logarithmic scale, in which it's way
  > easier to go from 1 to 10, than it is to go from 10 to 20.
- Tool preference: he tracks domain authority via a frequently-updated backlink index —
  he **prefers Ahrefs over Moz's Open Site Explorer/PageRank because it updates more
  frequently** (2017-01-18, paraphrase). Both source pages name Ahrefs (paid) and Moz's
  free Open Site Explorer.
- Link-building corollary: chase links from sites whose domain authority is *higher* than
  yours to move faster (2017-01-18).
  > try to go after links from sites with domain authorities that are larger than yours.
  > By doing that, your rankings and traffic are going to increase at a much quicker pace.

## On-page & CTR optimization (rank "without building a single link")

Neil argues a large share of ranking improvement comes from on-page fixes and
click-through tuning alone. From [[2020-05-11-yt-PXDPqXHLSOY]] (2020-05-11, SEO Unlocked
Week 2) and [[2018-01-11-yt-jhnNOlhPbyc]] (2018-01-11):

- The GSC click-through play (2018-01-11): find pages that get impressions but few clicks
  in Google Search Console, add those keywords, and rewrite title tags / meta descriptions
  to lift CTR.
  > This will increase your rankings without you having to build even one single link.
- Why CTR feeds ranking (2020-05-11, paraphrase): if searchers consistently click the #2
  result over #1 because its title is more appealing, Google promotes the more-clicked one.
- **Title-tag formula** (2020-05-11): keyword near the *front* (people search by keyword)
  + a modifier near the *end* — modifiers such as *buy, guide, review, online, offers,
  cheap,* or *the year*; adding the year "drastically increases click-through rate,"
  especially on detailed guides.
- **Headline formula** (2020-05-11): number-or-trigger-word → adjective → keyword →
  promise (and the promise must not be deceptive). Keep it ~6 words; per BuzzSumo, "how to"
  out-shares any other two-word phrase and "X Ways To" is the best opener.
- The stakes of the headline, quoting Ogilvy (2020-05-11):
  > David Ogilvy always said that 80 cents of advertising campaign out of a dollar is
  > spent in the headline. And it's true.
  > Eight of 10 people will read your headline, but only two out of 10 people will click
  > through and read the rest of your copy.
- Interlinking as an on-page lever (2020-05-11): heavy internal linking is why sites rank
  for many terms; put keywords in anchor text without stuffing; ~3–4 internal links per
  1,500 words, only where natural.
  > I rank very highly on Google for a lot of my head terms because I'm interlinking
  > throughout my site. Another site that does this is Wikipedia.
- URL structure (2020-05-11): ~50-character URLs rank better than 60/70/80 (attributed to
  Brian Dean of Backlinko); hyphens not underscores, lowercase, safe characters, ≤~2
  folders. **Removing dates from URLs** — his own result: SEO traffic up ~50% in ~30 days,
  because a dated URL signals a time-bound article (requires a 301 from the old URL)
  (2020-05-11, self-reported).
  > I removed dates from my URL structure on neilpatel.com. By doing this, I've boosted my
  > SEO traffic by 50% in roughly 30 days. I kid you not.
- The user-first guardrail (2020-05-11): every on-page tactic serves humans *and* the
  algorithm.
  > It's not just about adding in links and keywords. It's about doing what's also best
  > for the user. You're not just optimizing for Google. You're also optimizing for users.

## Content depth & the refresh loop

Instead of always writing new posts, Neil's signature move is to **expand what already
ranks**. Sources: [[2018-04-05-yt-Uar2hXRrckY]] (2018-04-05), [[2019-01-26-yt-fGOv5oU5vww]]
(2019-01-26), [[2019-06-10-yt-zK4oTuiFV1E]] (2019-06-10), [[2018-01-11-yt-jhnNOlhPbyc]]
(2018-01-11), depth principle from [[2020-05-04-yt-pIbQfOcsEsE]] (2020-05-04).

- The refresh loop (2018-04-05, paraphrase): in Search Console find posts with <5%
  click-through rate, pull the keywords they get impressions for, and rewrite to
  "encompass all the relevant keywords" — not just insert them — making the piece 2–5×
  more in-depth. He self-reports an Instagram-followers post going from ~12,000 to ~30,000
  monthly views this way (2018-04-05, self-reported).
  > we integrate those keywords into that blog post... we rewrite the content we just
  > don't add in the keywords we rewrite it to encompass all the relevant keywords and
  > make it two three four or five times more in-depth
- The GSC → Ubersuggest → rewrite → tighten title/meta → outreach sequence (2019-01-26):
  he grew his own digital-marketing guide (~10,000 words) from over 9,000 to 17,000 monthly
  visitors "just by adding in more long tail phrases" (2019-01-26, self-reported).
  Timeframe: new long-tail rankings land "within 30 to 60 days at the latest."
  > I went from over 9,000 visitors a month... I went to 17,000 visitors. Just by adding
  > in more long tail phrases. It works that well.
- Keyword stuffing does not work (2019-01-26): "if you spam, it creates a terrible
  experience and people are gonna bounce back. Instead, you need to adjust your content."
- Single-page, exhaustive depth beats broad-and-shallow (2020-05-04, paraphrase): cover a
  topic deeply across its subtopics (a cats page also covering kittens, cat food, healthy
  weight). "The more detail that you go into on each page around the same topic, the better
  you're going to end up ranking" — but long articles full of fluff won't do well.
- Anti-cannibalization / consolidation (2018-04-05): publishing constantly causes topic
  overlap; consolidate to **one page per topic** and lengthen it — his "content clustering"
  (a pillar article linked to distinct sub-articles), *not* near-duplicate intros.
  > have you ever been on Wikipedia and notice that they had 20 pages on the same topic
  > never it's only one page per topic... that's why they do well there's no cannibalization

## Long-tail keywords: the single growth lever

Neil credits long-tail expansion as *the* driver behind NeilPatel.com's scale. From
[[2023-01-21-yt-7mMFfJE8cEc]] (2023-01-21) and [[2019-06-10-yt-zK4oTuiFV1E]] (2019-06-10):

- The site reaches ~4 million visitors/month, and long-tail is the lever behind it —
  "doubling down on long-tail keywords rather than only chasing new head-term keywords"
  (2023-01-21, self-reported traffic).
- The mechanism (2019-06-10): the head term is the hardest to rank for; once you rank it,
  Google ranks the same page for its long-tail variations easily, and combined they can
  out-produce the head term.
  > once you rank for the main head term, it's super easy to rank for all the long tail
  > variations... Google naturally just pop you for all of the other ones within 30 days.
- Why it wins (2023-01-21): long-tail phrases (3+ words) carry low volume, low competition,
  high conversion; over 70% of queries are long-tail; his agency NP Digital sees results
  "two to three times faster" on long-tail than head terms (depending on site age/authority).
  > typically, we're seeing results for long-tail keywords two to three times faster than
  > people going after head terms.
- Tools (2023-01-21): AnswerThePublic (questions from Google Suggest) and Ubersuggest (up
  to a million related long-tail phrases per seed). He applied the hack "to every single
  one" of his 2,000+ articles (2019-06-10, self-reported).
- Corroboration: [[2019-01-26-yt-fGOv5oU5vww]] and [[2020-09-05-yt-zHDATkRygTE]] (favor
  3–4-word phrases; they convert better even at lower volume).

## Link building & the Skyscraper Technique

Backlinks are, in his framing, Google's heaviest single factor — and he insists you never
pay for them. From [[2017-05-26-yt-BkR7L41SroU]] (2017-05-26) and
[[2019-07-15-yt-MjO8lIQ68-8]] (2019-07-15):

- Backlinks as "votes" and the #1 factor (2017-05-26): Google uses 200+ factors; he says
  backlinks most affect rankings (citing a Moz expert survey).
  > Google has over 200 factors; and the number one factor that affects rankings is
  > backlinks.
  > Google's looking at it as votes. The more links that your website has, the higher
  > you're gonna rank.
- Quality > quantity, and relevance can beat authority (2017-05-26, paraphrase): a
  Huffington Post link beats "Joe the plumber," but for a plumbing site a *relevant* Home
  Depot link beats a higher-profile but off-topic one. Grow links at a natural pace; never
  pay for them.
  > And Google knows that it's way harder to get a link from Huffington Post than it is to
  > get a link from Joe the plumber.
- Roundup posts as the fastest link tactic (2017-05-26, paraphrase): email every expert in
  your space one question, compile and link them all, then ask them to share.
- The no-pay **Skyscraper outreach** process (2019-07-15) — content quality is the single
  most important step:
  1. Reverse-engineer a competitor's most-linked pages in a backlink tool.
  2. Rebuild it substantially better and deeper (his example: 1,000 → 5,000 words) with
     genuine expert detail, images, GIFs, custom graphics — not padding.
  3. Send *custom* (non-templated) outreach to everyone linking to the competitor.
  > Whatever that article is that your competitors wrote... I want you to write a better
  > version of that article. You know, if it's 1,000 words I want you to make it 5,000
  > words.
  > This step, the second one, is the most important.
  > don't get caught in shady gray hat tactics.
- The Content Ideas → Skyscraper loop is operationalized inside Ubersuggest
  ([[2020-03-02-yt-_ve9cmRaem0]], 2020-03-02): sort competitor content by shares,
  backlinks and traffic, then "create something that's 10 times better" (Brian Dean's
  Skyscraper Technique).

## The Ubersuggest workflow (the canonical tool spine)

Ubersuggest is Neil's own tool and the recurring reference other tool mentions defer to.
The master walkthrough is [[2020-03-02-yt-_ve9cmRaem0]] (2020-03-02), with the Chrome
extension in [[2020-06-20-yt-eXBgXnKGTAw]] (2020-06-20) and [[2020-09-05-yt-zHDATkRygTE]]
(2020-09-05):

- Canonical section order (2020-03-02, paraphrase): **Keyword Overview** (volume, CPC, SEO
  & paid difficulty, links needed to rank top 10) → **Keyword Ideas / Content Ideas** →
  **Traffic Analyzer** (domain traffic + Top Pages + Keywords) → **Site Audit** →
  **Backlinks** → **Dashboard** (project tracking, weekly crawls, competitor comparison).
- The underused report he pushes hardest is **Content Ideas** (2020-03-02):
  > If you start typing in specific keywords, the Content Ideas Report will tell you all
  > the popular blog posts that are popular based on that topic.
  > create something that's 10 times better.
- Site Audit prioritization logic (2020-03-02): it front-loads the fixes with highest
  impact and easiest implementation.
  > It puts the stuff at the top that will have the highest impact, and is the easiest to
  > implement... So, you want to do all the stuff at the top first.
- Extension heuristics for judging a keyword before you target it (2020-06-20):
  - **No-click share as a traffic-potential filter** — a high-volume term is worthless if
    most searchers click nothing.
    > if a term is searched a thousand times or ten thousand times, but if 90 percent of
    > the people aren't clicking on anything... you're probably not going to get that much
    > traffic from that term.
  - **Searcher age-range as a commercial-intent proxy** — terms searched mostly by under-18s
    convert worse.
  - **Domain score (0–100)** on each ranking result as a difficulty gauge.
    > Domain score is a metric from zero to 100. The higher that number the more
    > authoritative those websites are.
- Signature extension move (2020-09-05): expand a competitor's backlinks on the SERP, then
  pitch those same linkers for your own links — "keyword opportunities and link
  opportunities" in one view.
  > you want to see who's linking to your competition and you can hit up those websites and
  > try to get them to link back to you.

## Technical SEO & site audits

From [[2020-05-16-yt-k04rHijEPSw]] (2020-05-16, SEO Unlocked), [[2019-07-04-yt-JiqNIUQNQ7I]]
(2019-07-04) and on-page/technical detail in [[2020-05-11-yt-PXDPqXHLSOY]] (2020-05-11):

- **Error-prioritization heuristic** (2020-05-16): fix in the order **critical errors →
  warnings → recommendations** — biggest bang for buck, easiest wins first, and audit the
  *whole* site not a subset.
  > you want to fix the stuff on the critical errors tab first, then warnings, then
  > recommendations. By going in that order, you're going to get the biggest bang for buck
  > when it comes to increasing your traffic.
- Baseline checks (2020-05-16): confirm indexation with `site:yourdomain.com`; serve one
  canonical version (https only; a single www/non-www).
- **301 vs 302** (2020-05-16): 301s pass links/equity permanently; a 302 gives Google a
  "fuzzy answer"; use one redirect per page, avoid loops, don't leave a 302 up beyond ~3
  months.
  > 301s tell Google this page has moved, transfer all the links, transfer all the
  > equity... 302 is like, "Hey, we're temporarily not right here right now, but we don't
  > know when we're coming back." That's giving Google a fuzzy answer.
- Also in the audit (2020-05-16, paraphrase): fix HTTP errors (400/403/404 → 301, 500 → dev
  team), thin content where sensible, unique titles/metas, image alt tags, internal links
  to top pages, review anchor text and disavow spammy links, schema markup (Google's
  Structured Data Markup Helper), sitemap + robots.txt in Search Console, and a
  manual-actions penalty check.
- SSL as a near-prerequisite (2019-07-04): he self-reports **90% of top-ranking sites have
  SSL**; non-secure sites trigger browser warnings that raise bounces.
  > 90% of websites that rank at the top have SSL installed. It's pretty much become a
  > prerequisite if you want to rank well.
- AMP's uneven payoff (2019-07-04): little added US mobile traffic, but a "huge increase"
  in weaker-infrastructure markets like Brazil (self-reported).
  > In the United States by leveraging the AMP framework I didn't really get much more
  > mobile traffic, but other countries where mobile devices and infrastructure isn't as
  > good, such as Brazil, I saw a huge increase in mobile traffic.
- WordPress plugin setup (2019-07-04, paraphrase): W3 Total Cache, Smush, Yoast SEO,
  Sucuri, AMP, a3 Lazy Load, schema rich snippets (only where relevant — irrelevant markup
  "will irritate Google"), and SSL. His refrain: "Now Google wants you to do every little
  thing right."

## Brand & user signals as a ranking factor

- **Brand queries** are a ranking factor Neil surfaces via Google Trends
  ([[2018-01-11-yt-jhnNOlhPbyc]], 2018-01-11): when other factors are similar, the brand
  people search for more wins — his car example, BMW vs. General Motors.
  > one of the factors within Google is brand queries. And Google Trends shows you this. As
  > you can see BMW is more popular than General Motors.
- User/brand signals are overlooked but heavy (2020-05-04): he cites Google's ex-CEO on
  brands and Matt Cutts on a quality classifier.
  > The ex-CEO of Google once said, "Brands are the solution, not the problem." "Brands are
  > how you sort out the cesspool."
- Social signals: "no effect on ranking according to Google," but a correlation between
  high-ranking pages and social signals ([[2020-03-26-yt-wc_uo1prLg8]], 2020-03-26).
- **The 2017 origin of the brand→branded-search→rankings thesis.** The brand-queries idea
  is older than the 2018 BMW-vs-GM framing: as early as 2017 Neil taught (a) using **Google
  Trends as a brand-size diagnostic** — type your own company name in and aim for a line
  "climbing up and to the right"; flat/declining diagnoses too little marketing or marketing
  that fails to connect emotionally ([[2017-05-20-yt-q5wHgm7xtEk]], 2017-05-20); and (b) that
  **building a real brand drives branded search volume, which Google reads as a trust/
  authority signal** lifting rankings beyond links/on-page ([[2017-06-04-yt-nTOGKiy4F_0]],
  2017-06-04). Together these are one thesis — brand → branded search → rankings — and the
  datable seed of the 2026 "unlinked brand mentions count like links" refinement (see
  [[wiki/topics/ai-marketing|ai-marketing]]).

## Search Everywhere Optimization (the 2026 thesis)

Neil's flagship reframing, dated and kept distinct from his 2017–2020 Google-centric
teaching. From [[2025-07-23-yt-39o0uYPo4jU]] (2025-07-23):

- SEO isn't dead — it got bigger. The discipline moved from "getting found on Google" to
  "getting **chosen** across the entire internet" (2025-07-23).
  > Traditional SEO was about getting found on Google. Search everywhere optimization is
  > about getting chosen across the entire internet.
- ~73% of search activity now happens off Google; Google's ~13.7B searches/day is only
  ~27% (2025-07-23). The rest is scattered across TikTok, Reddit, Amazon, YouTube, ChatGPT,
  Instagram, the app store.
- The buyer journey is a "constellation of micro-decisions," each platform serving a
  psychological function (what to click = Google, what to trust = Reddit, what to buy =
  Amazon/TikTok Shop, what to believe = AI models) (2025-07-23).
  > The modern consumer journey isn't a funnel anymore. It's a constellation of micro
  > decisions.
- **Visibility vs. validation** distinction (2025-07-23): visibility is table stakes;
  validation — being cited/mentioned/trusted by others — is what AI summarizers pull from
  and what actually drives decisions.
  > Visibility is what you do. Validation is what others say about what you do.
  > AI... summarizes based on who gets mentioned the most and trusted the fastest.
- **RICE channel prioritization** (2025-07-23): score Reach, Impact, Confidence, Ease to
  pick the two-to-three platforms to win, not omnipresence.
  > The goal isn't omnipresence, it's strategic presence.

## SEO in the AI era — career adaptation & Reddit (Marketing School, 2025–26)

From the co-hosted @MarketingSchoolPod archive (only Neil-attributed). The GEO/AEO *market*
and business framing lives in [[wiki/topics/ai-marketing|ai-marketing]]; this section keeps the
SEO-practitioner mechanics.

- **"SEO has always evolved — no sacred cows" (2025-10-18).** His answer to "is an SEO career
  dead": SEO has changed continuously and the skill is *adaptability*. Write **content for humans
  but package it for AI**; **brand mentions now count like links**; curiosity + adaptability keep
  the career "booming" ([[2025-10-18-yt-suqrDn5N_D4]]).
  > ⚠️ Date-note: "brand mentions count like links" is a 2026-era refinement of his long-standing
  > "backlinks are the #1 factor" teaching (see *Link building* above, 2017-05-26). Treat as an
  > additive evolution — links still matter for classic Google ranking; unlinked brand mentions
  > are the *new* signal AI engines and Google's brand-authority models reward.
- **The Reddit SEO playbook (2025-09-25).** Reddit is a heavily-cited source for both Google and
  AI answers, so it is an SEO surface — but you **participate natively** rather than buy authority
  accounts, and do active **Reddit reputation management**. His own **100-post experiment showed
  Reddit removes 70%+ of AI-written replies** — automation doesn't work here
  ([[2025-09-25-yt-BfPayCQHNcE]]). Cross-link: [[wiki/topics/social-media|social-media]].
- **"Old-school SEO is dead, not SEO" + the zero-traffic reality (2024-03-01).** The competitive
  bar is being the minority of content that earns *any* traffic: he cites his Ubersuggest finding
  that **~94.29% of pages get zero search traffic** ([[2024-03-01-yt-LZuR52_nNFA]]). The five-lever
  modern reframing and the financial "Google won't kill SEO" argument are on
  [[wiki/topics/ai-marketing|ai-marketing]].

## 2017 solo-era tactical playbook (@neilpatel P2)

A cluster of concrete, dated tactics from Neil's 2017 solo teaching run. They are
period-accurate 2017 mechanics — several predate or extend the frameworks above; date-check
before reuse.

- **Site architecture — subdirectory over subdomain.** Google treats a subdomain "almost
  like a separate site," so a `/blog` **subdirectory** borrows the main domain's authority and
  ranks faster than a `blog.` subdomain ([[2017-06-16-yt-ancECphk_9c]], 2017-06-16). He later
  quantifies it with an own-test figure — **~90% of link juice transfers through a subdomain
  vs. ~100% through a subdirectory** — and gives the routing rule: **single-topic site →
  subdirectory; multi-topic → subdomain**, because "Google favors niche sites"
  ([[2017-07-26-yt-xZAM1l8cxbs]], 2017-07-26).
- **Tier-two link building.** A distinct link-building play: build links **to the pages that
  already link to you**, strengthening those linking pages so their link to you carries more
  weight ([[2017-07-19-yt-peXc2E-Lzgw]], 2017-07-19).
- **Internationalization / hreflang.** Neil calls international SEO (hreflang tags, per-country
  content) **"one of the biggest driving factors of my growth,"** noting the US is under half
  of neilpatel.com's traffic ([[2017-07-20-yt-GcIRv2AE59I]], 2017-07-20).
- **Local link building.** For local SEO: Moz Local directory listings, Yelp city sections,
  a chamber-of-commerce link, non-compete local cross-promotion, and Ahrefs
  competitor-backlink outreach ([[2017-08-21-yt-xpmvv0IfhTM]], 2017-08-21).
- **Removing dates from URLs — the earlier (2017) datapoint.** The date-removal win (captured
  above from 2020 as ~50% in ~30 days) has a 2017 antecedent: removing dates from permalinks
  (`/month/year/title` → `/blog/title`) drove **+58% search traffic (283k → 449k, Dec 2016 →
  Jan 2017)**, with his recurring refrain that **"one structural change beat years of link
  building"** ([[2017-07-17-yt-TwQhVr9MXyE]], 2017-07-17; requires a 301 from the old URL).
- **Blog-URL categories are size-dependent.** Skip URL category folders under ~100 pages;
  use them at 1000+ posts to signal topic clusters to Google; single-hyphen slugs, never
  double dashes ([[2017-08-26-yt-MB9cfH1OfH8]], 2017-08-26).
- **Reverse-engineer CTR copy from the top *paid* ads.** Study the highest-CTR paid AdWords
  ads for a term and mirror their title/meta framing organically — positioning click-through
  and user signals as a ranking lever independent of link building
  ([[2017-06-02-yt-qSDVFMTWN6w]], 2017-06-02).
- **Pogo-sticking is a ranking signal.** On "product pages = money pages," Neil names
  **bounce-back-to-Google (pogo-sticking) as a negative ranking signal**, and cross-linking
  → better crawl/indexation → collective ranking lift for the linked pages
  ([[2017-08-07-yt-_e_0iu54wUQ]], 2017-08-07).
- **Get a new site indexed (3-step).** Google Search Console (see crawl errors / indexed
  pages) + an XML sitemap via Yoast + submit the sitemap — with an explicit new-site patience
  caveat of **~4–6 months to a year** before real ranking ([[2017-08-22-yt-4xBWaXpZD7M]],
  2017-08-22).
- **Site speed = revenue.** He frames speed via an Amazon "**~1% of revenue per second of
  load time**" figure (attributed to an "Amazon study," not independently verified) and a fix
  ladder: Google PageSpeed → AMP for mobile → CDN (names Akamai) → caching (W3 Total Cache on
  WordPress) ([[2017-08-24-yt-13jOMJhKISE]], 2017-08-24).
- **Google News as fast-but-short-lived traffic.** Trends-sourced news content + a Google
  News sitemap in GSC + unique keyword URLs can spike traffic — but explicitly for hours, not
  days ([[2017-08-14-yt-13BsYXkenhs]], 2017-08-14).

> ⚠️ Dated/superseded prediction: in 2017 Neil predicted **AMP would boost rankings**
> ([[2017-07-07-yt-kFCs5dx9LVI]], 2017-07-07) — now falsified by Google's later deprecation of
> AMP. Do NOT present as current; his own 2019 read already found AMP's payoff uneven (above).
