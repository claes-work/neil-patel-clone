# How To Create The Perfect Blog Post

## Summary
A short tutorial walking through Neil's own blog-post structure as a checklist,
element by element. Opens with headline craft (citing David Ogilvy's "80 cents on the
dollar is spent on the headline"; an 8-out-of-10-read/2-out-of-10-click ratio;
numbers/list-based titles, "how to" titles, curiosity-gap titles; a target of roughly
seven words). Then walks the anatomy of a post: a top image ("a picture says a
thousand words"), a structured introduction that links out to stats/data sources for
E-A-T-driven social proof, subheadings (the "book with chapters" analogy) to support
skimming, mixed media (images/video/embedded podcast audio), ordered/bulleted lists,
a labeled conclusion (often ending in an italicized question to drive comments — and
Neil says he personally replies to comments to encourage return visits), and scrolling
social-share bands for non-Google traffic. Closes with a style note: write in
second/first person ("you"/"I") for a conversational tone, and use bold/italic
sparingly.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-09-12, Neil's stated headline benchmark: about 8 in 10 readers read a
  headline, but only 2 in 10 click through to the article (self-reported, no source
  data given).
- As of 2020-09-12, Neil's target headline length is "generally around seven words, a
  little bit less is great, not bad."
- As of 2020-09-12, Neil frames Google's E-A-T signal (expertise, authority, trust) as
  a reason to link out to external stats/data in a post's introduction, claiming pages
  that showcase E-A-T "rank higher in the long run" (self-reported).
- As of 2020-09-12, Neil says he personally responds to blog comments, framing it as a
  loyalty/return-visit driver ("shows them that you care, that you engage").

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "David Ogilvy, one of the greatest marketers of all time, once said that 80 cents on the dollar is spent on the headline."

> "A picture says a thousand words."

> "You want to think about your blog posts as a book. You have the title of the book... and then you'll have like chapter one, chapter two, chapter three."
