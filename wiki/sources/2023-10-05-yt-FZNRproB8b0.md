# Is Rebranding A Good Idea? - Neil Patel

## Summary
Marketing School episode on whether companies should rebrand, using Facebook→Meta as the
anchor example. Neil says he generally dislikes rebranding an already-popular/profitable
brand, distinguishes cosmetic evolution (logo tweaks, a parent-company structure like
Google→Alphabet) from a full name change, and argues a rebrand makes more sense when the
existing name carries a negative connotation (e.g., Charter→Spectrum). He also tells the
origin story of NP Digital's own name.

## Key claims (dated, paraphrase unless noted)
- Not a fan of rebranding a brand that is already popular or generating meaningful
  revenue ("a million bucks... a hundred grand... a billion" — any size once it's
  established), because in most cases he's seen, a full rebrand hurts more than it helps;
  logo/design evolution (his example: Apple's logo over time) or adding a parent company
  (Google → Alphabet, while Google itself stays Google) is fine, but dropping a working
  name entirely is not. (Neil, 2023-10-05) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Calls the Facebook → Meta rebrand a mistake: Facebook.com didn't change, so the
  "rebrand" was really about forcing a new narrative (the metaverse) that, in his
  reading, the general public never asked for — contrasted with how the public has
  broadly embraced AI/ChatGPT without being pushed. Cites ChatGPT as (self-reported,
  via SimilarWeb) the 19th most popular website globally at the time, and an informal
  poll of Brazilian executives where nearly all raised their hands as ChatGPT users.
  (Neil, 2023-10-05) — agency-entrepreneurship / ai-marketing.
- General rule: a rebrand is more justified when the existing name has a negative
  connotation (agrees with Eric's Charter→Spectrum example) than when the business is
  simply "still growing well" under its current name — rebranding a healthy brand with no
  negative baggage is something he says he "hates" to see. (Neil, 2023-10-05) —
  agency-entrepreneurship.
- Origin story: the agency was originally meant to launch as "Patel" (patel.com was
  unavailable), then as "NP Digital" (npdigital.com was also unavailable) — he made the
  domain owner an offer that wasn't accepted for about a year and a half after the
  company had already launched under "Neil Patel Digital," at which point the owner
  finally sold, and the agency shortened its public name to "NP Digital" partly because
  clients worried the agency was overly dependent on Neil personally ("what happens if
  Neil's not on my campaign?"). Internally the team just calls it "NPD." (Neil,
  2023-10-05) — agency-entrepreneurship / biography.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> I'm not a fan of rebrands, especially if you have a really popular brand... I would not
> consider rebranding. I just don't like it.

> If I was like Facebook, I wouldn't be like, "we're now known as Meta." ... They're
> trying to go after this whole metaverse, and they're trying to make a whole different
> narrative... none of us are really like, "Yeah, we need a metaverse."

> Typically if something has a negative connotation, a rebrand is not a bad idea. But I
> hate it when companies do a rebrand and there's not any negative issues or their
> business is still growing well.

> Technically, the agency was supposed to be called Patel. I couldn't get patel.com. And
> then we were trying to be NP Digital, and we couldn't get npdigital.com... he came back
> like a year and a half later after we created the company, and then he accepted the
> offer, or else we would've been NP Digital from day one.
