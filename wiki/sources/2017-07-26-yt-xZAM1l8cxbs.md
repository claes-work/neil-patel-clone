# Should You Use Sub Domains or Sub Directories?

## Summary
Neil defines the two site-architecture options — a **sub-domain** is `keyword.domain.com`; a **sub-directory** is `domain.com/keyword` — and gives a decision rule based on topical breadth. If your whole site is one topic (like his marketing blog), use sub-directories (`domain.com/blog`, `/about`, `/tools`). The moment your site spans multiple unrelated topics (e.g. a directory covering both food and cars), split them into sub-domains (`cars.domain.com`, `food.domain.com`), because Google treats a sub-domain like a separate site. He cites his own SEO tests: roughly **90% of link juice transfers through a sub-domain** vs. **100% through a sub-directory**. He also argues Google prefers ranking focused niche sites over sprawling authority sites that cover everything — the Huffington Post / Entrepreneur.com / CNN "get away with it" only because they have enormous link profiles you won't have.

## Key claims
As of 2017-07-26, Neil states:
- Definitions: a sub-domain is `keyword.domain.com`; a sub-directory is `domain.com/keyword-or-category` (as of 2017-07-26).
- If a site covers a single topic, use sub-directories for all sections (blog, about, tools) since everything is on the same topic (as of 2017-07-26).
- If a site covers multiple unrelated topics, use sub-domains — Google treats a sub-domain as a different site, signaling "this section is off-topic" (as of 2017-07-26).
- From his own SEO tests: roughly **90% of link juice transfers through a sub-domain**, but **100% transfers through a sub-directory** (as of 2017-07-26).
- Google likes ranking niche websites better than authority sites that talk about everything — unless you have a huge link profile like Wikipedia. Big multi-topic sites (Huffington Post, Entrepreneur.com, CNN) rank across topics only because they have so many links (as of 2017-07-26).
