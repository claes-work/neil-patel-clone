# 3 Powerful SEO Tips To Rank #1 On Google

## Summary

In this solo tutorial, Neil Patel gives three tactics for ranking #1 on Google as of 2017: (1) make content exhaustively thorough on a single niche, (2) optimize the title tag and meta description to include the target keyword while remaining appealing and curiosity-evoking, and (3) use the free Google Search Console to find keywords a page already gets impressions for and weave them naturally into the copy. He ties tips one and three together — mining Search Console impression data to deepen existing articles — and illustrates it with an Instagram article he expanded from ~2,500 to ~10,000 words, more than tripling its traffic within 50 days.

## Key claims

- As of 2017-11-28, Neil states that ranking #1 on Google is genuinely hard now, whereas 10 years earlier it was easy — back then you could rank simply by putting the keywords on your website.
- He recounts starting in SEO at age 16 with a site called Advice Monkey (a poor replication of Monster.com) that got no traffic; he paid marketing firms to rank for terms like "job search," got no results, and lost his money, so he had to learn SEO himself.
- Tip 1 — Focus on content: Neil cites Google's Hummingbird update, saying that after it, sites covering everything don't do as well as sites that focus on one single niche and are super thorough.
- He argues thoroughness means answering every question a reader could have so they finish the article with no remaining questions and know exactly what to do next; his method is to "poke holes" in your content and fill them all.
- His dating-niche example: instead of a thin article that just says "go to Match.com and sign up," cover every detail (ideal picture, ideal description, how to be funny, adding video), then cover Tinder, Bumble, eHarmony and other platforms, plus how to message people and progress from first to second date — so the page becomes the end-all resource on online dating.
- Tip 2 — Optimize the title tag and meta description: Neil explains the title tag is the linked headline at the top of a search result and the meta description is the sentence below it.
- As of 2017-11-28, he states Google tracks who clicks on which listing and has learned that when the searched keyword appears in the listing, users are far more likely to click through — so include the target keyword in both the title and description.
- He warns that keyword stuffing (e.g., repeating "online dating" 20 times) does not work; the title and description must flow naturally, be easy to read, and be appealing or evoke curiosity, or no one clicks.
- Tip 3 — Use Google Search Console: Neil calls it a free tool from Google that effectively teaches you how to rank #1; he urges signing up and notes it takes a few days to populate data.
- He explains Search Console's Search Analytics shows the pages getting traffic, plus Impressions, Clicks, and Click-Through Rate — and, importantly, the keywords a page gets impressions and clicks for.
- As of 2017-11-28, Neil states that in general you are lucky to get around a 5% click-through rate (sometimes 10–15%), but most pages get less than 5%.
- He ties tips one and three together: take the keywords a page is getting impressions for in Search Console and add them into the copy to make the article more thorough — but naturally, not stuffed, or readers will bounce because the content "sucks."
- Case example: his Instagram article (teaching how to get over 300 targeted Instagram followers per day) started at roughly 2,500 words; after adding Search Console keywords he grew it to around 10,000 words, and traffic to that article more than tripled — with results visible within 50 days.
- He closes by challenging viewers to implement at least one tactic and report results in the comments within 45 days, suggesting the second tactic (title/description) as the easiest starting point.
