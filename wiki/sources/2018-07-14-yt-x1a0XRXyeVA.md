# Why Kids Should NEVER Start a Business + (Do THIS Instead)

## Summary

An interview-style monologue where Neil is asked what he'd do as a 16-17-year-old today to
start a business. His answer is deliberately counterintuitive: **nothing — he wouldn't start
a business at 16, 17, or 18.** Reflecting on his own path (he started at 16 and made many
mistakes and failures), he now advocates **intrapreneurship** — being an entrepreneur inside
someone else's company first. He wishes he had started his career working for and learning
from others, so he'd know what mistakes to avoid. His concrete playbook for a young person:
find a company (even virtual), volunteer / work free for a month or two or intern, then
convince them to pay you; apprentice, learn all you can, and hop to another mentor when you
stop learning; only once you feel ready go start your own business. He notes companies
encourage this (citing Google's ~20% time for personal projects). The upside of learning
from others first: you know what not to do, and you build a network — relationships,
business-development contacts, partnerships, and people you can raise money from — that a
16-year-old starting from scratch lacks. He caveats that outlier founders (Mark Zuckerberg)
exist and rates himself "not even 1/1000" of Zuckerberg's monetary success, but says most
people are better off learning first. Closes with his recurring "I don't care for the money,
leave a comment and I'll help" offer.
