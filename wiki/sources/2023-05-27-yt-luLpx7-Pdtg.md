# Navigating Growth: Essential Tips for Expanding Your Small Business

## Summary
In-person Brazil interview with finance creator Thiago Nigro, who opens by crediting Neil as
one of his earliest SEO references from his own Rico/Rico XP days. **NP Digital's global
footprint** (as stated 2023-05-27): "getting close to 800 people globally," just added
Germany, with France, Italy, Spain, and Portugal expansion imminent. **Origin of the Brazil
office**: Neil traces it to being invited to speak at a Hotmart conference years earlier,
seeing outsized market potential, and a long (2-3 hour), purposeless-turned-pivotal
conversation with **Andre Esteves** (founder of BTG Pactual), who told him Brazil was "the
wild, wild west... like it was in America" — after which Neil decided to expand, initially
serving US public companies entering Brazil before winning large Brazil-based (including
publicly traded) clients directly. States NP Digital's **first employee was hired 5 years and
1 month before this interview** (i.e. ~April 2018, officially in the US; the Brazil office
itself was stood up earlier by a colleague named "Marine," ahead of the formal US company).
On failure: recounts funding a pre-AWS **cloud-computing startup roughly 18 years before this
interview** (i.e. ~2005) with "over a million dollars" of his own money that "didn't work
out" — framing it as a correct thesis (cloud computing became a $1T+ category via AWS) undone
by poor execution, specifically staffing it with people who weren't subject-matter experts,
which he generalizes into a broader principle about the cost of hiring non-experts even when
the underlying idea is sound. On why his current ventures succeed where past ones failed, he
frames it as **"cheating"**: (1) he now starts with capital ($5M of his own money into NP
Digital "from day one," another $8.6M of his own money "in February" on an acquisition — the
AnswerThePublic deal, elsewhere dated Feb 2023 — meaning he doesn't need investor/board
approval to move fast); (2) two decades of accumulated mistake-avoidance; (3) **hiring
leadership with a proven track record**, illustrated at length by **NP Digital CEO Mike
Gullickson**: previously president of **iProspect** (owned by Japanese conglomerate Dentsu,
~60-70K employees globally, with Gullickson's division alone at ~5,000), before that ran a
company in the same space that sold for **$50M+**, and before *that* headed sales/operations
at a company sold to **Hearst** for an estimated **$300-400M** roughly a decade-plus prior —
Neil frames "already successfully done it, more than once" as his specific hiring bar (echoes
and is roughly consistent with the entity-page record of this history, which also carries an
unreconciled name-spelling variant, "Gullaksen," and a differing $325M Hearst-sale figure
from another 2022 source — see [[../entities/mike-gullickson]]). On confidence: describes
himself as believing "I can do anything," caveats that this is false in absolute terms
(explicitly not Elon-Musk-level, not Neymar/Messi/Ronaldo-level athletically), but claims
top-tier confidence specifically *within marketing*, which he ties to continuous learning
from anyone regardless of seniority or track record ("some of the best ideas come from young
people who don't have a proven track record"). Advises aspiring entrepreneurs to first work
for an existing company to learn functional skills (marketing, fulfillment, customer success,
operations) and to deliberately try many things before committing, arguing talent alone isn't
enough without also enjoying the work (or the required hours won't get put in). Closes with a
plain-language **SEO definition** for the host's audience: organic (non-paid, algorithmically
ranked) results below the "sponsored" paid results, driven by keyword-targeted content,
on-page and code-level optimization, and inbound links/social shares — cites "over 200
factors" in Google's ranking algorithm.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> So now when I create businesses it's almost like I'm cheating... I've been an entrepreneur
> for over 20 years and I have money. So when I started this current business, I put $5
> million of my own money from day one.

> The reason our business didn't work out well was great idea, poor execution. It was the
> wrong people who weren't subject matter experts.

> I think I can do anything and I know that's not true... But in business I think I'm one of
> the best... within my space. Okay, so my space is marketing.

> If you keep hiring people who have a track record of being successful in your space,
> you're much more likely to succeed.

> Being open minded and always learning from people doesn't matter how much money they have,
> but being open to learn from anyone has really helped me succeed.
