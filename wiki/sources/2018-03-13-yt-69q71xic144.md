# How to Raise Venture Capital | Entice Investors to Fund Your Startup Idea

_Neil solo, direct-to-camera. All persona data._

## Summary
Neil's playbook for raising venture capital, opening with the dilution logic ("would you
rather own 100% of a company that makes $100K/year or 50% of one that makes $10M/year?").
He says he's **raised in excess of $20M** over his career and learned **not to raise more
than you need** (plus a little padding, "because things always go wrong"). His central thesis:
**VC is a relationship business, not a cold-pitch business** — early on he pitched strangers
("next best thing since sliced bread") and they ghosted him; investors back **people they
know**, because ideas pivot (he cites **Twitter starting as Odeo**, a podcasting company).
So **network first** at events like **TechCrunch** conferences and **Mashable** meetups in
hub cities (SF, Boston, NYC), provide value, get feedback, *then* pitch. Tactics: build a
**deck** (he points to a **Dave McClure** SlideShare fundraising deck as an evergreen model);
show up with **passion** and **co-founders** (a business person should find an engineer; an
engineer should find a marketer/designer — aim for **2–3 co-founders total**); pitch a **big
idea** (VCs want the next Uber/Airbnb/Google, "they'd rather you go for a gigantic idea or
lose money," and they take **20–30% per round**). Finally, **always be fundraising** — raise
**a year before** you run out of cash (most wait until ~6 months out) to keep a healthy
reserve and negotiating leverage.

## Key claims
As of 2018-03-13, Neil states:
- He has **raised in excess of $20M** across his career, and believes you should **raise only
  a little more than you need** — over-raising can hand the company to VCs if it fails.
- **VC is about who you know**: build a relationship and provide value *before* pitching;
  cold-pitching strangers "in most cases ends up with a disaster."
- Investors back **people, not ideas**, because ideas change/pivot — example: **Twitter began
  as Odeo, a podcasting company**.
- **Where to meet investors**: TechCrunch conferences, Mashable meetups, startup events in
  SF / Boston / NYC.
- **Use a deck** — he recommends a **Dave McClure** SlideShare fundraising deck as still valid.
- You need **passion** and **co-founders** (complementary skills; ~2–3 total including
  yourself) or raising is very hard.
- **Pitch a big idea** — VCs seek the next Uber/Airbnb/eBay/Google/Microsoft; a company that
  does $20M/yr and sells for $50M "doesn't do much for them." They take **20–30% of the
  company each round** and expect you to keep raising.
- First round can be **a few million with no revenue**, but after that you must generate
  revenue and **always be fundraising ~1 year before running out** (most do ~6 months, which
  is riskier).
