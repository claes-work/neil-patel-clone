# Why Kylie Jenner is a Different Kind of Celebrity (Marketing School)

## Summary
A co-hosted, banter-heavy Marketing School episode debating "the best type of fame." Neil argues the best fame is being well known inside a massive TAM (total addressable market) — it's not about the number of people but the number of dollars — using Kylie Jenner / Kylie Cosmetics (famous in the high-dollar beauty niche) as the model, backed by influencer-income benchmarks by follower tier. Eric counters with "niche fame" — being famous within a lucrative niche gives you power in the industry while protecting your privacy/safety (recognized at conferences, left alone in the real world), citing Sam Altman in AI as the current example. The two converge on a shared thesis: don't get so broadly famous that safety suffers, do get niche-famous where the money is, and the real money is in building a product business, not a personal brand. Neil's spiciest claim (notable given his own personal brand) is that the real money is not made in personal branding — you earn far more building a Google, Coke, SpaceX, Tesla, Kylie Cosmetics, or Skims — leading into a dupe-economy aside (Skims copycats from the same warehouses; a no-logo Loro Piana factory in Italy). Roughly half the talk time is Eric.

## Key claims
- The best type of fame is being really well known inside a massive TAM (total addressable market): the bigger the TAM, the more dollars are being spent — and it's not about the amount of people, it's about the amount of dollars. Kylie Jenner / Kylie Cosmetics is the model (famous in beauty/hair extensions/lipstick), which is why she pushed the company to be more beauty-oriented rather than leaning on general fame. (Neil, 2024-02-07)
- Influencer-income benchmarks (from a dataset of influencers Neil reviewed): the average influencer earns ~$323.19/month; those with 100,000–999,999 followers average ~$1,727/month; those with over 1M followers average ~$6,198/month. (Neil, 2024-02-07) `(the 100k–999k figure was garbled in captions as "$1,727 129" — dollar amount reconstructed as ~$1,727/month; treat the cents as uncertain)`
- The big mistake creators make is accumulating followers around something that is hard to monetize (e.g. bikini or swim-trunk modeling photos) — you can be a male/female model, but there just isn't much capital in people paying you to pose; the audience doesn't convert to meaningful revenue. (Neil, 2024-02-07)
- The real money is not made in personal branding: despite people pointing to Tony Robbins, LeBron James, or the Kardashians, you make far more money building a product business — a Google, a Coke, a SpaceX, a Tesla, a Kylie Cosmetics, or a Skims — that doesn't revolve around a personal brand. (Neil, 2024-02-07) — notable/spicy given Neil's own career is built on his personal brand.
- Brian Dean example: Neil praises Brian Dean (formerly Backlinko, sold to Semrush; now Exploding Topics) for launching his first-ever brand YouTube channel under the corporate name Exploding Topics rather than under himself — the debut video broke down "three products generating a million per month right now" (fitness gummies, a pet air purifier, an electrolyte mix), analyzing reviews, purchases, star ratings, revenue, and TikTok view counts. Selling Backlinko to Semrush was a "win-win." (Neil, 2024-02-07)
- Dupe economy: Skims-type products go viral on TikTok because dupes are made in the same warehouses at roughly 1/34 the cost with no meaningful product difference beyond the label; as a real-world parallel, at a Loro Piana factory in Italy you can buy the same garments with no logo for about a tenth of the price. (Neil, 2024-02-07)
- Mr Beast has enormous view counts but relatively low revenue-per-view because his mass/wide audience forces him into thin-margin products (chocolate, burgers); the same view volume in a high-value niche (e.g. Enterprise software) would be worth vastly more — he'd be "worth over a hundred billion." (Neil, 2024-02-07) `(attribution: likely Neil — it restates his TAM/dollars-not-people thesis — but the Mr Beast exchange is traded back-and-forth with Eric, so treat as co-articulated)`

## Eric Siu (co-host, context — NOT persona data)
[[wiki/entities/eric-siu]]
- His counter-thesis is "niche fame": being famous within a niche (like Neil in the marketing space) gives you a lot of power in that industry while letting you protect your privacy — you get "swarmed" just enough at conferences for the dopamine hit, but you're not bothered out in the real world where your safety could be at risk. Kylie is also "famous for being famous," but her money comes from the niche. (Eric, 2024-02-07)
- Self-deprecating fame benchmark: Eric says he analyzed their autograph/photo line sizes at joint events (e.g. the HubSpot event, Marketing School on stage) and his line is always ~25% the size of Neil's — 100 people for Neil equals ~25 for Eric. (Eric, 2024-02-07) — a claim about Neil's relative fame, but Eric's.
- Noah, Eric's videographer, runs a YouTube channel called "Bikini Yoga" (~200,000 subs, women doing yoga in bikinis, growing rapidly) — but the audience is ~99% male and hard to monetize, illustrating the "big audience, nothing to sell" problem. (Eric, 2024-02-07)
- Sam Altman is "niche famous" in AI right now: highly respected and recognizable in tech because everyone talks about ChatGPT, but not yet as universally known as a Bill Gates, Jeff Bezos, or Elon Musk (who were/are among the richest in the world) — though he could get there. (Eric, 2024-02-07)
- Frames the shared takeaway: there are two ends of the spectrum — don't be so famous that your safety/privacy is jeopardized, but do aim to be niche-famous in a niche with a lot of money in it. Mr Beast has to sell chocolate because his audience is so wide, which is the consequence of making broad viral videos (a real-life Squid Game, walking 10 hours in big shoes); he's now "famous for being famous." (Eric, 2024-02-07)
