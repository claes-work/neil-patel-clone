# How to Use LinkedIn for Deals & Sales - Module 2 - Lesson 1 - LinkedIn Unlocked

## Summary
LinkedIn Unlocked Module 2 Lesson 1: a strategic-alliances/partnerships framework framed
through LinkedIn as the discovery tool for finding partners and decision-makers. Neil
cites survey stats on alliance adoption and ROI, lists alliance types (joint ventures,
affiliate marketing, co-branding, licensing, cross-sells), and walks through vetting
criteria (complementary strengths, track record, decision-maker access) before closing
with execution/measurement advice. He restates his own international-expansion-via-local-
alliances model (Brazil, India) as a live example of the framework.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-11-02, Neil cites a survey (unnamed "enterprise.com") reporting alliance
  users see 49% higher reported net worth, 21% higher revenues, and 38% increased
  productivity; also cites 90% of surveyed corporate executives calling alliances/joint
  ventures essential to competitive advantage, and >20%/year growth in alliance counts
  (~10,000 new alliances/year), with >20% of top 2,000 US/European companies' revenue
  attributed to alliances (self-reported/third-party stats, not independently verified
  in this video).
- As of 2020-11-02, restates his NP Digital international-expansion model: expanding
  into regions like Brazil and India via local alliance partners who take equity/"skin
  in the game," compared to a majority-owned-but-locally-operated franchise-like model
  rather than a fully controlled subsidiary (paraphrase; reinforces the international-
  expansion origin story already in the wiki, e.g. synthesis pass 8's Google-employee-
  friend/Brazil-first entry).
- As of 2020-11-02, favorite alliance type stated as affiliate marketing, reasoning: the
  partner bears marketing risk/cost and is only paid on results (paraphrase, restates
  the existing affiliate/results-based-cost doctrine).
- As of 2020-11-02, cites Bill Gates/Microsoft's early IBM partnership (building MS-DOS/
  Windows) as an example of a smaller company out-growing a larger partner via alliance,
  arguing alliances aren't "archaic" just because incumbents also use them (third-party
  historical example, paraphrase).
