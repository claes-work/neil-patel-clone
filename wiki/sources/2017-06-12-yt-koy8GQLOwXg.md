# The Insider's Guide to Writing Clickbait Titles - The Secret Ingredient of Viral Marketing

## Summary
In this short 2017 tactical video, Neil Patel argues that most readers never get past the headline — eight out of ten people read the headline but only two of ten click through to the article — and that the fix is writing catchier headlines. He grounds the advice in having personally reviewed over two thousand blog posts, and (self-deprecatingly noting he is dyslexic and not the best writer) says doing something long enough teaches you what not to do. His concrete tactics: keep titles to roughly six to seven words because three words is too little to signal what the reader gets while twenty words is overwhelming and, importantly, a six-to-seven-word title usually won't get cut off in Google search results (a cut-off title means less search traffic); use adjectives to lift click-through; consider adding the year (e.g., "updated 2017"/"updated 2020") on detailed evergreen pieces like a beginner's guide so it reads as fresh, while noting this won't fit every post; use Portent's title generator to spin up snazzy title ideas from a keyword; google "Copyblogger headline formula" for plug-in templates; and above all know your reader base — because his readers are marketers just starting out, titles about getting more search traffic without spending money earn heavy social shares, click-throughs and reads. Combining these tactics, he says, produces catchy titles people actually read and click.

## Key claims
- Eight out of ten people read your headline but only two out of ten click through to read the rest of the article; the reason is headlines that aren't catchy (as of 2017-06-12).
- Neil ran over two thousand blog posts, and doing something long enough teaches you what not to do even if you're not a strong writer (as of 2017-06-12).
- Blog post titles of roughly six to seven words tend to perform better than three-word or twenty-word titles (as of 2017-06-12).
- Titles that are too short (about three words) don't explain what the reader will get, while overly long titles are overwhelming and go unread (as of 2017-06-12).
- A six-to-seven-word title tends to fit well and in most cases won't be cut off in Google search results; a cut-off title costs you search traffic (as of 2017-06-12).
- Using adjectives in a headline gets more people to click on it (as of 2017-06-12).
- Adding the year to a title (e.g., "updated 2017" / "updated 2020") drives more clicks by signaling freshness, but only fits certain posts such as detailed guides — not every blog post (as of 2017-06-12).
- Portent's title generator lets you enter a keyword and returns snazzy title ideas (as of 2017-06-12).
- Googling "Copyblogger headline formula" gives you a list of templates where you plug in your keywords to get headlines that work (as of 2017-06-12).
- Knowing your reader base is essential: Neil's readers are marketers just starting off, so titles about getting more search traffic without spending money earn lots of social shares, click-throughs and reads (as of 2017-06-12).
