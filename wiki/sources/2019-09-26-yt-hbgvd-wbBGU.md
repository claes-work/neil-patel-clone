# How to Monetize Your Blog Without Destroying Your User Experience

## Summary

Neil argues that monetizing a blog does not have to degrade user experience — the damage comes from irrelevant and excessive ads, not from monetization itself. His core UX rule is to **limit banner ads to one or two per page** and keep them **relevant**, since banner blindness stems from irrelevance, and too many ads slow site speed. He reframes **pop-ups** as poor direct-sales tools but strong **email-capture** tools: offer a free resource (e-book, tool, quiz, cheat sheet) in exchange for an email, then monetize indirectly through **email drip sequences**, and fire the pop-up on **exit intent** (recommending **Hello Bar**). He recommends **retargeting/remarketing** your users with relevant offers — your own or partner companies' products — while being transparent in your privacy policy and terms of service, and building **custom audiences** on Facebook/Google once sales come in. He stresses only presenting **highly relevant offers** to protect audience trust, avoiding spammy ad networks in favor of **solo ads** or larger networks like Google. Finally he lists higher-yield alternatives to ads: **info products** (cites Pat Flynn's podcasting course), **coaching**, **gated content** (TechCrunch), **freemium tools** upselling into paid apps, and **speaking gigs**, where he says he earns 25k–50k per hour-long talk.

## Key claims

- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said monetizing a blog does not inherently destroy user experience — the belief that it does is "not true," and the real culprits are irrelevance and ad overload.
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said to limit banner ads to **one or two per page**; more than that turns a site into "a site full of ads instead of text," slows load time, and hurts UX.
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said **banner blindness is caused by irrelevant ads**, not banners per se — relevant ads still get clicks.
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said **pop-ups rarely generate direct sales** but work very well for capturing **emails** when they offer a valuable free resource, which you then monetize via email drip sequences.
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said pop-ups should fire on **exit intent** (when someone is leaving, not entering), and recommended **Hello Bar** to build them.
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said you can **retarget/remarket** to your users with relevant offers — your own or other companies' — but should disclose this transparently in the privacy policy and terms of service, and then build **custom audiences** on Facebook/Google.
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said to only present **highly relevant offers** and never push products you don't believe in, because irrelevant offers destroy audience trust; he suggested **solo ads** as an alternative to spammy ad networks.
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said **non-ad monetization** methods make more money and are less spammy: info products (cites **Pat Flynn's Power-Up Podcasting**), coaching (cites **Mike Matthews / Legion Athletics**), gated content (cites **TechCrunch**), and freemium tools upselling into paid apps (his own example: a free SEO audit tool capped at 50–100 pages).
- As of 2019-09-26, Neil said **speaking gigs** are highly lucrative — he rarely speaks for free and earns **$25k on the low end to $50k in most cases** for a one-hour speech, having started at only a few thousand and worked his way up.
