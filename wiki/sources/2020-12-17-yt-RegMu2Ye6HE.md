# How to Beat Google by Leveraging User Metrics

## Summary
Neil argues user-engagement signals (click-through rate above all) can outweigh
backlinks and on-page SEO in Google's ranking decisions, opening with the marginal-
gains philosophy of British cycling coach Dave Brailsford (many small 1% edges compound
into a win) and Rand Fishkin's now-famous "click the second result, not the first"
Twitter SEO experiment (a page moved from position 4 to 1 in ~70 minutes purely from
directed click behavior). The bulk of the video is a data-driven CTR-copywriting
checklist sourced from a Clickflow/Brian Dean (Backlinko) study plus Ubersuggest data,
giving specific lift percentages for title-tag tactics: question-format titles,
character-count sweet spots, exact-match-keyword URLs, power words, emotional titles,
meta descriptions, year-stamped titles, curiosity gaps, and educational framing.
Closes with a tip to mine paid-ad copy (optimized for Google's Quality Score/CTR) as a
free source of high-converting language for organic titles.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-12-17, restates the standing thesis that user signals (especially CTR)
  can outrank pages with superior backlink profiles and on-page SEO — "nothing beats
  user signals, because user signals tell Google definitively what they like and what
  they don't" (paraphrase + quote below).
- Cites Rand Fishkin's SEO experiment (attributed to Fishkin, sourced from an external
  study, dated by Neil as "years ago... still very relevant today," not independently
  re-verified here): directing Twitter followers to search "best grilled steak,"
  click the #1 result, hit back, then click the Serious Eats listing moved that
  listing from position 4 to 1 in roughly 70 minutes, and it also gained rank on the
  broader term "grilled steak" (paraphrase, third-party case study Neil is citing).
- CTR-lift data points cited from a Clickflow/Brian Dean (Backlinko) study (external
  source, not independently verified, presented as established data): question-format
  titles +14.1% CTR; title length of 15–40 characters +8.6% CTR; exact keyword match
  in the URL +45% CTR; "power words" (e.g. "effortless," "amazing," "incredible")
  +13.9% CTR; titles conveying emotion +7.3% CTR; presence of a meta description
  +5.8% CTR; titles containing a year +4.9% CTR; curiosity-gap titles +5.9% CTR;
  educational-framed titles (vs. directly promotional) +10.4% CTR — with a live
  example citing an Olay/Think-with-Google case study on educational framing driving
  more clicks AND more sales than a directly promotional title.
- Practical tip: mine competitors' paid-ad copy for high-CTR language, reasoning that
  advertisers are already forced to optimize ad copy for Google's Quality Score (CTR-
  driven) or pay more per click — so winning ad copy is a proxy for what drives
  clicks organically too (paraphrase).
- Notes ranking movement from title/meta changes typically takes about 30 days to show
  up after Google re-indexes (paraphrase, standing operational guidance).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Google wants to rank websites that you love. Their goal isn't to rank a website
> because it has a million more links on another site, they want to rank a website
> because you, the user, loves it.

> Just because you have more backlinks or better on-page SEO doesn't mean you'll
> outrank someone — nothing beats user signals, because user signals tells Google
> definitively what they like and what they don't.

> Your rankings can go up without building any extra links, or without doing really
> any on-page SEO. It's just simple copy hacks.
