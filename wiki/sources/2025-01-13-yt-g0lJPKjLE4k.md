# How This Influencer Gained 300k IG Followers in One Week (Marketing School)

## Summary

A short, light co-hosted Marketing School episode. Neil (the main talker) breaks down how creator Chloe Shih gained ~300k Instagram followers in a week: it started with a viral video of her being terminated from her Discord job, but the sustained growth comes from extremely high-production, highly-edited, warm/"inviting" videos on wide-TAM topics (e.g., how to be more confident), with extra-mile production tricks like appearing in multiple outfits at once and a snap-of-the-fingers outfit switch — "she makes hard things look easy." Neil relays that Instagram itself told him higher-quality videos tend to get more reach. He then pivots to his own follower growth, which came mainly from being tagged when he guests on other people's podcasts/interviews, and recounts his viral School of Hard Knocks street-interview clip (a clip is replayed): asked how he'd make $1M in a year from zero, he answered "buy a boring/ugly business from someone retiring — roofing, plumbing, HVAC — add technology and automate what they're not doing." That clip did 16M+ views and, via the host tagging him, netted ~5,000 followers within a few hours. Neil then defends against hater claims that his dad worked at Goldman Sachs (his father worked at an ordinary bank, bottom of the totem pole). Eric prompts the topics, agrees/adds, reads the Agency Owners Association sponsor spot, and drives a political tangent on the MAGA-vs-tech-right H-1B visa fight (Elon, David Sacks), which Neil closes by affirming that brilliant immigrants have helped shape America.

## Key claims
- Creator Chloe Shih gained ~300k Instagram followers in one week (roughly ~100k → the week's gain, then ~200k more, reaching ~600k); the initial spike came from a viral video of her being terminated from her job at Discord. (Neil, 2025-01-13) — figures approximate/recalled; arithmetic is loose.
- Her sustained growth is driven by super high-production, highly-edited videos with a warm, inviting style, on wide-TAM topics (e.g., how to be more confident) and articulate delivery. (Neil, 2025-01-13)
