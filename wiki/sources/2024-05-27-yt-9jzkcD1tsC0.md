# OpenAI's marketing head on CMOs' biggest AI mistake, and Sam Altman on in-person experience's future (Marketing School)

## Summary
Reacting to a Fortune interview with OpenAI's marketing head, this episode develops Neil's clearest
public statement (to this point in the ledger) of his AI-and-marketing-jobs thesis, paired with a
detailed, first-person defense of why his own site traffic has declined — a deliberate strategic
choice, not a failure. Also covers Sam Altman's "community will matter more" prediction and an
in-person-business-development anecdote from a recent Brazil trip (including a poker game with the
president of Burger King Brazil). Strong persona material: dated AI opinion, a direct rebuttal to
public criticism of his own numbers, and concrete Enterprise-lead-gen tactics.

## Key claims
- AI memo thesis (Neil wrote an internal memo on this): over the next 5–10 years the top ~5% of
  marketers — the most strategic, creative, innovative — will keep their jobs and increasingly
  direct AI agents (5, 10, 100, even 1,000 per client) to do the repetitive work; the CMO role
  itself won't disappear because strategy comes from pattern recognition built on experience, and
  businesses will always need to sell things. (Neil, 2024-05-27)
- AI will free up marketer time from boring/mundane tasks (keyword research, on-page fixes, title
  tags, stale-content flagging) so people can focus on higher-leverage work AI can't easily do;
  marketers who adapt will earn a higher ROI and get paid more, while those who refuse to adapt
  ("regulation's going to slap it away") will be left behind. (Neil, 2024-05-27)
- Distinguishes the internet ("a network") from AI ("a computer") — AI's real bottleneck is compute
  and energy, at a scale far beyond what a human brain can do (a brain can power roughly a light
  bulb's worth of energy). (Neil, 2024-05-27)
- Historical analogy: the Industrial Age displaced jobs but people reskilled into new ones; Neil
  predicts a similar pattern with AI, plus a psychological twist — people who lose jobs to AI and
  get leisure time will realize leisure wasn't actually what they wanted, and will look for
  creative outlets instead. (Neil, 2024-05-27)
- Sam Altman reportedly said community (in-person and online) is one of the few things that will
  get *stronger*, not weaker, over the next 5–10 years, even as AI produces things like "virtual
  girlfriends" — cited approvingly. (Neil, reporting Altman, 2024-05-27)
- **Direct rebuttal to public criticism of declining traffic:** Neil states plainly that his
  traffic has gone down, and explains why on purpose — NP Digital stopped chasing rankings for
  generic high-volume keywords (e.g. "Pinterest," "Instagram," a crypto-community platform name he
  couldn't recall) that brought irrelevant SMB traffic instead of Enterprise buyers. The company
  deliberately shifted marketing channels from traffic/brand-awareness metrics to Enterprise-lead
  and referral generation: PR/coverage in outlets CMOs actually read (Ad Age, Forrester), original
  research/data pitched to press ahead of public release, in-person conference talks paired with
  private lunches for invited decision-makers, and direct RFP generation. In Brazil specifically,
  traffic dropped to roughly one-third of its peak by design, while the market now generates
  roughly 3 RFPs a month from billion-dollar-plus corporations. (Neil, 2024-05-27)
- A paid-ads test example cited live on air: a regional NP Digital paid-ads test spent $36K and
  closed $82K in signed business — profitable, but Neil flags it as "expensive" and wants a better
  return, noting the number improves once early failed tests are excluded. (Neil, 2024-05-27)
- Frames the takeaway as "marketing 101": traffic without relevance is vanity; the goal is reaching
  the specific people (CMOs, CEOs, decision-makers) who actually buy, wherever they hang out.
  (Neil, 2024-05-27)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Some people will tell me — they'll post online — "Neil, your traffic's going down, your traffic's
> going down, you're doing a terrible job at marketing." And I'm like, yeah, the traffic's going
> down. We stopped ranking for generic keywords... we had tons and tons of traffic, it was a lot of
> SMBs hitting us up — which I love SMBs — but we were rarely getting invited to RFPs or closing
> multi-million-dollar contracts. So we shifted our marketing from "let's focus on getting more
> website traffic and brand awareness" to "let's focus on getting more Enterprise leads and
> referrals."

> Traffic's great, but if it doesn't drive an ROI, who gives a crap? ... And when I say brand, I'm
> not talking about your brand like Nike — like, oh yeah, more people know me when I walk down the
> street, who gives a crap if that doesn't cause more revenue? It doesn't matter.

> It's all about testing and doing what drives an ROI, not what builds up your brand or your ego.

> The CMO job is probably not going to go away, because as long as people need to sell things,
> there's going to need to be marketing.
