# Your Blog Won't Get Traffic Unless You do This

## Summary
Neil Patel argues that blog-content saturation (he cites "over 1 billion blogs," roughly one blog per
7.5 people) means new content rarely needs to be topically novel — it needs to be presented in a new
*format*. He illustrates the saturation problem with a search-volume-vs-results ratio: "auto insurance"
returns over 1.2 billion results in the US against only ~165,000 monthly US searches for the term, a
~7,272:1 results-to-searches ratio he says is typical across topics. His fix: take well-known information
(his example — cheetah running speed, something "everyone already knows") and present it in a novel
format, such as an animated infographic, rather than trying to say something no one has said before. He
cites The Oatmeal as an example of a blog that built its audience through interactive infographics and
quizzes on already-familiar premises (e.g., "how would you survive against a T-Rex chained to a bunk
bed"), arguing that format novelty — not topic novelty — is what earns backlinks, social shares, and
viral traffic. He allows that genuinely new content is still worth pursuing when possible, but frames
format innovation as the more reliable, repeatable lever for most bloggers. Closes with the standard NP
Digital content-marketing agency pitch.

## Key claims
- There are over 1 billion blogs in existence, roughly one blog per 7.5 people, making blogging
  extremely saturated (as of 2022-06-30, unsourced figure presented as fact).
- "Auto insurance" has over 1.2 billion search results in the US against ~165,000 monthly US searches for
  the term — a roughly 7,272:1 results-to-searches ratio Neil presents as broadly representative of
  competitive topics (as of 2022-06-30, unsourced figure presented as fact).
- The core content strategy: most content should reuse already-familiar information but present it in a
  new *format* (infographic, interactive quiz, animation) rather than chase topical novelty, which is
  rarer and harder (as of 2022-06-30).
- The Oatmeal is cited as a case study of a blog that grew via format-novel content (interactive
  infographics/quizzes on familiar premises) rather than new information (as of 2022-06-30).
- Format-novel presentation of familiar information is claimed to drive more backlinks, social shares,
  and viral traffic than restating the same old information in the same old way (as of 2022-06-30).

> I'm not saying that you have to create content that no one has ever written on before. Instead, I'm saying that you have to present the same old information in a new way.

> It doesn't have to be new, it just has to be presented in a new way.
