# 97% hit by Google's HCU, Newspaper publishers sue Microsoft/OpenAI, CNET+Best Buy, and more

## Summary
Continues a Google Helpful Content Update (HCU) discussion: citing Lily Ray-sourced data
(via BuiltWith, ~1,500 sites analyzed) that 97% of sites hit hardest by the update ran
ads/AdSense (40% used AdSense specifically), framed as evidence the update targeted poor
ad-heavy UX rather than "AI content" per se; both hosts note some good sites were
collaterally hit and some low-quality sites weren't. Neil states his SEO philosophy:
play the long game and build for user value, but diversify traffic sources because "even
Google themselves don't know where SEO is going in the next 5-10 years" — citing a
private conversation with an SEO-tool founder, a lunch with three other SEO
practitioners, and a meeting with a senior Google engineer, all of whom expressed
uncertainty about AI's long-term effect on organic search, though the engineer maintained
organic results won't disappear entirely because different query types need different
answer formats. Moves to Google Trends data comparing "Google" vs. "ChatGPT" search
volume: Neil argues ChatGPT's growth rate is slowing (OpenAI's total traffic MoM growth
cited as ~8% in the most recent month vs. faster prior months, per SimilarWeb) and that
usage spikes track specific feature launches rather than steady organic growth, while
Google's own search volume keeps climbing. Reports HCU-hit categories by average traffic
decline: news (-61%), pets, and adult sites the hardest-hit categories. Discusses
newspaper publishers suing Microsoft/OpenAI over copyright, contrasted with Google's
scraping practice, which Neil frames as more "symbiotic" because Google (mostly)
sends traffic back to sources — with a side-anecdote that an early rival, "Vo" (a San
Diego-based video-hosting company, name uncertain in captions), absorbed the legal risk
that "paved the way" for YouTube. Reacts to a CNET/Best Buy content-commerce partnership
(CNET owned by Red Ventures, described by Neil as "an agency like Eric and I" running
performance/affiliate deals) and to creator-monetization trends: relays a case study of
"Mike" (Legion Athletics, a supplements brand), estimating Mike's book/podcast/social
content personally drives $30-40K/month in trackable revenue, while ~700+ micro-
influencer partnerships (including Eric, cited by name as a paid lifestyle influencer for
the brand) drive a separately estimated $400-500K/month — used to argue influencer
marketing is a "buy low, sell high" opportunity because most creators are still
under-monetized. Neil discloses his own agency's revenue mix: NP Digital's first year of
revenue (~$5M) came almost entirely from his personal brand; by the second year
(~$16-18M) that dropped to ~10%; as the company scaled further, revenue attributable to
his personal brand capped around 10% ("for me it's capped at 10") — contrasted with
Kylie Jenner's much higher personal-brand revenue share — and framed as a deliberate
point that durable, large companies are built on "systems, processes, distribution,"
not personal-brand pull alone.
