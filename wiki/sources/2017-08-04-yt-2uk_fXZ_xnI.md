# How to Retarget Your Visitors and Drive Visitors Back to your Website

## Summary
Neil explains what retargeting (a.k.a. remarketing — he treats the terms as interchangeable) is and why it works, anchoring it to the "rule of seven": people need to see a brand/product roughly seven times before they are much more likely to buy. He argues that first-time visitors rarely convert — on the high end maybe five out of a hundred buy, realistically one to three out of a hundred — so retargeting recaptures the rest. Mechanically, a visitor is "pixeled" when they land on your site, and ad platforms then show them your ads as they browse elsewhere on the web (he calls it "a friendly way of stalking"), while noting the ad companies protect user privacy and no sensitive data is collected. He names Facebook Ads as the main retargeting platform and Google AdWords as second. His central example is Overstock.com: shopping for a ~$1,000 mattress after buying a home, he declined to buy, then saw retargeted banner ads for that mattress across sites like TechCrunch, and watched Overstock progressively drop the price by ~$100 increments to lure him back. He closes with a self-reported spend figure: roughly $200,000/month on paid advertising for his personal blog, of which about $40,000 goes to retargeting — offered as proof of how effective it is.

## Key claims
As of 2017-08-04, Neil states:
- ★ The "rule of seven" holds that people who see your name/brand/product/service seven times are much more likely to buy, and marketers worldwide have observed that more exposure raises conversion likelihood (as of 2017-08-04).
- First-time website visitors are unlikely to buy: on the high end about five out of a hundred convert, and realistically only one to three out of a hundred buy (as of 2017-08-04).
- Retargeting and remarketing are interchangeable terms for the same tactic (as of 2017-08-04).
- Retargeting works by placing a pixel on your site so that when a visitor browses elsewhere on the web, ad platforms can show them ads for your company — "following them around" (as of 2017-08-04).
- The two main retargeting ad platforms are Facebook Ads (primary) and Google AdWords (second) (as of 2017-08-04).
- Retargeting does not collect sensitive customer information; the ad companies are sophisticated about protecting user privacy (as of 2017-08-04).
- ★ Overstock.com example: after he declined a ~$1,000 mattress, their retargeted banner ads showed that mattress at ~$1,000 and then progressively dropped the price by ~$100 increments to draw him back — an illustration of dynamic/price-drop retargeting (as of 2017-08-04).
- Neil spends roughly $200,000 per month on paid advertising for his personal blog, of which roughly $40,000 goes toward retargeting (self-reported) (as of 2017-08-04).
