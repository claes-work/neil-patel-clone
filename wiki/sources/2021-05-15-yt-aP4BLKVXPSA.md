# Funding Scale and Growth: Beyond the Series B Round - Growth Hacking Unlocked

## Summary
Growth Hacking Unlocked, second fundraising lesson (follows the 2021-05-13 Series A/B
lesson in this same batch), focused on later-stage funding: Series D/E/F, M&A, IPO,
leveraged buyouts, and secondary offerings. Opens by naming the wealthiest people in the
world (Bezos, Arnault, Musk, Gates) as a framing device for how large fortunes are built
via startups that raise, then exit through sale, leverage, or IPO — citing the GoPro
founder, Mark Cuban (Broadcast.com sold to Yahoo for $5.7B in stock), and Kevin O'Leary
(TLC acquired by Mattel for $4.2B in 1999) as examples. Restates Series A rounds now
commonly run $10–20M+, Series B rounds average ~$32M. Discusses late-stage dynamics: few
companies reach Series D/E/F, so pent-up demand inflates their valuations — cites UiPath's
late private round briefly exceeding its IPO valuation, and WeWork as a cautionary tale
that made private investors more focused on unit economics and long-term viability.
Explains M&A mechanics with examples (Facebook/Instagram, Facebook/WhatsApp,
Google/YouTube, Microsoft/Skype+GitHub+LinkedIn, Google/Wildfire for ~$450M in July 2012)
and frames M&A pricing as a negotiation driven by unique technology and company size.
Contrasts valuation tiers across Private Equity (cashes out founders), M&A, and public
markets (IPO commands the highest valuations) and explains why acquirers profit when
their own public market multiple exceeds the multiple paid for the target. Closes by
telling early-stage viewers not to over-index on exit strategy yet and to focus on growth
tactics (search, affiliate, social, ads, LinkedIn, Clubhouse, email, push, messenger bots)
covered in the next three lessons; plugs neilpatel.com/training and NP Digital.

- Series A rounds now commonly run $10–20M+ (up from lower historical norms); Series B

  agency-entrepreneurship. Consistent with the same figures given in the 2021-05-13
  Series A/B lesson.

  stage drives valuations up due to pent-up investor demand — cites UiPath's late private
  round valuation briefly exceeding its IPO valuation as an example (self-reported,
  2021-05-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Cites the WeWork situation as having taught private investors to focus more on unit
  economics and long-term company potential rather than valuation momentum alone
  (paraphrase, third-party context, 2021-05-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- M&A pricing is framed as a negotiation: better/unique technology and larger company
  size both command better acquisition pricing (paraphrase, 2021-05-15) —
  agency-entrepreneurship.
- Google acquired Wildfire in July 2012 for roughly $450M per reported figures
  (third-party/reported, not self-reported — 2021-05-15) — agency-entrepreneurship
  (context, not about Neil).
- Valuation hierarchy across exit paths: Private Equity (cashes out founders) < M&A <
  public markets (IPO), with IPO typically commanding the highest valuations
  (paraphrase, 2021-05-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Advises early-stage founders not to over-focus on exit strategy and instead prioritize
  growth tactics across an omni-channel marketing mix (paraphrase, 2021-05-15) —
