# Alternative Marketing Methods Outperforming AI

## Summary
Second Digital Social Hour appearance (title is generic/AI-clickbait relative to the actual
range of topics covered). **AI timeline**: says he does not expect AI to replace marketing
humans within the next 5 years (pushes back on the hosts framing 60 months as "not that
long"), reasoning that AI output quality is bottlenecked by input quality — AI systems scrape
an imperfect, partially-inaccurate web, so even a "small percentage" of bad inputs corrupts
outputs, and this is a much harder problem to fix than raw model capability; frames the
correct posture as embracing AI as an efficiency tool (not fearing it) while acknowledging
those who refuse to engage with it risk being displaced by AI-fluent peers rather than by AI
itself directly. Says NP Digital uses OpenAI's APIs for internal decision support and (in a
smaller aside) for AI-drafted contracts reviewed by a lawyer rather than lawyer-drafted from
scratch. Predicts Google's Bard will eventually be strong given Google's larger raw
information advantage over OpenAI, but that being first-to-market matters less than usually
assumed — draws the same "Facebook wasn't the first social network, Google wasn't the first
search engine" argument recorded elsewhere in the corpus, and separately flags China as an
AI-race entrant not to be discounted. **2023/2024 opportunity picks**: names **podcasting**
as underrated specifically because of low competitive density (cites "less than 10 million
podcasts" against "over a billion blogs"); names **TikTok ad cost efficiency** — cites NP
Digital client data showing TikTok running 40-50% cheaper than Facebook ads for some
accounts, with a better-performing algorithm; and names **content freshness/updating** as the
"dead simplest" underused lever, using Wikipedia's constant-editing model as the example of
why frequently-updated pages retain search/social favor. Gives current NP Digital headcount
as **~750-800 people** ("seven-something... just in that company") with continued
international hiring (France, Italy, Germany, Singapore, a new head of LATAM covering Mexico/
Colombia/Chile/Argentina, and open head-of-Malaysia and head-of-Spain roles at time of
recording) — explicitly frames AI adoption internally as making staff "more efficient," not
as a replacement strategy, and says AI use is encouraged but not mandatory. On celebrity
endorsements: calls generic paid-endorsement deals largely wasteful because audience-product
fit is usually poor (a Kylie Jenner endorsement of NP Digital, his own hypothetical, would
generate irrelevant leads); praises the Kardashian family specifically for converting fame
into *owned* businesses rather than pure endorsement income, and cites **Ryan Reynolds/Mint
Mobile** (sold for $1.3B; Reynolds given equity rather than a flat endorsement fee) and
**LeBron James/Tonal** (equity-linked fitness-equipment partnership — Neil says he personally
bought a Tonal specifically because of the LeBron association) as the model that works:
alignment between the endorser's known domain and the product. Separately praises
**micro-influencers with a tightly aligned niche audience** (his example: a boxer with
250K Instagram followers endorsing a specific glove brand) as more effective than broad-reach
celebrities, because audience trust transfers only within the endorser's specialty. States
**LinkedIn is "massively underrated," especially for B2B**, and says it is NP Digital's
**#2 social revenue channel after YouTube** for its own consulting-lead-gen business. Lists
personal (non-agency) staff he credits by first name: nanny **Edith** (his wife's top pick),
driver **Philmon**, and housekeeper **Franny** (his own top pick, credited specifically for
packing his travel suitcase given his near-weekly work travel). Gives e-commerce tactical
advice: build upsell/downsell funnels aligned to the core product's use case (subscription
reminders for consumables like underwear/t-shirts, using his own 12-identical-white-t-shirts
habit as an example); prioritize SMS over email-only marketing, citing better current revenue
from text than email at NP Digital's e-commerce clients; add PayPal as a checkout option,
citing an observed **~18% revenue lift**; and use Survey Monkey-style qualitative surveys
(distinct from quantitative analytics) to learn *why* customers abandon pages, not just that
they do.

## Key claims
- States he does not expect AI to replace marketing-industry humans within 5 years, citing
  input-data-quality (garbage-in-garbage-out) as the binding constraint rather than model
  capability growth (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-05-29).
- Names podcasting as an underrated 2023/2024 marketing opportunity due to low competitive
  density relative to blogging (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-05-29).
- Cites NP Digital client data showing TikTok ads running 40-50% cheaper than Facebook ads
  for some accounts (self-reported/client data, dated 2023-05-29).
- Reports NP Digital headcount at ~750-800 employees with active expansion into France,
  Italy, Germany, Singapore, and LATAM (Mexico/Colombia/Chile/Argentina), plus open
  head-of-Malaysia and head-of-Spain searches (self-reported, dated 2023-05-29).
- States adding PayPal as a checkout option produces an observed ~18% revenue lift for
  e-commerce clients, and that NP Digital sees better current revenue from SMS than email
  marketing for those clients (self-reported/client data, dated 2023-05-29).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> I do not see AI replacing a human [in marketing, within the next five years]... if you
> want to think about like, everyone talks about AI writing content... if they're gathering
> false inputs... it can screw up the output.

> If you embrace AI, and you learn how to leverage it to do a better job... you're going to
> get even further in life, but if you stay afraid of it... you're going to start becoming
> replaced by other people who either understand AI... or you're going to get replaced by
> AI, one or the other.

> I feel celebrity endorsements are a waste, because most of them are too generic... it

> I think LinkedIn's massively underrated, especially if you're in B2B... I'm pretty sure
