# The Craziest Marketing Experiments I Have Ever Done and Why They Worked

## Summary

Neil recounts four of his most memorable marketing experiments and why each worked. **The 100K Challenge** (published on his old, since-sold blog **Quick Sprout**): he built a nutrition niche site anonymously — audience picked the niche, he wrote large volumes of content, did link outreach without using his name, collected emails and drip-sold affiliate products; most affiliate offers failed but Amazon products (notably fish oil) worked by driving site visitors to Amazon, lifting reviews and Amazon rankings, and he self-reports clearing over $100K/month before the 12-month deadline. **"Who is Neil Patel?"** — a brand-awareness stunt where he paid a social-media marketing agency to get Instagram influencers (a bodybuilder flexing the name on his pecs; a makeup artist blending the name into a face) to hold up his name, driving name searches, traffic, and organic ranking lifts via increased brand queries; he flags a lesson that he should have vetted each profile rather than handing over a budget with no oversight. **Expanding to Brazil** — his first international market (later Germany, Italy, Japan, China), on advice from a Google employee to translate English content into less-competitive languages; he stresses needing local partners ("feet on the ground") and targeting high-GDP countries. **Ubersuggest** — bought for ~$120K to make marketing tools simpler and free; it grew into his largest traffic source, more popular than his text content, and he argues tools generate traffic more cheaply and with less upkeep than content.

## Key claims

- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said experimentation has brought the biggest results to his company, sometimes multiplying his business by 2x, 3x, or even 4x.
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil recounted the **100K Challenge** on his former blog Quick Sprout (which he says he no longer owns): a claim that anyone can build a business generating $100K/month within 12 months, which he tested by building an anonymous nutrition niche site chosen by his audience (self-reported result: over $100K/month in revenue before the 12 months elapsed).
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said the nutrition site's affiliate products mostly failed, but selling physical products via Amazon (e.g. fish oil) worked by sending site traffic to Amazon, gaining reviews and boosting Amazon rankings.
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said "follow-along" experiments — where an audience can watch a journey step by step from beginning to end — typically work extremely well, comparing them to weight-loss challenges people can relate to and replicate.
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil recounted the **"Who is Neil Patel?"** experiment: paying a social-media marketing agency to have Instagram influencers display his name, which lifted brand awareness, name searches, traffic, and organic rankings.
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said increased brand queries raise organic rankings ("Google loves brand inquiries") and cited a paraphrased Eric Schmidt line that brands are how you sort quality sites from "the crap."
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said his lesson from the influencer stunt was that he should have vetted each influencer profile (engagement, appropriateness) instead of giving an agency a budget with no oversight — taking personal responsibility rather than blaming the agencies.
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said his **Brazil expansion** was a favorite experiment; the key lessons were to secure local partners ("feet on the ground") and to target countries with large total GDP (not necessarily high GDP per capita) where languages like Portuguese, Hindi, and German are far less competitive than English for SEO.
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said a Google employee gave him "the best advice I ever got" — to translate his English content into other languages to rank and dominate quickly in less competitive markets.
- As of 2019-09-21, Neil said he bought **Ubersuggest** for roughly $120,000 (self-reported), made it free, and it grew to make up the majority of his traffic — more than his text-based content — arguing tools drive traffic more cheaply and with less ongoing updating than content, since Google now demands content be kept fresh or rankings tank.
