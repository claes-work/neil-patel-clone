# 7 Blogging Tips That Will Make Your Blog Successful (Storytelling Techniques)

## Summary
Neil Patel argues that blogging has become so common that most people no longer actually read blog posts — eight out of ten will read a headline but only two out of ten click through and read the article — because readers are desensitized to blog content. To write a post people will actually read, he gives seven tactics: (1) write using the words "you" and "I" so it reads as a conversation rather than a college lecture, which drives more engagement, reading, and response; (2) keep paragraphs to no more than five or six lines, noting that many of his own paragraphs on neilpatel.com are only two or three lines and some are a single line; (3) use subheadings so readers can skim, grasp the gist, and jump to the section they want; (4) always write a conclusion and label it "conclusion," because a common reader pattern is to scroll down, read the conclusion, then scroll back up to decide whether to read the rest; (5) cite your information and data so readers do not "call B.S." and abandon the site; (6) use photos and graphics, invoking "a picture says a thousand words," to help convey the message and keep people reading; and (7) make the article thorough and actionable so readers feel they can go do something afterward. He illustrates the last point with a personal anecdote: he once googled "how to install a bidet" and landed on a How Stuff Works article that did not actually teach him how to do it, so he never returned to that site.

## Key claims
- Blogging has become so common that people no longer read all blog posts, because they are desensitized to them (as of 2017-06-11).
- Eight out of ten people will read your headline, but only two out of ten will click through and read the article (as of 2017-06-11).
- Tip 1 — Write with the words "you" and "I"; without them, readers feel it is a college lecture rather than a conversation and leave, whereas a conversational tone makes them more likely to engage, read, and respond (as of 2017-06-11).
- Tip 2 — Paragraphs should not exceed five or six lines, as longer paragraphs feel overwhelming; Neil says many of his own paragraphs on neilpatel.com are only two or three lines, and some are a single line (as of 2017-06-11).
- Tip 3 — Use subheadings to break a post into sections so readers can skim, get the gist without reading the whole thing, and jump to the part they want; without them you get fewer readers (as of 2017-06-11).
- Tip 4 — Always write a conclusion and label it "conclusion," because a common reader pattern is to scroll down, read the conclusion, and then scroll back up to decide whether to read the rest (as of 2017-06-11).
- Tip 5 — Cite your facts and data; uncited claims lead readers to "call B.S." and not return (as of 2017-06-11).
- Tip 6 — Use photos and graphics ("a picture says a thousand words") to explain your message, which makes people more likely to read and stick around (as of 2017-06-11).
- Tip 7 — Make articles thorough and actionable so readers feel they can go do something after reading, which keeps them reading; unactionable, uninformative content loses readers (as of 2017-06-11).
- Neil recounts googling "how to install a bidet" and landing on a How Stuff Works article that failed to teach him how, so he never returned to that site — cited as an example of non-actionable content driving readers away (as of 2017-06-11).
