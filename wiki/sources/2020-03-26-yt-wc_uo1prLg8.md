# How Long Does SEO Take to Work For a New Website?

## Summary
Neil Patel addresses what he calls his most frequently asked question: how long a new website takes to see SEO results. He argues that SEO has become far harder because Google now has "prime pickings" among 1.7 billion websites (as of January 1st, 2020) and can afford to be picky, whereas five to ten years ago it could not. He walks through how SEO has changed — keyword strategy has shifted from focusing on 5–10 generic keywords to lists of 50–100+ keywords with multiple keywords targeted per page, and content has moved away from keyword density toward satisfying search intent and delivering high-quality answers regardless of word count. On timing, he cites Forbes' four-to-six-month figure and his own experience of roughly six months to start seeing traction, stressing that traction is not the same as ranking at the top, and that long-tail phrases are the fastest path to early results. He closes with a checklist of ranking factors: technical health (HTTPS, robots.txt, sitemaps, speed), mobile-friendliness, authoritative/original content, quality backlinks, social signals (correlation not causation), and local business listings — all anchored to a "put the user first" principle.

## Key claims
- SEO is now much harder than it was because Google can be selective among a vast pool of sites; as of January 1st there are over 1.7 billion websites online (as of 2020-03-26).
- There are over a billion blogs on the web — roughly one blog for every seven people (as of 2020-03-26).
- In his earlier SEO days, ranking on the top of page one for head terms like "auto insurance" and "credit cards" was achievable in a few months, maybe six months at the latest (as of 2020-03-26).
- Keyword strategy has shifted: where companies once focused on 5–6 (maybe 10) generic keywords, sites now need lists of 50–100+ keywords; each page should target at least one keyword, and in most cases three or four keywords per page (as of 2020-03-26).
- Google no longer weighs keyword density the way it once did; it uses a thesaurus/dictionary-like understanding to identify a page's topic through synonyms even without the exact keyword present (as of 2020-03-26).
- Ranking is not about word count (contradicting the common "Google wants a 2,000-word article" belief); whoever answers the user's query fastest and best — whether in 500, 2,000, or 5,000 words — wins long term (as of 2020-03-26).
- Per Forbes, a new site averages four to six months to start seeing traction; there is no definitive answer because of the many factors involved and industry differences (as of 2020-03-26).
- Based on Neil's experience, a new site actively working on content, on-page SEO, and link building takes roughly six months to start seeing traction — which is not the same as ranking at the top (as of 2020-03-26).
- Going after long-tail phrases is the fastest way to see traction, and they convert better than head terms (as of 2020-03-26).
- Site speed is part of Google's algorithm and mobile/desktop speed also affects conversions; Google PageSpeed reports load times and fixes for both device types (as of 2020-03-26).
- Over half of Google searches are done on mobile devices, so non-mobile-friendly sites should not expect high rankings (as of 2020-03-26).
- Blogging on genuinely new topics generates on average 47.6% more traffic than blogging on regurgitated, old content (as of 2020-03-26).
- Ubersuggest is the section of Neil's site that attracts the most links, having generated over 10,000 backlinks on its own without him asking for links (as of 2020-03-26).
- Social signals have no effect on ranking according to Google, but there is a correlation between high-ranking pages and a decent number of social signals (as of 2020-03-26).
- Local businesses should claim Google My Business, gather Yelp/Google reviews, and maintain a good Better Business Bureau page (as of 2020-03-26).
