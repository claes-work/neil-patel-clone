# Do This Before Google's Next Algorithm Update

## Summary
Argues that Google makes far more algorithm changes than the public hears about —
self-reports/cites that Google made 4,500 changes to its search algorithm in 2020
alone, most too small to notice individually. Frames the practical response not as
chasing individual updates (unrealistic) but as continuous content optimization,
because Google's algorithm changes fundamentally optimize for user experience/
satisfaction. Gives a two-part monthly routine: (1) in Google Search Console, compare
year-over-year traffic per page, identify the pages that dropped the most, and update
those first — argues these have the most upside since a traffic drop signals the page
is now less fresh/thorough than competing content; (2) separately, review the top
30–50 "money pages" (highest-traffic pages) monthly (a "few hours a month") and flag
which should be refreshed for user value regardless of whether their traffic has
dropped. Uses Wikipedia's persistent top rankings as an example of a
continually-fresh-content payoff, and contrasts a real search-volume/results-glut
example (the "auto insurance" query: 1B+ results but under 200,000 searches in the
US) to argue that volume of content isn't the lever — freshness and user-value are.
Closes by reiterating that Google ultimately ranks for user satisfaction, not raw
backlink counts, illustrated with a hypothetical of a page with a million backlinks
that still gets low rankings if users bounce immediately.

## Key claims
- Self-reports/cites that Google made "4,500 changes to its search algorithm" in 2020
  (as cited, dated 2023-02-02).
- Cites (source unnamed): the term "auto insurance" in the US has 1B+ Google results
  but under 200,000 searches — used to argue against adding more low-differentiation
  content.
- Recommends a specific monthly Search Console workflow: compare year-over-year
  traffic by page via the date picker, prioritize refreshing pages with the largest
  traffic declines (self-reported method, dated 2023-02-02).
- States Google's ranking signals — including backlinks and site aesthetics — are
  ultimately proxies for user satisfaction, and that user satisfaction/bounce
  behavior outweighs raw backlink volume as a ranking determinant.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Just because your traffic isn't dropping doesn't mean that page shouldn't be updated. You want to do what's best for the user and not focus too much on Google.

> Even if you had a million back links, if users go to your website and they all bounce and click the back button 'cause they think your content sucks, Google's not going to rank you high.
