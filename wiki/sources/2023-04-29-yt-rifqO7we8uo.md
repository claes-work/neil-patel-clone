# How to Build a Strong Personal Brand in Business

## Summary
Interview on the limits and mechanics of personal-brand-driven business growth. Neil says he
mostly doesn't see hate/criticism directed at him ("I honestly don't read most of it... I don't
really know about 99% of my haters") and doesn't let it affect strategy, including hate
received for things he had no stake in (a friend's software launch wrongly attributed to him).
Confirms the agency was originally **"Neil Patel Digital"**, renamed to **NP Digital** (still
his initials) partly to shorten the name and partly because he is "less and less the face" of
it — large enterprise clients (his example: Ford-scale accounts) care about the specific
account team and its track record, not his personal brand, and **~77% of NP Digital's business
comes from referrals, RFPs, and employees**, so his face isn't typically the door-opener at
that scale (though he says it helps disproportionately in a business's early years). Gives an
extended, largely admiring but pointed comparison to **Gary Vaynerchuk/VaynerMedia**: argues
personal brand is "only a small piece of the pie" of VaynerMedia's success — the bigger drivers
are operational execution and a pricing strategy of underpricing competitors on RFPs in year
one and expanding margin in later years — and that Vaynerchuk's biggest paydays came from
ancillary businesses he built and sold (names one sold to Amex — Resy) rather than the agency
itself. Recounts NP Digital's early growth: **~$5-6M revenue year one (99% personal-brand-
driven, near-zero profit)**, **~$15-16M year two** as hiring capacity increased, continued
"crazy growth" years three through five; says if he started over he would skip the personal-
brand route entirely and instead front-load hiring of experienced operators (his own CEO, a
former **iProspect** (Dentsu) president who "drove seven-plus million dollars in revenue last
year by himself") — an expensive but faster path to scale. Lists the agency's regional
build-out by name: US, Canada, Brazil (already live) plus active hiring/expansion into UK,
Germany, India, Australia, Singapore, Latin America (Mexico, Colombia, Argentina), Portugal,
EMEA (France, Italy, Spain), and feelers into Indonesia/Thailand/Malaysia — explicitly
avoiding Russia and China for political/regulatory risk (partnering locally in China instead).
Describes his own role as non-operational (no management calls, no finance/HR/legal
involvement) and concentrated on client strategy/tactics, 60-80 hours/week, because he finds
it fun rather than obligatory. States he is "not doing this for money anymore," frames the
work as "a game" he loves while his wife "loves giving it away," and describes the family's
wealth philosophy: they intend to give away money rather than pass it to their children
("our kids can go earn it, like everyone else"), credits his mother as the entrepreneurial
role model in his own upbringing, and gives a small anecdote about teaching his 3-year-old
daughter negotiation ("Why should I give it to you? What am I going to get?") while trading
cartoon time for CNBC/Bloomberg exposure.
