# On-page and technical SEO Part 1 - SEO Unlocked - Free SEO Course with Neil Patel

## Summary
In Week Two of his free SEO Unlocked course, Neil Patel walks through on-page and technical SEO, the changes a site must make "from a code perspective" so Google can crawl, understand, and rank it. He opens by showing how Google actually reads a page — as code and text rather than the styled design a user sees — using Browseo to render that view, then runs through roughly ten on-page elements: title tags (with keyword near the front and a modifier near the end), headlines (formula-driven, short, numbered, share-optimized), the first hundred words, internal linking, external linking, URL structure, readability, images/alt tags, subheadings, calls to action, related (LSI) keywords, and social sharing. Throughout he ties on-page tactics to both Google's algorithm and human users, insisting optimization serves both. He supports points with specific data (headline read-vs-click ratios, share stats from BuzzSumo, a 50-character URL benchmark from Brian Dean) and repeatedly points viewers to his own tool Ubersuggest for keyword ideas, site audits, and content research. He closes by previewing the next lesson on off-page SEO.

## Key claims
- Google does not view a website as a styled design; it reads the underlying code and text, and a tool like Browseo renders roughly how Google sees a page (as of 2020-05-11).
- Linkable content wins: the more sites that link to you, the easier it is for Google to find and rank your site, and links include both external sites linking in and pages within your own site linking to each other (as of 2020-05-11).
- On a Google result, the top colored text is the title tag, the green line is the URL, and the text below is the meta description (as of 2020-05-11).
- Title tags impact clicks, and click behavior feeds ranking: if searchers consistently click the second listing over the first because its title is more appealing, Google will promote the more-clicked result (as of 2020-05-11).
- Write appealing title tags by placing the keyword near the front (since people search by keyword) and adding a modifier near the end — words like buy, guide, review, online, offers, cheap, or the year (as of 2020-05-11).
- Adding the year to a title, especially for detailed guides, drastically increases click-through rate (as of 2020-05-11).
- A headline is distinct from a title tag: the title tag shows in Google's results, while the headline is what appears at the top of your own page (as of 2020-05-11).
- Roughly eight of ten people read the headline but only two of ten click through to read the rest of the copy, so a weak headline loses most of the opportunity (as of 2020-05-11).
- Headline formula: number or trigger word at the beginning, then an adjective, then a keyword, then a promise — but the promise must not be deceptive or mismatched to the content (as of 2020-05-11).
- Headline tips: keep them short (around six words tend to perform better), use numbers, use interesting adjectives, try negative words like no/without/stop, and make the headline match the content to keep reader trust (as of 2020-05-11).
- Per BuzzSumo share data, the two-word phrase "how to" generates more shares than any other two-word phrase, "X Ways To" is the best-performing headline opener, and the numbers five and ten perform well for social sharing (as of 2020-05-11).
- Headlines of roughly six to ten words get the most shares; going too long or too short reduces shares (as of 2020-05-11).
- Social-sharing plugins such as Social Pug or Super Socializer add share icons and generate a modest lift in social shares (as of 2020-05-11).
- Google weighs the first ~100 words of a page heavily to determine what the content is about, so keywords should appear naturally within the first paragraph or two — not stuffed — and Ubersuggest can show which keywords are more popular (as of 2020-05-11).
- Internal linking is critical: linking page to page helps Google's crawler index content, and heavy interlinking is why sites like Wikipedia (and Neil's own site) rank for so many terms (as of 2020-05-11).
- Authority flows through internal links: the homepage typically has the most authority (most inbound links), and cross-linking to and between other important pages can raise the rankings of the whole site (as of 2020-05-11).
- Put keywords in internal-link anchor text to help rankings, but avoid keyword-stuffing anchor text because unnatural links hurt user experience (as of 2020-05-11).
- For a ~1,500-word article, aim for at least three to four internal links, but only where natural — do not shove links in for their own sake (as of 2020-05-11).
- External linking to trustworthy, reputable sources — even competitors with strong data — builds credibility and authority, and signals to Google which topical "neighborhood" your site belongs to for ranking purposes (as of 2020-05-11).
- URLs around 50 characters tend to rank better than 60, 70, or 80 characters (attributed to Brian Dean of Backlinko); keep URLs short, clean, and free of query characters like question marks and equals signs (as of 2020-05-11).
- Overly long keyword-stuffed URLs can make Google treat the page as relevant only to that exact long string, though you may still rank for long-tail phrases (as of 2020-05-11).
- Remove dates from URL structures: doing so on neilpatel.com boosted SEO traffic by 50% in roughly 30 days, because a dated URL signals a time-bound/news article Google may rank lower over time (as of 2020-05-11).
- Removing dates from URLs requires a 301 redirect from the old dated URLs to the new ones — not just deleting the date (to be covered in an upcoming lesson) (as of 2020-05-11).
- Use an SSL certificate (HTTPS rather than HTTP); most pages that rank on Google use SSL, and it helps both rankings and conversions (as of 2020-05-11).
- URL tips: use hyphens not underscores, use lowercase, use only safe characters (letters, numbers, dashes — no ampersands, pound signs, or tildes), and keep to a maximum of about two folders/slashes (as of 2020-05-11).
- Readability matters for engagement: keep language simple, use short sentences, keep paragraphs to about three to four lines (five to six for desktop-heavy audiences), and use subheadings, images, quotes, lists, and calls to action (as of 2020-05-11).
- Use custom-made, compressed images (via free sources like Pexels, or Envato / GraphicRiver as alternatives), avoid stock photography and blurry images, and size images for social-channel dimensions so they share well (as of 2020-05-11).
- Because Google cannot see images, use an alt tag to describe each image; alt tags also help visually impaired users via assistive programs, and Google prefers to rank sites that are accessible to everyone (as of 2020-05-11).
- Subheadings work like book chapters under the page's H1 title and typically use H2 or H3 tags; Ubersuggest's Site Audit can analyze whether headings and code are set up correctly (as of 2020-05-11).
- Calls to action must stand out in color and be clear; avoid red (it reads as "stop") and pick a color that contrasts with the page (as of 2020-05-11).
- Quotes, bullet points, and infographics make content easier to skim, which matters because many visitors skim rather than read fully; relevant videos also help, and YouTube is the second-most popular search engine (as of 2020-05-11).
- Integrate related (latent semantic indexing) keywords — the "related searches" Google shows at the bottom of results, also surfaced in Ubersuggest's keyword ideas report — because ranking for related terms is an easy way to get more traffic (as of 2020-05-11).
- Google bolds the searched keywords where they appear on a ranking page, so include the right keywords in the title, headings, and body to rank (as of 2020-05-11).
- Use Ubersuggest's content ideas report to find topics that already earn many social shares, backlinks, and estimated visits; writing on already-popular topics is more likely to succeed, and the "holy grail" is content that wins on all three metrics (as of 2020-05-11).
