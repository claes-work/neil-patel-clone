# How I Rank #1 For Very Competitive Keywords on YouTube

## Summary
Neil breaks down how he grew his own YouTube channel from zero to 490,000+ subscribers
since 2017 and ranks for competitive keywords like "SEO" and "content marketing."
Tactics: (1) niche down into one topic-authority (he stays strictly on marketing/SEO;
recounts that a video his friend Derek Halpern suggested on sales underperformed
because he isn't known as a sales expert); (2) watch audience feedback (views,
likes/dislikes, comments) to double down on what resonates; (3) understand search
demand via Ubersuggest's Keyword Ideas report (questions/prepositions/comparisons tabs)
— notes search is his second-biggest traffic source at 25.5% of channel views; (4)
prioritize content value/retention over keyword-stuffing, since YouTube ranks on watch
time and how long a viewer stays on YouTube afterward (references "Sunny" and her "hot
script" framework for retention); (5) follow a consistent video format/formula: hook →
subscribe/notification-bell call-to-action → step-by-step bulleted content with stats
→ closing CTAs (agency mention, like/share/comment); (6) study competitor channels and
adapt (not copy) their best-performing concepts; (7) push hard in the first 24 hours
(cross-promote via Twitter, email, and push notifications via Subscribers.com) since
YouTube's algorithm rewards early-engagement velocity, unlike Google's slower-burn SEO.
Bonus tip: strong headlines matter — cites the same "8 out of 10 read, 2 out of 10
click through" stat and Copyblogger's headline formula.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-03-12, Neil said he grew his YouTube channel from zero to over 490,000
  subscribers since 2017.
- As of 2020-03-12, Neil said search accounts for 25.5% of all views to his YouTube
  videos, making it his second-biggest traffic source to the channel.
- As of 2020-03-12, Neil said a video he made on sales (at the suggestion of Derek
  Halpern) underperformed despite correct SEO/format execution, because he isn't known
  as a sales-topic authority — illustrating that channel-topic focus outweighs format
  optimization.
- As of 2020-03-12, Neil described his standard video formula: hook → subscribe/
  notification-bell CTA → step-by-step bulleted content with stats and actionable
  advice → closing CTAs (agency mention, like/share/comment).
- As of 2020-03-12, Neil said YouTube's algorithm rewards videos that perform well in
  the first 24 hours (unlike Google's SEO, which rewards steady performance over a
  longer time), and that he pushes new videos via Twitter, email blasts, and push
  notifications (Subscribers.com) during that window.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Channels that are very specific versus talking about everything, tend to do better when it comes to ranking... whenever I try to go out of this expertise, I don't get much traction and I don't rank well even if I do all the stuff that's right. But when I focus, I do really well."

> "YouTube's SEO algorithm is the opposite of Google's SEO algorithm. See, with Google, you rank well over time. It's a long and steady race. With YouTube, the videos that tend to do really well in the first 24 hours tend to rank really quickly."

> "I just don't want to give a lecture and be like, 'Hey, I'm Neil, I know everything about SEO or marketing.' No, I want to break things down so you can implement and get results, because if you do, you'll keep coming back."
