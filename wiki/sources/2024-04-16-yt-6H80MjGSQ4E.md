# Should you optimize for Reddit and Quora for higher SEO rankings?

## Summary
Three segments. (1) A debate on whether the interview podcast format is "stale," sparked by an Eric Siu Twitter poll (~77-82% said yes). The diagnosis offered is that it's not the interview *format* that's stale but the *questions* — 70-80% of the questions hosts ask (both of Neil and generally) repeat the same handful of generic topics (AI, SEO tactics, growth takeaways). Sam Parr (My First Million) is cited as a positive example: he crowdsources unique questions from his X audience before recording. A parallel conversation with David Perell concluded hosting is harder than being a guest (requires research across a guest's entire back catalog plus audience-sourced angles). Eric's own social stats reportedly grew from ~200-300K views/30 days to ~12M views/30 days doing a similar research-driven interview-style format on social, framed as evidence that engagement/interest, not follower count, now drives reach (the "social graph vs. interest graph" framing — platforms increasingly surface content based on topic-engagement history rather than who a user follows). (2) A personal anecdote: a joking social clip where Neil called Tesla "a crap car" and Eric defended it outperformed their other content — and Neil subsequently bought a Tesla anyway, explicitly "voting with his wallet" on price and safety/self-driving tech, while maintaining his one complaint is seat comfort versus a Mercedes. (3) A closing item on Reddit and Quora traffic gains following a recent Google update: confirmed that Google buys data from Reddit (reported at ~$60M/year), unconfirmed whether Google also buys from Quora; NP Digital reports no client inquiries yet about optimizing specifically "for Reddit" the way they get inquiries for Google SEO. Search behavior is described as shifting toward a question-and-answer pattern driven by ChatGPT-style expectations, with a secondhand data point from Jim Yu (BrightEdge, cited as a $100M+-ARR enterprise SEO company) that BrightEdge is seeing more Google SGE-related queries reward longer-tail, more direct-answer content.

## Key claims
- **The interview podcast format itself is not stale — the questions are.** An estimated 70-80% of interview questions repeat the same generic topics (AI use cases, SEO tactics, 3 takeaways for growth); interviews that "do the best" tend to be the ones where the host asks genuinely novel questions. `(opinion, dated 2024-04-16; attribution: Neil, first-person "the questions that I'm being asked")`
- **Neil historically did 2-3 podcast interviews per day as a guest, then deliberately cut back to focus on fewer, bigger shows.** `(self-reported, dated 2024-04-16; attribution: Neil)`
- **Reach is increasingly governed by an "interest graph" (engagement/topic-relevance driven distribution) rather than a "social graph" (follower-count driven distribution) — YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram are cited as now primarily merit/engagement-based, with Twitter/X still more follower-weighted.** `(opinion, dated 2024-04-16; attribution: uncertain — undiarized, both hosts build on the framing)`
- **Neil bought a Tesla after a joking social clip calling it "a crap car" outperformed other content; he maintains the car is good on price, safety, and self-driving tech but has one lasting complaint — seat comfort relative to a Mercedes.** `(self-reported, dated 2024-04-16; attribution: Neil, first-person)`
- **NP Digital is not yet fielding client inquiries specifically about "optimizing for Reddit" (distinct from general SEO), as of this recording.** `(self-reported business detail, dated 2024-04-16; attribution: Neil)`
- **Google is confirmed (per prior reporting cited in-episode) to pay Reddit for data access; whether Google has a similar data deal with Quora is unconfirmed by the hosts.** `(third-party claim relayed, dated 2024-04-16)`
