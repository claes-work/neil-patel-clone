# Uber's $650M secret business, Deepfake CFO, and Email marketing hacks for 2024

## Summary
Three segments, well diarized. (1) Eric surprises Neil with the fact that Uber's advertising business does ~$650M/year, and shows that its advertiser-signup "funnel" is just a plain Google Form — Neil draws the lesson that great businesses don't need complicated funnels, tying it to how simple their own Agency Owners Association signup form is; the two also note Uber Eats' ad targeting feels effective (deals/discounts tailored to order history). (2) A deepfake-scam news item (a finance team wired $25M after a deepfaked video call) leads Neil to tell, at length and in first person, his own experience being impersonated: during COVID a former employee (a cook) messaged Neil's wife claiming to be having an affair with "Neil" and extorted ~$500 in Bitcoin from her before she debunked it (wrong skin tone, wrong shoe habits, wrong login) — a rare biographical/voice anecdote covering his OCD tendencies, Indian ethnicity, and marriage. (3) Reacting to a friend's (Matt Paulson's) 24-point email-deliverability checklist, Neil lays out NP Digital/Neil Patel's actual, much simpler in-house email playbook: daily rolling-30-day list scrubbing, spam/bot defenses (Cloudflare, captchas), short text-based emails (100–250 characters, one or two links max), and per-email-type ROI tracking, noting that low-open/low-click "pitch us" emails often generate the most revenue.

## Key claims
- **Simple funnels can support huge businesses.** Uber's advertiser sign-up funnel is a bare Google Form, yet the ad business generates ~$650M/year — Neil's takeaway: "if you want to build a great business, sometimes you don't have to overcomplicate [things]." He draws a direct parallel to the equally simple Google-Form signup for his and Eric's Agency Owners Association. (Neil, 2024-02-28)
- **Uber Eats' ad targeting feels precise.** Neil says the ads he sees for restaurants match places he'd likely order from based on past orders, with deals/discounts that get him to order; he believes the underlying targeting tech is more complex than the sign-up funnel suggests. (Neil, 2024-02-28)
- **Personal deepfake/impersonation scam (first-person, self-reported).** During COVID, a former employee (a cook, unnamed) messaged Neil's wife via Instagram/TikTok claiming an affair with "Neil" and demanded reimbursement after being scammed out of ~$500 in Bitcoin by an account impersonating him; his wife debunked it herself (wrong shoes worn on a bed, basketball shoes he doesn't own, and — most decisively — the impersonator was Caucasian while Neil is Indian). Neil's stated takeaways: be vigilant, expect scams (and "evil marketers") to grow more sophisticated, and secure your identity — predicting blockchain-based identity verification will eventually be necessary beyond 2FA/passwords. (Neil, 2024-02-28)
- **NP Digital / neilpatel.com's actual email playbook (in-house, self-reported, much simpler than industry checklists).** Daily list scrubbing on a rolling 30-day open-activity window (contacts who haven't opened in 30 days stop being emailed); spam/bot defenses via Cloudflare blocking plus developer-built captcha triggers for aggressive signups; emails are short (100–250 characters), text-based with little to no images, and capped at one or two links (he gets "pissy" when his team adds a third because it hurts deliverability); performance is tracked by revenue generated per email type, not just opens/clicks — noting that low-open, low-click "get pitched by us" emails often generate the most revenue of any email type. (Neil, 2024-02-28)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> If you want to build a great business, sometimes you don't have to overcomplicate.

> [My wife] was pissed off because she said your husband duped me out of $500 in Bitcoin... my wife's like, my husband wouldn't try to get $500 — she's not trying to be rude, but... I do dinners that sometimes cost $2,000, $3,000, or $4,000, $5,000... I'm not trying to boast, I'm just saying that's a reality.

> The takeaway here is: one, be vigilant; two, [scams] are going to become a lot crazier, there's also evil marketers as well so you got to be careful; three, you got to learn how to secure your stuff... eventually you're probably going to have to have some type of token that can verify your identity, and that's going to have some blockchain technology tied to it.

> Ours is really simple: we get traffic, we have spam checks on our end... we collect emails, we scrub our list on a rolling 30 [days]... we send really good emails with great subject lines, content with text-based copy, little to no images — technically no images — short emails, between 100 and 250 characters, one link, two max... sometimes my team puts in three and I get a little pissy because I just feel that's too many and it starts affecting deliverability.

> Sometimes the ones with the least open rates and least clicks make you the most money — believe it or not, like consulting emails, "hey sign up for a pitch to get pitched by us on us doing your marketing" — you're going to get way less opens and clicks but it's going to generate more revenue.
