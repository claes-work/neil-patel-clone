# How to Write Headlines People Can't Help but Click [Powerful Formulas Included]

## Summary
Neil argues that the headline is the highest-leverage part of any article because eight out of ten people read the headline but only two out of ten click through to the body, so getting the headline right disproportionately drives traffic. He lays out a data-driven, test-first process: write at least five different headlines per piece and A/B test them so decisions come from measured click-through rate (from social and Google) rather than gut, and keep headlines under roughly 50 characters, which his own testing found outperforms longer ones. His tactical formulas are: use specific, granular numbers instead of vague ones (e.g., an exact dollar figure like $3,452,928 rather than "millions") because specificity signals honesty and transparency; use rationale/action words (tips, reasons, lessons, tricks, ideas, facts, wins) that promise actionable content; use creative, powerful adjectives ("game changing" over "interesting"); and leverage freshness and fear of missing out to create urgency (e.g., framing a strategy as one that "less than .1% of SEOs are using"). Throughout he stresses that the content must deliver on the headline's promise — over-promising erodes audience trust and kills future clicks — and he closes by pitching his agency, Neil Patel Digital.
