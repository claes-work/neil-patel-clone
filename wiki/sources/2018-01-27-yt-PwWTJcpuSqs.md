# How to Hire Writers to Create Killer Content For Your Blog

_Filmed with **David** (an NP colleague, in-house content lead); Neil leads, David explains
process detail. Only Neil-attributed material is persona data; David's operational answers
are context._

## Summary
How NP produces content at industrial scale. Neil frames it with a **Ford assembly-line**
analogy — "if Ford can keep cranking out cars, why can't we crank out content?" — and says
that across their own blog (7 posts/week), a daily podcast, 3–4 videos/week, plus other and
client blogs, they publish roughly **150–200 pieces a week**, and could hit **1,000/week** in
30 days without dropping quality. The mechanism: a **Trello assembly line** where each column
is one stage (topic → assign → outline → approve/edit → write → approve → publish) and each
person owns clearing their column into the next — "you cannot mess that up." Staffing is
**freelancers, never full-time writers**: Neil and David "strongly disagree" with full-time
hiring because salaried writers slip into a 9-to-5 mindset that caps output, whereas
freelancers set their own hours and stay creative. David invokes **Paul Graham's "maker vs
manager schedule"** — creatives need uninterrupted 3-hour blocks that office hours destroy.
They source writers from **jobs.problogger** at **~10–15¢/word average** (up to ~20¢ for
technical work). Neil's sharpest tactic: **hire writers who already have their own audience** —
even at 20¢/word they're cheaper than "shitty writers," because they market their own posts to
their following, so the content goes further without extra Facebook-ad spend.

## Key claims
As of 2018-01-27, Neil states:
- NP publishes **~150–200 content pieces per week** across their blog (7/week), a daily
  podcast, 3–4 videos/week, and other/client blogs — and could scale to **1,000/week** within
  30 days at quality (self-reported).
- **Content is an assembly line** (the Ford analogy): break production into stages so one
  content creator (who can only write ~3–4 pieces/week) is multiplied across a team of ~20–30+.
- The workflow stages: topic → assign to writer → outline → approve/edit the outline → write →
  approve → (additional edits) → publish.
- Run it on **Trello, kept deliberately simple** (inspired by KISSmetrics' simplicity — no
  colors/extra features): one column per stage/person; your job is to move your item to the
  next column, the next person clears theirs — "you cannot mess that up."
- **Do not hire full-time writers.** Salaried writers default to a 9-to-5 mindset that caps
  output and creativity; freelancers who set their own hours produce more.
- (David) Creatives need uninterrupted **3-hour blocks**; per **Paul Graham's** "maker schedule
  vs manager schedule," daytime office interruptions kill creative output — supports using
  freelancers over full-time office staff.
- **Writer cost: ~10–15¢/word average**, up to ~20¢ for highly technical content; NP sources
  writers from **jobs.problogger**.
- **Best tactic — hire writers who already have a following**: whatever they write, they market
  to their own audience, so even at 20¢/word they're cheaper than cheap writers and it "goes so
  much more viral" without paying for Facebook ads.
- "You write content — I don't care how amazing it is — it doesn't mean it's going to do well":
  content without marketing/distribution fails.
