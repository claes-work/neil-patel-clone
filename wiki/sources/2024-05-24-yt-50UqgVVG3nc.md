# Is the ad spend on Super Bowl or Olympics worth it?, and Disney+, Hulu, and Max streaming bundle (Marketing School)

## Summary
A news-roundup episode that turns into one of the richer **biographical** sources in this batch:
Neil tells the origin story of his mother's unlicensed daycare business ("Tina's Daycare"),
marketed via a hand-lettered sticker on the family Honda Civic and word-of-mouth referral
partnerships with competing daycares that were full — a formative bootstrap-marketing story from
childhood. The episode also covers Super Bowl/Olympics ad ROI data, the John Wanamaker "half my
advertising is wasted" quote, a frugality riff (Neil's habit of buying and then never wearing
expensive clothes), and concrete Agency Owners Association event/dinner lead-gen tactics
(Toronto, Brazil), including a clear "curate your crowd" cautionary tale.

## Key claims
- Super Bowl/Olympics TV ad spend showed strong brand-awareness lift (~41% per Sports Business
  Journal) but weak direct ROI on the same-day dataset discussed. (Neil/Eric, joint, 2024-05-24)
- Pushes back on the misconception that only TV ads build brand and performance ads (Google/Meta)
  don't: buying millions of targeted clicks builds brand exposure over time just like a TV
  commercial, but with an easier path to directly attributable ROI. (Neil, 2024-05-24)
- Frugality philosophy: being frugal on small things correlates with being disciplined on big
  things — "the moment you stop being frugal is when..." — while acknowledging he is *selectively*
  frugal (very loose on some categories, like clothes he buys and never wears). (Neil, 2024-05-24)
- **Origin story — his mother's daycare business ("Tina's Daycare"):** as a kid, the family's Honda
  Civic had a hand-lettered (visibly amateur, hand-done-by-mom) sticker in the rear window with
  "Tina's Daycare" and a phone number. His mother also drove around to local schools/preschools
  that were full (capped by licensing limits on the number of children) and left flyers asking them
  to refer overflow families to her daycare — with **no referral commission or affiliate fee**.
  The business grew from near-empty to "a little more than $100,000 a year in revenue" and roughly
  "$80,000 a year in profit" — a large sum for the family at the time — but never scaled much
  beyond that ceiling. His mother was never embarrassed to hustle and never let ego get in the way
  of getting business. (Neil, 2024-05-24) ★ candidate for `persona/biography.md`.
- Cites Charlie Munger's line that "the first $100,000 is a son of a—" (i.e. brutally hard to
  earn) as the throughline lesson of his mother's story — you have to be willing to do every
  unglamorous thing to get initial traction. (Neil, 2024-05-24)
- Agency Owners Association lead-gen playbook: host in-person events (talks, panels, networking)
  in target cities, often partnered with a local trade association (e.g. the Canadian Marketing
  Association in Toronto) or piggybacked on an existing conference's attendee list, inviting CMOs
  and marketing directors from large corporations; sometimes free (venue partners want future
  conference business), sometimes paid. (Neil, 2024-05-24)
- **Curate your crowd, or lose your shirt:** a small ~6–8 person dinner in San Francisco with the
  right marketing leaders (a mix of VPs/directors/one CMO) led to 3–4 clients; other dinners
  costing $8,000–$10,000 with the wrong crowd generated nothing. The lesson: event ROI depends
  entirely on attendee quality, not spend. (Neil, 2024-05-24)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> My mom was never embarrassed, she never had ego, she didn't care what people think. She just
> thought outside the box — we didn't have tons of money, we barely had income, so she couldn't pay
> for ads, but this strategy worked for her.

> It's really whatever it takes to get the initial traction — like Charlie Munger talks about this,
> the first $100,000 is a son of a-. It's hard to get that, and it's still true to this day.

> You have to make sure you curate your crowd, otherwise you can lose your shirt.

> Money is expensive, it takes so long to earn it and it's hard to earn it — the easiest way to
> make more is just to save it.
