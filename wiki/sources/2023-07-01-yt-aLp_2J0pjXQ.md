# Marketing in the Post-Great Reset Era: Thriving Amidst Change and Uncertainty

## Summary
Short co-hosted segment on the post-pandemic "Great Reset" in business. Neil frames the 2020-23
period: many companies (including his own, plus peers like Google/Facebook) over-hired during the
pandemic-driven digital boom, and none anticipated the subsequent inflation spike or Fed rate
hikes, which forced companies carrying growth debt to cut costs and right-size. Frames this as
"adjusting," not necessarily headcount-only — could mean trimming redundant software tooling or
cutting unprofitable divisions/business lines that had become cost centers, reallocating that
budget elsewhere. Notes he's observing this pattern across company sizes, from SMBs to
multi-billion-dollar-revenue peers in his own friend circle (explicitly distinguishing
revenue/EBITDA-based "multi-billion dollar" companies from venture-valuation "unicorns"). Argues
for reasserting operational discipline post-reset: companies should return to whatever culture/
policy actually worked for them pre-pandemic (remote or in-office) rather than following external
trend pressure, and should audit redundant software spend (cites a hypothetical of paying for
Basecamp, Asana, and ClickUp simultaneously while using none well). Closes on a "be scrappy"
thesis: even profitable companies should stay lean, both because downturns are a rare window to
acquire competitors or assets cheaply with dry-powder cash, and because competitors who do stay
scrappy will otherwise take market share from those who don't.

## Key claims
- Attributes the pandemic-era over-hiring/over-expansion wave (including at his own company) to
  nobody anticipating the subsequent inflation spike and Fed rate hikes, which then forced
  debt-funded companies to cut costs (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-07-01). (Neil)
- Argues the "reset" should mean returning to whatever operational/cultural approach previously
  worked for a given company, rather than following external trend pressure (e.g. blanket
  remote-work adoption) (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-07-01). (Neil) — extends existing
  capital-discipline belief in `persona/beliefs.md`.
- Argues downturns are a rare opportunity to deploy "dry powder" cash to acquire competitors/assets
  cheaply, and that staying scrappy (even while profitable) is necessary because non-scrappy
  competitors will otherwise capture market share (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-07-01). (Neil)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> The point of the reset is, hey, we're going to reset back to what worked for us and what worked
> for our culture and screw what everyone else in the public says.

> You have to be more scrappy, whether it's in your marketing or operations. ... The ones who
> aren't scrappy aren't going to survive.

> This is where you can use your dry powder or money to expand and find better opportunities...
> if you're not going out there and you're not being smart with your money, what will happen is
> your competition will and they'll start taking your market share.
