# Google confirms links are less important, What will be the future of search in 10 years?, and more

## Summary
Three segments. (1) A response to Google downplaying links as a top-three ranking factor: the
hosts (agency-experience claim attributed with "I have an agency, you have an agency") say they
have never seen a well-optimized site fail to rank purely for lack of links, and that
domain-authority/link-profile strength consistently correlates with sites that won recent
"helpful content" update rankings. Google's public statements about ranking factors are treated
skeptically — framed as algorithm-protection messaging from the web-spam team rather than
straightforward technical disclosure — while acknowledging AI-driven devaluation of links is
likely 5-10 years out. Rand Fishkin's ~2014 MozCon click-through-signal experiments are cited
(the "best steak" test and the "imch lab" gibberish-search test, where directing searchers to
click a specific result moved it to the top of the SERP within hours) as older evidence that
user click signals influence rankings, corroborated by a claim that SEO practitioner Mike King
referenced similar findings in a recent conference talk. (2) A Search Engine Journal recap of
Google's CEO discussing the 10-year future of search: the article is characterized as offering
few concrete predictions, essentially conceding uncertainty; a claim is made that Google hasn't
had a literal "10 blue links" results page in years (dismissing periodic claims that blue links
are about to disappear as a persistent misconception) and that Google iterates cautiously (small
test rollouts, revert if user experience or revenue is hurt). A proprietary data point is cited:
an NP Digital survey run in 2020 (n≈98% search engines / 1% social / 1% other, methodology/sample
size for that year not stated) versus a repeat run "this year" (2024, n=1,115) found search
engines dropped to a plurality with social media (~30-77%, the transcript's percentage figures
are internally inconsistent/garbled) and AI chatbots (~30%) rising sharply — framed as a
significant multi-year shift in how people search, while noting Google's own traffic is still
growing and questions are only ~8% of its ~8.5B daily searches. (3) A tangent on AI agents,
prompted by a Brian Halligan retweet of Airon Levy (Box) and a reference to Vinod Khosla: a belief
that businesses will increasingly be able to reach $1B+ revenue with fewer than 100 employees via
AI-agent leverage, plus a garbled, self-corrected recollection of a market-size study (a claimed
Salesforce market-cap figure of "$35 billion," followed immediately by self-doubt and conflicting
numbers — flagged as an in-source recall error, not a reliable data point) used to argue the
CRM/sales-tooling space (and "buying" broadly) is a far larger addressable market than commonly
assumed. Closing stance: AI agents are "underhyped," not overhyped.
