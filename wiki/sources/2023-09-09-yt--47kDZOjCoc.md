# 7 Factors to Help You Decide Which Business To Start

## Summary
Solo interview on Hala Taha's "Young and Profiting" podcast. Despite the title, the
captured segment is mostly Neil's origin story plus his framework for picking a market
("big TAM") and a compact marketing formula (omnichannel + upsell/downsell + LTV). He
recounts starting his first side hustle around age 15–16 (selling CDs and cable TV as a
kid), then building a Monster.com-style job board with ~$500–900 raised from high-school
classmates that got traffic but made no money. A Speech 101 class presentation on how
Google's algorithm works led to a marketing hire at a power-supply manufacturer for
$5K/month, where he eventually learned he was driving ~$25M/year in revenue for the
business; that led to an agency-arbitrage arrangement (via the owner's son) serving
clients including Blue Cross, Countrywide, and ING Direct, taking him to ~$20K/month by
age 16–17. He says he didn't learn to focus until 13–14 years into his career and calls
it his biggest regret. ★ landmark biographical detail (specific origin-story chronology,
dollar figures, employer type) — candidate for `persona/biography.md`.

## Key claims (dated, paraphrase unless noted)
- Started first money-making activities around age 15 (selling CDs/cable TV to
  classmates' parents); "shouldn't have done some of those things." Self-reported,
  undated beyond "~21 years ago" from 2023-09-09 (≈2002). — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Built a Monster.com-style job board around age 16 after failing to qualify for
  higher-paying Oracle-consulting jobs; raised ~$500–900 from high-school classmates to
  pay contractors; funded by earnings from picking up trash at a theme park, cleaning
  restrooms, and selling CDs. Got ~100K visitors/month but made zero revenue.
  Self-reported. — agency-entrepreneurship.
- A Speech 101 presentation (age 16) on how Google's algorithm works led a classmate's
  employer (a power-supply manufacturer) to hire him for marketing, at $5K/month
  (~$60K/year). He later learned he had driven ~$25M/year in revenue for that business.
  Self-reported, unverified. — agency-entrepreneurship.
- The employer's son ran an ad agency and arbitraged Neil's work to clients including
  Blue Cross, Countrywide, and ING Direct at $5K/month per client; by ~age 16–17 Neil was
  making ~$20K/month. Self-reported, unverified. — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Says he didn't learn to focus until 13–14 years into his entrepreneurial journey and
  considers this his biggest strategic mistake — "if I focused from day one... I would
  have a much, much, much bigger business." (2023-09-09) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Passion-finding framework (echoes *Hustle*, co-authored with Vlaskovits & Koffler):
  most people don't discover a pre-existing passion; they find it through trial and
  error, gravitating toward what they turn out to be naturally good at. (2023-09-09) —
  agency-entrepreneurship.
- Market-selection framework: pick a large TAM (total addressable market) over a narrow
  niche — dominating even 0.1% of a multi-billion-dollar market beats trying to dominate
  100% of a small niche, since the marketing effort required is roughly the same either
  way. Cites Tesla/automotive, Microsoft/software, Google/search, Apple/hardware as
  large-TAM examples. (2023-09-09) — content-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
- Marketing formula (his stated sequence): (1) pick a big TAM; (2) go omnichannel
  (LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, email, SEO, paid ads — "if it's profitable,
  it's profitable"); (3) build upsells/downsells into the funnel because acquisition
  cost rises over time; (4) engineer recurring revenue, which need not be a subscription
  — repeat-purchase behavior (his Amazon example) counts. (2023-09-09) — content-marketing.
- Upsell vs. downsell, defined via a McDonald's analogy: an upsell offers *more* at
  purchase (fries, supersize, happy meal); a downsell offers a *cheaper* alternative
  after a "no" (small fries instead of large). The best upsells/downsells deliver faster
  or more-automated results (his device/toothbrush examples). Learned this framework
  from Ryan Deiss. (2023-09-09) — content-marketing.
- LTV (lifetime value) is grown primarily by directly asking customers what would
  improve their experience and iterating the product/service accordingly — not just by
  adding upsells. (2023-09-09) — content-marketing.

> Did too many businesses, some I loved, some I hated but live and learn.

> The way most people find their passion is they actually try a lot of different
> things... they stumble upon stuff that they're naturally good at and you tend to love
> the stuff that you're naturally good at.
