# Twitter's algo has been open-sourced

## Summary
Marketing School reacts to Twitter/X open-sourcing its recommendation algorithm (Elon
Musk had promised this "a year ago," per Eric). Neil frames open-sourcing as a
trust-building move despite inviting copycats, walks through algorithm details from the
released code (follower/following ratio, Twitter Blue boost, engagement-weighting
multipliers, relevancy decay), and argues transparency builds stronger communities long
term. The episode also surfaces a first-person biographical detail: in his mid-20s
(~10+ years before this taping, so roughly the mid-2010s) Neil ran a dedicated computer in
his Seattle home whose sole job was an automated Twitter follow/unfollow bot targeting
keyword-matched users.

## Key claims (dated, paraphrase unless noted)
- Elon Musk open-sourcing Twitter's algorithm (via GitHub) is a smart trust-building
  move even though it will spawn copycats — "the more transparent you are... the
  stronger community" you build, and it's worth the tradeoff despite inviting forks and
  extra regulatory/political scrutiny. (Neil, 2023-10-09) — social-media.
- From the released code: follower-to-following ratio affects the algorithm; Twitter
  Blue (paid) subscribers get a slight algorithmic boost; engagement is weighted
  heavily — a >2-minute dwell on a tweet counts ~20x a like, clicking into
  profile+like/reply ~24x, a reply ~54x, a reply-to-your-reply ~150x, and a report on
  the tweet is a roughly -738x penalty (source: the leaked/open-sourced weighting
  figures, not independently verified by Neil). Tweet relevancy also decays ~50% every
  six hours, so early engagement in a tweet's life matters most. (Neil, 2023-10-09) —
  social-media.
- ★ Biographical: recounts that when he and Eric first met (Neil then ~25–26, so roughly
  the mid-2010s, "10 plus years" before this 2023 taping), Eric visited his home in
  Seattle and found a separate Mac running full-time doing nothing but an automated
  Twitter growth bot — following accounts matched to a keyword (e.g. "SEO," "digital
  marketing"), then auto-unfollowing after ~7 days anyone who hadn't followed back. Neil
  confirms he was doing this follow/unfollow growth-hacking heavily in Twitter's early
  days. (Neil, 2023-10-09) — biography / social-media.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> If you end up releasing... everything out there, you're letting people know what
> you're doing, you're actually building a bigger sense of community than if you didn't
> do that, which I thought was a really smart move on Elon's end.

> Yeah, because it was following people, and then it would unfollow them... I would put
> in a keyword of all the followers you want to follow you, so like, I would put in SEO
> or digital marketing, and it would find all the people tweeting about that stuff,
> follow them, and then seven days later, for the people who didn't follow back, I would
> unfollow them, but I had a computer just running, doing that one thing full time.

> I'm a big believer in being transparent with the community. The more transparent you
> are, and the more open you are, I believe you're going to build a stronger community,
> and you're going to do better in the long run. You're going to get some haters from
> it, but it is worth it.
