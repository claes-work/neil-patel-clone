# Why You Should NEVER Ask a Rich Person for Advice (Do THIS Instead)

## Summary

A mindset monologue answering a viewer question about how to filter conflicting advice from
different friends/colleagues. Neil's core argument: people default to taking advice from
whoever is the most financially successful, but that's a mistake — **success in one field
doesn't transfer to another**. A rich doctor giving real-estate advice, or a construction
magnate advising on technology, is unreliable. The right move is to weight advice by
**domain expertise**: ask the person who is actually in the relevant field. He models
intellectual humility on himself — people might ask him (a successful marketer) for health
advice, but he says he knows little about nutrition ("I work out 15 minutes a day... I know
jack about nutrition"); ask him marketing, ask a certified nutritionist about nutrition. His
method for conflicting advice: hear people out, but cross-check by asking an industry expert
the same question; if they agree, execute; if the domain expert disagrees, follow the
expert. He uses Warren Buffett as a nuanced example — brilliant, but historically missed
Microsoft/Google/Amazon and is stronger on insurance/railroads than on tech, so ask Buffett
about his areas of deep experience, not cryptocurrencies.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-06-30, Neil argues you should weight advice by the giver's DOMAIN EXPERTISE,
  not their overall wealth/success — "the biggest mistake you'll make is asking rich people
  advice when they're not familiar with something."
- As of 2018-06-30, Neil's filtering method: hear advice out, then ask an actual industry
  expert the same question; if answers agree, execute; if the expert disagrees, follow the
  expert and ignore the non-expert.
- As of 2018-06-30, Neil cites Warren Buffett as smarter/more successful than himself but
  historically weaker on tech (missed Microsoft, Google, Amazon; only later bought Apple),
  strong on insurance (Berkshire Hathaway) and traditional companies — so match questions
  to a person's real expertise.
