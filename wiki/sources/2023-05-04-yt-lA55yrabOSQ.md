# The Power of AI: Discovering Its True Capabilities

## Summary
Part 2 of the same "Marketing Against The Grain" conversation as
[[2023-04-27-yt-l_U4aQTj6Mg]] (cold open reuses a line — "when you own [it]... you don't have
to worry about the ChatGPTs" — from later in this video's own content, and the opening question
picks up directly from Part 1's closing theme about search/AI disruption). States NP Digital
is ~750 employees, bootstrapped to nine figures, and that **no client considers SEO or paid
search a "moat"** — even large enterprise clients' revenue is diversified across many channels,
and moving clients to a true omnichannel approach (vs. running channels in silos) drops CPAs
across the board by **"more than 10%"**. Offers what he frames as a controversial marketing
thesis: **"software is a race to the bottom"** — give tools away free to acquire customers
cheaply, citing HubSpot's Website Grader as a precedent and doing back-of-envelope math that
MailChimp's ~$11B acquisition price implied a business where the actual email-sending
infrastructure cost (his estimate: ~$5M/year via third parties) is trivial next to customer
acquisition cost — the real expense worth optimizing. Extends a similar logic with a market-cap
comparison: **Paychex (~$39.43B market cap, payroll) vs. UnitedHealthcare (~$485B, health
insurance)** — argues payroll processing is cheap to run at scale, so a payroll company could
give the core product away and cross-sell high-margin attached products (health insurance) the
way Paychex or a similar player could. ★ Gives the most detailed **AnswerThePublic acquisition
narrative recorded to date**: bought for **$8.6M** (self-described overpay — target was
originally trying to buy for ~$6M with an earn-out), at the time doing ~$100K/month EBITDA
that he says was not "fully burdened" (the seller had no real employee costs); post-acquisition
they got it to ~$200K/month EBITDA fully burdened (~$2.4-3M/year); the acquisition thesis was
never software-revenue-first but **lead-gen and "land and expand"** — diligence showed ~70% of
Fortune 1000 companies used the tool, so NP Digital cold-called those users and sold agency
services division-by-division inside the same enterprise account, sometimes growing a single
customer relationship to $5.5M/year in services revenue against an $8.6M total acquisition
cost. Confirms **Ubersuggest** bought for $120K plus ~$3M invested, now accounting for
**>40% of NP Digital's clients**; AnswerThePublic now gets ~80% of Ubersuggest's traffic but
**<5% of agency revenue** (still lead-gen-only, not yet monetized as heavily). States NP
Digital avoided venture capital (declined pitching Sequoia, had a call with Bezos Expeditions)
once a contact told him the business was profitable enough to get banks to float
accounts-receivable on ad spend instead — the pitch to investors at the time was that most
marketing-industry money is spent on **services, not software** (contrasting HubSpot's market
cap against Omnicom's despite Omnicom generating comparable EBITDA to HubSpot's revenue), so
the opportunity was to disrupt old-line holding companies (WPP, Omnicom) rather than compete
with software incumbents (HubSpot, Zapier) who were "too late" to disrupt. States NP Digital's
revenue mix has shifted to **~71-72% word-of-mouth** in recent months (up from software-tool-
sourced leads), driven by enterprise land-and-expand referral patterns within multi-division
conglomerates (his example: a Mitsubishi-style client referring the agency across sibling
brands/divisions). Closing answer to "most underrated part of marketing right now": **creative**
(cites Squatty Potty and Harmon Brothers as examples of the kind of high-leverage creative work
he says AI cannot yet replicate) paired with **A/B testing**, both framed as underused levers
given continually rising ad costs.
