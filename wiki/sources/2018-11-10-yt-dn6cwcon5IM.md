# Why You Should NEVER Burn Bridges In Business

_Solo Neil-to-camera monologue with a personal story. All material Neil-attributed → persona data._

## Summary

Neil states one of his personal business rules: **never burn bridges**. In business you *will* get
screwed over — expecting only "colored rainbows" is naïve — but when it happens he wants you to
**seek a solution/agreement that works for both parties**, and if that fails, to **not retaliate**:
no bad-mouthing, no bad reviews, no public humiliation. He grounds it in a **belief in karma** —
doing good to others so good comes back (acknowledging "it may not always work that way, but that's
okay"). He tells a dated **personal story**: a deal in **Rockwall, Texas** (caption garble
"rockwell") — "a long time ago, before AWS existed" — where he funded people to build a
cloud-computing company, **lost over $1M**, and they stole from him; one woman **falsely claimed she
had cancer** and he **paid for treatment that didn't exist**. He knew where she lived but **never
pursued the money** — because **she has children** and he wouldn't want to wreck their lives. His
frame: life is too short; **forgive and forget**, treat setbacks as learning experiences, and hope
the other party ends up better and pays it forward.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-11-10, Neil's rule: **never burn bridges** — getting screwed over is inevitable in
  business, but retaliation (bad reviews, public humiliation, ruining reputations) is off-limits.
- As of 2018-11-10, when wronged: **try for a mutually-workable solution first**; venting privately
  to friends is fine, public retaliation is not.
- As of 2018-11-10, he **believes in karma** — do good to others and (hopefully) good comes back;
  he concedes it doesn't always work that way.
- As of 2018-11-10, **treat setbacks as learning experiences** and move forward; "life is too short
  to burn bridges and try to screw other people over."
