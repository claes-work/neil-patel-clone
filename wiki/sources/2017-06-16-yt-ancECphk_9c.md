# How to Setup a Corporate Blog: 8 Rules to Making Your Blog Successful

## Summary
Neil Patel walks through how to set up a corporate blog correctly, arguing that a successful business blog is built on helping the ideal customer solve their problems rather than promoting the company's own product or service. He covers the technical foundation (using WordPress and hosting the blog at yourdomain.com/blog because subdirectories rank better than subdomains), establishing the blog's voice with visible author bios and photos, and adding multiple staff writers through WordPress. He then lays out a set of writing rules for those authors: content must benefit the reader, use a conversational "you and I" tone, include subheadings, keep paragraphs to five or six lines, close with a conclusion that poses a question, and respond to every comment. He stresses cross-linking related posts, patience (results in six to twelve months), and a publishing cadence of ideally three posts per week, with at least one per week as a minimum and no more than seven. He cites the growth of his own NeilPatel.com blog as evidence that corporate blogging is a long-term play.

## Key claims
- A corporate blog should be about helping the ideal customer solve the problems they face, not about promoting your own product or service; blogging only about your product means "no one's really going to read your blog" (as of 2017-06-16).
- Blogs should be built on WordPress and hosted at yourdomain.com/blog (a subdirectory) rather than on a subdomain (as of 2017-06-16).
- Subdirectories rank better than subdomains because search engines like Google treat subdomains almost like a separate site; a blog placed in a subdirectory of an already-performing main site will do better quicker (as of 2017-06-16).
- Every post author should have a visible bio on the site, including their photo, one or two lines about who they are and what they do at the company, and optionally a link to their Twitter or Facebook profile — even for a brand (as of 2017-06-16).
- A blog can use one writer or many; additional authors are added easily in WordPress by entering a username and email under the users profile tab, and the person is then notified to log in and write (as of 2017-06-16).
- Writers should be given a rubric/template of rules, otherwise they will produce inconsistent content (as of 2017-06-16).
- Rule one: the content created should benefit the readers; if it doesn't, there's no point in blogging (as of 2017-06-16).
- Rule two: write in a conversational tone using the words "you" and "I" so it reads like a two-way conversation rather than boring and monotone text (as of 2017-06-16).
- Use subheadings to make posts easier to skim, and keep paragraphs to no more than five or six lines so information is easy to digest (as of 2017-06-16).
- Wrap up every post with a conclusion, and end the conclusion with a question to encourage readers to respond and leave comments (as of 2017-06-16).
- The post author should respond to each and every commenter, because ignoring a customer's question would be rude (as of 2017-06-16).
- Cross-link related posts within the body text (e.g. an SEO post linking to an online-marketing post) so posts get found (as of 2017-06-16).
- Corporate blogging is a long-term play requiring patience; results typically start showing in six to twelve months (as of 2017-06-16).
- Ideal publishing frequency is three posts per week; one per week is the minimum, publishing every two weeks is too infrequent, and more than seven posts per week is too much content — start slow and increase from there (as of 2017-06-16).
- Neil's own NeilPatel.com blog, started in January 2015, grew from roughly 9,000 monthly visitors from Google to 88,000 in January 2016 and over 400,000 in January 2017, illustrating that blogging is a long-term play (as of 2017-06-16).
