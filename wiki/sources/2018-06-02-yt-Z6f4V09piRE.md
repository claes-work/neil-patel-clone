# The #1 Key to Live a Successful Life

## Summary

An interview-prompted monologue ("Neil, what does a successful life mean to you?") in
which Neil defines success as **happiness**, not money or status — and stresses that
happiness is personal ("the eye of the beholder"). His central warning: don't "keep up
with the Joneses." When you achieve someone else's version of success, you may still not
be happy. He illustrates with his friend **Tim Sykes** (stock-trading educator): over
lunch in Vegas, Neil suggested Tim could double his business by working more, and Tim
replied he already has plenty in savings, doesn't care for more money, and loves helping
people and traveling. Tim then cited his student **Tim Grittani** (caption "tim gratani"),
a millionaire trader who made "500 grand in December and 500 grand in January," then took
February and March off because he "doesn't care for money" and lives a minimal life, big
on building schools. Neil's conclusion: figure out what happiness means to you and pursue
that — whether that's being a 9-to-5 employee at Microsoft who values family dinners and
park walks, or a CEO. There's nothing wrong with not being the richest or smartest person
in the room. He closes offering genuine, un-monetized help to viewers in the comments.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-06-02, Neil defines a successful life as happiness, and holds that happiness
  is individual — achieving another person's version of success won't necessarily make you
  happy (self-reported belief).
- As of 2018-06-02, Neil warns explicitly against "keeping up with the Joneses" —
  pursuing Ferraris, CEO titles, or Harvard professorships only to match others; do it
  only if it genuinely makes YOU happy.
- As of 2018-06-02, Neil recounts (secondhand) that Tim Sykes declined to grow his
  business further because "money is not everything," valuing helping people and travel.
- As of 2018-06-02, Neil recounts Tim Grittani (via Tim Sykes) making ~$500K in December
  and ~$500K in January, then taking two months off — cited as an example that more money
  isn't the goal for everyone (secondhand, unaudited).
- As of 2018-06-02, Neil affirms it's fine not to be the richest or smartest person in the
  room; the goal is to identify and pursue your own happiness.
