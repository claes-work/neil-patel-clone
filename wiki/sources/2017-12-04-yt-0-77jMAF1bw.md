# Business Advice 101: How I Lost $1 Million When I Was 21 ★

> ★ L3-candidate: One of Neil's rare first-person failure/origin stories — the $1M loss on a failed cloud-computing venture in his early twenties — with concrete biographical detail (agency at 16, borrowed not invested money, paid back in under a year) and the durable lessons he draws from it. Strong basis for a distilled biography/entrepreneurship page.

## Summary

In this rare personal solo video, Neil Patel recounts how he lost roughly $1 million by age 21 on a failed cloud-computing startup. He frames it against the survivorship-bias glamour of entrepreneurship ("everyone talks about private jets and Ferraris"), then walks through the specific mistakes: an amazing idea killed by poor execution and the wrong (non-scrappy, corporate-spending) people, moving too slowly, being "in over his head" and ignoring feedback from his parents and other entrepreneurs, and tolerating people who kept repeating the same mistakes. He notes the money was borrowed — not investor money — which he had to repay, and says he paid it back in under a year. He teases a follow-up on how he made his first million if the video gets over 1,000 likes.

## Key claims

- As of 2017-12-04, Neil says he lost about $1 million by the time he was 21 years old (self-reported).
- He states he ran an ad agency starting at age 16, and learned internet marketing himself after being ripped off by agencies that couldn't do the "simple thing" of ranking him on Google. (self-reported biography)
- He notes ranking on Google was "one of the easiest things to do" when he was 16, versus very hard now (2017); he says he had been paying thousands of dollars to people who couldn't even add keywords to his website. (dated opinion + biography)
- He says he hated being a consultant, so he decided to build a company — a cloud-computing solution — at a time when there "really wasn't any cloud computing solutions." (self-reported; he explicitly disclaims credit for the cloud-computing category)
- He says he invested over $1 million in the venture and lost it all; the business never launched.
- Lesson 1 — Ideas are a dime a dozen; execution is everything. The people he worked with, who claimed to know hosting/cloud computing, weren't executing, so nothing shipped. His rhetorical point: a company that never releases makes zero.
- Lesson 2 — He picked the wrong people. They weren't scrappy; they came from the corporate world (he names Microsoft and Google as archetypes) and were used to spending expensively — big servers/racks from Sun Microsystems, wanting their own centralized data center, bandwidth and pipeline rather than cheaper co-location. This drove high fixed costs he paid every month.
- He clarifies the money was borrowed money, not investor money — so he had to pay it back — meaning he "technically lost more than a million bucks." He says he paid it back in less than a year (a "totally different story"). (self-reported)
- Lesson 3 — Time is money; execute as fast as possible. Competitors will come; first-to-market tends to do better via press and branding.
- Lesson 4 — He was "in over his head" and ignored people around him: his parents warned he'd lose all his money (they were right), and he ignored feedback from entrepreneurs. His argument: even people who don't understand the business understand human interaction, emotions, and psychology, so you should at least consider their advice — you may make fewer mistakes.
- Lesson 5 — Learn from your mistakes. He let his team repeat the same mistakes over and over without correcting them; you can't succeed if you keep repeating the same mistakes.
- He offers a content-engagement bargain: if the video gets over 1,000 likes and comments/shares, he'll release a video on how he made his first million.
