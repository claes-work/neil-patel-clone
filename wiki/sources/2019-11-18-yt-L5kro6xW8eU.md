# Types of Keywords That Never Sell (Stop Wasting Your Time With Them)

## Summary
In this solo talking-head video, Neil Patel catalogs seven types of keywords he says are a waste of SEO effort because they either don't convert or are too costly to win, and argues marketers should chase specific, buyer-intent terms instead of raw traffic volume. The seven to avoid: (1) generic keywords (e.g. "credit cards") that pull unqualified traffic; (2) industry jargon (e.g. "schema markup") that attracts peers, not buyers; (3) wrong-intent keywords, especially anything with "free" in it; (4) out-of-context ambiguous keywords (e.g. "storage," "apple") whose mixed searcher intent wrecks user metrics and rankings; (5) unrelated informational-intent keywords that drive top-of-funnel awareness but not sales; (6) keywords that are simply too competitive (including high buyer-intent commercial terms like "cheap laptops"); and (7) over-reliance on ultra-long-tail keywords whose volume is too low to move the needle. He recommends Ubersuggest (free) to gauge keyword difficulty (closer to 100 = harder) and advises targeting three-to-four-word phrases rather than six-to-eight-word city-qualified strings. He closes plugging his agency Neil Patel Digital.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said pharmaceutical, online-gambling, payday-loan, credit-card and banking keywords are dominated by multi-billion-dollar companies spending hundreds of thousands to millions, so going after them means "you're not going to do well."
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said generic keywords attract visitors with no interest in your offering and don't qualify the visitor; ranking for a term like "credit cards" gets traffic but a tiny conversion rate, and the cost/time is so high relative to revenue that in most cases you lose money and get no positive ROI.
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said to avoid industry-specific technical jargon because most buyers aren't in your industry and don't use it; he gave his own example that ranking for "schema markup" brings other SEOs, not ready-to-buy customers who don't even know what schema markup is.
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said keywords with the wrong intent — anything bringing in visitors for free information ("free college advice," "free SEO advice," setting up an LLC "for free") — are the worst because those searchers rarely spend money, though he noted rare industries where "free" can still monetize.
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said to avoid out-of-context ambiguous keywords ("storage" could mean self-storage, boxes, computer storage or cloud; "apple" the company or the fruit) because they're unlikely to convert and the resulting bad user metrics hurt search rankings.
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said keywords with unrelated informational intent give top-of-funnel awareness traffic but not sales; if you need revenue, avoid them, and only consider them later once the business is established and profitable.
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said overly competitive keywords — including specific buyer-intent phrases like "cheap laptops" or "where's the best place to buy a laptop" — are super competitive and hard to rank for, and recommended checking difficulty in Ubersuggest (free): the closer the number is to 100 the harder it is, the lower the number the easier.
- As of 2019-11-18, Neil said focusing only on long-tail keywords is a mistake: they're easier to rank and bottom-of-funnel, but their volume is too low to grow traffic or generate many sales; over-qualified strings like "Black 2017 Mustang in Los Angeles" get almost no traffic, so target three-to-four-word phrases (e.g. "Black 2017 Ford Mustang") instead of six-to-eight-word ones.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "There're a lot of extremely competitive keywords out there. From pharmaceutical keywords to online gambling keywords to payday loans and credit cards and banking term."

> "The amount of cost, the money, the time you put into to rank for that term is going to be so expensive for how much revenue you make, it's not worth it."

> "Sure I'm going to get more SEO's to my website, but I'm not going to get potential buyers coming to my website who are ready to buy. Most of my buyers don't know even what schema markup is."

> "Keywords that only bring in visitors for free information are the worst!"

> "If you're not sure if the keywords you're going after are easy to go after or hard, use Ubersuggest, it's free. It'll tell you how hard that keyword is to go after versus how easy it is. The closer that number is to 100, the harder it is to rank for."

> "Try to go after three, four word phrases instead."
