# AI is stealing clicks, Google's war vs publishers, Google's long-term strategy & more (Marketing School)

## Summary
★ A co-hosted Marketing School episode framed as a Q&A: a paid-ads guest interviewer (the "paid guys," who refers to Neil and Eric in the third person and is therefore neither host) presses "the organic guys" on whether AI/SGE is just "stolen clicks" siphoning high-value traffic. Neil's core, quotable thesis is that AI won't kill organic search because Google is **financially disincentivized** to let it — Google earns $30B+/yr from its partner network, those partner sites run on SEO traffic (not paid arbitrage, which is dead), and per Google's Danny Sullivan the search engine has driven *more total clicks* to websites every year even as click-through *rates* fall, because more people keep using search. Neil concedes SGE will dent website traffic ("yes, I don't know by how much"), argues SGE is mostly iterations of features Google already had (weather, currency, knowledge graph) and often surfaces "crap" answers from crap sources, and that sophisticated/enterprise buyers don't hire off an SGE snippet — they dig deeper and call the company. A separate voice (uncertain — possibly Eric or the guest) counters that Google would sacrifice/flatten near-term revenue to avoid being replaced as the primary utility; the rebuttal (SEO-centric, likely Neil) is that hedge-fund/institutional ownership means execs get fired for crushing earnings, so Google's real playbook is incremental 0.1%-of-searches beta testing until it finds a more-profitable formula — it "can't break that money printer" and will be the slowest to change while competitors run laps. Landmark-worthy because it is Neil's clearest articulated defense of organic search in the AI era (the financial-disincentive argument + the Danny Sullivan clicks-keep-growing data), a strong L3 promotion candidate for the ai-marketing/seo persona layer.

## Key claims
- AI/SGE will not kill organic search because Google is financially disincentivized: Google makes $30B+/yr from its partner network, and those partner sites get their traffic from SEO — not from buying paid traffic to resend to Google (that arbitrage model is essentially dead for most industries). Google cannot tell the public it "killed all the SEO results" and wiped out that $30B in revenue. (Neil, 2024-02-15)
- Per Danny Sullivan (Google), Google has driven *more* total clicks to websites every single year: as they improve the search experience, more people use the engine, so even though a smaller *percentage* clicks through to any website, the absolute total number of clicks keeps growing. (Neil, 2024-02-15)
- SGE will affect website traffic — "yes" — but by an unknown amount. (Neil, 2024-02-15)
- SGE is largely iterations of features Google already had: typing "weather in Los Angeles" or "USD to British pounds" now shows the answer inline instead of sending you to weather.com — the same substitution the knowledge graph already did. And the inline/knowledge-graph answers are often not good, because much of the source content SGE pulls from is "crap," which produces crap responses. (Neil, 2024-02-15)
- Sophisticated / enterprise buyers don't act on an SGE response directly: a big corporation isn't going to read an SGE snippet and hire someone on a million-dollar contract — they dig deeper and get on the phone. They may *see* the SGE response, wonder who a cited company is, then go talk to that company directly — so the sophisticated buyer still reaches the source. (Neil, 2024-02-15)
- The debate rebuttal (SEO-centric, likely Neil): content creators are what feed Google — if Google simply cut off all publisher traffic and answered everything itself, creators would block Google and its search engine would be "screwed." Google can't break that "money printer," is too disincentivized to, and will therefore be the *slowest* to change while competitors run laps around it. `(attribution: likely Neil — continuous with his $30B/partner-network argument; no caption labels in a 3-way exchange)`

## Notable verbatim quotes
> If you look at what Danny Sullivan from Google said, Google has continually driven more clicks each and every single year to websites, because as they improve the experience they're getting more people using the search engine — a smaller percentage click through to the website, but the total number is still greater.

> You guys are paid ad guys — did you know that Google makes a bit more than $30 billion a year from their partner network? ... How do you think those sites get their traffic? They're not spending money on paid ads to get traffic to then send it back to Google — that's a losing battle. It's not arbitrage anymore. ... They're getting it from SEO.

> You think they can report to the public, "yeah, we just killed all the SEO results, and oh by the way it affected that $30 billion that we generated in revenue, and we're going to make it up but we don't know how yet"? It's not that simple.

> Do I think it'll affect a website's traffic? Yes. I don't know by how much though.

> Sometimes SGE is great, sometimes it's not — because a lot of the places they're pulling the answers from, the content's crap, which is creating crap responses.
