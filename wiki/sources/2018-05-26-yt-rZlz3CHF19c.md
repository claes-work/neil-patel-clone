# Business Mistakes: When to Apologize (And When to Keep Your Mouth Shut)

## Summary

An interview-style clip (an unnamed interviewer asks whether businesses should always apologize for mistakes or be unapologetic). Neil's rule of thumb: mistakes are inevitable; the response depends on the type. **When a topic is genuinely controversial with no clear right or wrong** (opinions split), the best move is to **keep quiet and let it die down**, because responding creates more press, controversy, and attention. His example: a debate over hunting (he references "the GoDaddy founder" and hunting) where it's legal but polarizing — nothing you say wins. **When you are clearly in the wrong**, you have no choice but to get in front of it and apologize. His examples of clear wrongs: Facebook's Cambridge Analytica data situation, and an old Comcast incident where a technician, kept on hold ~40 minutes calling his own company, fell asleep on a customer's couch and was recorded — Neil frames that as Comcast's fault (bad service/no tech priority), not the technician's, and notes Comcast eventually rebranded to Xfinity. He then discusses a third option — **leaning into polarization deliberately** — citing "Tim Sykes" firing hate back at critics to build a "cult-like following," and Chick-fil-A taking a side aligned with its demographic. But he stresses this is high-variance ("it'll either really help you or really hurt you") and says he personally doesn't like taking that risk. Ends on his usual "I don't care for the money, leave a comment, I'll help you out" sign-off.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-05-26, Neil says that when an issue is controversial with no clear right or wrong (split opinions), a business is better off staying silent and letting it die down, because responding generates more press and controversy.
- As of 2018-05-26, Neil says that when a company is clearly in the wrong, it has no choice but to get in front of it and apologize — citing Facebook/Cambridge Analytica and the Comcast technician-asleep-on-couch incident as clear wrongs.
- As of 2018-05-26, Neil frames the Comcast incident as the company's fault (poor service, no priority line for technicians) rather than the technician's, and notes Comcast later rebranded to Xfinity.
- As of 2018-05-26, Neil says a company can instead deliberately lean into a polarizing side to build a loyal following — citing Tim Sykes firing hate back at critics and Chick-fil-A siding with its demographic — but that this is high-variance and he personally avoids the risk.
