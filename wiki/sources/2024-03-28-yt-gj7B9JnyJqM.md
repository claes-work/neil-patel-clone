# 2500 LinkedIn leads on Retention.com, When should you sell?, The end of Google? (Marketing School)

## Summary
A three-topic co-hosted Marketing School episode. In part one Neil drives a walkthrough of Adam Robinson's (CEO of Retention.com, ~$22–24M/yr run-rate) viral post claiming 2,500 LinkedIn signups and 328 booked meetings with VP/C-level marketers in 90 days via an "inbound-led outbound" playbook; Neil's core read is that the results come from a *novel lead-capture mechanism* (Retention.com's software surfacing anonymous visitors' email addresses) plus a strong offer — "what works for them won't work for most people" — and he loops back to his standing dislike of business coaches. Eric adds that the novelty and legality will not last and that the replicable meta-lesson is "have an amazing offer." Part two (Eric-driven) is a "when should you sell" riff off a thread of famous declined acquisitions (Facebook/Yahoo, Groupon/Google, Yahoo/Microsoft, Path, Digg); Eric's take is to sell once the money makes you "set," and he cites Clubhouse as founders overrating their own company — with Neil's Clubhouse skepticism (which proved right) reported as a data point. Part three reacts to an ex-Google engineer's viral post arguing Google already "lost" to OpenAI over the last decade on clock speed; Neil's synthesis is that Google loses *some* search share but won't die (still "one of the best money printers"), that its ad money lives in transactional/commercial-intent keywords not informational queries, and — his notable forward call — that as searches move to gen AI ("generative engine optimization"), affiliate marketing will tick up. Attribution is roughly 50/50 and heavily interleaved; parts of the AI segment are back-and-forth and flagged.

## Key claims
- Retention.com case study (Neil relaying/endorsing Adam Robinson's post): using LinkedIn, Robinson generated 2,500 signups and 328 booked meetings with VP- and C-level marketers in 90 days for a "person-level ID inbound-led outbound" playbook; Robinson is CEO of Retention.com, reportedly ~$22–24M/yr run-rate. The mechanic is enriching the traffic already coming to the site, then reaching out to start conversations. (Neil, 2024-03-28) `(figures recalled/second-hand)`
- The playbook Neil walks through, step by step: (1) create the best content, full stop — killer headlines where each line is good enough to make them read the next (Robinson says it took a year to crack and is still a ton of work); (2) end posts with "DM me if you're interested," with hired LinkedIn consultants doing inbox management — hundreds of inbounds/month, either setting calls or waitlisting (reps taking ~5–8 calls/day); (3) spend "way too much time" in the comments to build relationships; (4) share successes *and* failures by working in public; (5) get other people on your team posting too for a compounding reach effect; (6) connect with site visitors over LinkedIn *and* email — add the ICP-fit visitor on LinkedIn, send a thoughtful email, a LinkedIn message 24h later, then a second email — a multi-touch sequence. (Neil, 2024-03-28)
- The engine behind the whole thing is Retention.com's software: when a known visitor lands on your site, it surfaces that person's email address without them ever submitting it. Neil questions whether that is even legal in every country and says NP Digital does not use it on their own websites. (Neil, 2024-03-28)
- "What works for them won't work for most people": the results come from capturing leads without people knowing (a novel mechanism) plus a strong offer — not from copying the content steps alone. People react in one of two ways when told how their email was obtained — creeped out, or "yes, I want this for my business." (Neil, 2024-03-28)
- Neil reiterates his standing anti-coach stance (ties it to a prior "why I hate coaches" episode): many self-styled coaches "make their money by telling people they'll help them make money," with no specific skill set, claiming to help with everything ("I'll help you get more fit, I'll help you do better in business"); he draws a direct parallel between those coaches' sales playbook and the Retention.com pitch — impressive results that most buyers can't reproduce. (Neil, 2024-03-28)
- On Google vs. gen AI: Google will lose *some* search market share to OpenAI/ChatGPT and Gemini but is not going to die — it's still "one of the best money printers in the world," and things don't die fast (Microsoft slowed for years, then came back). OpenAI won't get 100%, or even necessarily the largest chunk, of search. The realistic outcome is a mixture of both, each with a purpose. (Neil, 2024-03-28)
- Google's ad money comes from *transactional* keywords, not informational queries: nobody monetizes "what's the average height of a pyramid in Egypt." If people shift research/informational queries to ChatGPT it eats some share, but the commercial-intent money keywords are stickier. (Neil, 2024-03-28)
- Forward prediction: over the next 10–20 years, as more searches move to gen AI ("generative engine optimization" / GEO), affiliate marketing will tick up and become more popular — AI answers handing off affiliate transactions "at a much higher level." Neil does *not* expect gen AI to replace the ad model completely. (Neil, 2024-03-28)
- Neil was skeptical of Clubhouse and was proven right (reported by Eric): he "didn't see it," and the platform's value evaporated the moment you left it; followers don't necessarily equate to revenue. (Neil-fact, reported by Eric, 2024-03-28)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> If someone visits your website and they have that email in their database, they tell you that person's email address without them even giving it to you — which I don't even know if it's legal in every country. Like, we don't use it on our own websites.

> What he's doing, no one else is going to generate the same amount of leads with a similar amount of effort — even if your content's great — because he's capturing leads without people knowing it, and then letting them know. And they'll be like "wow, how do you know this? This is cool." Either I'm creeped out, or "yes, I want this for my business." The sales pitch that works for him won't work for most people.

> So you made your money by telling people you'll teach them how to make money — you made your money by just telling people you'll help them make money. And there's not necessarily a specific skill set: they help with everything — "I'll help you get more fit, I'll help you do better in business." I'm like, what business experience do you have?

> Google's going to lose some share — they're not going to die, and search is probably going to be around for a while. It's still one of the best money printers in the world.

> The big money-making revenue for them is ads — but it's from these transactional keywords. It's not from people Googling a specific question like "what's the average height of a pyramid in Egypt." That's not where they're making money.

> In the next 10 to 20 years, if more and more people are using gen AI for their searches, I believe we could be moving into a world where it's much easier to do affiliate marketing... I think affiliate marketing is going to tick up and become more popular over time.
