# The Importance of Building a Brand | How to Measure Size of Your Brand

## Summary
Neil Patel argues that a strong brand — not any single traffic channel — is what builds a durable, large company. He frames branding as "anchoring" your name into a specific term, category, feeling, or emotion, citing McDonald's/Burger King/In-N-Out with hamburgers, Visa and American Express with credit cards, Kleenex with tissues, Slack/Skype with workplace communication, and Amazon with cheap, fast-delivered goods. The practical method: identify the problem your industry lets you solve, then relentlessly repeat ("hammer down") your solution across every channel until people mentally connect your company with that one thing. He invokes Warren Buffett's advice — that when forced to choose between more money and protecting your brand, protect the brand, because that builds the bigger company long-term. To measure brand size, he recommends typing your company name into Google Trends: no results means a small brand, and a line climbing up and to the right means the brand is growing (he cites his own "Neil Patel" trend line, with spikes from press and interviews). A flat or declining line signals insufficient marketing, or marketing that fails to connect emotionally through storytelling.

## Key claims
- A strong brand — not Google, social media, or TV commercials — is the main long-term source of a company's revenue and what lasts (as of 2017-05-20).
- The power of branding is anchoring your name/company into a specific term, feeling, or emotion so people mentally associate you with one service or category (as of 2017-05-20).
- Established brands own their categories in customers' minds: Visa/American Express for credit cards, Kleenex for tissues, Slack/Skype for workplace communication, Amazon for cheap and fast-delivered goods (as of 2017-05-20).
- The method to build a brand: figure out what problem your industry lets you solve, then repeatedly push ("hammer down") that solution everywhere until people know your company does that one specific thing (as of 2017-05-20).
- Neil attributes to Warren Buffett (relayed via his business partner Mike) the advice that when choosing between more money and protecting your brand, you should protect the brand because it creates a bigger company in the long run (as of 2017-05-20).
- Neil notes Buffett deliberately writes a short one-page yearly letter to his companies' CEOs because longer letters won't get read by busy executives (as of 2017-05-20).
- You can gauge brand size with Google Trends (google.com/trends): no results means a small brand; the goal is a graph that climbs "up and to the right," indicating a rising brand (as of 2017-05-20).
- Spikes in a brand's trend line come from press, mentions, interviews, magazines, or TV coverage (as of 2017-05-20).
- A flat or declining trend line means either not enough marketing, or marketing that fails to connect — from too little emotion, storytelling, or audience connection (as of 2017-05-20).
