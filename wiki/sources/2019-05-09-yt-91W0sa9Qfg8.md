# Ranking #1 With a New Website on Google in 2019 — Is it Even Possible?

## Summary

Neil argues that yes, even a brand-new or long-stagnant site can rank — but only by putting
**the user first**, not by grinding the mechanical fundamentals. His centerpiece is an
**Ahrefs case study**: Ahrefs couldn't rank for "backlink checker" despite building links
and internal linking, because searchers wanted a *free* tool and Ahrefs was paid; the moment
they shipped a **free version limited to ~100 backlinks** (preserving upsell to the paid
tool), they shot to **#1 within weeks**. The lesson: Google now weighs **user behavior** —
if people click your result then immediately hit Back (pogo-stick to the next result),
Google reads that as dissatisfaction and won't keep ranking you, no matter how strong your
on-page/off-page SEO. Conversely, satisfying the user can rank you even with imperfect
backlinks/internal links. To learn what users want, survey them (e.g. **SurveyMonkey**)
with questions like "What else would you like to see on this page?" ★ L3-candidate: crisp
"user-first / search-intent over fundamentals" framework with a memorable case study.

## Key claims

- As of 2019-05-09, Neil said a new or stagnant site **can still rank** organically, but the
  method differs from conventional SEO advice — you must put the **user first**.
- As of 2019-05-09, Neil cited **Ahrefs**: unable to rank for "backlink checker" via links
  alone, they released a **free ~100-backlink tool** and reached **#1 within weeks** by
  matching searcher intent (a free tool).
- As of 2019-05-09, Neil said Google weighs **user behavior** — quick bounces back to the
  SERP ("pogo-sticking") tell Google users dislike the page, overriding on/off-page SEO.
- As of 2019-05-09, Neil said satisfying the user can rank you **even with imperfect
  backlinks/internal links**, and to learn user wants via surveys (**SurveyMonkey**:
  "What else would you like to see on this page?").
