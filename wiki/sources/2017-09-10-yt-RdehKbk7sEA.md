# How to Increase The Lifetime Value (LTV) of Your SaaS Customers

## Summary
Neil argues that for a SaaS business the hardest part is acquiring subscribers, so the payoff comes from keeping them long enough to maximize lifetime value. He lays out three tactical levers plus one overriding factor. First, optimize the onboarding experience so new subscribers start using the product immediately — he warns that a customer who buys but does not use the product in the first week is likely to cancel within a few months. He suggests following up by email, adding chat, and using a heatmap/session tool like Crazy Egg to watch how people navigate onboarding, then doubling down on what works and fixing where people get stuck. Second, track login/usage frequency because usage drives higher LTV and non-usage drives churn; re-engage with email reminders, reports, and text messages. Third, survey users to learn what they love and hate, but also watch actual in-app behavior and talk to customers by phone rather than relying on words alone. Above all, he insists the real driver of LTV is product quality: a bad product produces bad LTV and a great product produces amazing LTV — citing Slack as a product with high LTV because people love it despite flaws he sees in its website.

- The method is to study users who complete onboarding versus those who drop off, find where people get stuck, and fix it (as of 2017-09-10).
- Login/usage frequency predicts retention: usage causes higher LTV and non-usage causes lower LTV, so infrequent logins signal likely churn (as of 2017-09-10).
- Re-engage users with email reminders, reports, and text messages to keep them active (as of 2017-09-10).
- Survey users to find what they love, hate, and want improved — but do not rely on their words alone; also watch what they actually use and get results from inside the app (as of 2017-09-10).
- Talking to customers on the phone and watching them use the product reveals more than words alone and helps you build a better product (as of 2017-09-10).
- The true driver of LTV is the product itself: a bad product yields bad LTV and a great product yields amazing LTV (as of 2017-09-10).
- Slack has amazing LTV because people love the product, even though Neil is not a heavy Slack user and sees things wrong with its website (as of 2017-09-10).
