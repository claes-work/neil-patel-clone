# Why I Feel Investing In Real Estate Is A Mistake

## Summary
Family-office interview hosted by **Andy Haggins**, who says he met Neil around 2006 at a
marketing conference through **Patrick Gavin** (whose company Neil worked for early on —
Neil is unsure whether it was called Text Link Ads or Position One). Same taped conversation
as [[2023-05-01-yt-f6NHHY0LAJs]] (shared "family office" framing, overlapping subject matter,
a cold open reused mid-transcript). ⚠️ **Self-reported/media-cited credentials, unverified**:
the host's on-camera introduction states Neil is an NYT-bestselling author, was called a "top
influencer" by the Wall Street Journal, one of the "top 10 marketers" by Forbes, built one of
Entrepreneur Magazine's "100 most brilliant companies," and was named a top-100-entrepreneur-
under-30 by (then-President) Obama and top-100-under-35 by the United Nations — consistent
with the self-reported, no-primary-document-found awards/recognitions pattern already flagged
in SUBJECT.md; Neil does not correct or elaborate on any of these claims on camera. Recaps
origin story: started building websites/traffic at 15-16, cold-called Google-ads-buying
businesses offering free marketing work ("if you're happy, pay me. If you're not, don't"),
built an agency to a few million dollars in revenue before it "crashed really hard" in the
2008 financial crisis — while **Crazy Egg** (his separate analytics company) kept growing
through the crash, forcing an involuntary pivot from services to software; later returned to
agency work with NP Digital (currently ~750 employees, projects ~1,000 within a year). Gives a
detailed and pointed **real-estate-is-a-mistake argument for personal residences** specifically
(not real estate generally): buying a home you live in isn't a realized investment since the
gains can't be captured while occupying it, versus renting and compounding the difference in
the S&P (~9%/year); says the family holds roughly **$20-30M in real estate equity, mostly
cash/unleveraged**, is building new homes in **Las Vegas** and **Beverly Hills** (currently in
a rental), and separately still owns real estate as an investment class when structured as an
LP/co-GP position rather than a primary residence — citing a prior tuck-in acquisition
("bought a business last year in February for 8.6 [million]," matching the AnswerThePublic
figure) as an example of the kind of return real estate can't match. Details his angel-
investing history: started around age 20-21, now 16+ years in, LP in a fund run by a friend
he calls **"Andy [Liu]"** (founder of a venture fund he refers to informally as "Unlock
[Venture Partners]"), whose own track record (sold a company that was later acquired by
Microsoft via **aQuantive**, then sold a second company to **Vizio**) is what earned Neil's
trust to write checks with minimal diligence. Describes his LP-selection heuristic as
**relationship- and "skin in the game"-based rather than formal due diligence**: he sizes his
own check based on how much a trusted friend is personally committing, scaled to that friend's
net worth, on the logic that meaningful personal exposure signals real conviction. Frames
tech investing as power-law/unicorn-driven (compares it to a baseball batting average — a
".300 hitter" is elite despite failing most of the time) versus real estate's lower-variance,
harder-to-total-loss profile (unless over-leveraged). Closes on the administrative pain of
scaling angel investing without a fund wrapper: describes sending **50-60+ K-1 tax forms** to
his accountant in a single tax season as the practical trigger for consolidating check-writing
into fewer, larger LP commitments.
