# The Effective Ranking Factor That Google Doesn't Want You to Know About

## Summary
Neil Patel argues that beyond the three conventional levers of Google rankings — building backlinks, improving on-page SEO, and improving user metrics — brand signals are an underrated ranking factor. He claims that the more people search Google for a brand's name (which for him is also his domain, neilpatel.com), the more Google reads that brand as popular and ranks it higher. Neil reports that he moved from page two to page one for the term "online marketing" without changing his content or generating new backlinks, attributing the jump purely to increased brand signals. As evidence, he says he "artificially inflated" his brand signals by having people on Instagram and Facebook hold up signs asking "Who is Neil Patel?", driving thousands of viewers per hour to search his name and click his result. He cautions that artificially inflated signals decay (and rankings drop with them) unless sustained by good content and a strong product or service that keeps people returning to search the brand. He illustrates the mechanism with BMW vs. General Motors: once sites have millions of backlinks, Google leans on brand preference — if three times more people search one car brand, it should rank higher for "cars." His takeaway is to keep building a strong brand like McDonald's, American Express, VISA, or Nike, not just optimize on-page and off-page SEO.

## Key claims
- The three main things you can do to improve Google rankings are building links, improving on-page SEO, and improving user metrics (as of 2017-06-04).
- Brand signals are a key additional strategy that can meaningfully increase rankings (as of 2017-06-04).
- The more people who Google a brand's name, the more it signals to Google that the brand is popular, and Google ranks popular brands higher (as of 2017-06-04).
- Neil moved from page two to page one for "online marketing" with no content changes and no new backlinks — only increased brand signals (as of 2017-06-04).
- He deliberately/artificially inflated his brand signals by having people on Instagram and Facebook hold up "Who is Neil Patel?" signs, which drove thousands of hourly viewers to search his name and click his result (as of 2017-06-04).
- Artificially inflated brand signals are not durable: if they drop, rankings drop too — sustained rankings require ongoing good content (video, audio, etc.) and an amazing product or service so people keep returning to search the brand (as of 2017-06-04).
- Once competing sites both have millions of backlinks, backlinks stop being the differentiator, and Google uses brand preference (branded search volume) to decide which ranks higher — e.g., BMW vs. General Motors for "cars" (as of 2017-06-04).
- Google Trends shows spikes in searches for "Neil Patel" (filtered to the entrepreneur), which he presents as visible evidence of brand-signal activity (as of 2017-06-04).
- Marketers should continually build a strong brand (like McDonald's, American Express, VISA, Nike), not only optimize on-page and off-page SEO (as of 2017-06-04).
