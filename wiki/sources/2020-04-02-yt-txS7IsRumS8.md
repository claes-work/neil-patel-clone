# How to Choose a Great Business Name | Creating an Amazing Brand

## Summary
A branding-fundamentals video (not SEO-specific). Neil opens citing an estimated 30
million companies worldwide (roughly one per 26 people) and, per the Small Business
Administration, 627,000 new U.S. businesses opening each year, framing naming as
increasingly hard given saturation. He cites a University of Alberta 2010 study finding
consumers respond well to repetitively structured brand names (Coca-Cola, Kit Kat,
Jelly Belly). He lists what makes a good name (meaningful, visual, distinctive,
accessible) but stresses audience fit above all — walks through naming categories with
examples: founder names (Jordan Brand, Musk's Tesla honoring Nikola Tesla), acronyms
(KFC softening "fried," GEICO originally "Government Employees Insurance Company"),
descriptive names (American Airlines, Home Depot), suggested names — real dictionary
words (Uber = "outstanding/supreme"), composite words (Facebook), invented words
(Pinterest = "pin" + "interest"), associative/metaphorical names (SiriusXM), foreign-
language-derived names (Hulu, from a Chinese term for a bowl storing precious things),
and abstract/phonetic names (Rolex). Advises naming to evoke emotional appeal over
literal description (Nike = winning/overcoming adversity; Coca-Cola = nostalgia/
togetherness), testing candidate names via targeted ad campaigns, landing pages, and
surveys/focus groups, keeping names simple/pronounceable/memorable — retells the
story that Jeff Bezos originally wanted to name Amazon "Cadabra" (from "Abracadabra")
until his incorporation lawyer misheard it as "Cadaver," prompting the rename — and
closes with due-diligence steps: trademark searches per country, and checking the name
doesn't carry a negative meaning in other languages. Plugs his agency, NP Digital.
