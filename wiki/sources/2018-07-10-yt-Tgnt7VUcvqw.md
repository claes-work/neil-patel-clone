# You Are Pricing Your Product WRONG! How to Determine Optimal Price for Profit

## Summary

A solo tactical monologue on how to set the optimal price for a product or service. Neil
gives two frameworks. First, **benchmark against competitors**: compare your feature set,
usability, and quality to theirs — if yours is as good or better, price at parity or higher
(a better product can even command more); if yours has fewer features / is weaker, price
below them. His default move is to match features and price roughly the same or a bit less,
because an equal-or-better solution at a slightly lower price is much easier to sell. Second,
if the goal is **maximizing profit rather than grabbing market share**, split-test the price:
run an A/B test (he names Crazy Egg, Optimizely, VWO) with two price points, usually testing
higher first, and optimize for total revenue/profit — not raw conversion count. He warns to
check that higher prices aren't driving more refunds/chargebacks (which erode the net), and
illustrates that 10 sales at $100 ($1,000) beats 100 sales at $1 ($100) even though it "converts"
10× lower. Underpricing is the lever to crush competitors and gobble market share; price
testing is the lever to optimize profitability.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-07-10, Neil frames pricing off competitor comparison: equal/better product →
  price at parity or higher; weaker product → price lower.
- As of 2018-07-10, his personal default is to match features and price roughly the same as
  or slightly below competitors, because an equal-or-better product at a lower price wins
  business more easily.
- As of 2018-07-10, to maximize profit he recommends A/B split-testing price points (naming
  Crazy Egg, Optimizely, VWO), typically testing a higher price first.
- As of 2018-07-10, he insists you optimize for **total revenue/profit per variation**, not
  the conversion percentage — 10 sales × $100 beats 100 sales × $1.
- As of 2018-07-10, he cautions that a higher price can raise refunds/chargebacks, so the net
  dollar figure (not gross sales) is what matters.
- As of 2018-07-10, he distinguishes two goals: underprice a better product to seize market
  share, vs. price-test to optimize profitability.
