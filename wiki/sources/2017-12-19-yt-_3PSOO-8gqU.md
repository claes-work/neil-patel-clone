# How to Rank #1 on Google for the World's Most Competitive Keywords | Ranking First Page for 'SEO'

## Summary
Neil Patel breaks down how his neilpatel.com page ranks on page one of Google for the term **"seo"** — which he calls the most competitive term because he's competing against other well-funded marketers who know what they're doing. His steps: (1) **pillar content** — the ranking page must be super-detailed and thorough (he cites Moz's "Beginner's Guide to SEO" as the #1 result, noting his own page is less thorough but "the more thorough you are the better off you're going to be"); (2) **beautiful design** — "no one likes looking at ugly web pages"; (3) **fast load speed**, especially on mobile (>50% of Google users are on mobile); (4) **internal cross-linking** — link to the pillar page from every other page on the site to pass link equity and signal importance to Google; (5) **link outreach via Ahrefs** — find who links to competitors and email them a short, relevant pitch (send 100, expect ~5 links; fewer than 5 means you're emailing irrelevant people); (6) **patience** — his /seo page took **a year and a half** to reach page one, climbing top-1000 → top-100 → page 5 → 4 → 3 → 2 → 1 while he kept updating it; (7) **brand as the tiebreaker** — when two sites have equal links and budget (e.g., BMW vs. General Motors), Google ranks the one with the bigger brand / more branded searches higher, so build a brand consistently over years to lift rankings for *everything*. This reinforces Neil's recurring brand-as-ranking-signal and skyscraper/outreach themes with a concrete keyword case study.

## Key claims
As of 2017-12-19, Neil states:
- He ranks on **page one for "seo"**, which he frames as the hardest possible term because the competition is other skilled, well-funded marketers, not just ordinary companies (as of 2017-12-19).
- **Pillar content**: the page must be "super detailed and thorough" — one piece that "explains everything out there under the sun for that one topic" (Moz's Beginner's Guide is his cited #1 example); "the more thorough you are the better off you're going to be in rankings" (as of 2017-12-19).
- **Design matters**: better-designed, more beautiful pages rank better because "people don't like looking at ugly web pages" (as of 2017-12-19).
- **Speed matters**: pages must load fast, especially since ">50% of the people using Google are on their mobile phone or mobile device" (as of 2017-12-19).
- **Internal linking**: cross-link the pillar page from every other page on the site — it passes "more juice" and tells Google the page is important (he does this to his /seo article site-wide) (as of 2017-12-19).
- **Link outreach via Ahrefs**: find who links to competitor URLs and email a short, relevant pitch; "if you send 100 of those you should get roughly five people linking back," and fewer than 5 means you're targeting irrelevant sites (as of 2017-12-19).
- **Patience**: the /seo page "took a year and a half before it popped onto the first page," climbing from top-1000 → top-100 → page 5 → 4 → 3 → 2 → 1 while he continually updated and improved it (as of 2017-12-19).
- **Brand is the ranking tiebreaker**: for two equally-resourced sites (BMW vs. General Motors), Google ranks the one with more branded searches higher; building a brand consistently for "a year, two, three years" lifts rankings "not just for competitive terms but for everything" (as of 2017-12-19).
