# 7 Advanced Link Building Tactics That Skyrocket Rankings

## Summary
In this solo video, Neil Patel argues that generic link building tactics (guest posts, "you linked to my competitor, link to me too" outreach) no longer work well, that you can't buy links, but that you still need links to rank on Google. He then walks through seven "advanced" tactics, each aimed at earning links from high-authority sources: (1) run an industry survey of CEOs/executives and package the data into pretty graphs; (2) host a live event or virtual summit with a niche theme, downloadable resources, and industry speakers, then build a dedicated recordings page as the main link magnet; (3) produce highly detailed case studies and "ego bait" — including teardowns of big companies like Airbnb, Delta, and Microsoft — then pitch reporters covering those companies; (4) leverage interactive maps as "the new infographics"; (5) use Brian Dean's "Moving Man Method" to reclaim links from migrating/rebranding competitors; (6) run a podcast tour, pitching 15–30 podcasts and offering a free on-site resource for the show notes; and (7) find unlinked brand mentions via Google Alerts, BuzzSumo, and SEMrush and convert them into links, ideally with richer anchor text. He closes by promoting his agency, Neil Patel Digital.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said that to rank on Google you need to build links, but link building is hard and everyone is doing the same generic tactics (guest posting, competitor-backlink outreach) that mostly "doesn't work that well anymore." (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said you can't buy links. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil recommended running an industry survey: contact CEOs, executives, and thought leaders, ask them questions about a hot industry topic, compile the results, send them your findings, and produce "really pretty graphs" — using Fiverr (~$5), Canva, or Infogram — so the piece goes viral and earns shares and links from the experts involved. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said he goes "above and beyond" by framing surveys around big industry trends (e.g. "the state of digital marketing," "the state of the internet," "the state of the healthcare industry"), because featuring executives around a big topic makes people more likely to read, share, and link. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil recommended hosting a live event or virtual summit around a specific niche theme, packaged with downloadable resources (PDF action guides, templates, swipe files, transcripts, cheat sheets), audience Q&A with guests, and speaker collaboration — speakers promote it for publicity and can pitch their own products in their talks. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said to build relationships with influencers/guests before the event (comment on their social profiles, subscribe to their email lists, promote their speaking spots); when you land many influencers, they link to and promote the event from their own websites. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said the biggest link source from an event is a dedicated landing page with all the recordings, cheat sheets, PDFs, and courses emailed to attendees — attendees both share and link to it. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said case studies and ego bait earn links only when they are "super detailed" and break down step by step how someone improved conversion rates or traffic — vague case studies teach nothing and get few links. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said detailed case studies attract "enterprise"/larger brands with higher domain authority, whose links count more, and that one case study won't get many links but doing five or ten across an industry compounds. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said you can do teardowns of businesses you don't work with — e.g. how Airbnb grew from a startup to a multibillion-dollar company and dominated the hotel industry, how Delta Airlines turned around, or how Microsoft went from flat growth to growing "like a rocket ship" — and then pitch the specific reporters (e.g. The New York Times, Business Insider) covering those companies so they link to the case studies. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said maps are "the new infographics," crediting Glen Allsopp; people love seeing how things are distributed worldwide, a Google search for `intitle:"this map shows"` returns over 46,000 results, and interactive maps on your site earn "a ton of links" because they constantly change. (paraphrase; cites the New York Times interactive map of what you need to earn to be in the one percent by state as an example)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil described Brian Dean's "Moving Man Method": when a competitor rebrands/migrates domains, do a backlink analysis, create a better or equivalent version of their most-linked content, then after they migrate, contact everyone still linking to the old outdated URL, tell them the pages no longer exist, and suggest linking to your equivalent/better piece. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil recommended a podcast tour: search for podcasts that host guests in your industry (e.g. google "podcast interviews for Tony Robbins" for a life coach, or "podcast interviews for Rand Fishkin / Neil Patel" for a marketer), pitch 15–30 of them on a specific valuable topic, offer a free resource hosted on your website, and they'll link to it in their show notes. (paraphrase)
- As of 2019-10-28, Neil said to find unlinked brand mentions by monitoring your brand via Google Alerts, BuzzSumo, and SEMrush (and searching Google directly with quotation marks for multi-word brands), then reach out and ask people who mention you to link — and, when relevant, pitch for keyword-rich anchor text to boost rankings. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You all know that if you want to rank on Google, you need to build links."

> "But link building is hard, and everyone is already doing the same generic tactics like let me post on your site, hey, I notice you linked to my competitors, can you also link to me? A lot of that stuff doesn't work that well anymore, but you still need links, what should you do? Well, you can't buy them."

> "Now I want you to produce really pretty graphs. You can go to places like Fiverr if you want to pay someone five bucks to produce the graphs for you, or you can just use tools like Canva or Infogram to produce the graphs."

> "The more detailed you get, you'll get more enterprise companies linking to it. It doesn't matter if you're B2B, B2C. What I mean enterprise, I'm talking about larger brands, they're the ones with the higher domain authority. So when they link, those links count more."

> "Now you'll find that if you just release one case study, you won't get too many links, but if you do five or 10 in an industry, that's when it starts adding up."

> "The fourth tactic is leveraging maps. Maps are the new infographics. Glen Allsopp figured this out."

> "go to Google and search intitle this map shows. There's over 46,000 results."

> "The fifth strategy is leveraging the Moving Man method, and this tactic is by Brian Dean."

> "And when you do a podcast tour, and you hit up 15, 20, 30 of these podcasters, and you get on a handful of them, all of them will link back to your website."

> "You'll find not only that you'll get a link, but you'll get a link with a richer anchor text which will help boost your rankings."
