# The future of SEO, Influencer marketing's evolution, & Why 100% of sales execs' LinkedIn posts fail (Marketing School)

## Summary
★ Landmark-worthy for later L3 promotion: Neil's crisp, reusable **personal-brand-vs-corporate-brand** stance — build (and compound over decades) an amazing *corporate* brand rather than over-indexing on a personal one; "keep the main thing the main thing."

A co-hosted Marketing School episode (Neil Patel + Eric Siu, two hosts only — no guest). Four interleaved topics. (1) *Future of SEO*: Neil argues SEO is no longer just Google — it's ranking across Google, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube and "Search GPT," and that companies pump out social content but forget to optimize it to actually *rank/recommend* on each platform; Eric extends it to TikTok/Amazon/YouTube search and LLMs (RAG pulling from search engines, so LLMs build on top of Google rather than replacing it) plus an omni-channel/diversify thesis. (2) *"SEO as we know it is dead"* (Neil-owned card off a Charles-SEO tweet): the old Google-only SEO is dead, each channel is its own game (YouTube = headlines/packaging/first-30s retention; TikTok/Reels = hook/anti-swipe/thumbnail), and NP Digital now fields weekly requests to optimize for TikTok/Instagram (even Reddit/WhatsApp) that were rare 1–2 years ago. (3) *Influencer marketing's rigorous, metrics-driven era* (Neil-owned card off an Ad Age piece): mega-celebrity deals are an expensive "black hole," engagement ≠ ROI, and micro-influencers — though more work — give trackable, repeatable ROI via unique landing pages, promo codes and swipe-up links, tracked with plain analytics + campaign UTMs. (4) *LinkedIn / Jason Lemkin's "~100% of prolific-posting sales/marketing execs fail"*: both hosts call the 100% exaggerated; Neil's substantive take is the personal-vs-corporate-brand framework above, that the real corporate money comes from doing great work and getting promoted at ever-bigger companies, and that he'd scrutinize a VP marketing himself. A middle *branding-vs-performance ("and," not "or")* exchange is heavily interleaved and largely quarantined below. Attribution split is roughly balanced; the SEO-dead and personal-brand blocks are cleanly Neil, the opening-SEO extension and Single Grain/close are cleanly Eric, and the branding-vs-performance and several LinkedIn asides are uncertain and kept out of the Neil bank.

## Key claims
- The future of SEO is not "just Google." Most people wrongly think SEO = Google rankings; it's really about ranking across Google, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube and "Search GPT" (and the list keeps growing). Companies create tons of social content but forget to optimize it to *rank* on those platforms — you can actually rank so that when people search, the platform recommends your videos/content and you keep earning views, instead of one-off posts. (Neil, 2024-08-13)
- Consistency is why the show has lasted ~8 years: if the attention is there, you stay consistent with that channel no matter what — "that's the reason we're still here." (Neil, 2024-08-13) `(co-host staple; attributed to Neil via continuity into the "Eric and I" block)`
- Channel-selection rule: adapt to and optimize for the channels where the attention is, but the caveat most people get wrong — optimize for channels once they're *becoming popular / proven*, not brand-new unproven ones (he avoided Be Real). Pick platforms big enough that they won't die — e.g., Threads (crossed ~200M monthly active users, per Meta) is safe; if you already create content, it takes little extra to copy-paste/port it to Threads (and LinkedIn), so you should. (Neil, 2024-08-13) `(Threads 200M MAU — figure recalled)`
- "SEO as we know it is dead" — meaning the *old* Google-only SEO. He's not saying stop doing Google SEO, but SEO is much more than that now and you have to evolve. Each channel is its own game: YouTube = good headlines, packaging, and retaining viewers in the first 30 seconds; TikTok/Reels = a strong hook, stopping the swipe-away, and a good title/thumbnail. You can't keep playing the same game, especially a dying one. (Neil, 2024-08-13)
- Demand is visibly shifting: at NP Digital, more and more clients each week ask for help optimizing content for TikTok/Instagram — something that rarely came up 1–2 years ago — and requests now extend even to Reddit and WhatsApp marketing. (Neil, 2024-08-13)
- Influencer marketing is moving into a rigorous, metrics/ROI-driven era. The old mega-celebrity model (pay a Ronaldo/LeBron/Kardashian-type for reach and point to likes/engagement) is "a big expensive black hole"; engagement does not mean ROI. Big corporations don't care as much at small spend, but as budgets ramp toward $10–50M they demand ROI. (Neil, 2024-08-13) `(dollar figures illustrative — Neil says "I'm making up a number")`
- Influencer ROI *is* trackable: unique landing pages, promo codes, and swipe-up/story links for direct conversions. Micro-influencers deliver much better (more positive) ROI — it's far more work (you must recruit many more people) but trackable and repeatable quarter after quarter, and it's easier to keep getting budget because you can prove it drives ROI. The tech is usually just the brand's own analytics plus campaign (UTM) variables. (Neil, 2024-08-13)
- Personal brand vs corporate brand (his core LinkedIn-segment take): Jason Lemkin's point is well taken — "keep the main thing the main thing." If your main focus becomes building a personal audience, it's hard to crush your actual role. He'd scrutinize a VP of Marketing at his own company who spent lots of time posting/engaging on LinkedIn to market *themselves* — "don't do it on my dime." (Neil, 2024-08-13)
- People post on social believing a bigger personal brand → more money; but the real way to make a lot of money in the corporate world is to do an amazing job, keep getting promoted, and keep working for bigger and bigger companies. People are riding the personal-brand hype too far. (Neil, 2024-08-13)
- He once thought personal brands would become "the new big corporations" — that hasn't panned out. Kylie Cosmetics did an amazing job but is still not Estée Lauder or L'Oréal; "I, Neil Patel, am no WPP or Publicis or Omnicom." A personal brand is nice to have and branding matters, but building an amazing *corporate* brand — compounded over decades — matters more. He (and Eric) have personally cut way back on personal-brand content vs. 10 years ago and now focus on the business. (Neil, 2024-08-13)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Most people just optimize for Google. They think SEO equals Google rankings. I think the future of SEO is going to be Google rankings, TikTok rankings, Instagram rankings, YouTube rankings, Search GPT — and the list continues on and on.

> On YouTube you've got to learn good headlines, good packaging, how to retain them within the first 30 seconds. With TikTok, with Reels, you've got to have a really good hook and make sure they're not swiping away. There's all these games you have to understand how to play, and you can't keep playing the same game over and over, especially when that game is dying. We're not saying don't do Google SEO — but SEO is much more than that now. SEO as we know it is dead, like the old SEO. You've got to evolve.

> At our agency NP Digital, we're getting more and more people each and every single week saying, "Hey, can you help us optimize our content for TikTok, Instagram?" And a year to two years ago, that would rarely ever come up in a conversation.

> Engagement doesn't mean ROI. A lot of big corporations don't care as much, but when you want to start ramping up to 10, 20, 30, 50 million dollars, they actually care about ROI.

> With micro-influencers you can get a much better ROI. It's way more work because you have to recruit so many more people, but the ROI is much more positive... it's trackable, and it's easier to continually do it quarter after quarter, year after year and get the budget, because you can prove that it's driving ROI.

> There's a lot to do at my company with marketing, and you're spending time marketing yourself. Don't do it on my dime.

> The real way to make a lot of money in the corporate world is you do an amazing job, and you continue to get promoted, and you keep working for bigger and bigger companies. People are riding this personal-brand hype too far, in my opinion.

> I had this notion that personal brands would be the new big corporations, and that hasn't really come around. Kylie Cosmetics did an amazing job, but it's still not Estée Lauder or L'Oréal. And I, Neil Patel, am no WPP or Publicis or Omnicom. Is a personal brand better to have than not have? Sure. But what's more important is building a really amazing corporate brand.

## Eric Siu (co-host, context — NOT persona data)
[[wiki/entities/eric-siu]]
- Future of SEO is multi-surface search: TikTok search, Amazon search (for shopping), and YouTube as "the second largest search engine in the world." LLMs like OpenAI use RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) and pull from search engines for their data — so LLMs are building *on top of* search engines like Google and working in tandem; he doesn't think LLMs will necessarily replace Google. (Eric, 2024-08-13)
- SEO is getting tougher overall: AI overview results, local packs, and everything being pushed down by ads. Attention is fragmenting across social platforms, so the future of SEO is omni-channel — you have to diversify. "Marketing 101 is bringing people to the point of sale; to find those people you have to understand where they're hanging out — and they're not just on Google anymore." They used to be "one-trick ponies," all-in on SEO. (Eric, 2024-08-13)
