# The Cheapest Way to Write Lots of Content At Scale ★

## Summary
Solo production-systems video: content is "simple, but not easy," and volume at scale
drives traffic — so how do you get volume cheaply? Six levers. (1) **Content clusters** —
the Moz Beginner's-Guide-to-SEO structure again (hub + subsections that themselves rank),
plus a new analogy: **Wikipedia doesn't publish 20 articles on Abraham Lincoln, it keeps
expanding one**. Work a new set of keywords and variations inside the cluster each month;
"that's what we do at our ad agency." Source the sub-pieces from Ubersuggest's
keyword-ideas report (comparisons, questions, prepositions, related, suggested). (2)
**Match intent to your actual customer** — he uses himself as the counter-example: writing
"a beginner's guide to SEO" is wrong when the ideal customer is a large corporation with
its own SEO department; write what *they* hit, e.g. what to do when launching a site
redesign. Prefer intent-loaded queries ("SEO agency") over surface ones ("SEO"). (3)
**Outsource — fully or partially.** Writers and editors via **TextBroker** and
**jobs.problogger.net** (his stated favourite, cheap and good). His hiring protocol:
request samples, **pay per word**, set **minimums and maximums**, and require an **outline
before writing**. If you won't outsource the writing, outsource the *inputs* — pay someone
to dig up data, statistics and academic research for a piece you write yourself. (4)
**User-generated content.** Ask for reviews, experiences, guest posts. His proof point is
biographical: **KISSmetrics — now folded into the Neil Patel blog — scaled to over a
million visitors a month purely through guest posting**, paying a few authors at the start
and then almost nothing as people wrote for free. The recruiting hack: find the guest
authors already writing on competitors' and industry sites and pitch them. On the review
side he cites Consulting.com and its "thousands" of positive customer video reviews. (5)
**Speak, don't type.** Record video or voice and transcribe — he names Gary Vaynerchuk's
team as doing exactly this — with the arithmetic that **the average person speaks 125–150
words per minute**, far faster than they type, so a 1,000-word post is minutes of talking.
Tools: **Rev.com** for transcription, or **Google Docs voice typing**, which he says he
**prefers over writing**. (6) **A content management platform**, not a spreadsheet —
Monday.com, "Sparrow.io," Asana — plus a bonus: **Trello** to manage updates, because "I
only write four or five pieces of content in a month, but I have a team of three that's
updating 90 pieces a month" — updating old content beating new content for traffic, and a
three-year-old post of his needing an update he has no time to do himself.
