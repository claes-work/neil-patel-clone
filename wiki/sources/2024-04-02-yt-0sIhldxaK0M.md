# Andrew Huberman hit piece has reverse effect, Ad Creative is a 12x ROI multiplier?, and more

## Summary
Four segments. (1) A discussion of a New York Post exposé alleging Dr. Andrew Huberman was concurrently dating multiple women without disclosure; both hosts note Huberman never publicly addressed the story and that his follower growth accelerated afterward (YouTube: ~200-700/day before, 3,000-7,000/day after), framing his silence as effective crisis-PR and observing that "hit pieces" can function as unintentional publicity/attention-generation, while stating a personal-life-is-not-my-business stance (declining to judge Huberman's private conduct) and reiterating a "form your own opinion by listening to multiple sources" media-literacy view. (2) A segment on ad-creative effectiveness citing a 2014 "top 10 ways to improve advertising ROI" study (brand size/market share: 18x profit multiplier; creative tagging/ad length: 12x; budget-setting across geography: 5x); Neil states creative is the single biggest leverage point in paid media today because most of the underlying ad-buying technology/software is commoditized and equally available to competitors, so a strong creative "X-factor" is the real differentiator; states this is a mix of "brand, creative, and data" (referencing a framing credited to "Gary" — presumably Gary Vaynerchuk — around his forthcoming book "Day Trading Attention," described as purple to represent the blend of "blue" data/math and "red" brand/creative); describes hiring a dedicated creative lead (named Alex) at NP Digital because Neil self-identifies as strong on the data side but weak on creative, illustrating a broader "design your organization around your own and others' strengths, not around trying to personally fix every weakness" management philosophy, crediting an idea (attributed on-air to entrepreneur Brad Jacobs, author of a book on building multiple multibillion-dollar companies) that scaling ultimately comes down to people and technology. (3) A tangent on why authentic, low-production "14-year-old from Missouri"-style social content often outperforms over-produced content, because it feels native to a platform's community rather than over-engineered; segues into a case study of Flexport founder Ryan Peterson's rapid, founder-authored X-thread-then-NYT-op-ed response to the Baltimore bridge collapse, which vastly outperformed The New York Times' own coverage in views (1.8M vs. ~4K), used to argue founders speaking directly and quickly, with genuine subject-matter authority (E-E-A-T), beat slower corporate/press-mediated communication — with an explicit self-aware caveat that neither host has the authority to write such a post themselves ("we don't know anything about bridges and logistics"), extended to a critique of VCs publishing sweeping AI-search-traffic-decline claims based on a small, non-representative sample of their own portfolio companies' analytics. (4) A closing note that Neil posted an "I'm not an economist, but..." tweet observing marketing spend is recovering in the US and has been strong in India for years, while Australia, Canada, UK, and the rest of Europe remain slow (roughly where the US was ~6-9 months prior), Latin America (ex-Mexico) is where the US was ~9-12 months prior, and most of Asia (ex-India) mirrors the US ~6-9 months ago — explicitly caveated as observation from "our small subset of data," not formal economic forecasting.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> The biggest leverage point you have as a marketer when you're running ads is the creative, because a lot of the technology and the software, a lot of people have access to that, it's at their fingertips — but the creative really can help you stand out from the competition, we call that the X-factor.

> I'm better at the data side than the creative side, so we will hire — we have an amazing creative person who leads up that department, named Alex, and that's how her brain works. Mine is all the zeros and ones, and you combine them, and that's how you can start [doing] better campaigns.

> Don't try to fix all your weaknesses, because nobody can be perfect... instead focus on your strengths, because that is what you're valued for at the end of the day — it's your strengths that make you world-class.

> I'm not an economist, but in a bad economy marketing is the first thing to get cut... and it's one of the first things that companies rehire because it's easier to add back marketing than most things, like adding hiring employees, which takes longer.
