# Marketing on a Budget? These 3 Hacks Will Blow Your Mind (Marketing School, Ep. 2591)

## Summary
Short classic daily Marketing School episode. The subject seat is heavily Neil this time: Neil owns the whole thesis and delivers the three low-budget "hacks" almost end to end. His argument: in competitive markets you can't outspend the incumbents, marketing is a "race to the bottom" (CPCs rise year over year, channels get more saturated), so the scrappy play is to give something away for free that sits in a small addressable market and monetize a much bigger adjacent market. His anchor example is payroll software given away free (small TAM) as bait to sell health insurance and 401k plans (huge TAM) to companies that onboard employees — with Oral-B/Orabrush, Gillette, Ubersuggest/AnswerThePublic, and HubSpot cited as precedents. He then extends this into blue-ocean / product-led-growth thinking (free AI-built software, riding under-saturated features like LinkedIn organic, moving faster than slow "behemoths") and a third hack of collaboration/partnerships (partner webinars, quid-pro-quo asset swaps).

Eric Siu's contribution is a mid-episode tangent, quarantined below: he narrates that he bought a senior-living website a week before Neil bought Ubersuggest (referring to Neil in the third person, which is the main attribution anchor), riffs that attention — not capital — is the real scarce commodity (crediting an unnamed All Summit speaker), and recommends Andrew Chen's "law of shitty click-throughs" essay (rising CPCs, banner blindness).

The give-away-free-small-TAM / monetize-big-TAM framing is a crisp, reusable Neil framework, but it restates his well-known product-led-growth/free-tool playbook rather than introducing anything new, and this is a short daily ep — **NOT flagged ★ L3-candidate**.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- In competitive markets you can't outspend competitors — there will always be players with far more money; copying everyone else's SEO/paid/social just means you're outspent. (Neil, 2023-11-03) — agency-entrepreneurship / paid-ads.
- Marketing is a "race to the bottom": averaged over time, marketing gets more expensive year over year, channels get more competitive; bad economies dip it temporarily but the trend is up. Still do the channels as long as they generate ROI. (Neil, 2023-11-03) — paid-ads.
- In hyper-competitive verticals (auto insurance, healthcare, credit cards) CPCs can hit $50–$100+ per click for a single visitor. (Neil, 2023-11-03) — paid-ads.
- **Hack 1 — give away free, monetize a bigger market:** if you're bootstrapped in a competitive industry, it's cheaper to give something away for free than to buy the traffic. Find a small-TAM product to give away and sell them on a much bigger TAM. (Neil, 2023-11-03) — agency-entrepreneurship / content-marketing.
- Worked example: health-insurance CPCs are through the roof, but companies buying health insurance run payroll — so give away payroll software free (nearly all payroll tools charge; the free ones are janky and cap at 1–2 employees), then when a company onboards e.g. 500 employees, call them to sell health insurance, 401k, and other ancillary services. Works in B2B and B2C. (Neil, 2023-11-03) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Precedents for the free-bait model: Orabrush/Oral-B (free tongue cleaner → sold toothbrushes/mouthwash), Gillette (free razor handle → money on the blades), Ubersuggest + AnswerThePublic (Neil did this), HubSpot (free website tool). (Neil, 2023-11-03) — content-marketing.
- The future of marketing over the next 5–10 years (if you want to make millions to hundreds of millions) is "free," because AI (ChatGPT multimodal — snap a photo of a dashboard/landing page and it codes it) makes building giveaway software cheap; most software is "just a database / spreadsheet at the end of the day." (Neil, 2023-11-03) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
- **Hack 2 — find the blue ocean / move faster:** the winning angle in a competitive niche isn't classic marketing but product-led growth / growth hacking — go where it's a blue ocean (uncontested) vs. a red ocean (piranhas). Examples of current blue oceans: giving away free software (few are willing to), or newly released under-saturated features (e.g. LinkedIn organic is "super strong" right now). Ride these before they saturate; you must move faster than slow-moving behemoths. (attributed Neil — see notes, 2023-11-03) — agency-entrepreneurship / content-marketing.
- TAM = total addressable market: look for a small TAM to give away, sell on a big TAM. Payroll example sizing: Paychex ~$40B+ company; UnitedHealth(care) ~10x bigger at ~$400B+ (stock figures approximate). (Neil, 2023-11-03) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- **Hack 3 — collaborate / partner:** with little money, ask who you can collaborate or partner with — e.g. partner webinars in B2B, quid-pro-quo asset swaps where your email list may be smaller but you bring another relevant asset. Be scrappy and find the angles. (Neil, 2023-11-03) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- What a bootstrapper lacks in capital they make up for in an abundance of time to make the angles work. (Neil, 2023-11-03) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Attention — not capital — is the truly scarce commodity: capital is more of a commodity you can buy, but you can't just buy attention, and that's the name of the game in marketing (credited to an unnamed speaker at the All Summit). (Eric, 2023-11-03) — content-marketing.
- Andrew Chen's "law of shitty click-throughs" essay: click-through rates keep decreasing while CPCs rise; the first banner ad got tons of clicks at <5¢, but now people have banner blindness as channels get oversaturated. (Eric, 2023-11-03) — paid-ads.
