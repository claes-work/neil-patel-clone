# CRO Best Practices - CRO Unlocked

## Summary
CRO Unlocked Week 1, Lesson 2. Core doctrine: CRO wins through iteration, not one big
test — "you're not going to do a test and find out that you doubled your conversions...
it happens, but once in a blue moon." Prescribes running exactly one test at a time PER
funnel step (not multiple simultaneous tests on the same page, but parallel tests across
different pages is fine if traffic supports it), starting with simple tweaks before
escalating to drastic changes once simple wins dry up. Broadens what counts as an
"optimization" page beyond SEO metatags/schema to on-page copy, bullet points,
descriptions, and CTA-button wording — and notes there's no universal priority order
across page types; prioritize by traffic volume (e.g. optimize checkout before contact
if checkout gets 10x the traffic). Uses the Gumroad pricing case (118% conversion lift
switching $2.00 → $1.99) to illustrate charm-pricing psychology. Covers exit-intent
testing as a lever, and closes on a B2B lead-funnel math example showing a 3-point lead-
capture-rate improvement (5%→8%) growing revenue from $740K to $1.2M at equal traffic,
price, and sales-close rate.

## Key claims
- States that dramatic single-test wins ("doubled your conversions" from one test) are
  rare ("once in a blue moon"); CRO gains typically come from many small, compounding
  tweaks (paraphrase, 2021-04-29) — analytics-cro.
- Prescribes running only ONE test at a time per individual funnel step/page (e.g. don't
  run two tests simultaneously on the same pricing page), but running parallel tests
  across DIFFERENT pages (pricing, contact, homepage) simultaneously is fine given
  sufficient traffic for statistical significance (paraphrase, 2021-04-29) — analytics-cro.
- Testing sequencing rule: start with simple tweaks; escalate to more drastic page
  changes only once simple-tweak wins stop coming (paraphrase, 2021-04-29) —
  analytics-cro.
- No universal page-priority order for CRO — prioritize whichever page gets the most
  relative traffic (e.g. checkout over contact if checkout sees 10x the visits)
  (paraphrase, 2021-04-29) — analytics-cro.
- Cites Gumroad's reported 118% conversion lift when they changed pricing from $2.00 to
  $1.99, illustrating that charm-pricing psychology (perceived-cheapness) can outweigh
  the trivial real price difference (self-reported third-party case, source not named,
  2021-04-29) — analytics-cro.
- B2B funnel-math example: at 5,000 visitors and a $20,000 price point, raising lead-
  capture rate from 5% to 8% (with an unchanged 15% sales-close rate) grows revenue from
  $740,000 to $1.2M — a $460,000 gain from a 3-point lead-capture improvement alone
  (worked example, 2021-04-29) — analytics-cro.
- States exit-intent popups are a worthwhile CRO lever and should be A/B tested (simple
  email capture vs. more complex offers like discounts), consistent with his existing
  exit-intent material in the wiki (paraphrase, 2021-04-29) — analytics-cro.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> The key to CRO is iteration. You're not going to do a test and find out that you
> doubled your conversions. That's very, very rare. It happens, but once in a blue moon.
> Typically it's a lot [of] little things that add up that truly help you optimize your
> conversion rate.

> The big secret is[:] one test at a time per step within your funnel.

> You'll never be as good as you want. You'll never be perfect, and that's okay. And you
> should have the mindset that you can improve and be better, that's how you win.
