# How to Make Millions By Starting an Ugly 'Tumbleweed' Business

## Summary

Neil Patel argues that most people chase "sexy" startups — the next Uber, Google, YouTube, or Airbnb — but those are extreme rarities, and people rarely hear about the many failures behind such success stories. He calls his counter-thesis "Tumbleweed Marketing," summarized as "the ugly is now the sexy": the biggest, most profitable opportunities often sit in unglamorous, uncompetitive markets nobody wants to enter. His anecdotal example is Mike Rigby, a man from Utah who sells tumbleweeds — which grow free in his backyard — to movie companies for thousands of dollars, at one point being flown in on a plane with tumbleweeds when a film production ran short. Neil generalizes this with the Koch brothers (multi-billionaires from "ugly" businesses) and Procter & Gamble (large because it makes necessities like toilet paper, tissue, shampoo, and toothpaste), then gives a concrete validation method: don't chase big niches, chase uncompetitive ones. Run a Google search for a prospective industry and read the total results count; keep terms with under ~50 million results (Mike-level luck is under ~10 million), and cross-check demand on Google Trends — if there's no chart, nobody is searching and there's no market.

- Validation method: Google the industry you want to enter and read how many people are competing via Google's total results count; hundreds of millions of results for the keywords means it's competitive, so look for industries without that much competition (as of 2017-06-06).
