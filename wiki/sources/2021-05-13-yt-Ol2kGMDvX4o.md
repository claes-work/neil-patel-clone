# How to Raise Money as an Entrepreneur to Fuel Your Growth - Growth Hacking Unlocked

## Summary
Growth Hacking Unlocked Lesson 2, on fundraising. Neil's rationale for including a
fundraising lesson in a growth-hacking course: his research for the course found that
most companies growing rapidly via growth hacking also had venture capital "fuel." Walks
funding-stage vocabulary (self-funding → seed → Series A/B/C → venture debt → IPO →
later-stage M&A/LBO/secondary offerings) and typical round sizes (states Series A rounds
were $1–4M when he started raising, now commonly $10–20M+; Series B averages ~$32M).
Delivers a notable **biography ★ detail**: names **KISSmetrics** specifically as the
company where he raised **$16.4 million over seven rounds** ("a little bit too many
rounds"), states KISSmetrics was a spinoff of **Crazy Egg**, and — consistent with the
2021-04-26 source in this same batch — again states his **brother-in-law and sister now
own and run Crazy Egg**, with Neil no longer involved. Compares KISSmetrics to competitor
Mixpanel (raised $77M over five rounds from investors including Andreessen Horowitz,
reaching $100M+ revenue in fewer rounds per his own reading of press coverage). Names
**Sequoia Capital** as a case study (1,000+ portfolio companies since 1972 incl. Apple,
Google, PayPal, Stripe, YouTube; ~22% of Nasdaq market cap by his account) and
**Crunchbase** as his go-to free research tool for building an investor-prospect list.
Gives fundraising process advice: warm introductions outperform cold outreach; optimize
for investor FIT, not just minimizing dilution; a "no" often means "not right now," not
"never" — illustrated with a notable ★ anecdote: **True Ventures rejected his first
pitched company but later invested in KISSmetrics**. Closes with a Facebook comparison
(their angel round was only $500,000, considered large at the time vs. today's norms) and
previews next lesson on later-stage funding (LBOs, IPOs, M&A).
