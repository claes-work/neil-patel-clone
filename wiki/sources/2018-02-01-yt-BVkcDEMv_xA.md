# Surefire PR Marketing Tips and Tricks | How To Approach Brands and Get Press

_Q&A Thursdays; **Adam Lodolce** (viewership.com) hosts/asks, Neil answers. Only
Neil-attributed material is persona data._

## Summary
Neil's PR playbook, built on **warm intros and giving first** rather than cold-pitching
editors. Most people want to be featured on TechCrunch, Business Insider, CNN, Mashable, or
The Next Web and go about it wrong — emailing editors to "please write about me" gets ignored.
Instead: study **who the journalists already write about** (the top 10 / top 50 people they
keep mentioning), **network with those people**, and ask **them** for an introduction. His
"more hardcore but it works" move as a marketer: he did **free marketing** for TechCrunch,
Mashable, Business Insider, Gawker, GigaOm and their whole network, so that when he needed PR
he was already an insider who knew their stats and had earned coverage. The anchor anecdote:
he pitched **Michael Arrington** (TechCrunch) to do his marketing and got ignored; so Neil did
free marketing for Arrington's competitors, made sure **his logo was on the sites** so it was
visibly his work, and once Arrington saw the competitors' traffic jump, Arrington came back to
him. Neil grew TechCrunch's traffic, then asked Arrington to email his **investor friends** with
screenshots of the results — which drove more agency business. The through-line is **"business
karma"**: give and give to the right people (the ones with audiences everyone else is trying to
take from), and it comes back. He closes by noting this is literally how Adam met him — Adam
offered free work in exchange, and a friendship (and Adam's on-camera appearances) followed.

## Key claims
As of 2018-02-01, Neil states:
- **Cold-pitching editors doesn't work** — emailing a journalist to "write about me/promote me"
  gets ignored; you need a **warm introduction**.
- **Reverse-engineer the writer's network**: find who a journalist repeatedly writes about
  (their top 10/50 sources), network with those people, and ask them for the intro.
- His marketer's move: **do free marketing for the publications themselves** (TechCrunch,
  Mashable, Business Insider, Gawker, GigaOm and their networks); then when you need PR you're
  already an insider — "not only did I know their stats… they always wrote about me because I
  did it for free."
- **Michael Arrington (TechCrunch) anecdote**: Arrington ignored his first pitches, so Neil did
  free marketing for Arrington's **competitors**, put his **logo on their sites** for
  attribution, and once the competitors' traffic jumped Arrington came back and asked Neil to
  market TechCrunch too (self-reported).
- After growing a publication's traffic, **ask them to email their investor friends** a
  screenshot of the results — a longer-term play that drives referral business.
- When emailing into a company for PR, target the right roles — **PR, the CEO/founder, or
  marketing** — not random employees, or you won't convert.
- If you can't engineer an intro digitally, go to **local/startup networking events** and meet
  people who've already gotten press and can introduce you.
- Governing principle — **"business karma"**: everyone with an audience is being taken from
  constantly; give to the right people and it comes back around (the moment an inbound message
  reads as generic, he "clicks the back button").
- Meta-example: this is how **Adam** met Neil — Adam offered free work in exchange for SEO help,
  which became a genuine friendship (no money exchanged).
