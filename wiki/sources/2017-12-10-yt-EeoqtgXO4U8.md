# How to Become a New York Times Best Selling Author | 3 Tips to Write and Publish Your Book

## Summary
Neil Patel candidly reverse-engineers how his co-authored book *Hustle* (2016) hit the New York Times bestseller list "not once but three times in a row," and — unusually — argues it wasn't worth it financially for him. The mechanics he describes: (1) **pre-sell a large volume of copies** (pre-sales up to a year out all count toward release-week numbers); (2) sell **across channels** (Amazon + Barnes & Noble + local/college bookstores + corporations), because NYT scrutinizes Amazon-only sales as gaming; (3) it's **discretionary** — a strong traditional publisher and a well-known name backing you materially raises your odds; self-publishing is "pretty much out of luck"; (4) **generate press** to make the sales believable. The two things he says *actually* got *Hustle* on the list: **large corporate bulk book purchases** (he traded his marketing services to five big businesses for $100k of book buys each = ~$500k pre-sold), and using **ResultSource** — a company that distributes pre-sale money across retailers so purchases look organic to NYT — releasing sales **spread over several weeks** (to hit weekly and monthly lists) rather than all at once. His candid caveat: the bestseller status "didn't do much for me" — no higher speaking fees, no traffic, no business growth — which he attributes to the book being off his core topic (marketing); he got a **$500k advance**, but believes a single-author, on-core-topic book would have paid off better. This page contains guest attribution only where he references Gary Vaynerchuk as another example.

## Key claims
As of 2017-12-10, Neil states:
- *Hustle* hit the NYT bestseller list **three times in a row** despite Neil having (self-described) "terrible grammar and writing" (as of 2017-12-10).
- **Pre-sales count toward release week**: books sold up to a year in advance all count for the first week, so pre-sell as many as possible (as of 2017-12-10).
- **Sell across many channels**, not just Amazon; Amazon-only patterns look like gaming the system to NYT, while multi-channel sales (Barnes & Noble, local/college stores, corporations) look organic and "deserving" (as of 2017-12-10).
- Bestseller placement is **discretionary**: a strong, well-known publisher raises your odds even if the deal terms are modest; self-publishers are "pretty much out of luck" (as of 2017-12-10).
- **Generate press** to make the sales believable (as of 2017-12-10).
- The two decisive levers for *Hustle*: (a) **corporate bulk sales via service trades** — he told five big businesses "I will do your marketing in exchange for you to buy $100,000 worth of books," pre-selling ~$500k; and (b) **ResultSource**, which redistributes pre-sale money across Amazon/Barnes & Noble/other retailers so it "seems legitimate and quote-unquote natural" to NYT (as of 2017-12-10).
- **Spread the sales over a few weeks** rather than all at once, because NYT is "catching on," and you can hit both the weekly and monthly lists (as of 2017-12-10).
- Gary Vaynerchuk similarly gets conferences to **buy his books in bulk** before a launch, which helps him hit the list (attributed to Gary, not Neil — context only) (as of 2017-12-10).
- **Candid caveat**: the bestseller status "didn't do much for me" — no higher speaking fees, no extra site traffic, no business growth — because the book wasn't related to his core profession (marketing) (as of 2017-12-10).
- He received a **$500,000 advance** to write the book, "which was nice," but believes an on-core-topic book (even selling fewer copies) would have paid better financially (as of 2017-12-10).
- Advice: **write under your own name only** (not co-authored) for better career payoff; and a book helps early-career people more than it helped him, since he was already 16+ years in and charging "tens of thousands" per corporate speaking gig (as of 2017-12-10).
