# Biggest winners and losers of Google AI Spam Update, 95% of agency work will be taken by AI, & more

## Summary
Three segments. (1) Reaction to Google's AI/spam algorithm update (via Lily Ray's SEO data): steep visibility-score losses for Wikipedia (-500pts), IMDb (-144), Britannica (-90), Reddit (-867/-52%), and Instagram (-77%); YouTube itself dropped slightly despite being a Google property. "Winners" barely gained (a few points) and skew toward smaller/spammier-looking sites (TechRadar, an MP3-download site, Pornhub) rather than clean wins. General commentary that SEO "influencers" who publicly brag about black-hat tactics and traffic screenshots reliably get "torched"/de-indexed by Google afterward (a pattern going back ~15 years) — the advice given is to never publicize black-hat wins. Also covers an upcoming May 5 Google deadline targeting "parasite SEO" (third parties borrowing an established site's domain authority via guest content, e.g. IndiaTimes.com), distinguishing it from legitimate guest contribution — with a "curious to see what happens to Forbes" aside given its heavy affiliate/guest-contributor content. (2) A structured debate on a Sam Altman claim ("AI will handle 95% of agency/marketing/creative work"): Neil argues both Sam and the hosts are biased (Sam by his AI business, the hosts by their agency businesses) and stakes out a "meet in the middle" estimate of 30-50% of agency work being AI-replaceable over a ~10-year horizon, not 95% — reasoning that AI regurgitates existing, sometimes-inaccurate web information (citing conflicting pro/anti-vaccine content and a study on political lean across AI models — Gemini and OpenAI rated "very left-leaning") and produces average/mediocre output rather than the top-1%-caliber work agencies are actually hired to deliver; argues clients hire agencies specifically for above-average results they can't achieve with in-house AI-augmented teams, and that people not using AI-augmentation (not "AI" per se) will be the ones actually displaced, analogous to internet adoption. (3) A description of a study (team based in India, "tens of thousands" of Instagram images scraped and filtered down to accounts that explicitly disclosed AI vs. human-made images) finding AI-generated images get meaningfully lower engagement than human-generated images (41 vs. 66 average likes; 2.3 vs. 3.1 average comments per post, n=304 accounts), with the caveat that comparing different accounts' absolute engagement is confounded by follower-count/reach differences.

## Key claims
- **Predicts AI will replace roughly 30-50% of agency/marketing/creative work over a ~10-year horizon, explicitly rejecting Sam Altman's 95% claim as biased and too high**, while also rejecting the opposite bias (his own, as an agency owner) — "meet in the middle." Reasoning: AI-generated marketing content converges toward sameness/mediocrity when many competitors use the same AI tools, and AI's web-sourced information includes contradictory/low-quality material it cannot reliably distinguish from accurate material. (Neil, 2024-03-27) `(opinion, dated)`
- **Argues clients hire agencies for above-average, "top 1%" results, not for marketing execution per se — and current AI does not yet produce top-1%-caliber creative/strategic work**, only average/mediocre output derived from regurgitating existing web information. (Neil, 2024-03-27) `(opinion, dated)`
- **The people who will actually be displaced by AI are those not using AI-augmentation themselves, not simply "AI users vs. non-AI-workers" — analogous to internet adoption ("someone using the internet has a bigger advantage over someone that's not").** (Neil, 2024-03-27) `(opinion, dated)`
- **Never publicly showcase black-hat/gray-hat SEO wins — Google reliably "torches" (de-indexes) SEO influencers who brag about traffic gained through such tactics**, a pattern observed over roughly 15 years. `(opinion, dated 2024-03-27; attribution: uncertain — undiarized, joint framing, consistent with Neil's long-established anti-black-hat SEO positioning)`
- **An internal study (team based in India, tens of thousands of Instagram images scraped down to 304 accounts with explicit AI-vs-human disclosure) found AI-generated images underperform human-generated images on engagement**: 41 vs. 66 average likes, 2.3 vs. 3.1 average comments per post. `(attribution: uncertain — likely a joint NP Digital/Single Grain study; presented by Eric with Neil confirming the directional result)`

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Yes AI will be able to replace a lot of agencies and people for any type of jobs, but AI is not the top 1%. AI is creating average, mediocre results, and I don't see it producing really amazing quality work anytime soon.

> People don't hire agencies to get marketing work done, they hire agencies because they want much better results... they're looking for that 1% who's going to produce amazing results that they weren't able to achieve on their own.

> The only people that are going to be replaced by AI is people that are not enhanced by AI... it's basically like, are you using the internet or are you not using the internet — someone that's using the internet has a bigger advantage over someone that's not.
