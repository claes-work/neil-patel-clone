# The Rock SAVES the WWE, Trump Media shares surge, MKHB destroys AI company, and more

## Summary
Five segments. (1) The Rock is credited with revitalizing WWE ratings/storylines by pushing to become the top "heel" (villain) ahead of WrestleMania, which reportedly sold out and drove record ratings — a shareholder-incentive angle is raised (The Rock is believed, though not confirmed by the hosts, to hold equity in TKO, the Endeavor-owned parent of WWE and UFC), illustrated as an example of incentive alignment driving unusually strong personal investment in an outcome. (2) A follow-up on Trump Media (DJT) stock, which the hosts had previously called too risky to short: the stock popped 15% in the days following their prior episode, reinforcing the earlier point about the unpredictable power of a loyal fanbase-driven stock. (3) MKBHD's viral, highly critical review of the Humane AI Pin ("this may be the worst product I've ever reviewed") is cited as a real-time example of an individual creator's ability to materially damage (or, alternately, inadvertently promote via Streisand-effect awareness) a company's reputation within roughly 41 seconds of screen time. (4) A Bain & Company M&A study is cited showing that companies which are frequent acquirers earn substantially higher shareholder returns than companies that stay out of the M&A market (the specific figure is stated inconsistently within the same episode — first estimated/guessed near "112%," confirmed by the host as "130% higher," and later restated while summarizing as "57% higher"; this inconsistency appears to be a citation/recall error within the source itself and should be flagged, not resolved, if promoted). This frames a discussion of Neil and Eric's own active M&A pipeline, including interest in acquiring a friend's PR agency (~$4M revenue) — which Neil says is a lower priority than his firm's usual targets because PR-only client relationships have limited cross-sell potential — versus Neil's stated preferred acquisition profile: a smaller regional agency (e.g., based in Hong Kong or Singapore) serving major enterprise clients (Google, Apple, Domino's are used as illustrative examples) via only one or two services, which can then be cross-sold NP Digital's full service range and expanded regionally (APAC, then Europe, Latin America, North America) — turning a ~$100K/month account into a multi-million-dollar one over time. (5) A running update on the "Agency Owners Association" community (co-run with Eric), now at roughly $2,700/month recurring revenue, prompting a comparison to Neil's earlier ventures: Crazy Egg took about two years to reach $1M ARR, while getting from $0 to its first $10,000 MRR reportedly took only about a month. This leads into an explainer on organic vs. inorganic growth, using Crazy Egg (which has never made an acquisition — purely organic growth) as the organic example and NP Digital's own blended growth (organic growth plus acquired companies' revenue counted toward the same top-line growth-rate figure) as the inorganic example — explicitly noting this is standard practice across marketing holding companies and that some report positive blended growth despite negative underlying organic growth. The episode closes on a personal-belief aside: Neil says he follows feng shui-influenced real-estate principles (e.g., avoiding homes where the staircase directly faces/exits the front door, associated with money "going out the door") strongly enough that he has passed on at least one profitable house-flipping opportunity (an estimated $1-1.5M gain in under three months) specifically because of the staircase layout — alongside a shared closing note of "cautious optimism" about the economy, tempered by acknowledged geopolitical instability and it being a US election year.
