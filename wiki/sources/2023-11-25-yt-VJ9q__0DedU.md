# The TikTok Shops Revolution: Zero to $100M in 1 Year (Ep. 2614)

## Summary
Co-hosted Marketing School episode (Neil Patel + Eric Siu; roughly half the talk time is Eric). Eric drives the segment: he shares his screen, introduces community-builder Greg Eisenberg's claims (someone will make $100M in 2024 on a TikTok shop; ~1 in 10 Americans will buy on TikTok Shop this year), and walks a chart showing ~33M US social buyers on TikTok in 2023. Neil supplies the marketing commentary: he confirms DTC on TikTok is highly profitable and scalable, distinguishes indirect (discovery-driven) conversion from direct in-app purchase, and argues the same social-commerce playbook extends across Snap, Instagram and an underrated Pinterest. Neil's core original contributions are the "be the connector/middleman between distribution (creators) and a good white-label product" thesis, the observation that the cost of failure for starting a business is now near-nil (aimed at students), and a clear limit: TikTok Shop does not work for high-ticket B2B — he uses his own agency NP Digital's million-dollar consulting contracts as the counterexample. Not a landmark; Neil's share is applied commentary on Eric-driven material.

## Key claims
- DTC on TikTok is currently much more profitable and scales better than most other social channels; many operators are doing "amazingly well" on it right now. (Neil, 2023-11-25)
- Neither host has bought anything directly inside TikTok, but Neil has bought products *because of* TikTok — i.e. TikTok drives indirect/discovery conversion even when the checkout happens elsewhere. (Neil, 2023-11-25)
- If you can't create a good product yourself, find a good product, white-label it, and sell it on TikTok. (Neil, 2023-11-25)
- The same social-commerce playbook ports across platforms — what works on TikTok will likely work on Snap, Instagram and Pinterest with minor per-platform adjustment; people wrongly treat it as TikTok-only. (Neil, 2023-11-25)
- Pinterest is an underrated, decent-sized opportunity — a ~$20B market-cap company (cited ~$750M in 2023 revenue) that is bigger than Snap by market cap, yet most people take it for granted. (Neil, 2023-11-25) (attribution: likely Neil, block continuous with his NP Digital remarks)
- The winning position is being the "connector"/middleman: build relationships with distribution (creators), source an interesting product, and drive the strategy without having to build the product or own the distribution yourself; relationship-building takes years but is a real path. (Neil, 2023-11-25)
- The cost of creating a business today is near-nil, so the cost of failure is little to nothing — a reason (especially for people in college) to just try something like a TikTok shop. (Neil, 2023-11-25)
- TikTok Shop won't work for every business: high-ticket B2B fails there. Neil's example — a million-dollar NP Digital consulting contract needs a sales conversation before purchase, so it won't sell via a TikTok shop; match the channel to your ideal customer and price point. (Neil, 2023-11-25)
- Platforms heavily push newly launched features that tie directly to their revenue (as with Instagram Stories, Facebook Live), so TikTok Shop carries strong "tailwind" for early movers. (attribution: uncertain — sits between Eric's tweet-reading passages; not assigned to Neil)
- Framing stats (via Greg Eisenberg): someone will make $100M in 2024 with a TikTok shop, likely with no inventory, no VC, and ~24 years old; ~1 in 10 Americans (~33M of ~330M) will buy on TikTok Shop this year; you're more likely to become a millionaire building a TikTok shop than as an early founder at a VC-backed startup. (Eric, context — reading Greg Eisenberg)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "I can see the graph and I believe it too, because I know a lot of people that are doing amazingly well in DTC on TikTok right now. It's much more profitable, you can scale it quite well than most the social channels out there."

> "I have bought things because of TikTok though — but not directly from TikTok... indirect conversion."

> "You just have to make sure the product is really cool. If you can't create a good product, go find a good product, white label it, and then go sell it on TikTok."

> "What most people get wrong is they look at it as just like, oh, TikTok — well if it works well on TikTok it'll probably work well on Snap, it'll probably work well on Instagram, and it'll probably work well on Pinterest. So you can just run the same playbook. You may have to adjust it a little bit here and there, but you can end up generating quite a bit of revenue from all of them."

> "Here's how I see it: if you're good at building relationships... you can be the connector, and you being the middleman you can actually make this all happen — because you drove the strategy, you drove the ideas, and you don't necessarily need to go through all the pain of creating all these products and services or finding distribution."

> "The cost of creating a business nowadays... the cost of failure really is nil. It's little to nothing."

> "If you're in B2B and you're selling high-ticket items — and when I mean high ticket, I'm not talking about people who are like 'buy my coaching program for five grand,' I'm talking about someone wanting to sign up for a consulting client from my agency NP Digital and it's a million-dollar contract — it's not going to happen on TikTok. It just won't. Keep in mind who your ideal customer is and the purchasing price, because if your purchasing price is so large that someone needs to talk to you before they buy, it's less likely to do well on a TikTok shop."
