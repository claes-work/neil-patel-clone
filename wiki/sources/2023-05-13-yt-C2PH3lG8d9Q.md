# Human Intelligence vs Artificial Intelligence: Winning at Content Creation

## Summary
Multi-panelist AI-in-marketing discussion at Web Summit Rio. Neil frames AI's near-term
marketing impact around **two specific mechanisms** rather than content generation broadly
(which he says is "already very cheap" regardless of AI): (1) **real-time analytics/waste
detection** — AI flagging underperforming ad spend continuously rather than via periodic human
review, prompting a human decision to reallocate; and (2) a **"winner take all" search
landscape**, citing (per a then-recent New York Times report) Google's **"Project Magi"** —
a reimagined search UI that would show a single best-match product recommendation instead of
ten ranked results — which he predicts will consolidate SEO winners to one or two results per
query and push paid search from impression/click pricing toward pure per-transaction pricing.
Pushes back on the hyper-personalization trend: cites **Walmart Labs'** personalization
initiative as a case where heavy investment in per-user recommendation tuning did not move
conversion or revenue, generalizing this to NP Digital's own client work ("when you get too
personalized, we're not really seeing much, if any, increase in revenue... it's almost an
overkill"). Introduces **Google's E-A-T framework (Expertise, Authority, Trust)** explicitly by
name and pairs it with a **"Where's Waldo" analogy**: as AI-generated content proliferates,
everyone's output increasingly "looks the same" (because AI scrapes and remixes existing web
content rather than originating genuinely new expertise), so the only durable differentiation
is content that demonstrates real, unique expertise/experience that AI cannot fabricate. Cites
ex-Google engineer **Matt Cutts'** ~decade-old estimate that 20-30% of internet content was
already duplicate, predicting AI will push that ratio sharply higher. On humanization: cites a
**self-commissioned survey of "a little bit more than 11,000 people"** finding the top social-
media pet peeve is that content "no longer feels personal," and connects this to why
celebrity-fronted brands (his examples: Rihanna/Fenty, LeBron James, Neymar, Ronaldo, Kylie
Jenner) outperform faceless corporate brands. Gives specific **TikTok optimization** guidance:
the platform reads the actual spoken/visual content of a video (not just tags/descriptions) to
classify it, and weights **comment engagement quality** heavily in distribution — illustrates
with his own anecdote of posting a (by his own admission, incorrect) statistic claiming there
are more blogs (1B+) than podcasts (<10M), which drew disagreeing comments that drove the
video's spread, framing deliberately debatable claims as a comment-engagement tactic. States
(attributing the claim to "a Google SVP," not a primary source of his own) that younger users
increasingly search Instagram/TikTok rather than Google for discovery use cases like finding a
place to eat.
