# The Loudest Person in the Room in the Weakest

## Summary
Neil opens with a quote he attributes to *American Gangster* (Denzel Washington): "the
loudest person in the room is the weakest" (as of 2022-06-02). He recounts that
earlier in his career he sought out visibly wealthy people at networking events
(flashy jewelry, luxury cars) believing they were the most successful and worth
learning from, but says this mostly taught him to waste money on materialistic
objects rather than anything useful. He says he's since learned that genuinely wealthy
people tend not to show off, citing a Kiplinger stat that 6.71% of U.S. households are
millionaires. His current approach: learn from quiet, experienced people directly in
or adjacent to his industry — asking what worked, what failed and why, and what they'd
avoid repeating — rather than judging advice-worthiness by outward wealth signals. He
extends this into a hiring philosophy: prefer candidates who've achieved the specific
outcome he needs **at least twice** ("you did it once, could have been luck; you did
it twice, probably good at it"), and cautions that someone's expertise (e.g. a wealthy
dentist) doesn't transfer to unrelated industries. He self-describes driving a Honda
Odyssey rather than a "fancy car" — a new concrete anti-flashy self-presentation
detail — and repeats the Inc. Magazine "21st fastest-growing company" figure
(recurrence).
