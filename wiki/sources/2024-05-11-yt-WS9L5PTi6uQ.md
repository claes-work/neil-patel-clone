# Bonus episode #2: A sneak peek of what our AOA hangouts look like

## Summary
A preview clip of a live Agency Owners Association (AOA) coaching call hosted solo by
Neil (Eric not present). Neil opens with a riff on founders' unwillingness to write:
references a podcast interview he did with the CEO of Eight Sleep (a company he
invested in), whose policy is that anyone wanting a 1:1 with him must submit a written
memo first; cites the book *Working Backwards* (Amazon) as the origin of this
memo-driven culture, and says a second investment of his ("Levels") does the same. Neil
frames memo-writing as forcing clarity of thought and preventing "half-baked ideas" from
wasting executive time, and says he personally writes strategic memos (e.g., on a flight)
about recruiting, pace, sales, and marketing issues. He states his biggest mistake was
expecting a hired GM/CEO to push pace as fast as he personally would. The call becomes
Q&A: member Alanov (Austin-based software-services agency, pivoting to "zero-to-one"
positioning for early-stage startups) and Carrie (New York, YouTube-marketing agency)
introduce themselves; Neil coaches Alanov on writing to surface a personal "unfair
advantage" and coaches another member (identified only by handle context) on committing
to 30 minutes/day of writing with an accountability structure, noting it's fine to miss
some days. A member named Brian (early-stage agency, "just scratching six figures") asks
how to scale; Neil shares a concrete cold-outreach tactic NP Digital/Single Grain is
testing: using the tool "Smartlead" to send ~500-1,000 emails/day, leading with a
concrete, de-risked, guaranteed offer (e.g., "we'll AI-design 10 landing pages for
$2,000, guaranteed 10%+ lift or money back, with a legally binding document") rather than
floating free content — reporting a 3-4% response rate — and cautions that outsourced
cold-outreach contractors need close oversight ("micromanage them") because the offer and
copy require continuous iteration.

## Key claims
- **Neil states he invested in Eight Sleep and Levels, both companies whose CEOs require
  a written memo before granting a 1:1 meeting, a practice he traces to the book
  *Working Backwards* (Amazon) and personally adopts, writing strategic memos on topics
  like recruiting, pace, sales, and marketing.** `(self-reported, dated 2024-05-11;
  attribution: Neil, first-person)`
- **Neil's stated biggest management mistake: expecting a hired GM/CEO to push
  organizational pace as fast as he personally would — a mistake he says he shares with
  his friend Sayad (a WordPress-business holding-company operator).** `(self-reported
  belief, dated 2024-05-11; attribution: Neil, first-person)`
- **Neil describes a live cold-outreach experiment at his agency using the tool
  "Smartlead" (500-1,000 emails/day across many inboxes), where switching the first
  email from floating free content to a concrete, guaranteed, de-risked offer (e.g., 10
  AI-redesigned landing pages for $2,000 with a money-back guarantee) raised response
  rates to a self-reported 3-4%.** `(self-reported / unaudited operating data, dated
  2024-05-11; attribution: Neil, first-person)`
- **Neil's coaching guidance to an outsourced-cold-outreach agency owner: contractors
  running high-risk, iteration-dependent tactics (offer/copy/headline testing) must be
  closely overseen, not left alone, because it's "a high-risk thing."**
  `(self-reported operating advice, dated 2024-05-11; attribution: Neil, first-person)`

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Writing is like casting a spell on your organization... you're basically writing a
> document and then things actually happen from it... all of you as Founders have this
> invisible hammer where you can push things through.

> My biggest mistake is expecting that I could hire a GM or a CEO and expect them to
> push the pace as quickly as I will — that's just not the reality.

> The key thing with the email is... we've tried floating content to people, that
> doesn't really generate responses, but now we changed our first email to a really good
> offer... it comes down to that the offer that you have at the end of the day — how can
> you give people a no-brainer offer and really de-risk it for people.
