# Biggest SEO losers of 2023, why you're wasting time on SEO, and more (Marketing School)

## Summary
★ Landmark-worthy for later L3 promotion: this episode is a compact, quotable statement of Neil's evolved 2024 SEO thesis — "SEO isn't dead, *old-school* SEO is" — backed by his signature Ubersuggest stat that 94.29% of indexed pages get zero search traffic, and a five-part modern-SEO playbook (content freshness, newness/trending topics, brand + E-E-A-T authority, CTR optimization, tool-based link building). That thesis and its stat are the reusable persona core worth pulling into `wiki/topics/seo/` and `beliefs.md`.

A co-hosted Marketing School episode in which Neil Patel drives a screen-shared SEO discussion and Eric Siu interjects, before the two turn to agency growth. Neil opens on Lily Ray's Sistrix "biggest SEO losers of 2023" study, but pushes back on the alarmist read: the study measures *visibility* on a small per-category sample, not traffic, and Google (per Danny Sullivan) keeps driving more traffic to sites year over year. He then delivers his real argument — from a tweet titled "you're wasting your time on SEO" — that 94.29% of indexed pages get no traffic not because SEO is dead but because people still do it the old-school way (keyword research → mass regurgitated me-too content → links). His fix: continually refresh content (why Wikipedia dominates), cover genuinely new/trending topics (AI is fine for research but "people want new stuff"), build brand authority (E-E-A-T) and leverage the "rule of seven" across 6.6 social networks, optimize click-through rates, and build links creatively via the free-tool "playbook" (AnswerThePublic, Ubersuggest — which he bought for $120K) rather than manual outreach or buying links. His umbrella maxim: "don't half-ass, go full-ass," and play the channel-specific game (their Perpetual Traffic YouTube collab hit a 7.5% CTR). The second half — agency growth in a down economy, the new Agency Owners Association, and levers like acquisitions, super-serving, practitioner-salespeople, multi-service-line selling, and middle-60% lead follow-up — is framed as Neil asking Eric about Single Grain, but the answer references NP Digital and its acquisitions, so its host attribution is genuinely mixed and is quarantined below (kept out of Neil's persona bank per rule 6).

## Key claims
- Lily Ray's "biggest SEO losers of 2023" study (a Lily Ray × Sistrix collaboration) ranked categories by average visibility loss: travel directories & blogs −50% (67 domains), hobbies & collectibles −50%, home & furniture −49%, advocacy & nonprofit −48%, e-commerce −47%, fashion/apparel −47%, adult content −47%, business & finance −42%. Neil cites it approvingly but caveats it heavily: it measures *visibility*, not traffic, on only ~30–40 domains per category (~2,500 domains total, deduped; Sistrix tracks 100M+ domains and 100M+ keywords), so it is not a high sample size. (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Neil doubts those categories actually lost 30–50% of *traffic* ("that's too high — I would have seen that data in our system," i.e. Ubersuggest). Search traffic is not dying: per Danny Sullivan (Google), Google has driven more traffic to websites each year than the previous year — overall traffic is increasing, not decreasing; people are still searching. (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Signature stat (from Neil's tweet titled "you're wasting your time on SEO"): ~8.5 billion Google searches happen per day, yet over the last 30 days in the Ubersuggest database, 94.29% of web pages indexed in Google got no search traffic. Most people read that as "SEO is dead" — Neil's point is the opposite: it means most people are doing SEO the old-school way (keyword research → crank out regurgitated me-too content everyone has already covered → build some links). That playbook no longer works. (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Modern SEO lever 1 — freshness: to rank now you must continually update your content. Wikipedia dominates rankings because its content stays fresh; "no one wants to read three-year-old content when they can read three-day-old content." (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Modern SEO lever 2 — newness: talk about genuinely new/trending things no one else is covering. AI is not bad and helps with research and improving content, but avoid pure AI-written articles because people want to hear new stuff. The #1 factor (after the hook) that sends their own social views "through the roof" is a trending or new topic. (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Modern SEO lever 3 — authority: build your authority in the space (E-E-A-T / "double E-A-T"). (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Modern SEO lever 4 — CTR: optimize your click-through rates — that is marketing, not just SEO — by making content more appealing so people want to click; Ubersuggest has a tool that raises rankings by improving CTR. (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Modern SEO lever 5 — creative link building (the "tool playbook"): give away free tools (Neil has done this with AnswerThePublic and Ubersuggest) — free tools build more links than any form of content you'll ever post. Don't just manually reach out to 500 people, and don't buy links (that's bad too). Neil bought Ubersuggest for $120K (a caption/host reference to "250" was the higher figure; Neil confirms the $120K he actually paid). (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Brand + rule of seven: over the past few years, what has really dominated search results is *brands*. Leverage the "rule of seven" — be present across all channels (the average social user is on 6.6 social networks, per DemandSage): SEO + email + SMS/text + podcasting + social. It's a lot of work, but people see you repeatedly and you build a brand over time. (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- "Don't half-ass — go full-ass" on content, and play the channel-specific game (on YouTube that's thumbnails and average view duration; SEO now needs a different playbook than before). Proof point: their collaboration with Perpetual Traffic — the first video in the series got a 7.5% CTR on YouTube (well above their usual B2B content) plus higher average view duration (the video ran ~25 minutes) — because they brought in new people and made it longer. (Neil, 2024-03-01)
- Collaborations are one of the best traffic/brand-awareness plays: partner with other companies (like the Lily Ray × Sistrix study) — they are starved for good content, love hearing from influencers, and are always down for webinars — so you leverage their audience combined with yours. (Neil, 2024-03-01)

### Agency-growth second half — attribution: UNCERTAIN (mixed Neil/Eric), kept out of the Neil bank
Neil (holding the floor after the AOA discussion) asks the other host for "your top three things you've done to grow **Single Grain** in a bad economy," which frames Eric as the answerer — but the answer references **NP Digital**, the **Rebel House** acquisition, and **private equity** data, which point to Neil. Because the captions have no speaker labels and the signals conflict, none of the following trains the persona; a later L3 pass should re-resolve it against audio. (mixed Neil/Eric, 2024-03-01)
- Growth lever — acquisitions (inorganic but cheap): the agency did two acquisitions; acquisitions don't have to be expensive (seller-based financing etc.). When you buy a company (e.g. Rebel House, a creative shop doing TV commercials for brands like ones featuring Snoop Dogg and Jennifer Lopez), you're buying cross-sell: how many of their clients can you sell your services to, and vice versa. International/English-speaking expansion is a related growth path.
- Growth lever — super-serve in a down economy: deliver more when clients are under pressure (e.g. the SaaS recession) so they remember you had their back; contracts then grow longer-term.
- Growth lever — talent + service-line selling: hire amazing practitioners from industry (clients follow great talent over time); make salespeople practitioners (SEOs, paid-ads, account managers), not pure salespeople, because trust and conversion go up "like going to a doctor"; and sell multiple service line items per customer (a lesson from private equity) — churn drops drastically when a client buys 3–4 service lines, and compensation was restructured to reward selling multiple lines based on a real needs analysis, not pushing whatever earns commission.
- Lead follow-up: the middle 60% of leads is where the money is (20% buy immediately, 20% never buy); "the fortune is in the follow-up" (attributed to John Maxwell). Follow up over years (emails, texts, calls, in-person) in a non-annoying, value-adding way — e.g. point out three fixes you spotted on their site with case studies, hold back the rest for the call.
- Don't charge for proposals or advice — do the business analysis and proposal free as a cost of doing business; the "charge-for-the-proposal / move up the waitlist" model only tends to work for small agencies that aren't making much money.
- Plug: the **Agency Owners Association (AOA)** — a community/mastermind for agency owners focused on industry-specific growth (name coined by "Saeid"); apply at marketingschool.io/agency. (Co-described by both hosts; not a persona claim.)
