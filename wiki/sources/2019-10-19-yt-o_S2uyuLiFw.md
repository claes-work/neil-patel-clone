# 7 SEO Experiments to Test in 2023

> Note: the channel-supplied title says "2023", but the video was published 2019-10-19 and Neil refers to "2020" throughout the transcript — treat the tactics as his 2019/2020-era advice.

## Summary
In this solo video, Neil Patel argues that SEO is never "done" — because (per him) Google makes over 3,000 algorithm changes per year, continual testing is the only way to keep outranking competitors. He walks through seven experiments centered on his core thesis that Google increasingly rewards user metrics (click-through rate, bounce rate, time on site) over raw backlinks: (1) A/B test headlines/title tags (using ClickFlow) with tactics like appending the year, asking questions, and evoking curiosity; (2) write separate headlines for social media vs. SEO because what wins social shares reads as "clickbait" to searchers; (3) publish far less new content and instead aggressively update old content — he blogs once a week but a team of three refreshes ~90 old posts, which he credits for growing neilpatel.com from ~4M to ~5.3M monthly visits; (4) improve time on site via embedded video/audio, longer content, internal linking, quizzes, and comment engagement; (5) serve HTML from a CDN (e.g., Cloudflare, Akamai) to speed global load times and boost rankings; (6) prioritize LSI/search-intent and long-tail buyer keywords over high-volume head terms — using Ubersuggest to mine long-tail phrases (sorted by volume and CPC, where higher CPC signals buyer intent), integrating them into already-ranking pages and resubmitting via Google Webmaster Tools for re-indexing within 30 days; and (7) double down on video content, since YouTube SEO ranks within 24-48 hours of upload and audiences increasingly prefer video — he makes ~3 videos a week vs. 1 blog post.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said Google makes over 3,000 algorithm changes per year, so SEO requires continual testing rather than being learned once.
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said you should A/B test headlines/title tags (he names ClickFlow), because Google ranks on user metrics — if searchers consistently click the second result over the first, Google treats the second as more relevant regardless of its backlinks or on-page SEO.
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said three CTR tactics help title tags get more clicks: appending the year (e.g., "updated in 2020"), phrasing the title as a question (which he said increases click-throughs by over 10% per ClickFlow), and evoking curiosity (e.g., "the seven benefits of green tea — number six will shock you").
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said to create separate headlines for social media vs. SEO — shocking/hype words ("amazing", "effortlessly") win social shares but read as clickbait to searchers — and to use Open Graph tags so the social title, the SEO title tag, and the on-page headline can all differ.
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said to produce much less new content and update old content instead: he blogs once a week while a team of three updates ~90 old posts, making them thorough and fixing dead links — which he credited with raising neilpatel.com traffic from ~4 million to ~5.3 million monthly visits.
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said to improve time on site (a ranking-relevant user metric) by embedding videos, audio/podcasts, and SlideShare slideshows, lengthening content, internal linking, adding quizzes, and encouraging/responding to comments.
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said to load HTML files from a CDN (naming Cloudflare and Akamai) so pages load faster worldwide — since a faster server alone doesn't guarantee fast global delivery — which should improve rankings.
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said to focus on LSI keywords and search intent rather than only high-volume head terms, preferring qualified buyer traffic: "I'd rather have 1,000 amazing, ready-to-buy visitors than 100,000 visitors who are likely to bounce away."
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said to use Ubersuggest to pull long-tail phrases for keywords you already rank for (sorted by volume and CPC, where higher CPC indicates more buyer intent), integrate them into the ranking page, and resubmit the URL via Google Webmaster Tools to be re-indexed and rank higher within 30 days.
- As of 2019-10-19, Neil said to double down on video: YouTube SEO differs from Google SEO in that videos tend to rank within 24-48 hours of upload, Google is ranking video and transcribed podcasts more, and he recommends creating more video than text (he makes ~3 videos a week vs. 1 blog post) — noting his text content still gets roughly 3x the traffic of his video content.
