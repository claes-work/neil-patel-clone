# How to Create the Perfect Tweet Storm

## Summary
Neil Patel gives a tactical breakdown of "tweetstorms" (chains of self-replies forming one
overarching thought), using a worked example: a storm on SEO diagnostic tools (Ubersuggest for
keyword rankings, Google PageSpeed Insights, MxToolBox Domain Health Report, FE International's
Website Penalty Indicator, Google Search Console), which he says generated 72,556 impressions, 667
likes, and 215 retweets across six tweets. He distills four rules: (1) pick a topic with proven
interest, using Ubersuggest's keyword-popularity data (based on Google search volume) as a topic-
validation tool; (2) a tweetstorm needs at least 5 tweets (5–10 tends to perform best, more is fine,
but 2–3 tweets isn't really a "storm"); (3) back claims with stats and data points, which he says
people like retweeting; (4) keep each tweet terse and self-contained enough to stand alone as a
retweetable/likeable unit.

## Key claims
- Worked example: a 6-tweet tweetstorm on SEO diagnostic tools (Ubersuggest, Google PageSpeed
  Insights, MxToolBox, FE International's Website Penalty Indicator, Google Search Console) generated
  72,556 impressions, 667 likes, 215 retweets (as of 2022-07-30, self-reported account analytics).
- Rule 1: validate topic interest before writing a tweetstorm, using Ubersuggest's popularity data
  (based on underlying Google search volume) as the topic-research tool (as of 2022-07-30).
- Rule 2: a tweetstorm should contain at least 5 tweets, with 5–10 as the sweet spot; fewer than that
  "really isn't a tweetstorm" (as of 2022-07-30).
- Rule 3: include supporting stats/data points in tweetstorms, framed as both a credibility signal and
  a driver of retweets (as of 2022-07-30).
- Rule 4: keep each tweet terse and impactful enough to work as a standalone retweetable/likeable unit
  (as of 2022-07-30).
