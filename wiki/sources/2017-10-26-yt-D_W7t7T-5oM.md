# 3 Simple Steps to Marketing Your Mobile App | Get More Exposure and Installs!

## Summary
Neil lays out a three-step playbook for marketing a mobile app and driving more installs, contrasting it with the comparatively simple SEO/content/social approach used for websites. He recommends that anyone without an app yet build one using BuildFire (a no-developer app builder), then (1) generate press — either through a performance-based PR service like prserv.com or by giving relevant niche bloggers free early access before public launch, (2) earn coverage in the blogosphere by reaching out to bloggers who have already written about topics related to the app, and (3) spend money on advertising such as Facebook app-install ads and cheap in-app ad space bought from other app owners (e.g. mobile games). He closes by noting that once users are inside the app, virality mechanics like invite flows and bonuses/points for sharing, rating, and reviewing amplify installs over time — but that these viral loops only work if traffic is already coming in.

## Key claims
As of 2017-10-26, Neil states:
- Marketing a mobile app is harder than marketing a website, where SEO, content marketing, and some social media marketing can make you popular (as of 2017-10-26).
- BuildFire is a quick, low-cost way to build an app without being a developer (as of 2017-10-26).
- The first marketing step is to create press; a performance-based PR service like prserv.com (founded by Chris Barrett) charges only if it actually secures coverage, which Neil argues is how all PR should work (as of 2017-10-26).
- Charging $10,000–$20,000 monthly retainers without guaranteeing any press is unjustified (as of 2017-10-26).
- To generate press yourself, identify who covers apps in your niche (e.g. beauty bloggers for a beauty app) and give them the app free with early access before public launch, so they feel special and cover it (as of 2017-10-26).
- The second step is getting blogosphere coverage, which can simply mean being mentioned within a relevant existing blog post rather than a dedicated app-launch article; Neil reaches out to bloggers who have already written articles related to his app (as of 2017-10-26).
- The third step is paid advertising: Facebook ads can drive a lot of app-install traffic (as of 2017-10-26).
- Buying ad space inside other people's apps — such as the ad breaks shown between mobile-game sessions — is "dirt cheap" and can drive a large volume of installs (as of 2017-10-26).
- Once users are in the app, virality mechanics (invite flows, bonuses and points for sharing, rating, and reviewing) drive more installs long-term, but these viral loops fail if no one is entering the app (as of 2017-10-26).
