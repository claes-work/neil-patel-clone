# AI Operators Dinner, Marketing Loops & GPT Sol 5.6 vs Fable 5 (Marketing School)

## Summary
Co-hosted Marketing School episode driven largely by Eric Siu, who is in New York (Mozcon + a social-commerce summit) and recaps a dinner he hosted for "9-to-10-figure AI operators," using its questions as prompts. Eric owns most of the framing material this episode — the AI-operators-dinner recap, the Lenny's-podcast tech-worker sentiment survey, the "dental hygienist vs. dentist" agency-future analogy, the Moz/Profound/GEO industry read, AI marketing "loops," his Single Grain European agency roll-up, and the GPT Sol 5.6 [sic?] vs. Claude Fable 5 [sic?] model bake-off. Neil Patel is the responder and supplies the durable persona signal: his agency's actual experience with AI margins (not chasing margin, chasing "do more"; AI unlocks NEW client work rather than saving money; strict model-cost discipline), his M&A valuation frameworks (key-man risk, margin compression, high-margin = weak management team, earnouts, PE minority/majority "take chips off the table" plays), his cold-calling / follow-up sales philosophy (40+ calls a day, never leave voicemails, "the fortune is in the follow-up," deals close on the 2nd/3rd try), and his read that the marketing pendulum is swinging back toward humans because AI-forward pressure produced slop. Heavy AI-news content throughout is date-stamped and mostly ephemeral. Attribution split is roughly half/half; Eric is the presenter/screen-sharer, Neil the responder and owner of the agency-M&A and sales material.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- Neil's org is NOT internally focused on margin improvement from AI; the focus is getting people more productive and doing MORE for the customer at the same price point. Net-net they are seeing few margin gains and little extra profitability from AI — "just being really transparent" — because AI/token costs offset the efficiency. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
- Model-cost discipline: many organizations use the most advanced (expensive) models for the simplest tasks, which makes no sense when far cheaper older models cost a fraction; wrong-model choice is where margins get destroyed. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — ai-marketing.
- "Token maxing" (employees burning tokens freely) is over or will be over soon; companies are realizing they're overspending, and it has to be fair to the business, not just the employee. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — ai-marketing.
- AI's biggest agency impact isn't cost savings — it's letting the agency do NEW things that were never financially feasible for clients. Example: custom reporting interfaces for franchise clients (some with 1,000+ locations) that would have cost hundreds of thousands to millions to build traditionally now cost a few thousand to ~$5–10K including human time. Doesn't save money or make more money — just makes clients happier and reduces mistakes. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
- Not seeing margin expansion in developers/engineers — the good ones just want more money as they get more productive; "pay more and pay more." (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Burnout is unsustainable: A-player engineers Neil knows now work 6–7 days/week and 13–14-hour days despite AI "doing the coding"; not sustainable, especially with a family; insufficient sleep makes you less productive regardless of AI. Expects this to change within a few years. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Treat a company like a family and hire for cultural fit; support team members in genuine crisis (told an employee whose child was gravely ill to stop working and stay with the child, still paid) — but don't tolerate abuse (references a person who faked illness, took another job, and tried to poach clients while still being paid). (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Cold-calling / immediacy is Neil's edge: he picks up the phone and calls straight off the email number to do a deal "right then and there," makes 40+ calls a day overall (more than 20, not 40, if only cold outreach), and does NOT leave voicemails (so prospects aren't irritated and eventually just answer). ~1% of people complain about the immediacy; worth the trade-off. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Branding pays off in deals: on marketing/M&A calls ~60–70% of people already know who Neil is; on random business deals (e.g., line of credit) only ~5%. People take his calls and are more flexible because of the brand. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- "The fortune is in the follow-up" (cites John Maxwell via Eric): Neil almost never gets an M&A deal on the first try — it's the 2nd or 3rd, by staying in touch as sellers "come to reality." Example anecdote: went through old intro emails and cold-called; 18 calls → 3 people reached → one turned into a $30M-ad-budget RFP opportunity in SF. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- M&A valuation frameworks (durable): high margins (30–40% profit) usually signal an under-built management team and thus more key-man risk; AI-native consulting shops face margin compression over time as competition floods in; watch what portion of revenue is genuinely AI/new-service and how sticky it is; won't overpay for a declining business (rejected 8x profit on a shrinking company). Uses earnouts to bridge unrealistic seller expectations — pay X only if they hit the profit. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Roll-up / cross-sell thesis: buy performance-marketing agencies region by region (Spain, Italy, France, Germany), merge them into the parent agency, and cross-sell clients across regions to grow faster. (Attribution note: this specific European hunt is Eric's Single Grain plan; Neil articulates the same cross-sell logic for his own creative/agency acquisitions.) (Neil, 2026-07-20; Eric drives the Europe specifics) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- PE advice for a founder afraid of debt/venture: don't take debt or raise venture — sell a minority (40–49%) chunk to private equity to take chips off the table (enough to never work again) while they fund growth and tuck in companies (he becomes "the platform"); or sell majority and take ~70% off the table — the back-end (even a diluted 30%) often ends up roughly the size of the front-end, so he can make double. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- "Strike while the iron is hot": AI-consulting/GEO valuations are heavily inflated now and will normalize/come down within ~5 years as the space floods with competitors; take chips off the table while valuations are hot; "what goes up must come down," and fast growth invites price-cutting competition. (Neil, 2026-07-20; Eric concurs re: Profound/Scrunch) — agency-entrepreneurship / seo.
- The marketing pendulum is swinging BACK toward humans: a year ago companies pushed hard on marketers/agencies to be AI-forward and cut headcount under finance pressure ("do more for the same money"); now there's much LESS pressure because AI produced a lot of "slop" and the same budgets didn't yield more growth. Big corporations ran experiments across their many divisions (old models/old ways vs. new) and saw little difference; companies are rehiring humans. Expectations have "dropped a little" and normalized. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — ai-marketing / paid-ads.
- Don't lock into a long-term contract with either Claude or OpenAI — they constantly leapfrog each other; what's best today may not be tomorrow. Neil personally prefers ChatGPT over Claude "right now" but won't force his team to switch (too much of a logistical pain). (Neil, 2026-07-20) — ai-marketing. *(vendor-lock-in stance is durable; model preference is ephemeral 2026-Q3.)*
- On product/IT teams being rebuilt: Neil let go some product people who "wanted to do things in an old-school way" and refused to adapt — "adapt or die"; product got better and moved faster afterward. (Neil, 2026-07-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- On the boring-wins investing instinct: asked which Amazon bet is more appealing (supply-chain/logistics services vs. same-day grocery), Neil picks logistics — "it's boring. Usually the boring stuff makes more money." (Neil, 2026-07-20) — context.
- Services/agencies are here to stay alongside AI: as more people build with AI, MORE humans get hired to maintain, optimize, and strategize; expects large-scale reskilling over the next 10+ years; net positive. (Neil + Eric, 2026-07-20) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> We're seeing margin expansion by AI by cutting, and you have to be careful which models you use, because if you use the wrong models they're very expensive. What we find is a lot of organizations use the most advanced models to do some of the most simplest things, which doesn't logically make sense when they can use older models that are a fraction of the cost.

> Just honestly, internally, we're not thinking about margin improvement from AI. We're... focusing our time and energy is how to get people more productive and do more.

> AI's created the opportunity for us to do more for our clients and do different things that would have been too costly based on their budgets... These kind of things haven't made us more money. They haven't really saved us any money. We're just now doing new things that were not financially feasible for a client.

> I believe being a mother is the hardest job in the world. I think being a stay-at-home mom is much harder than working 9 to 5... If my son had a choice from making millions and millions of dollars and being miserable, or being a teacher and being happy, I would tell him be a teacher and be happy.

> I would suggest that you don't lock yourself into a long-term contract with Claude or OpenAI, because they're just constantly leapfrogging each other... It may be better now, but who knows if it's better or worse [later].

> The problem with AI-native consulting companies is... you have to look at what portion of their revenue is from these new services and how sticky is it... And I do believe over time when people have higher margins, it's because they haven't built out their management team to reduce the risk.

> Most my deals that I get, it's always from staying in touch. If I look at most M&A, I never get the deal the first time I try to get the deal. I tend to get the deal on the second or third time because people come to reality with how things are going.

> The hardest part that I end up having is when I get an email from someone and they just shoot me an email and I'm trying to do a deal, I tend to just pick up the phone and call them based on the number I have in the email and try to do it right then and there. But a lot of people don't like that.

> I do not leave messages, because what I found is... they're more likely to get irritated. But because I didn't leave a message, they probably don't know who I am. So eventually they answer and they don't get irritated as much.

> We're seeing a whole new shift in marketing right now. A year ago companies were putting a lot of pressure on marketers and agencies... Now they're rehiring, because they found that AI is creating a lot of slop and they still need the humans involved in the process... The pendulum is swinging more towards the human side than the AI side.

> The logistics — it's boring. Usually the boring stuff makes more money.

> If they're just not willing to adapt and they want to do things in an old-school way when everyone else is changing, it's either adapt or die. I hate to say it, but that's reality.
