# How To Optimize For The Page Experience Algorithm (Google's Upcoming Algorithm Update)

## Summary
Ahead of Google's 2021 Page Experience algorithm rollout, Neil walks through a live
Crazy Egg heatmap/click-tracking session on his own site (homepage, blog index, and a
blog article, on both desktop and mobile) to show how he diagnoses UX problems: where
attention drops off down the page, which images get clicked despite not being
clickable, whether the comment box is reachable, and how navigation clutter (sidebar
links, footer) gets ignored. He frames the whole exercise as "you can't just optimize
for [Google], you got to also optimize for the user" — usability data as the practical
lever for a page-experience algorithm Google won't fully disclose. Also recounts one
concrete win: replacing a generic content-topic dropdown ("I want to learn about
everything") with a binary consulting-intent question ("do you want me to do your
marketing for you?") to route visitors into either the consulting funnel or
self-serve content, based on what the heatmap showed people clicking.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-12-05, frames Google's page-experience push as fundamentally about
  making sites optimize for users, not just for the algorithm's known ranking signals
  (paraphrase, standing belief).
- Recommends Crazy Egg (his own product) snapshot testing — separate desktop/mobile
  heatmaps, ~60-day or 2,500-visit sample windows — as the practical tool for
  diagnosing page-experience problems (paraphrase, tool endorsement/self-promotion).
- Concrete UX findings from his own heatmaps (self-reported, single-site case study):
  stock photography with no informational value gets almost no attention and should
  be replaced with earlier-positioned text; users try to click non-clickable images on
  mobile, which should either be made functional or removed; sidebar/footer
  navigational elements get minimal engagement and are candidates for removal; a blog
  page missing top navigation shows heavy, "unusable" reliance on the footer nav.
- Reports a category-dropdown UI element ("I want to learn about... everything") as
  one of the most-clicked areas on both mobile and desktop, and that changing it into
  a binary consulting-intent gate ("do you want me to do your marketing for you?")
  was a direct lead-generation improvement discovered via the heatmap test
  (paraphrase).
- Notes his blog has no pagination and relies on infinite scroll, which works
  differently by device — worth testing pagination on mobile where users rarely
  scroll far.
