# How to Start a Business and Grow it to a Billion Dollar Company ★

> ★ L3-candidate: Neil's core thesis that billion-dollar outcomes are governed by total addressable market (TAM) size — not effort or team talent — plus concrete validation tools (Google Finance for public-company market caps, Google Trends benchmarked against "digital marketing"). A durable business-framework worth distilling into a concept page.

## Summary

In this solo talk, Neil Patel argues that the difference between building a multi-million-dollar business and a billion-dollar one is not how hard you work or how smart your team is — it is whether you are solving a big enough problem in a large enough market. He introduces TAM (total addressable market) as the governing variable, using Crazy Egg (his own company) as an example of a business that solves a real but too-small problem to ever reach a billion dollars, and Uber as a company that grew its entire market rather than taking a slice of it. He closes with practical validation tactics: check public companies' market caps on Google Finance, and use Google Trends to confirm your space is at least 10x the search demand of "digital marketing."

## Key claims

- As of 2017-12-16, Neil states he has not yet built a billion-dollar company, and frames the video around the mistake that stopped him from doing so.
- Neil claims to have friends whose salary (not business revenue) is in the hundreds of millions of dollars a year, friends on the Fortune 1000 list, and several people worth between five and ten billion dollars (self-reported).
- The difference between a hundreds-of-millions business and a billions business is solving a problem for a really big market — not working harder or hiring smarter people.
- Crazy Egg (one of his first businesses) helps optimize websites so more visitors convert into customers; Neil says it has hundreds/thousands of companies using it each month, but it will never be a billion-dollar business because it does not solve a big enough problem.
- Uber solves a huge transportation problem and, as of 2017, grew the taxi market rather than just taking a share of it — Neil claims the taxi industry was actually smaller before Uber.
- Competitors need not be direct: Neil argues Uber does not directly compete with taxis; people choose Uber over owning a car rather than deliberating taxi-vs-Uber.
- Rather than inventing a brand-new category, Neil advises innovating within a sector that already has a really big TAM (total addressable market size).
- Going after a huge market means even 1% of it can build a large company; he cites capturing ~5% of Salesforce's ~$50 billion CRM market as still being a billion-dollar company.
- Example markets Neil calls "big enough": advertising, CRM, support, transportation, automation, trucking, shipping containers.
- As of 2017-12-16, Neil cites Nextiva (a VoIP phone-services company that hosted the NextCon conference in Arizona where he spoke) as doing over $100 million in revenue while not being the biggest player in its space (self-reported, relayed from Nextiva's founder).
- Neil relays that the VoIP market had only ~8% penetration as of 2017 (92% still on landlines) — a small penetration that signals large future growth headroom.
- He names RingCentral and Vonage as VoIP companies already worth a billion-plus, contrasted with the lesser-known Nextiva still making hundreds of millions.
- Neil says he builds multiple products in the marketing space not because he wants four or five companies, but so he can eventually combine them into one business chasing a really big TAM.
- Validation tactic: use Google Finance to look up publicly traded companies' financials and market caps (e.g., typing a stock quote like AAPL for Apple) to judge whether an idea's market is big enough.
- Validation tactic: use Google Trends on keywords related to your space; a large chart is good, a small one is bad, and the space should be at least 10 times bigger than the term "digital marketing."
- Neil invites viewers to leave a comment with their idea and offers to tell them whether the market is big enough.
