# A Deep Dive Into Branding, Data & Experience - Module 7 - Part 1 - SEO Unlocked

## Summary
Opening lesson of SEO Unlocked's final module (branding), opening with the Bezos
quote "your brand is what other people say about you when you're not in the room."
Frames brand-building as five steps and covers step 1, **market assessment**, split
into a business side and a human side. Business side (four factors): **market size**
(Google `<industry> market size`), **GDP/scale** (`<industry> GDP`), **growth rate**
(`<industry> growth <year>`, projected 5-10yr out), and **market trends** (look at
the space's multi-billion-dollar incumbents — no billion-dollar players signals a
market that's too small; prefer large markets because capturing a small % of a huge
market beats capturing a larger % of a tiny one). Data-source list beyond Google:
government data sites, public-company financials, **founder-interview podcasts**
(names Leveling Up by Eric Siu, Growth Everywhere, Mixergy, Nathan Latka) as a favorite
source of competitor intel, trade associations, and **Statista**. Also flags
**profitability** (avoid markets where players spend $2 to earn $1) as a red flag.
Human side: look for **irrational passion + urgency + pain points** in a market
(ideally all three; validated via Ubersuggest search-volume signals), fewer
competitors as an advantage, and relationship-building (email, push, chatbot) as a
compounding branding/sales lever. On competitive assessment: explicitly endorses
"stacking the deck in your favor" in business (premium positioning, undercutting on
price, better service) as legitimate, unlike gambling. Self-critiques his own
positioning choice — competing in the crowded "marketing" space with neilpatel.com was
"a really big mistake" because of how many other marketers compete there; recommends
picking spaces with fewer marketer-competitors. Covers positioning (gold-standard /
cheapest / unique-solution — he favors unique-solution) and closes on primary
customer/market research methods: carrying a notepad for constant informal
observation, running surveys (own or existing), calling past customers, reading
competitors' Amazon reviews and customer-service threads, and eventually 15-minute
phone calls once patterns emerge from initial research.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-06-13, Neil said picking neilpatel.com's positioning as a general
  marketing brand was "a really big mistake" because it put him in competition with
  thousands of other marketers who do SEO, PPC, copywriting and CRO — and he advised
  picking markets with fewer competing marketers specifically.
- As of 2020-06-13, Neil's market-viability heuristic for profitability: avoid markets
  where players spend roughly $2 to generate $1 of revenue; prefer markets that flip
  that ratio.
- As of 2020-06-13, Neil said the ideal market has all three of irrational passion,
  urgency and pain points, validated via search-volume data in Ubersuggest.
  Higher search volume signals stronger buyer interest.
- As of 2020-06-13, Neil said "stacking the deck" in your favor via premium
  positioning, undercut pricing, or superior service is legitimate business practice
  (unlike in gambling), as long as the rules aren't broken.
- As of 2020-06-13, Neil named founder-interview podcasts (Leveling Up by Eric Siu,
  Growth Everywhere, Mixergy, Nathan Latka's show) as a favorite way to mine
  competitor strategy, calling founders on these shows prone to "spilling their beans."

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Your brand is what other people say about you when you're not in the room." [quoting Jeff Bezos]

> "Like when I look at neilpatel.com, I made a really big mistake. Neilpatel.com is a blog about marketing and I'm competing with thousands of other marketers like me."

> "In business, it's a total opposite [of gambling]. If you stack to favor in your odds, you're going to build a much more profitable business with a much larger brand."
