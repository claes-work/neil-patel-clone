# International SEO Strategy (Get Started NOW)

## Summary

Q&A Thursday (Neil with interviewer Adam Lodolce from Viewership). Question from "Jason
aka Mitchell": how do you get started in international SEO to reach Germany, India, China,
Brazil, Japan and Spanish-speaking countries? Neil says he's done a lot of international
SEO "and made a lot of mistakes," and frames the whole answer around **market selection
first, mechanics second**. Before expanding, understand where your paying customers
actually are: a market having many people or decent GDP does not mean there is enough
business for *you* there. He uses his own agency (services "start around $120,000") and
India as the example — despite his Indian heritage and love of the country, there are
fewer clients who can afford his service there than in the US/UK today; India is "still
developing" and will be a bigger opportunity in 5–10 years. So don't expand into a region
until you're already getting customers from it and the majority search in a non-English
language. On mechanics: **translate content manually, never with Google Translate** — he
once auto-translated his site into ~30 popular languages and (1) users emailed that his
German homepage contained profanity, and (2) his bounce rate "went through the roof,"
which (via Panda / RankBrain-era quality + user-metric signals) hurt rankings site-wide.
Hire manual translators (e.g. Upwork or in-region), then use **hreflang** to tell Google
which language/region each page targets (English-for-UK vs Spanish-for-Mexico vs
Portuguese-from-Brazil, which differs from Portugal's). Host translated content on
**subdomains** (e.g. `de.neilpatel.com`) rather than subdirectories — Neil notes he
himself uses `neilpatel.com/de` (subdirectories) and says from all the tests they ran he
"would have done better" with subdomains. Then build press, backlinks and social shares
within each region (e.g. boosting Facebook posts to that audience) — all of which costs
money. Adam suggests testing with PPC first; Neil agrees: test Google AdWords or Facebook
ads in-region (with a translated landing page and in-language ad copy) to see if you can
collect leads/sales before committing. Closing throughline: most people looking to expand
internationally haven't yet dominated their home region — **dominate where you are first,
then expand.**

## Key claims

- As of 2018-09-06, choose international markets by **where your paying customers are**,
  not raw population or GDP; a big or wealthy-on-paper market (he cites India, where his
  ~$120k services have a smaller affordable-client base than the US/UK) may not yet hold
  enough business to justify expansion.
- As of 2018-09-06, **translate content manually, never via Google Translate** — Neil
  auto-translated his site into ~30 languages and got profanity on his German homepage
  plus a spiked bounce rate that hurt rankings (Panda/RankBrain-era quality & user-metric
  signals).
- As of 2018-09-06, use **hreflang** to tell Google the language/region each page targets
  (e.g. Spanish-for-Mexico vs Spanish-for-Spain; Brazilian vs Portugal Portuguese).
- As of 2018-09-06, host international content on **subdomains** (`de.neilpatel.com`)
  rather than subdirectories; Neil says he uses subdirectories (`neilpatel.com/de`) on his
  own site and, from his tests, would have ranked better with subdomains.
- As of 2018-09-06, build region-specific **press, backlinks and social shares** to rank
  internationally — it is "a very expensive endeavor."
- As of 2018-09-06, **test a new region with PPC / Facebook ads first** (translated
  landing page + in-language ad) before investing in full international SEO.
- As of 2018-09-06, **dominate your home region before expanding internationally** — most
  would-be expanders haven't yet done well where they already are.
