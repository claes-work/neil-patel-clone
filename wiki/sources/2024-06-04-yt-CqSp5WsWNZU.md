# The impact of AI overviews on SEO, Google starts testing ads in AI overviews, and more (Marketing School)

## Summary
★ Landmark-worthy for later L3 promotion: Neil's monetization-timing rule — monetize too early and you starve your top-of-funnel; monetize too late and you've trained your audience to expect everything free — is a clean, reusable principle worth promoting.

In this co-hosted Marketing School episode, Neil Patel and Eric Siu discuss Google AI Overviews (formerly SGE), the emerging placement of ads inside AI Overviews, Google's stance toward content creators whose search traffic was wiped out, and the fallout for affiliate-dependent sites. Neil argues it is far too early (6–12 months) to draw conclusions on AI Overviews, cites early third-party data (Ross Hudgins / Siege Media) showing a *slight CTR increase* rather than a collapse, and stresses diversification (account-based marketing, audience/email building) as insulation against algorithm hits. The back half is a candid podcasting-economics conversation: DreamHost's ~$60k/month Marketing School sponsorship, monetization timing (Neil's Quick Sprout history and the Microsoft-Teams-vs-Slack example), and creative sponsorship deals. ## Key claims
- AI Overviews are essentially SGE (Search Generative Experience) and behave like Perplexity — you get the answer plus everything cited; Neil credits the "it's basically Perplexity" framing to David Sacks (name garbled — "David Sachs"). (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Early third-party data suggests AI Overviews caused a *slight CTR increase*, not a drop: citing Ross Hudgins / Siege Media (name garbled — "Ross hudgin"), average CTR rose from 3.64% (May 8–11, pre-AI-Overviews) to 3.75% (May 15–18), each on ~65M impressions; Hudgins himself concluded it is too early to say anything. (Neil relaying third-party data, 2024-06-04)
- It is too early to judge AI Overviews — give it at least another 6–12 months before calling it out; too many SEOs are diving in too early, and people mocking the citations as inaccurate should remember it just came out of beta and will keep improving. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Neil says he is getting more data from BrightEdge's CEO "Jim" (likely Jim Yu — surname not stated) and will share it on the podcast; advises SEOs to "hold your horses." (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Google is testing ads inside AI Overviews: the overview cites its sources, then as you scroll a bit further paid ads appear; Google will keep experimenting and testing until they find the formula that makes their earnings go up long-term. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- On Google's CEO (Sundar Pichai — not named in transcript) being "empathetic" to content creators whose search traffic was wiped out: Neil reads it as diplomatic PR — Pichai says it's not Google's goal and calls hard-hit sites possible "edge cases," but Neil doubts he genuinely cares, because to Google it's just a business there to monetize and do what's best for itself. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Affiliate-dependent sites got hit hard: Neil got a text from a mutual friend whose site "lost all its traffic," asking what to do besides chasing Reddit traffic back; Neil made an intro. Lesson: their business was all affiliate (monetize organic SEO traffic, then send it to affiliates) — putting all your eggs in one basket means you must be prepared for the repercussions, and most people aren't. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- What insulated Neil's own business wasn't primarily email — it was account-based marketing: identifying the ideal customer and getting in front of them directly to win business; even in a bad market his company expects to grow ~20–30% this year (some organic, some M&A). (Neil, 2024-06-04) — this gently corrects Eric's "it was the emails" framing (see Eric section).
- DreamHost sponsored Marketing School for ~3.5 years — a single ad spot of ~30–45 seconds per episode at ~$60k/month; with few running costs, "Eric and I would pocket around $27.5k each." (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Marketing School and Neil's first podcast weren't monetized until ~3–4 years in — it was for the learnings, not the money. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Neil's Quick Sprout history: he blogged and gave value away for free for 6+ years without monetizing; he learned from Ramit Sethi (name garbled — "RIT seti") that this trains your audience to expect everything free. The transition to charging was hard and took ~1.5–2 years, cost him a chunk of his existing audience (people called it selling out), and progressed free content → courses → group coaching → agency. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Monetization-timing rule: monetize too early and you don't build your top-of-funnel big enough; monetize too late and it hurts you because you've trained your audience to expect everything for free. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- Microsoft Teams did it right: giving Teams away free during Covid hurt Slack (even its stock) because "it's free" beats a better UI; Microsoft then started charging at the right time. Many corporations use the Microsoft Suite over G Suite because it's cheaper and "good enough." (Neil, 2024-06-04)
- On sponsorship money: it's real money, but weigh the long-term game — a quick $60k/month or $100k here and there vs. focusing all your energy on building a multi-billion-dollar business; there's no right or wrong answer, and some people did great purely through influencer/sponsorship deals. (Neil, 2024-06-04 — moderate confidence; consistent first-person "build a multi-billion-dollar business" framing, no contrary signal)
- Personal preference color: Neil doesn't use a corporate/Outlook email (uses his own domain) and refuses to use a PC. (Neil, 2024-06-04)
