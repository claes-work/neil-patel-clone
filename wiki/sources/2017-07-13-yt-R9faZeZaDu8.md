# 3 Easy Ways to Get More Blog Comments, Build Engagement and Boost Traffic to Your Website

## Summary
A tight three-step tutorial on generating more blog comments, which Neil treats as the real proof that people read and enjoy content. His three levers: write in a conversational tone (using "you" and "I"), always end a post with a question, and use subheadings so skimmers can grasp the post fast enough to decide to engage. He explicitly de-prioritizes commonly cited tactics (social sharing, Facebook comments vs native comments) as having little impact next to these three.

## Key claims
As of 2017-07-13, Neil states:
- Comments are the real signal that people read and enjoyed your content (as of 2017-07-13).
- Write in a conversational tone using the words "you" and "I" — this creates a conversation and encourages comments (analogy: a boring lecturer puts you to sleep, a conversational one draws a response) (as of 2017-07-13).
- Always ask a question at the end of a post; it tells readers what to do next, so they leave an answer — without a closing question they don't know to comment (as of 2017-07-13).
- Use subheadings (as on neilpatel.com) because people skim; subheadings let readers grasp the gist in a few minutes and decide whether to engage, comment, or share (as of 2017-07-13).
- Other tactics — social sharing, or Facebook comments vs normal blog comments — don't meaningfully move comment counts compared with these three; focus on the three (as of 2017-07-13).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "The real way to know that people read it and enjoy your content is if they leave a comment."

> "The way you create a conversation is use the words you and I. When you use the words you and I within your blog posts ... it creates a conversation and it encourages more people to leave a comment."

> "Always ask a question at the end of your blog posts ... If you don't leave a question at the end, they're not going to leave a comment because they don't know what to do next."

> "People skim content. By using subheadings within your blog posts ... they'll go through your content quicker and ... within a few minutes they'll be able to understand the gist of your blog post."
