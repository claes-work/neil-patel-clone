# Critical Agency Acquisition Mistakes - Neil Patel

## Summary
A co-hosted follow-up (billed as the sequel to an episode on whether agencies are right for roll-ups) where Neil and Eric share mistakes made acquiring agencies. Neil drives most of the durable framework content: the #1 mistake is not retaining the founders/management team and top talent for at least 1–2 years post-acquisition, because an agency is a people business and losing the leader (and their "lieutenants") after close destabilizes client relationships fast — he uses an organ-transplant-rejection metaphor for merging two organizations. He lays out a detailed diligence checklist (churn by client count AND revenue, segmented by industry; services sold per client; staff tenure and staff churn; key differentiators; how leads are sourced — founder-dependent vs. brand-driven, which changes risk after the founder's incentive to stay drops). He also stresses evaluating multiple target agencies before committing ("you got to kiss a lot of frogs"), requiring audited financials or independent review to catch fraud, using good lawyers, and targeting agencies that retain customers 4+ years rather than 1–2 (a "revolving door"). Eric contributes a first-hand acquisition story (a founder who was "super serving" every account personally, then left immediately post-acquisition along with a key lieutenant, destabilizing the business) and a broker-diligence horror story (a broker withheld correct diligence materials from him).

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- The #1 mistake acquiring an agency: not retaining the founders/management team for 1–2 years post-close. Without them, knowledge transfer and client relationships suffer badly — "expect a world of hurt." An agency is a people business; the people drive revenue and reduce churn. (Neil, 2023-12-02) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Merging two organizations post-acquisition is like a body accepting a new organ — "it might not take," i.e. get rejected, if the two chaotic organizations don't integrate well. (Eric, 2023-12-02; illustrating his own deal where the founder super-served every account personally, then left immediately after close, followed by a key lieutenant leaving too) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Diligence must cover: churn broken out by client count AND by revenue, segmented per industry; number of service units/items sold per client; staff on board, tenure, and staff churn; the target's key differentiator in market; how long they've been in business; and how leads are sourced (word of mouth vs. founder-driven) — founder-driven lead sourcing is riskier because the founder won't stick around forever post-sale, whereas brand-driven lead flow (tons of inbound RFPs) drastically lowers risk. (Neil, 2023-12-02) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Sellers always have an information advantage; buyers must spend real time on diligence specifically to shrink that advantage, not eliminate it. (Eric, 2023-12-02) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Evaluate multiple target agencies before committing — the first one you talk to isn't necessarily the best acquisition; comparing several teaches you what "good" vs. "bad" looks like in the category. (Neil, 2023-12-02) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Require audited financials, or have someone independently review financials, to catch fraud; use good lawyers throughout the process to flag what to watch for. (Neil, 2023-12-02) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Target businesses should retain customers for 4+ years; agencies retaining customers only 1–2 years are "a revolving door" and a red flag. (Neil, 2023-12-02) — agency-entrepreneurship.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> The agency's a people business. The people is really what matters. The people is what's driving the revenue, the people, it's what's reducing the churn.

> It's like when you put a new organ into your body, it might not take, so it might get rejected.

> When you are buying a company, the seller always has the advantage from an information standpoint. So you got to take your time doing the due diligence because you got to figure out how to decrease [it]. They're always going to have an advantage, but you got to decrease that advantage as you talk to them.

> You got to kiss a lot of frogs to find the right one.

> Ideally they should be keeping their customers for more than four years... You don't want someone who's only keeping customers for a year or two. That's like, it's just a revolving door at that point.
