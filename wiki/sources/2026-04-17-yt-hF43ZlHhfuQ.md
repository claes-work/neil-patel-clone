# Major Shift: Why Companies STOPPED Cutting People (Marketing School)

## Summary
This is a long (~17,900-word) **multi-episode compilation** of Marketing School daily episodes, concatenated with multiple ClickFlow/Single Grain sponsor breaks and a "rate, review, subscribe, see you tomorrow" sign-off at the end. It is co-hosted by **Neil Patel and Eric Siu**, with roughly half the talk-time (and most of the futurist/AI-infrastructure material) belonging to Eric. There are no speaker labels in the captions — attribution below is inferred from context anchors (NP Digital / Ubersuggest / AnswerThePublic / the Toronto trip = Neil; ClickFlow & Single Grain ad reads / Single Brain / Nemo Claw / the Chinese-heritage and Nvidia anecdotes = Eric). The opening block — **IKEA's "Billy" AI chatbot** (resolved 57% of L1 support, then IKEA studied the unresolved 43% and spun up a ~€1B interior-design consultancy by *reskilling* rather than cutting staff) plus the **OpenAI-losing-ground / Bret Taylor & Sierra** discussion — is the SAME content separately uploaded as **yt-IOH4fvYHf-I (2026-04-13)**, which the coordinator is deduping INTO this page. Beyond that opener, the compilation covers: the "companies stopped cutting people"/hiring-rebound thesis (tractor + ATM historical analogies, Keynes' "technological unemployment," Jevons paradox); **API pricing as an ad-auction** in an agent-first future; the future of ads inside LLMs (on-platform/in-app conversions, agentic commerce, hyper-personalized dynamic ads); who wins enterprise AI (Neil: Google/Microsoft & Gemini long-term, not Claude); AI cost discipline (Ferrari-vs-Honda model, "caveman" prompting); the four levels of AI-marketing maturity; China-vs-US AI sentiment; GitHub-as-marketing; NP Digital's co-branded events playbook; an Amex-vs-Ramp card switch; a "fake doctors" GLP-1 affiliate scandal; podcast-ad economics (Acquired FM's $4.7M midroll, OpenAI's $200M TBPN buy); and a closing 7-AI-tools list.

Durable **Neil** positions worth attention on a synthesis pass: (1) the two paid-ads levers are **creative** (incl. landing pages/messaging) and **first-party data**; (2) treat on-platform/in-LLM conversions as fine — "money's money," don't force the website visit; (3) **don't overuse shiny AI tools** — apply AI to the boring thing already driving ROI (his affiliate-scaling example); (4) **the money isn't in running media companies** — take the leads/eyeballs via co-branded partners, not ownership; (5) reskill-don't-cut and short-term-doomer/long-term-optimist on AI employment. ★ **L3-candidate** — dense, on-domain, multiple reusable Neil frameworks.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- IKEA's "Billy" chatbot resolved ~57% of L1 support inquiries; the smart move was studying the unresolved 43%, which revealed demand for interior-design help — IKEA reskilled support staff into an AI-powered design consultancy generating ~€1B in year-one revenue ("automation + augmentation = exponential growth"). (Eric, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing. *[duplicate-segment from yt-IOH4fvYHf-I]*
- Before cutting 40–50% of a team (which he's "hearing over and over" in meetings), ask what *other* problems your business's existing labor could solve — a business is a problem-solving machine; cutting is a last resort. (Eric, 2026-04-17) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- A Hims/Hers-style telehealth company he met in Canada used AI to produce ~4,500 creatives/month and scaled from ~$1.5M/month (built over years) to $20M+ monthly revenue in ~3 months. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.
- In paid ads the two main levers over competitors are **creative** (bucketed with landing pages, flow, and messaging — not just images) and **first-party data** (more/better upload data = better targeting; a business that can upload 200,000 customer records and flag its best 10,000 ICPs beats a data-poor new entrant). (Neil, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.
- Creative — not campaign structure or bid management — is now the limiting factor in paid ads. (Neil + Eric agree, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.
- Bidding on your own brand name is often wasteful for a large trademarked company that already controls the organic listing; only bid when competitors bid on your name. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.
- API pricing will start to look like ad auctions in an agent-first future: when many agents call an API at high frequency, providers may auction access ("pay more and we won't rate-limit you"). (Eric, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- The future of ads inside LLMs: platforms will copy Instagram/TikTok and keep users on-platform for the whole transaction (in-app / in-LLM conversions), aided by agentic-commerce rails (Stripe/Shopify/Google "Agentic Commerce Protocol"). (Neil, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.
- If on-platform conversions run 15–20% higher and cheaper, brands should let users sign up inside the LLM rather than forcing a website visit — the platform passes the brand all the needed data anyway. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.
- Ads will become hyper-personalized and dynamically generated (like YouTube TV serving different ads to different CNBC viewers), with on-the-fly copy/video tailored per person to lift conversions. (Neil + Eric, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.
- Enterprise AI long-term (5–10 yr) winners will be Google and Microsoft (Gemini + Microsoft), not Claude; Claude/Anthropic will take a big enterprise bite first but MS/Google catch up fast by bundling AI free into existing corporate suites. Consumer will split by generation — younger users lean Gemini over ChatGPT. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- Counter-view: pick Google over Microsoft (Google owns models, TPUs, data — the full stack) and Anthropic over OpenAI on execution; Anthropic has surpassed OpenAI's revenue and holds current mindshare/momentum. (Eric, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- If you can't win mindshare in marketing, you can win on price — half or a third the price makes many buyers switch (framed around OpenAI cutting price / integrating with Claude Code to "be the pipes"). (Neil, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- OpenAI's $200M acquisition of the ~18-month-old TBPN podcast (which did ~$5M in 2025 ads, ~$30M run-rate, 11 employees) is likely a waste of money and will change who wants to appear on it; owning media properties is usually worse than simply having distribution/eyeballs (a16z/Andreessen "own the media" thesis). (Eric raises; Neil skeptical, 2026-04-17) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- The money isn't in running media companies/conferences: NP Digital ran a co-branded Toronto event ("Full Circle") via conference operator Social Next — Social Next handles logistics and P&L, NP Digital gets the co-branded eyeballs and the leads. Roll the model out across coasts/countries without owning the conference. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- One-person / tiny-team billion-dollar companies are coming (AI writes the code, copy, and creative); the real wedge is aggressive AI-driven ads — but "aggressive" means aggressive with AI scale, NOT false claims (which get you sued). (Eric argues; Neil clarifies/qualifies, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- Creating 800 fake doctor Facebook profiles to run GLP-1 telehealth ads is unethical and will get shut down — "when you can make money in marketing that fast, something's wrong." (Neil, 2026-04-17) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- AI job displacement follows the tractor (US farm labor 41%→2%, 1900–1970) and the ATM (feared job-killer, then more branches opened): short-term fear, long-term employment growth. Short-term doomer, long-term optimist. (Eric presents; Neil agrees, cites Marc Andreessen's AI-boom thesis, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- AI token/compute costs will skyrocket (Jevons paradox — cheaper intelligence gets used far more), possibly rivaling headcount cost; hence on-prem hardware (Nvidia DGX Spark) and renting infra (CoreWeave). (Eric, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- Don't blindly reduce AI spend just because prices fall — but DO cut *wasteful* usage: much AI output is generated and then never used, or was never needed; for big companies this waste runs to millions/year. Go through spend line-by-line. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- AI cost mix should be ~15% frontier "Ferrari" models (for real thinking/coding) and ~85% cheap "Honda Accord" models (basic queries); route with tools like OpenRouter; telling the model to "speak like a caveman" can cut cost ~50–70%. (Eric, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- Four levels of AI-marketing maturity (from Anthropic's solo growth marketer): L1 automate what you already do (reporting/copy/data pulls); L2 use AI as a thinking partner where it's better than you; L3 do work that was below the ROI threshold before (negative-keyword mining, daily broken-link checks, competitor monitoring); L4 build custom tools only you would build around your own data/workflows/edge cases. (Eric presents, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- For L1 automation, companies won't use raw LLMs — they'll buy cheap ($10–15/mo) point-solution software or run cost-effective Chinese/open models (Qwen, Kimi, Google's Gemma) rather than maintain internal AI. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- Chinese public AI sentiment is ~80% optimistic vs ~35% in the US; in China people line up in parks to learn agent tools while the US narrative fixates on job cuts. (Eric, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- Some customers explicitly want human-generated content, not AI — Neil doesn't want AI content for his own business either; there's a viable market for human-only content. (Neil, 2026-04-17) — content-marketing.
- GitHub is becoming a marketing channel: publish free skill repos to drive leads and see what companies do internally. (Eric — his "AI Marketing Skills" repo at single.com/skills, ~1,700 stars; Neil agrees, 2026-04-17) — content-marketing.
- Don't let shiny AI tools pull you off the boring thing that drives ROI: a large company earning ~43–44% of new revenue from affiliates but staffing the affiliate team with only 2 people should point AI at *scaling affiliates* (more creatives, outreach, follow-up), not voice/content toys — "AI theater." (Neil, 2026-04-17) — ai-marketing.
- Everything you build with AI must tie to solving customer problems / hit a target ROI (Eric keeps a skill in his agent that pushes back when work isn't tied to goals or a 4:1 ROI). (Eric, 2026-04-17) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Recording wearables (AI pins, Meta glasses) make conversations awkward and risk out-of-context clips or voice cloning; Neil dislikes being recorded and only uses Meta glasses as a tennis coach (feeding footage to Gemini). (Neil, 2026-04-17) — (general).
- Podcast-ad economics are surging: Acquired FM prices a midroll at $4.7M (sold out through 2029; presenting sponsorship $5.6M) because the audience is entrepreneurial decision-makers — Neil regrets not buying such ads early. (Eric presents; Neil agrees, 2026-04-17) — paid-ads.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> when I always talk to companies about paid ads, I always tell them there's two main levers you have over the competition... One is creative. ... your creative is not just about the images. It's also about the messaging... The second thing is your first party data. What you upload to the system drastically impacts the output. If you have more data than the competition, it can help learn way better and figure out what people to target.

> If you're a bank, do you really care if they visit your website to sign up or they just sign up right there and then on the LLM? Because the LLM will pass you all the data... Who cares? Let them sign up wherever. ... money's money.

> We don't care for the profit because that's not our core business. I don't think the money is in running media companies.

> We just want the lead to make more money to be honest. ... Because we believe our business model is better than running a conference.

> I don't think Claude is going to be the winner. I actually think... it'll be Google or Microsoft in the long run... within five years... Gemini and Microsoft are going to be the leaders within enterprise.

> you should not avoid testing out AI. You don't want to be spending all day using tools and instead of focusing on what is driving you your ROI and letting your eye off the ball.

> instead of using all these cool AI tools like for voice or content creation or whatnot, why don't you focus on using AI tools to help you scale out your affiliate program from more creatives to more outreach to... maintaining relationships.

> Typically when you can make money in marketing that fast something's wrong. ... me personally, I wouldn't want to do something unethical like create fake doctor profile.

> even my own company I don't want AI content for my own business.
