# Everything Marketers Are Panicking About Right Now (Marketing School)

## Summary
Co-hosted Marketing School episode built around the "AEO panic" — the anxiety CMOs feel about AI answer engines (AEO/GEO) displacing SEO, framed off Eli Schwartz's tweet that "the AEO panic is the best thing to happen to SEO budgets in years... real SEO lives inside the product." This is a **long-form P1 landmark** with an unusually high concentration of substantive Neil-persona market intelligence, so the attribution split matters: the strongest, freshest content is Neil's, and it is genuinely new (dated 2026 GEO/AEO market data, plus a management doctrine).

**Neil's persona-relevant contribution** clusters in four areas: (1) a concrete, dated read on the AEO/GEO market from NP Digital data — SEO budgets still ~11x GEO budgets, GEO spend is enterprise-driven, local search stays on traditional Google, and it is still very hard to get $1M+ GEO budgets because of the ROI-tracking problem; (2) the "Profound misconception" argument — that AEO is NOT "buy a tracking tool and publish AI listicles," backed by Writesonic-founder data that ChatGPT does `site:` self-lookups only ~20% of the time at peak (so ~80% of the time it reads *other* sites); (3) an agency-business block — inflated AI-agency valuations (30x+ profit) won't last, and there's an M&A arbitrage in buying stable-but-unadapted agencies for ~2x profit; and (4) a management doctrine — "we don't have a money problem, you have a budget problem," Neil's approach to reports he actually uses (bank balance, RFP counts), his hatred of "dashboarditis," and his AI-abundance thesis (net more jobs, from his debate with Search Atlas's founder).

**Eric Siu's material** (co-host, ~half the talk time) drives the AI-native/agent thread: the "forward-deployed marketer" concept, AI sovereignty / open-weights (GLM, OpenRouter, self-hosted Nvidia), the Alex Karp / Palantir "thought-leadership marketing" segment, his "command by negation" directive, a WoW-inspired gamified-company video game, the "skills dojo" launch, and news-reaction charts (Financial Times headcount, AI-economy revenue, Jason Lemkin sales quotas). All quarantined below.

Because Neil introduces genuinely new dated positions (the 11x GEO read; "budget problem not money problem"; the Profound/AEO argument), this is flagged **★ L3-candidate**.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- SEO under product is real for tech/engineering-led companies (the startups that became unicorns), but NOT for traditional businesses like Procter & Gamble or Unilever — NP Digital has never seen it there. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — seo.
- Current NP Digital read: leads are down like everyone's, but lead *quality* is higher; they're NOT yet seeing SEO budgets shift, they're seeing AEO budgets increase but not become massive — because every enterprise, most SMBs and mid-market ask the same thing: track the ROI. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — seo / analytics-cro.
- AI-search conversions are hard to attribute: when people search on ChatGPT or Gemini they usually don't click through — they go direct to the site or Google the brand name — so conversion is AI-assisted/AI-driven but tracking is poor (SEO tracking was never perfect either). (Neil, 2026-07-06) — analytics-cro.
- Pitch shift: pitches that were pure-GEO are now much more likely to LOSE if they don't fold in technical SEO — NP Digital sees it in their data; customers are as confused about GEO as they were about SEO 15-20 years ago. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — seo.
- The "Profound misconception": customers want to "just pay a tool" (~$1k/mo or ~$50k/yr) and have it publish AI listicles — but if everyone can publish listicles, it doesn't work; it's the same fallacy as old-school "put keywords on the page and rank." (Neil, 2026-07-06) — seo / ai-marketing.
- Data point (via Writesonic founder "Sam"): ChatGPT's `site:` self-lookup behavior peaked around ~20% and goes in waves — meaning ~80% of the time the model reads *other people's* content to decide who gets mentioned for a prompt/region. So AEO is won on the whole web, not just your own listicles. (Neil relaying Sam's data, 2026-07-06) — seo / ai-marketing.
- NP Digital lead analysis: SEO budgets are still ~11x higher than GEO budgets (Neil guessed GEO would be higher; the data was the opposite). Local search still happens mainly on traditional Google, not ChatGPT; the aggressive LLM-optimizers tend to be enterprises (big, consistent, understood SEO budgets). (Neil, 2026-07-06) — seo / analytics-cro.
- GEO budget ceiling: the pushback isn't whether to do GEO (everyone accepts LLM usage) — it's ROI. You can get $50k-$300k, but getting LLM/GEO budgets above $1M is very hard right now; Neil expects that to change in a few years. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — seo / agency-entrepreneurship.
- Omni-channel thesis: what's said about you across the whole web now matters more than what's on your own site (which makes it harder to manipulate long-term); companies that focus on one or two channels will lose to omni-channel (even multi-channel beats one-or-two). (attributed Neil — see notes, 2026-07-06) — content-marketing.
- Tool-tracking is commoditizing: NP Digital sees customers of all sizes no longer wanting to pay the old prices for LLM tracking because many players now offer the feature (some free). (Neil, 2026-07-06) — analytics-cro.
- Agency valuations: AI-centric agencies trading at 30x+ profit won't hold — Neil doesn't believe a service business sustains that; traditional non-adapted agencies are getting valuations cut. The overlooked play: buy a stable low-margin agency whose growth stalled on AI for ~2x profit up front + an earnout they may miss, net ~3x, effective ~1.5x EBITDA after you fix it — dirt cheap, arbitrageable with bank money. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- AI = abundance, not net job loss (from Neil's debate with Search Atlas's founder): NP Digital works with hundreds-to-thousands of companies globally and sees essentially no AI-driven headcount reductions — the cuts they see are from firms that over-hired. Efficiency gains make companies want MORE (SMBs now hire more engineers because they can do things that used to need a $1-2M budget). (Neil, 2026-07-06) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
- In marketing specifically: efficiency doesn't mean "fire people" — companies expect you to do more or charge less, and they buy more strategy / human-element work while AI handles execution. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- AI-as-growth-engine example (HighLevel): after NP Digital partnered with them they went from ~2,000 to ~3,000 employees in roughly six months — not by cutting, but by using AI (e.g. voice AI so SMBs don't have to manually follow up on every lead) to make a better product that drives more demand → more hiring. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — ai-marketing.
- The real AI opportunity in orgs isn't "get more done" (table stakes) — it's doing genuinely new things you couldn't do before that give you a competitive edge in marketing. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — ai-marketing.
- "We don't have a money problem, you have a budget problem" doctrine: when regions blame "not in budget" for leads being down, Neil (who sits in no budget meetings) approves lump-sum spend for events/PR/GEO — but expenses it against the region's *future*-year revenue and ties it to bonuses so they own the risk. Inspired by Jamie Dimon ("we don't have a money problem"); for a profitable/bootstrapped company, if it produces ROI you make the case, you don't hide behind a number. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Reports Neil actually uses (anti-"dashboarditis"): daily bank balance by division with notes on why it moved ("live and die by your cash"), RFP counts by region/country over time (win/lost/stalled flags so he knows which big logos to push), a feed of big prospects + their location (so he can travel to close them), new-product-launch alerts (WhatsApp), and a weekly email-performance report where he reviews next week's emails before they send. He hates dashboards — he wants specific, actionable things. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — analytics-cro / agency-entrepreneurship.
- Enabling employees to take risk: entrepreneurs are higher risk-takers than hired guns (who balance you out); the way to get them to move is to remove the short-term expense burden — "I'll give you the money now, we deal with the cost later." (Neil, 2026-07-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- On revenge / a former employee who stole leads and clients: the human urge is to destroy them, but the right move is to move on — running a business is hard enough, and 50% of your attention on revenge is 50% less on your business. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Product-marketing critique (Snap Spectacles): Snap lost ~$1B in market cap because Evan Spiegel showcased "the future" of Spectacles instead of explaining what the product does and why it costs multiples more than Meta Ray-Bans; he should have covered why-they're-investing + what-it-does-now + the roadmap, like Apple does. A founder's talk should stay related to the business. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — content-marketing.
- On the Alex Karp / thought-leadership point: Neil pushes back that Karp actually does mention Palantir on nearly every CNBC appearance (Neil has been on CNBC a lot) — it's just not the majority of the talk track — and notes what Karp discusses is still related to what Palantir does. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — content-marketing.
- Regional culture shapes marketing delivery (via his LatAm MD "Javi"): in Latin America, relationships/wining-and-dining can retain a contract even with mediocre work — a Samsung client once praised Javi mainly for delivering *on time* — whereas the US is transactional/ROI-driven. Neil's stance: he'd still fire employees producing mediocre work regardless of the relationship. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Data-protection instinct: after the David Sacks post, Neil messaged his team to "be careful what we're sharing with OpenAI and Anthropic"; he expects trusted clouds (AWS/Google/Microsoft, where marketing data already lives) to offer solutions that make decisions from your data without training on it. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — ai-marketing.
- On Jason Lemkin's rising sales-quota data: quotas are up partly because SaaS moved to all-in-one (does more, charges more) and yesterday's mid-market logos are today's enterprise (bigger budgets); it's not purely AI efficiency — it's efficiency + growth + more competitive spend. (Neil, 2026-07-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
