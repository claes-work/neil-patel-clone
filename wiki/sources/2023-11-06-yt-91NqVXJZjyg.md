# How Grimes Is Shaping The Future of AI Generated Content

## Summary
Reacting to Grimes' public stance allowing AI-generated music made with her voice/
style in exchange for a 50% revenue split, Neil argues the right response to AI
likeness-generation depends on an artist's stage of career: for up-and-coming or
lower-income artists, revenue-sharing makes sense; for major established
celebrities, brand-quality control matters more than incremental revenue, since
low-quality AI output can tarnish long-term brand value (analogy: actors who
"crank out" low-quality films eventually losing audience trust regardless of past
prestige).

## Key claims (dated, paraphrase unless noted)
- Frames the AI-licensing decision as stage-dependent: an up-and-coming, less
  wealthy artist has clear incentive to take a revenue share (e.g., Grimes' 50%
  offer); a already-wealthy, brand-established celebrity (his examples: Drake, The
  Weeknd, Tom Cruise) should weigh brand/quality risk more heavily than the
  incremental money. (Neil, 2023-11-06) — ai-marketing / content-marketing.
- Argues low-quality AI-generated content bearing a celebrity's name/likeness can
  tarnish future revenue potential by association, drawing an analogy to actors who
  shifted to churning out low-quality films for a paycheck and lost audience
  goodwill even with a previously strong reputation. (Neil, 2023-11-06) —
  ai-marketing.
- Proposes a practical filter: some quality/brand-fit test (potentially
  algorithmic) should gate whether AI-generated content using a public figure's
  likeness is allowed to stay up and be revenue-shared, versus taken down; ties
  this to a broader belief that in the long run it should be up to each individual
  artist/creator whether AI content using their name, voice, face, or likeness
  stays up, since it is their brand. (Neil, 2023-11-06) — ai-marketing.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> If you're an artist and you're not making that much money, sure, why wouldn't you
> want 50%? But on the flip side... if I was a really, really popular celebrity...
> it's not just about the money, it's about the quality.

> I believe it is going to hurt my future revenue potential because people will
> associate my name with crap then.

> If it's high quality stuff, if it passes a test... this makes my brand look good,
> then go ahead, let's do a 50/50. But if it doesn't pass certain criteria that I'm
> looking for, then that's a no go. And you need to take it down.
