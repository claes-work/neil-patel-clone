# How to Rank on Google's First Page for Competitive Terms (Q&A Thursday)

## Summary

A Q&A Thursday episode co-hosted with **Adam [surname garbled in captions]**, answering a viewer (a London, Ontario mortgage broker) who followed Neil's SEO advice and climbed from rank 87 to 11 for his keyword but is now stuck outside page one. Instead of repeating link-building/content/title-tag basics, Neil teaches the user-metrics angle most people miss: rankings get harder to move the closer you get to page one, because Google increasingly weights user behavior — click-through rate, whether users stay without bouncing back to search, and whether they stop searching for related queries (a signal your page solved their problem). His office joke: "the perfect place to bury a dead body is page two of Google" (no one looks there), which is why page-one placement is guarded — Google is pickier there because that's where most clicks are and bad results would drive users away. The prescription: provide more value so users stick and don't pogo-stick back, then write a more appealing, curiosity-driving title tag (like YouTube titles) using the keywords that Search Console shows are already driving impressions/clicks. As real users prefer your listing over neighbors', Google gradually promotes you (11 → 10 → 9…). Adam asks whether running PPC helps organic rankings; Neil answers definitively no — Google gives no ranking benefit from paid search.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-11-29, Neil said ranking gets progressively harder as you climb: going from the top-thousand to top-hundred is easy, but moving from ~80 to page one takes longer and longer.
- As of 2018-11-29, Neil said Google weighs user metrics beyond backlinks and social shares — how many people click your listing, stay without bouncing back, and stop doing related searches (signaling your page solved their problem).
- As of 2018-11-29, Neil said a searcher is looking for a solution to their problem, so even a product/service page must be framed as the solution, not just a sale.
- As of 2018-11-29, Neil said "slow and steady wins the race" in SEO: Google gradually raises your rank if you earn more clicks than the listings around you, and page one is guarded because it holds the majority of clicks and bad results would drive users off Google.
- As of 2018-11-29, Neil said running paid search (PPC/pay-per-click) does NOT help your organic ranking — Google gives no ranking indicator for it.
- As of 2018-11-29, Neil said to use Google Search Console to find the keywords driving your clicks/impressions, then write a more appealing, curiosity-evoking title tag (like YouTube titles) around them — keyword-stuffed or boring titles won't earn clicks; an overly long appealing line can be repurposed as the meta description.
- As of 2018-11-29, Neil said that when enough real users prefer your lower listing over a higher one, Google eventually swaps the positions, and you can verify the effect in Search Console by watching CTR over the ~30 days after changing your title/meta.
