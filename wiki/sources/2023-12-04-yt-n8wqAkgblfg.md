# How Terrible Policies Will Kill Your Business

## Summary
A short, tightly-scoped co-hosted episode built around a real complaint Eric Siu brings to the table: a travel-deals subscription company (Dollar Flight Club) charged him for a renewal year he never used, and refused to refund despite his usage data showing zero use, offering instead a free extra year of access he didn't want. Eric asks Neil directly whether he should get the money back; Neil's answer is unambiguous and becomes the episode's persona-relevant core: yes, refund, and if a company won't, do a chargeback. Neil's stated general philosophy is to refund customers on small-dollar complaints (his examples: $99, $50, $10) even when the customer is technically wrong or already used the product, because the revenue protected by refusing a small refund is outweighed by the brand damage and negative word-of-mouth from a bad experience — "penny-wise, pound-foolish." He cites a Warren Buffett annual-letter framing (paraphrased, not verbatim-sourced) that protecting the brand should outrank short-term revenue gains that could hurt it.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- If a company charges a customer for a service year they demonstrably didn't use, the customer should get a refund; if the company refuses, escalate to a chargeback. (Neil, 2023-12-04) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- General refund philosophy: for small dollar amounts (his examples: $99, $50, $10) you should just refund the customer — even if they did use the product/service — because the alternative (a customer with a bad experience who badmouths the brand) costs more in the long run than the refund itself. (Neil, 2023-12-04) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- "Penny-wise, pound-foolish" is how Neil characterizes companies that chase small short-term revenue via restrictive refund policies at the cost of brand reputation; he references (paraphrased) a Warren Buffett annual-letter point that protecting the brand should outweigh short-term revenue when the two conflict. (Neil, 2023-12-04) — agency-entrepreneurship. *(Buffett attribution is Neil's own paraphrase/recollection, not a verified quote — flag as self-reported recollection.)*
- Owning up to mistakes matters more than getting the product/service 100% right the first time, since no company ever will. (Eric, 2023-12-04) — agency-entrepreneurship.
