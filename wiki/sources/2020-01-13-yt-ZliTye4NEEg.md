# I Spent $3 Million on this Marketing Tactic — Here's What I've Learned ★

## Summary
Solo video in which Neil discloses the cost side of the Ubersuggest bet: he says he has
spent **over $3 million** on the tool, has **not recouped the money yet**, and still calls
it one of the best investments he ever made and "by far" the thing that has driven his
results in the last 12 months. He has been at it "almost three years" (i.e. since the
2017 acquisition) but concentrated the spend in the most recent year. Five lessons: (1)
**Capitalize on existing market demand** — he did not invent Ubersuggest, he bought it;
go after a big **TAM** rather than inventing a new category, and use Google Trends plus
CodeCanyon to see what already has demand. His counter-example is his own **A/B testing
calculator**: people love it, but almost nobody cares about A/B testing calculators,
whereas **$100B+/yr is spent on Google ads**, so an SEO/ads tool sits in a huge market.
(2) **Free wins market share** — competitors' tools do what Ubersuggest does and some do
parts better, but giving away for free a combination of features others charge for grew
Ubersuggest to **~3M visits/month, 15M+ page views, ~740K unique monthly visitors**, far
ahead of SEO competitors on traffic: "people would rather have free over paid" even when
the free product is worse. (3) **It is expensive to build something amazing** — **server
cost alone is over $1M/year**; he explicitly does **not** recommend copying his approach
and says the biggest mistake was going big before traction: buy a ~$10 CodeCanyon tool,
release several small tools in your segment, and only pay for developers/designers once
leads or sales show up. (4) **Focus on what's working** — he keeps the app usable by
deleting features nobody uses (a top-100-rankings report used by "not even 1%" was cut),
and he obsesses about users, not competitors: he names Ahrefs and SEMrush people as
"amazing" but says he does not watch them. (5) **Usability over features** — if you need
explanations all over the product you are doing it wrong; he quotes **Leonardo da Vinci,
"simplicity is the ultimate sophistication,"** and says the principles apply to any
business, not just tools.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said he had spent **over $3 million** on building Ubersuggest, had **not yet recouped** it, and still considered it one of his best investments ever and the single tactic that drove his results in the prior 12 months.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said he had been running Ubersuggest for "almost three years" but spent the majority of the money in the last 12 months.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said he **did not invent Ubersuggest — he bought it** — and framed the lesson as capitalizing on existing market demand rather than creating something never done before.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil defined the target as a big **TAM** (he glosses it on-camera as "Total Adjustable Market" — the standard term is *addressable*), and recommended Google Trends + CodeCanyon to find what already has demand in your sector.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil used his own **A/B testing calculator** as the anti-example: the tool is good and users like it, but the TAM is tiny — "not too many people care about an AB testing calculator" — versus **over $100 billion a year spent on Google ads**.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said Ubersuggest gets roughly **3 million visitors a month, over 15 million page views, and roughly 740,000 unique monthly visitors**, far more traffic than SEO-space competitors, and attributed that gap to being free.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil's stated rule was that **free beats paid even when the free product is worse** than the competition.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said Ubersuggest's **server cost alone is over $1 million a year** and called building at that scale a mistake he would not repeat.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil's recommended alternative was to start small: buy a ~**$10 tool on CodeCanyon**, release **more small tools within your segment**, make up the traffic that way, and only spend on developers/designers after traction and indirect monetization (leads or sales).
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil disclosed **no affiliation with CodeCanyon** ("I don't even know who owns them").
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said he keeps Ubersuggest usable by **deleting features nobody uses** — he removed a report showing who ranks in the top 100 because "not even 1%" used it and the data existed elsewhere in the product.
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said he does **not obsess about competitors** (naming SEMrush and Ahrefs and their people positively) but obsesses about users — "that's how you win."
- As of 2020-01-13, Neil said users want usability, not just features or a free price: needing explanations everywhere in a product or service means you are doing something wrong; keep it simple enough for a five-year-old.
