# Escaping YouTube Jail: Tips and Tricks You Need to Know (Ep. 2602)

## Summary
A short co-hosted episode debating whether "YouTube jail" — a channel getting almost no views for months before suddenly breaking out — is a real thing. Eric frames the episode with two anecdotes (Patrick Campbell and YouTuber George Gammon) supporting the idea that YouTube deliberately holds channels back until the algorithm learns what they are about and trusts they will be consistent, using the analogy that "a YouTuber is an employee of YouTube." Neil's angle pushes back on the "jail" framing: he doesn't see a jail, he sees reputation/authority accrual (an EEAT-style trust build-up) plus engagement quality, and warns that inconsistency in topic is what kills reach. Both agree the practical takeaways are the same: be consistent in topic and cadence, produce content that provides value on par with competitors, and expect a lag before recognition — with volume/effort able to shorten the lag (Gammon at 80-hour weeks broke out in 3-4 months; Campbell spread it over ~a year). (2023-11-13)

## Key claims
- Neil doesn't believe there is literally a "YouTube jail"; he reframes it as building up reputation/authority over time — if you create many good videos, the algorithm "gives you more love." (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil draws an explicit parallel to Google's EEAT (experience/expertise/authoritativeness/trust): YouTube has a different algorithm, but he bets it similarly takes time to show the platform you have "their version of it," after which performance improves. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil: inconsistency in topic is what tanks reach. He used to get 20,000-50,000 views per video in the marketing niche, and performance dropped the moment he became inconsistent and started talking about other things. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil endorses the "employee of YouTube" analogy: if you hire someone and they start doing other things when you need one thing, you'll fire them — or at least take time to re-evaluate them at the new thing. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil: YouTube also wants good engagement metrics and penalizes low-quality ("crap") content. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil: YouTube was much easier to do well on 4-6 years ago; like most social platforms, once they attract a lot of people and competition they get pickier and clamp down on reach, which also helps them generate more ad revenue. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil on the Mr Beast counter-example: Mr Beast succeeds with mainstream content that appeals to almost everyone, but that kind of content is much harder to monetize, and people forget it took him a very long time (Eric: ~7 years) to reach 10,000 subscribers. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil: the bottom line is topic consistency, cadence consistency, and whether your video provides value on par with competing videos — do that and you'll eventually be rewarded. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Neil notes his own channel shifted back to long-form podcasting, so it will take time to be recognized again. (Neil, 2023-11-13)
- Eric's framing claim: YouTube "wants to see what you're all about," and the algorithm takes a while to understand your intent; the platform cares about ad revenue and whether you'll be consistent. (Eric, 2023-11-13)
- Eric: on Neil's channel, the longer episodes do "way better," reinforcing that engagement metrics like average view duration matter. (Eric, 2023-11-13)
