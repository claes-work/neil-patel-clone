# How a Bootstrapped Startup Out Marketed My MultiMillion Dollar Company

## Summary
In this short 2017 video, Neil Patel tells the story of how a bootstrapped, lightly-funded competitor out-marketed KISSmetrics, one of the analytics companies he co-created, and argues that a cheaper or free offer beats a better product. He reports that at KISSmetrics he raised somewhere between $15 and $20 million, while the rival, Mixpanel, raised far less money and (in his telling) copied KISSmetrics' exact product designs, released them, and out-marketed the original — yet at one point Mixpanel was worth over $800 million according to TechCrunch, while KISSmetrics "weren't even worth $100 million." He says this happened even though KISSmetrics got more traffic (a better Alexa rank than Mixpanel and a blog pulling over a million unique visitors a month), because Mixpanel went the freemium route: it took the copied product and shipped a free version, reasoning that whether the product was better or worse didn't matter since people are price-sensitive and won't pay thousands for what they can get free. He adds that Mixpanel's free plan required users to display a Mixpanel badge on their website, which many startups accepted, giving Mixpanel free promotion and fueling its growth. Neil generalizes the lesson: to grow fast against large competitors, don't necessarily build a better product — offer more for less, or give away a portion of the product/service for free, and big companies will be forced to acquire you. He cites Intuit's acquisition of Mint.com as the same pattern — Mint wasn't the best service, it just had a really good free one, and the multibillion-dollar incumbent bought it rather than lose billions in market share. His closing advice is to release something free or cheaper than the competition and gradually take their market share.

- KISSmetrics, a company Neil co-created (he no longer works there), raised roughly $15–20 million (self-reported, as of 2017-06-09).
- Competitor Mixpanel raised far less money, and its product initially had little innovation (as of 2017-06-09).
- Mixpanel copied KISSmetrics' exact product designs, released them, and out-marketed KISSmetrics (as of 2017-06-09).
- At one point Mixpanel was worth over $800 million according to TechCrunch, while KISSmetrics was not even worth $100 million (self-reported / cites TechCrunch, as of 2017-06-09).
- KISSmetrics had a better Alexa rank than Mixpanel and its blog got over a million unique visitors a month, i.e. more traffic than the competitor (self-reported, as of 2017-06-09).
- Mixpanel won by going freemium — releasing a free version of the (copied) product regardless of whether it was better or worse (as of 2017-06-09).
- Nothing beats a cheaper price; if you can create an exact replica of a paid product/service and release a free version, even without all the features, you will crush competitors, because people are price-sensitive (as of 2017-06-09, Neil's opinion).
- Mixpanel required free-plan users to display a Mixpanel badge on their site; many startups agreed, and that badge-driven promotion fueled Mixpanel's growth (as of 2017-06-09).
- To grow faster than big multimillion-dollar competitors, you don't need a better product — offer more for less money, or give a portion of your product/service away free (as of 2017-06-09, Neil's advice).
- If you grow fast this way, big companies have no choice but to buy you (as of 2017-06-09, Neil's opinion).
- Intuit bought Mint.com for the same reason — Mint wasn't the best service, it just had a really good free service, so the multibillion-dollar incumbent acquired it (for a few hundred million dollars) rather than lose billions in market share (as of 2017-06-09).
- Releasing something free or cheaper than the competition will, over time, let you gain on competitors' market share (as of 2017-06-09, Neil's advice).
