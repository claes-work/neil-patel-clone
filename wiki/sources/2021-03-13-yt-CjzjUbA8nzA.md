# How to Write Blog Posts That Consistently Rank Well on Google

## Summary
Solo tactical content-strategy video: pick popular competitors (not small/low-traffic
sites) and run their domains through Ubersuggest's traffic-overview + "top pages"
report, filtering to their top ~20 non-homepage content pages by estimated traffic.
For each page, review the ranking keywords (via the "View All" estimated-visits
breakdown) and the page itself, then write a more detailed/better version of the same
topic — avoiding duplicating overlapping topics across multiple competitors (so a
target list of ~200 candidate topics typically collapses to ~150 unique ones after
removing overlap). Recommends doing this across ~10 competitors. For extra link
equity, identifies who links to the competitor's top pages (via the backlinks "View
All") and personally emails those linking sites' authors, referencing the specific
article they already linked to and offering the new, more detailed version as an
alternative. States a rough math example: writing 200 articles at ~3 replies/links
each yields ~600 total links. Frames search-driven traffic as more durable/compounding
than social traffic, which spikes on publish day and decays afterward, arguing this is
why the competitor-gap-plus-outreach approach compounds over time even without new
content each month.

## Key claims
- Describes a systematic content-gap workflow: harvest ~20 top pages per competitor
  across ~10 competitors via Ubersuggest, de-duplicate overlapping topics down to a
  target list (~200 → ~150 in his example), and write superior versions of each
  (paraphrase — a more systematized version of the existing competitor-content-gap
  doctrine already in the wiki; the specific 200→150 de-dup math and the ~3-links-
  per-200-articles-written-plus-outreach math are new framings).
- Frames organic/search traffic as compounding and durable versus social traffic,
  which spikes at publish and decays (paraphrase; consistent with prior SEO-vs-social
  durability claims already in the wiki, not new).
