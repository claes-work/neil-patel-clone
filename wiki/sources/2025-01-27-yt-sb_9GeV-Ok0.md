# How Going Viral on Social Media Hurt Oreo (Marketing School)

## Summary

A short, light co-hosted Marketing School episode on whether virality actually helps a
brand. The main hook: Oreo has gone viral across the social web via a "blowtorch
experiment" — people line up Oreos and torch them for increasing durations (1s, 2s, 5s,
10s, 20s, 30s, even a minute) behind cardboard, and find the cookie barely burns or
chars even at 30 seconds while the wood behind it burns up. The takeaway the hosts push:
this virality is *bad* for Oreo, because it makes people question the ingredients ("this
thing won't even flame up — should I be putting it in my body?"), and one host predicts
it will hurt Oreo's sales. The trend is described as billions of views, spreading in
multiple languages, with people creating their own copycat experiments and sharing to
communities like the "seed oil Scout" app; the hosts note Oreo can't sue anyone because
it's just reality, and the lesson framed is to put clean ingredients in a product. After
Eric's Single Grain sponsor read, the second half pivots to a contrasting positive
case: Coca-Cola's growth story, framed as going from a ~$4B company (1981) to ~$150B
(1997) by asking "what percentage of the entire *liquid* market do we have?" — the
answer being a paltry ~2% (versus ~45% of the soft-drink market), which pushed them to
redefine their market beyond soft drinks into the whole beverage category (water, sports
drinks, energy drinks, etc.) via invention and acquisition. The episode closes on the
Marketing School agency-owners plug.

## Key claims

- This virality is *hurting* Oreo, not helping: it makes viewers question the ingredients ("should I even be putting it in my body?"), and the prediction is it will hurt Oreo's sales. (Neil — inferred, 2025-01-27)
