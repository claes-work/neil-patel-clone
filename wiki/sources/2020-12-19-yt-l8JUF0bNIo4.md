# How to Grow Your SEO Traffic by Updating Your Old Content

## Summary
Neil gives a step-by-step Google Search Console workflow for reviving traffic-losing
old content instead of only publishing new posts, opening with the "over a billion
blogs, ~7 billion people" saturation framing and Google's preference for fresh,
relevant, best-user-experience content over raw link/on-page counts. He cites his own
now-divested site Nutritionsecrets (100,000+ visitors/month built mainly through
continual updates, not new content) and quantifies his current operation: for every
new neilpatel.com blog post, a team of three updates 21 old articles a week. The bulk
of the video is the concrete workflow: GSC → Pages → compare last-28-days
year-over-year → sort to find pages losing traffic → identify which query terms
dropped → Google those terms → analyze what outranking pages do better → add/update/
delete content accordingly (never obsess over word count, judge by user experience) →
keep the URL (301-redirect if changed) → update the publish date → request re-indexing
in GSC. Notes evergreen content (e.g., "how to install a toilet") needs little
updating, unlike time-sensitive content (his own MySpace/Vine example of stale
relevance).

## Key claims
- As of 2020-12-19, restates and quantifies the standing "rank old content" doctrine
  already established in the corpus: his team updates 21 old articles per every 1 new
  blog post on neilpatel.com (paraphrase) — a specific ratio not previously logged.
- Nutritionsecrets (a site Neil "no longer owns") reached 100,000+ monthly visitors
  primarily via continual updating/expanding of old articles rather than new-content
  volume (self-reported case study, paraphrase).
- Concrete diagnostic method: in GSC's Pages report, compare the last 28 days to the
  same 28 days a year earlier; pages where the year-ago number is higher than the
  current number are the update candidates (paraphrase — a specific, actionable
  technique not previously logged in this granularity).
- Update guidance: add what competing top-ranked pages have that yours doesn't, delete
  what's outdated/irrelevant (his MySpace-mention example), don't fixate on word count,
  optimize for user experience (images/video/deletions as appropriate), update the
  publish date, and 301-redirect rather than change the URL (paraphrase).
- Self-reported result claim: following this process, publishers should "see an
  increase in SEO traffic" within about 30 days of Google re-indexing the update
  (paraphrase).
