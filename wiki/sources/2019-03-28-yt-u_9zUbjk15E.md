# Why Your Social Media Content Is Garbage and How to Fix It! (Social Media Marketing Strategy)

## Summary

Neil's six-part fix for low social engagement: (1) post **video content** — every network
(YouTube, LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram, even Twitter) is prioritizing video to compete with
TV; (2) stop chasing perfection — just publish and **test**, do more of what works, kill what
doesn't; (3) leverage **micro-content** — film your life/office/customers/speeches and cut it
into two-to-five-minute clips and images to funnel traffic to a main piece; (4) recognize that
**what works on one network won't work on all** — each algorithm differs, so tailor per
platform instead of cross-posting identical content; (5) **be yourself / leverage your edge** —
authenticity beats imitation. Closes with the standard NP Digital agency plug.

## Key claims

- As of 2019-03-28, Neil said all major social networks (incl. LinkedIn, which had just added a
  live feature) are craving video and pushing it hard to "crush the television networks," so
  posting video earns far more engagement than text.
- As of 2019-03-28, Neil said posting video content gets him "four or five times more
  engagement" than text-based posts on his own channel. [self-reported]
- As of 2019-03-28, Neil advised against perfectionism (makeup, posing) before posting —
  publish, test, and repeat only what performs; stop reposting formats (e.g. quote images) that
  aren't getting traction on a given network.
- As of 2019-03-28, Neil's **micro-content** method: capture raw footage of your day/customers/
  products/speeches, then cut it into bite-sized clips and images to drive traffic to a main
  piece — running ~10 small pieces gives "10 shots" at a hit vs. betting on one big piece.
- As of 2019-03-28, Neil said content is not portable across networks — e.g. quote images can do
  well on Instagram but flop on Facebook — because each algorithm is different; figure out what
  works per network.
- As of 2019-03-28, Neil urged creators to lean into their authentic "edge" (he calls himself
  "dorky and nerdy," releasing informative technical content rather than posing with models)
  instead of acting a part for the algorithm.
