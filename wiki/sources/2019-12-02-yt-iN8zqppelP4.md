# Wix vs Wordpress vs Squarespace — Which One is The Best For SEO

## Summary
In this solo talking-head video, Neil Patel compares WordPress, Wix, and Squarespace for SEO and concludes he prefers WordPress for its customization, while cautioning that platform-level traffic studies are inconclusive because they don't control for the operator's SEO skill. He opens with market-share and traffic stats (WordPress powers >50% of the web; a study showing far more WordPress domains get organic traffic than Wix), then breaks down each platform's pros/cons: WordPress — best for the SEO-savvy, huge plugin ecosystem (Yoast, All in One SEO, broken link checker), easy support, mobile-optimized templates, but too many plugins can slow or break the site and deep customization needs some code; Wix — great if SEO isn't your priority and you want simple drag-and-drop, includes an SEO wizard and AMP pages, but limited metadata/title/URL customization, slower indexing (heavy AJAX), and rigid architecture; Squarespace — good for small simple sites, pretty designs, custom title tags/metadata and XML sitemaps (but you can't edit them), yet slow/challenging indexing, confusing SEO field naming (meta description labeled "SEO description"), and difficulty adding image alt text (hurting Google Image traffic). Bottom line: all have trade-offs; he prefers WordPress and notes affordable contract labor ($5–$20/hr) can help. He closes plugging Neil Patel Digital.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said (citing BuiltWith) WordPress powers more than 50% of the web, Wix is second with roughly 7% of all websites, and Squarespace third with over 4%.
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil cited a study (attributed to an SEO tool he called "8Tracks") finding 46.1% of WordPress domains get organic traffic versus 1.4% of Wix domains and roughly 15.1% for Squarespace — but stressed the data is inconclusive because it isn't apples-to-apples: WordPress users may simply know SEO better than Wix users.
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said WordPress is best if you're SEO-savvy thanks to its flexibility and customization, and its plugin model (Yoast SEO, All in One SEO pack, broken link checker) solves almost any problem; support is easy to find (forums, YouTube, Quora) and templates offer mobile-speed optimization.
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said WordPress cons are that too many plugins can slow your site or break it if incompatible, and while no technical skill is strictly required, deep customization needs some code or hired help.
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said Wix is a great, simple, easy-to-use CMS if SEO isn't your priority, with pros including an SEO wizard that auto-optimizes (good for new sites) and AMP pages for faster mobile loading.
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said Wix cons are limited customization of metadata/titles/URLs, slower indexing due to heavy AJAX use, and rigid, traditional site architecture that limits creative SEO structuring.
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said Squarespace is good for small simple sites that don't require SEO expertise, with pretty designs, nice built-in architecture, custom title tags and metadata, and support for XML sitemaps and robots.txt — though you can't edit them (a pro-and-con).
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said Squarespace cons are challenging/slow indexing (users report weeks-to-months, sometimes due to the operator's own lack of SEO savvy), confusing naming (meta description labeled "SEO description"), and difficulty adding image alt text, meaning little Google Image Search traffic.
- As of 2019-12-02, Neil said he personally prefers WordPress for its customization, and that affordable contract labor ($5, $10, $20/hr) can help set it up, while Squarespace and Wix are easier but less customizable.
