# How to Start Your Own SEO Agency | Find Quality Leads For Your Agency

_Q&A Thursdays episode; **Adam Lodolce** (viewership.com) hosts/asks, Neil answers. Only
Neil-attributed material below is persona data; Adam's framing is context._

## Summary
Neil's playbook for starting and filling an SEO agency, built entirely on **outbound**.
He opens with his origin story — he started his SEO agency at **16**, when nobody wanted
to give money to a kid with no degree, no money, and no experience, working nights and
weekends around a school day that ran 8am–2pm; he still got "well into the six figures per
month" and personally capped around **$20,000/month at 16**, growing from there. His point:
if a 16-year-old could do it, anyone can — "believe in yourself." His lead-gen method
(still works today): find companies spending heavily on Google AdWords via **SEMrush**
(which estimates monthly ad spend), and target the **$20–30k/month** spenders — not the
$500k–$1M whales (too big, too hard to reach the decision-maker). Cold-email the founder
with an angle like "I noticed you spend a lot on AdWords — what if I got you similar traffic
from the same keywords **without** paying for ads?" He argues **outbound beats inbound** on
qualification: NP's inbound generates ~5,000 consulting leads/month but "90-something percent
are not qualified," whereas outbound can be nearly 100% qualified because you choose who to
reach. His signature twist: use **Crunchbase** to find recently funded startups, then email
**both the company and its investors** separately — a blunt multi-page Word doc listing
everything wrong with their SEO. Investors forward it down ("go figure this out"), the founder
gets pressured from the board, and the deal closes. He did exactly this to **Michael Moritz of
Sequoia Capital** and a portfolio company as a kid. A canonical Neil agency-sales source.

## Key claims
As of 2018-01-25, Neil states:
- He **started his SEO agency at 16** with no money, no college degree, and no experience,
  working nights/weekends around school (8am–2pm); still got "well into the six figures per
  month" and personally **capped around $20,000/month at 16**, then grew (self-reported).
- Mindset first: "don't be afraid, just believe in yourself" — if he could do it at 16,
  anyone can.
- **Find clients via ad spend**: use **SEMrush** with a company's URL to estimate their
  monthly AdWords spend; heavy ad spend signals they have budget and could pay for SEO.
- **Target the $20–30k/month AdWords spenders**, not the $500k–$1M spenders — the whales are
  big organizations where it's too hard to reach the right decision-maker.
- **Cold-email the founder** with a value hook: "I noticed you're spending a lot on Google
  AdWords — what if I got you similar traffic from the same keywords without spending on paid
  ads?" Many CEOs ignore it, but heavy spenders will at least get on a call.
- **Outbound beats inbound on qualification**: NP generates ~5,000 consulting leads/month
  inbound, but "90-something percent are not qualified"; outbound can be ~100% qualified
  because you choose who to reach ("they're all lazy… it's easier to get them from outbound").
- **Crunchbase play**: find recently funded startups (valuations, raise amounts listed);
  when a company raises $4–5M, email **the company and its investors separately** — a plain,
  unvarnished multi-page Word doc of everything wrong with their SEO, not a fancy PDF.
- Why hit investors: an investor who gets told "this company you funded is screwing up" won't
  fix it themselves but will forward it and tell the founder to "go check this out," which
  drives the founder to vet and hire you — "I got a lot of deals that way."
- He did this to **Michael Moritz of Sequoia Capital** and a Sequoia portfolio company as a
  kid (self-reported anecdote).
- Pricing logic: a company that raised $5–15M won't blink at **$5k/month** — "a drop in the
  bucket" — especially if you've already outlined all their problems for them.
- Referral flywheel: do well for an investor's portfolio company and they refer you across
  their whole portfolio.
