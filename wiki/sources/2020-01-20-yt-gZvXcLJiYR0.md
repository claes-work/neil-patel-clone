# 5 Industries That Are Getting Crushed by Google in their SEO ★

## Summary
Solo analysis video with an unusually explicit pro-Google framing: Google gets stricter
every day about industries where bad information changes lives, and — Neil says — that is
their right. "It's their company. They should be able to do whatever they want." He
concedes his own traffic rises and falls with updates and says he cannot be mad about it.
Five sectors: (1) **Travel agencies** — featured snippets pulled answers into the SERP,
then Google made flights/hotels bookable **directly on Google**; "Google itself has
virtually become a travel company." Great for searchers, bad for sites; as evidence of the
scale he cites Forbes reporting that **Expedia ousted its CEO over poor performance**. He
splits his own view: "As a marketer, of course, I'd rather have people on my website."
(2) **Startups** — SERPs are saturated, incumbents of 5–10 years dominate, PPC costs so
much that "most start-ups these days are raising money for sales and marketing," not
development. Worse, **Google favours brands** (his stated reason: the fake-news problem),
and brand queries are exactly what a startup has none of. His prescription is off-SEO:
guest posting, podcasting, video, social, conferences — "SEO is not going to help you
build a brand, content marketing is not going to help you build a brand." He offers
himself as proof and, notably, credits **Ubersuggest — a product brand, not his personal
brand** — for his own algorithm-resilience. (3) **Health** — the **2018 Medic update** and
**E-A-T** (expertise, authority, trust) mean Google wants doctors, not marketers, writing
about cancer treatment or nutrition. He calls this "**one of my favorite updates**" despite
NP Digital having medical and health clients, on the grounds that it protects searchers
from harm (his example: anti-vaccine content), and adds an agency-floor observation: sites
posing as health information sites while selling products everywhere took big hits. The
recovery play is **author personas** — bios on every post stating credentials, links to
social profiles, speaking at events, guest posting on known sites in the space; "short-run,
definitely not, long-run, definitely will." (4) **Finance and legal** — the **YMYL** ("Your
Money, Your Life") class: Google now ranks not merely the best content but content that
won't damage people's lives; his test is "is it better for them to read my information or
better for them not to?" (5) **Tech gadget companies** — a competitive rather than
algorithmic squeeze: Apple/Samsung hardware is expensive internationally (**the cheapest
iPhone ~$699**, punishing in Brazil or India) while Google ships good-enough hardware
cheap, ranks **Google Pixel first** for queries like "cellphone", collects the data, and
locks users into a Home/Pixel/Chromebook ecosystem competitors will struggle to breach.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said Google is getting stricter in industries where bad information has lasting impact on people's lives, and defended its right to do so: "It's their company. They should be able to do whatever they want."
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said Google's growth sometimes comes at other industries' expense, that his own traffic goes up and down with updates, and that Google has still "done a great thing for the world."
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said **travel** is the hardest-hit sector: featured snippets removed the need to click, and Google added direct booking of flights and hotels — "Google itself has virtually become a travel company" — while noting Google isn't simply scraping Booking.com/Expedia wholesale.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil cited **Forbes** reporting that **Expedia ousted its CEO** over poor performance as an indicator of how hard travel had become.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil separated searcher and marketer interests explicitly: direct booking is "a great experience" as a searcher; "as a marketer, of course, I'd rather have people on my website."
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said **startups** are squeezed from both sides: organic listings are dominated by 5–10-year-old authorities, and PPC costs are so high that most startups raise money for sales and marketing rather than development.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said Google **favours brands** — partly a response to fake news — so that when competitors' SEO is otherwise equal, the one with 10× the brand queries wins; startups lack brand queries by definition.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil asserted that "**SEO is not going to help you build a brand, content marketing is not going to help you build a brand**" — brand requires guest posting, podcasting, video, social media and speaking at conferences.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil credited his own algorithm-resilience not to updates but to **Ubersuggest**: a **product brand** (distinct from his personal brand) that generates traffic and brand queries not reliant on Google, lifting the whole site.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said the **health** sector keeps taking hits from the **2018 Medic update** and **E-A-T** (expertise, authority, trust), because Google wants doctors — not marketers like him — writing about cancer treatments or nutrition.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil called E-A-T "**one of my favorite updates**" *despite* NP Digital having medical and health clients, because it protects searchers from harmful misinformation (his example: "don't get vaccines, it's bad for you").
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil reported from agency work that sites **labelling themselves health-information sites while selling products everywhere** took big SEO hits — Google effectively asking whether you are an information site or an e-commerce site.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil's E-A-T recovery play was **authoritative author personas**: a credential bio in each post, links to social profiles, speaking at events, and guest posting on well-known sites — which also raises brand queries. He was explicit that it does not work short-run, only long-run.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil described **YMYL** ("Your Money, Your Life") as hitting **finance and legal**, and framed Google's algorithm as no longer only about ranking the best content but about not ranking content that harms people.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil's YMYL self-test was expertise-based: he said he is not a financial advisor and has no business advising retirees on savings.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said **tech gadget companies** are being squeezed by Google itself: the cheapest iPhone was roughly **$699** — a lot of money in Brazil or India — while Google sells "high quality products at affordable prices" that "may not be as good as iPhone" but are good enough.
- As of 2020-01-20, Neil said **Google Pixel comes up first** for searches like "cellphone" or "cellphones for sale," and that Google's data collection plus its Home/Pixel/Chromebook ecosystem strategy will make it harder for bigger competitors to compete.
