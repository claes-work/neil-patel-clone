# How to Optimize For Google's 200 Ranking Factors (And Watch Your Rankings Skyrocket)

## Summary
A solo screen-share tutorial: rather than learn Google's 200+ individual ranking
factors, Neil recommends running Ubersuggest's Site Audit (SEO Analyzer) report and
optimizing toward an SEO score above 80, not 100. He walks through the report's
site-speed section (desktop vs. mobile load time, estimated input latency), the
critical-errors/warnings/recommendations triage order, and several concrete issue
types (low word count, duplicate meta/title tags, missing H1 headings, 400/404 errors,
titles too short), showing judgment calls on which flagged pages are worth fixing
(e.g., a "how to tie a tie" page doesn't need 2,200 words) versus which don't matter
(pagination pages, tool pages, an about page that's mostly video). Ends with: fix
critical errors first, then recrawl to verify score improvement.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-11-14, his standing SEO-score target via Ubersuggest's Site Audit is
  **above 80, not 100** — his reasoning: a 100 score on an actively growing site
  usually means content/link/page production has stalled, since new pages are never
  perfectly optimized out of the box; a healthy score naturally dips as you publish
  more (paraphrase, tool-specific heuristic).
- As of 2020-11-14, cites (via the tool) that **the average page ranking on Google's
  page one contains ~2,200 words**, but explicitly qualifies this is not a universal
  target — pages matched to low-intent/low-complexity queries (his example: "how to
  tie a tie") don't need to hit that length and can rank with images/video instead
  (paraphrase, self-reported/tool-cited statistic, not independently verified here).
- As of 2020-11-14, recommends triaging Site Audit findings in order: critical errors
  first, then warnings, then recommendations, and explicitly says not every flagged
  issue is worth fixing — judgment over the raw score (paraphrase).
- States Ubersuggest deliberately throttles its own site-recrawl speed to avoid
  hammering a user's servers with requests, which he frames as protecting the site's
  other visitors' experience during a crawl (paraphrase, self-reported product-design
  rationale).
