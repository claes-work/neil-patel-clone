# Smart Philanthropy for Business Owners

## Summary
Solo "Productive Insights" podcast return-guest interview (host references a prior interview
together) opening with a candid philanthropy/wealth-philosophy segment, then pivoting to a
career retrospective. On giving: states he and his wife will end up donating "close to 100%" of
their wealth by the time they pass, but currently gives less than 10% of annual income and a
"very low single digit percentile" of wealth annually — explicitly self-labels as "not the most
generous person," "a capitalist," someone who "loves making money for the sport of it." Contrasts
his own "logic"-based giving approach (demanding measurable outcomes) against his wife's
"heart"-based approach (donation amount correlates with how much a cause makes her cry), framing
the combination as complementary. States he "should donate more" and attributes some reluctance to
being Gujarati (raised to prioritize business reinvestment). Discloses, as his **"biggest mistake
in my life,"** having placed shares of one of his businesses into an irrevocable trust for his
two children (currently age one and three) — he now estimates each child will inherit "nine
figures" and says he wishes he hadn't put as much in, but the trust cannot legally be broken;
clarifies this isn't full "give them nothing," citing a scenario floor (would still cover housing/
food/medical needs) and frames the real benefit to the kids as the relationship network they're
already building via family dinners with wealthy friends, not the cash itself. Gives a self-aware
anecdote about buying his not-yet-four-year-old daughter a $3,500 Harry Winston pendant on
impulse ("I'm not a saint"). Pivots to career origin: confirms age 38; got his first job at 15.5
(under-minimum-wage, ~$5.75/hr) cleaning restrooms/picking up trash at a theme park; first
business (a job board) "failed miserably"; a subsequent **cloud-computing company** attempt
(pre-dating AWS's popularity, by his recollection) burned "a million dollars of borrowed money"
that had to be repaid; recovered via consulting/traffic-growth work for clients done concurrently.
