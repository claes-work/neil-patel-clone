# Revenue comparison: 1B YT shorts vs. 1B YT long-form views, Non-endemic ads from Amazon, & more

## Summary
Three segments. (1) A cited (uncited-source, "saw it on Twitter") revenue comparison: a billion
YouTube long-form views were reportedly worth roughly $50,000, versus roughly $440 for a billion
YouTube Shorts views — illustrating how difficult short-form content is to monetize, attributed to
short-form training viewers to consume quickly and skip rather than engage deeply. A stated
personal preference follows: Neil says he watches more long-form YouTube content than shorts,
primarily long interviews with financial figures (Jamie Dimon and Andre Esteves/BTG Pactual named
as examples), and that a single long interview builds durable, detailed recall of a person in a
way short clips don't — framed as especially true for B2B, where it's hard to convey substantive
value in a sub-90-second clip. A YouTube-specific growth tactic is described: after a new
interview-podcast video was initially underperforming, texting the guest to ask them to share it
on their own YouTube "Community" tab caused view velocity to jump sharply (from ~100 to ~700
views/hour) — described as a lower-friction, less-recognized form of cross-promotion/collaboration
than Instagram-style tagged collabs. (2) An anecdote from a recent Brazil speaking engagement (a
conference described as having 5,000+ attendees): after being tagged in ~200-400 Instagram
Stories by attendees, Neil manually reshared each one to his own story, hitting Instagram's
(undisclosed) per-day story-limit and having some of his reshares auto-removed; he frames this as
worthwhile despite the effort because it builds loyal-community goodwill with people who message
him excitedly after being reshared, and notes he does this at most conferences except
enterprise-audience ones (citing a CMA Canada marketing-association event with
RBC-level attendees as an example of where this behavior is less common). (3) Amazon
advertising: "non-endemic ads" (allowing brands unrelated to a shopper's browsing category to
advertise, leveraging Amazon's first-party purchase data as third-party cookies phase out) are
described as a known-but-under-the-radar ad product; Neil says he already knew about non-endemic
ads before the segment's cited LinkedIn post, crediting Amazon's marketing-evangelist outreach
program for advance briefings to agencies like his. A distinct Amazon ad product is described:
branded-product placement swapped programmatically within Amazon-produced shows/movies (e.g.,
swapping a background water bottle for a sponsor's brand at scale across impressions), called the
"coolest" Amazon ad format either host has seen. Closing: the Kindle/Oprah origin story (Kindle
launched ~2004, unprofitable for its first ~3-4 years, until an Oprah endorsement caused sales to
"skyrocket") is relayed as an example of a company betting on an unprofitable product for years
before a breakout catalyst, alongside the counter-example of Amazon's failed Fire Phone.
