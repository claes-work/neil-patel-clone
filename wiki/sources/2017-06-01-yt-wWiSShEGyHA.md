# How to Do SEO in a Competitive Industry When You Have No Money ★

> ★ L3-candidate: a reusable three-tactic framework for ranking organically in competitive niches on a zero budget (roundup posts for links/shares → content depth via keyword-density research → CTR optimization of title tags and meta descriptions).

## Summary

Neil Patel lays out a no-budget approach to organic SEO in competitive industries, arguing that organic listings capture roughly 70% of Google search clicks versus about 30% for paid ads. He presents three tactics that require effort rather than ad spend: publishing expert roundup posts to earn social shares and links, using keyword-density analysis of page-one competitors to build more thorough and in-depth content, and rewriting title tags and meta descriptions to raise click-through rates, which he says in turn lifts organic rankings. His framing is that small players can out-maneuver slow-moving big corporations by moving fast.

## Key claims

As of 2017-06-01, Neil Patel argues:

- Across Google searches, roughly 30% of clicks go to the ads (top and bottom of the page) and roughly 70% go to the organic free listings in the middle; organic is where most traffic is and it costs nothing, but it is not easy to rank there.
- **Tactic 1 — Roundup posts.** First, make sure the site has a blog (add one via WordPress.com or .org if needed). Then interview every expert in your space: find them by Googling terms like "top marketers," "top marketing influencers," "top SEOs," and reach out to marketing blogs.
- A roundup post asks all the influencers in your niche the same single question and compiles their opinions (e.g., in the pet space, "what's the best way to get rid of dog hair on furniture and clothes?").
- When building the roundup post, link back to each contributor's website and include their face/image, then email them once it's published and ask them to share it.
- The mechanism: more social shares → more visitors → a portion of visitors link to you → more shares plus links → rankings climb. Roundups work because they benefit the site owner and also give the interviewed influencers free press, so influencers are willing to share across Twitter, Facebook, LinkedIn, etc.
- **Tactic 2 — Content depth via keyword density.** Use SEO Book's free keyword-density tool: enter the URLs of competitors already ranking page one for your target keyword (e.g., "SEO") to see the key phrases they use and how often, since heavier use of a phrase signals to Google what the page is about.
- The point is not to stuff content with repeated keywords, but to be thorough: Google tends to rank the more thorough, more in-depth page higher even when it has fewer backlinks and fewer social shares, because Google wants to rank the page that provides the most value to the user.
- Practically: review the key phrases used by page-one competitors, include some of those keywords in your page, and go more in-depth than everyone else; over time rankings climb.
- **Tactic 3 — CTR optimization of title tags and meta descriptions.** Google whatever key phrase you want to rank for (e.g., "cheap Las Vegas hotels") and study the titles of the paid ads bidding on it.
- Just as Google AdWords ranks paid ads partly on click appeal (better ad copy earns more clicks), organic works similarly: the more appealing your title tag and meta description (the "copy" Google shows), the more clicks you get, and more clicks push your ranking higher.
- Neil describes the CTR-ranking mechanism concretely: Google shows ten organic listings per page, and if it notices searchers clicking the second listing more than the first, it will move the more-clicked listing up.
- Organic listings have no ad copy — only the title tag and meta description — so those two elements are the levers. To edit them, go into the HTML, ask a webmaster, or Google how to change a title tag / meta description, then make them more appealing.
- Quick tips to make a title tag more clickable: include the year/date (e.g., "update it in 2017" or "in 2020"); use adjectives; and test click-driving words like "now," "fast," "get," "try," and "free" in the title tag and meta description.
- Small companies can win in competitive industries because big corporations move slowly; you can move fast and get results quicker without matching their ad budgets.
