# The Marketing Behind Sam Altman's Biggest Project, Jack Altman Raises $150M for Alt Capital, and More

## Summary
Reacting to Sam Altman's reported $7 trillion chip-funding ambition, Neil frames it as
deliberate anchoring (citing the book *Influence*): floating an absurd number makes any
actual raise (his guess: $5-10B) look like a bargain by comparison. This leads into a
discussion of whether founders should start venture funds after exiting — Neil's view,
via his friend Andy (of Unlock Venture Partners, who sold two companies, one to
Microsoft-via-Aquantive and one to Vizio, with an estimated 30-40% IRR as an angel
investor before starting his fund): most people underestimate how much of a "grind"
running a fund is, comparable to being an operator again, and that a fund only pays off
7-10 years after a first close. Neil also discloses being an LP in several funds. A
personal aside: Neil says he developed an alcohol intolerance after not drinking during
COVID — even a small sip now gives him a hangover-like reaction within minutes. The
second half covers platform risk (Meta cutting third-party Facebook Groups API access,
Reddit's API pricing changes killing the Apollo app, X's $42K/month API pricing killing
third-party apps) as a recurring argument for owning first-party channels (email, SMS,
website) rather than depending entirely on any single platform. Neil shares content-
volume stats by platform (daily new content: TikTok 23M, YouTube 216M, Facebook 350M, X
500M, LinkedIn 2M, Instagram 95M) and a stat that 59.41% of 5.2M analyzed social posts got
zero engagement — his conclusion is not "stop posting," but "stop posting low-effort
content."

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- Sam Altman's reported "$7 trillion" chip-manufacturing funding ambition is read by Neil
  as textbook anchoring (referencing Robert Cialdini's *Influence*): setting an
  outlandish headline number so that the actual raise (his guess: $5-10B, possibly more,
  but not close to $7T) looks comparatively cheap/reasonable. (Neil, 2024-02-14) —
  wealth / mindset.
- On founders starting VC funds after exiting: most people who do this successfully were
  already investing actively *while* still operating their business (building a track
  record of good bets first) rather than starting cold after exiting — the 2020-2022
  wave of new solo-GP funds oversimplified how achievable this is. Running a fund is "a
  grind" comparable to being an entrepreneur again (via his friend Andy/Unlock Venture
  Partners, who sold companies to entities that became Microsoft and to Vizio, and
  reported ~30-40% IRR as an angel before founding his fund); a fund typically only shows
  real (not paper) returns 7-10 years after the first close. (Neil, 2024-02-14) — wealth.
  ★ (a clear, dated stance on the "should I start a fund" question — synthesis
  candidate for the wealth topic page.)
- Self-reported personal health detail: Neil developed an alcohol intolerance after not
  drinking at all during COVID; even a small sip now produces a hangover-like reaction
  within about 5 minutes, which he attributes to his body losing tolerance after a year
  of abstinence (drawing an analogy to childhood milk tolerance loss). (Neil, 2024-02-14,
  self-reported) — biography / mindset.
- **Platform-risk thesis**: recurring examples of platform dependency risk — Meta cutting
  third-party API access to Facebook Groups (breaking businesses built on that data),
  Reddit's API pricing change killing the Apollo app (previously ~1.5M active users,
  reportedly $10-20M/year business), and X's API repricing (~$42K/month) killing many
  third-party apps — support Neil's standing recommendation to build owned channels
  (email list, SMS list, website) you control, rather than depending entirely on any
  single platform (including SEO/Google, though manual actions there are described as
  rare "as long as you don't do anything stupid"). (Neil, 2024-02-14) — content-strategy
  / social-media. ★ (a recurring, dated risk framework — good synthesis candidate.)
- **Content-volume-by-platform stat**: daily new content published — TikTok ~23M,
  YouTube ~216M, Facebook ~350M, X ~500M, LinkedIn ~2M, Instagram ~95M. In an analysis of
  5.2M social media posts, 59.41% received zero engagement (no likes/comments/shares).
  Conclusion: this doesn't mean social media doesn't work, it means most people are
  wasting time creating low-quality/low-differentiation content — echoes his standing
  "the bar for content keeps rising" position. (Neil, 2024-02-14) — social-media /
  content-marketing. ★ (quantified, dated — reinforces content-quality-bar material from
  [[2024-02-08-yt-CWUu48ev2oc]] and [[2024-02-05-yt-kCkQqr--xVw]].)
- LinkedIn Thought Leader Ads tactic (via a CXL blog post): upload your ideal-customer
  contact list (e.g., from Apollo.io) and run retargeting ads boosting your own organic
  LinkedIn posts to that specific list; costs roughly $10-20/day at small scale, more
  (~$100/day) for a starting/less-optimized account in the US; Neil describes it as
  working "decently," not a "game changer," but worthwhile for agencies given that ~95%
  of surveyed Agency Owners Association members cite lead generation as their #1
  struggle. (Neil, 2024-02-14) — paid-ads / agency-entrepreneurship.
