# The End of Salespeople, How to Rank on ChatGPT, Netflix's Ad-Supported Program & More

## Summary
A grab-bag news-reaction episode. Eric opens on Bland.ai (a YC-backed AI voice-calling
tool that can fine-tune a script and place calls at scale); Neil is skeptical that AI
phone calls beat human reps in the short run, citing NP Digital's own use of auto-dialers
to make reps more efficient rather than replace them, and a running example of ChatGPT
getting data-visualization tasks wrong ~80% of the time despite being fed exact data.
The persona-relevant centerpiece is Neil's first "how to rank on ChatGPT" framework: NP
Digital ran 100+ questions through ChatGPT, had a statistician/data-analyst review the
answers for patterns across 82 factors, and found six with strong correlation to being
recommended — brand mentions, relevancy (highest weight), age, recommendations,
authority, and a compound score (0 to 1). Also covered: Netflix's $6.99 ad-supported tier
now at 23M users (an opportunity for marketers to chase attention there); an NP Digital
experiment on 18 non-Amazon e-commerce sites showing that going onto Amazon *lifts*
total sales over a 12-month period (own-site sales dipped in months 1-3 then recovered
and grew past baseline by months 7-12, while Amazon sales kept growing) — Neil's
rebuttal to the common complaint that Amazon fees outweigh the benefit.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- ChatGPT/AI tools still produce meaningfully wrong output on data-visualization tasks
  even when given exact source data and instructions — roughly 80% error rate in NP
  Digital's internal informal testing; this doesn't mean the tools (esp. the paid tier,
  "$19 or $20 a month") aren't valuable, just that they aren't yet reliable enough to
  replace human review. (Neil, 2024-02-02) — ai-marketing.
- **"How to rank on ChatGPT" framework**: NP Digital asked ChatGPT 100+ product/service
  recommendation questions, had a statistician analyze the Q&A pairs across 82 candidate
  factors, and found six with strong correlation to being recommended: brand mentions
  (.87), relevancy (.91, the single strongest factor), age (.46), recommendations (.28),
  authority (.52), and an overall composite score on a 0–1 scale. Building these factors
  doesn't produce next-day results — like SEO, it compounds as ChatGPT's index updates.
  (Neil, 2024-02-02) — ai-marketing / seo. ★ (candidate for a dedicated ai-marketing
  topic page — this is an early, quantified version of what becomes Neil's recurring
  "AI/LLM search visibility" framework.)
- Marketing School's own AI-search track record: as of this recording it still comes up
  as the answer to "what's the best marketing podcast?" on ChatGPT, which Neil attributes
  to citation/relevancy density built from ~7-8 years and thousands of episodes, plus
  end-of-episode review asks. (Neil, 2024-02-02) — ai-marketing / content-marketing.
- Perplexity is Neil's preferred AI tool for in-depth research (e.g., analyzing his own
  supplement stack) because it gives more complete answers than ChatGPT or Bard/Gemini;
  he no longer uses Grok despite once finding it good for "entertaining" content. Working
  technique: don't dump all context in one prompt — feed information one item at a time
  and let the "prompt think," then combine. (Neil, 2024-02-02) — ai-marketing.
- Netflix's ad-supported tier ($6.99/mo in the US, less than half the standard plan)
  reportedly reached 23M users; Neil frames this as a real opportunity for marketers to
  chase attention on ad-supported streaming/CTV inventory rather than dismiss it as a
  niche. (Neil, 2024-02-02) — paid-ads.
- **Amazon halo-effect experiment**: NP Digital tracked 18 low-seasonality e-commerce
  sites that were not yet selling on Amazon, comparing pre/post total revenue (own site +
  Amazon) over four 3-month windows after they began selling on Amazon: months 1-3 own-site
  sales as % of the prior baseline ≈103.9%, sales shifted onto Amazon; months 4-6 ≈114.7%,
  own-site partly recovering; months 7-9 ≈135.8% (+35.8% total), with *both* own-site and
  Amazon sales growing versus the original baseline; months 10-12 dipped slightly versus
  7-9 but stayed above original baseline. Conclusion: selling on Amazon does not cannibalize
  a brand long-term — it grows total sales, and the fees are offset by Amazon's own ad/
  marketing spend and traffic. Neil is testing similar halo effects on eBay/Walmart
  (data inconclusive so far, expected smaller scale than Amazon). (Neil, 2024-02-02) —
  agency-entrepreneurship / analytics-cro. ★ (concrete quantified case study — good
  synthesis candidate for a topic page on marketplace/omnichannel strategy.)
