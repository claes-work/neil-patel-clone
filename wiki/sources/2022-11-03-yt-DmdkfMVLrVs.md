# What Google's Quick Read Test Tells Us About the Future of SEO

## Summary
Discusses a (then-active) Google search-results experiment labeling results "Quick
Read," and speculates on Google's motive (explicitly says "no one really knows the
answer" and invites viewer comments). Cites Ubersuggest data — "562 informational
search queries" analyzed — finding "41.9%" of first-page results contained over 2,000
words, then argues this doesn't mean length itself ranks: searchers "don't care what
the length is," they want the fastest possible answer. Illustrative example: no one
wants a 10-minute article to learn "two plus two" equals four. States "SEO isn't about
word count" — optimize for answer speed/quality, which sometimes needs 10,000 words
and sometimes only 300. Cites Google's direct weather-answer box (typing "Las Vegas
weather") as an existing example of Google already prioritizing instant answers over
sending users to a webpage. Frames "give people what they want in the least amount of
words as possible" as the long-run winning content-marketing strategy. Closes with an
NP Digital plug.

## Key claims
- Cites Ubersuggest-derived data: of 562 informational search queries analyzed,
  "41.9%" of first-page results contained over 2,000 words (self-reported/proprietary
  Ubersuggest data, dated 2022-11-03).
- States "SEO isn't about word count" and that optimal content length varies by query
  ("sometimes you just need 10,000 words... and sometimes you only need 300 words")
  (self-reported stance, dated 2022-11-03).
- Uses Google's direct weather-answer box as evidence Google prioritizes instant
  answers over directing users to webpages (observational claim, not sourced to a
  Google statement).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> SEO isn't about word count. ... Don't optimize your content for length, optimize it for quality, and give people what they want as quick as possible.

> Would you want to read a 10 minute article that breaks down the answer to two plus two? Of course not. You just want to know that the answer is four.
