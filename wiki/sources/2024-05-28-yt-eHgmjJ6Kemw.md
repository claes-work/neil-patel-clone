# How to think about agency M&A, and The downfall and rebirth of the Patels (Marketing School)

## Summary
★ **Landmark source — flagged for the next synthesis pass** (biography.md + beliefs.md
candidate). Two major, dated, first-person Neil threads: (1) a detailed M&A operating philosophy
plus a rare admission of failure — a candid post-mortem of two 2021 NP Digital acquisitions that
did not work out, with named root causes and the lessons drawn; and (2) an extended, previously-
undocumented **Patel family origin story**: the family's pre-expulsion wealth in Uganda, the
Idi-Amin-era nationalization/exile of Indian-Ugandan assets, relocation to the UK on a British
passport, his parents' meeting via family introduction, his mother's outsized entrepreneurial risk
tolerance vs. his father's "book-smart" but capped earning ceiling, the family's financial struggle
after emigrating to the US, and how his mother funded her own business launch with ~$5,000 loans
from each of her entrepreneurial brothers. This is the fullest family-history source in the ledger
to date and should anchor/verify the family-background section of `persona/biography.md` at the
next synthesis checkpoint.

## Key claims
### M&A philosophy and 2021 case study
- Neil writes lengthy internal memos to think through major decisions (e.g. an 8-page M&A thesis
  memo, expanded to ~10 pages by his leadership team's additions) — a decision-clarification habit,
  not just a communication tool. (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- Recommends Brad Jacobs' *How to Make a Few Billion Dollars* — Jacobs built seven multi-billion-
  dollar companies via industry consolidation/roll-up strategy. (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- Strategic tension Neil is actively wrestling with (per his coach conversation that morning): an
  M&A-roll-up thesis (buy when others are fearful, per Buffett) is in conflict with an AI thesis
  (the top 5% of talent survives, a lot of roles disappear) — buying companies with large headcount
  now, only to have to cut heavily post-close once AI substitutes for labor, is something he
  explicitly calls "cutthroat," while noting it is standard private-equity practice. His resolution:
  build and prove the CRO-automation product internally first, then decide. (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- **Active deal pipeline (as of 2024-05-28, NP Digital ~1,200 employees):** a Southeast Asia
  acquisition closing that month (~10 employees); a Spain/Mexico deal closing June/July; an
  in-progress Brazil deal (talks started ~January 2024); a UK target under evaluation; an Italy
  deal close to an offer (months of prior conversation). Neil says NP Digital avoids acquiring
  unless the founders are willing to stay on, because founders hold years of tacit knowledge a
  buyer can't replicate quickly — the standard practice is to leave the founder's approach alone
  for 6–12 months before making changes, to understand *why* things are done a certain way first.
  (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- **2021 acquisition post-mortem (candid, self-critical):** NP Digital closed two acquisitions
  2–3 months apart in 2021 — a paid-media/e-commerce/CRO firm (small, "hardcore" team, good logos)
  and an SEO shop (~30–35 people). Both effectively failed; revenue was "pretty much wiped out."
  Named root causes, in Neil's own ranking: (1) insufficient due diligence — deals moved too fast,
  pushed along by a broker with a closing-speed incentive; (2) he ignored his own gut feeling about
  one of the two leadership teams; (3) after closing, he installed an external GM to run day-to-day
  and personally disengaged to focus on five other projects — "I trusted but didn't verify," which
  he names as the biggest single mistake. A key lieutenant from the first acquired company burned
  out and left shortly after close; combining the cultures (a burnt-out overworked team merged with
  a team that would skip client calls for yoga classes) actively damaged morale on both sides.
  Neil takes personal responsibility rather than blaming staff: "it's always the leader's fault at
  the end of the day." (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- Lessons drawn for future deals: prefer earn-outs/equity holdbacks that keep founders financially
  incentivized to stay (would have helped in the 2021 deals); stay operationally involved in
  acquisitions for the long haul (these things play out over 10–20–30 years, not a quarter); and
  explicitly "trust but verify" rather than fully delegating post-close. (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- Content-format note: shorter (15–20 minute) episode length is getting positive audience feedback
  (viewers say it's now their go-to workout-companion content); Neil frames ongoing tactic-adjustment
  as a marketing constant — "I've never seen you or I do the same thing forever in marketing... not
  only do algorithms change, but people's preferences change." (Neil, 2024-05-28)

### Patel family biography (landmark)
- Neil's mother was born in India; his father was born in Uganda. The family had material wealth in
  Uganda (name-checked: nice cars including Mercedes) before Indian-Ugandans were exiled and their
  assets nationalized — Neil doesn't name Idi Amin directly in this transcript but describes the
  event as the "assets/businesses taken, everything." (Neil, 2024-05-28) — **self-reported family
  history; the Uganda-Asian expulsion itself is a well-documented historical event (1972, under Idi
  Amin), but Neil's specific family details are self-reported and not independently verified here.**
- Because of Britain's historical rule over India, the family was able to relocate to the UK; his
  father moved there, later met his mother through a family-arranged introduction, married, and
  Neil was born in the UK — the source of Neil's British passport. (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- His mother is described as far more entrepreneurial and risk-tolerant than his father, who was
  "book-smart" but never had a high-paying job (capped around $60,000/year at a bank). His mother
  earned roughly $80,000/year (after expenses) from her own business — comparable money for the
  family at the time. (Neil, 2024-05-28) — self-reported.
- After the family emigrated to the US, they struggled financially (couldn't consistently make
  rent; his father borrowed from his brothers). His mother got a teaching job by offering to teach
  for free for six months first — she'd walked several miles each way to the school, pushing a
  stroller with Neil's sister — after which the school agreed to pay her. (Neil, 2024-05-28)
- When his mother later started her own business (the daycare business — see also
  [[2024-05-24-yt-50UqgVVG3nc]] for the "Tina's Daycare" marketing story), the family didn't have
  monthly cash flow to support her quitting her job; her entrepreneurial brothers each lent her
  roughly $5,000, enough to start and to cover the family's mortgage during the transition. Neil
  frames this as the turning point that "changed our life." (Neil, 2024-05-28) — self-reported.
- References a **Monish Pabrai** podcast episode (Pabrai discussed the Patel diaspora's historical
  concentration in the hotel industry, both in Uganda and later in the US, with Marriott franchises
  cited as an example) as external corroboration of the "Patels are hardcore entrepreneurs" framing
  — Neil had not fully listened to the episode at time of recording. (Neil, citing Pabrai,
  2024-05-28) — third-party claim, not independently verified by this clone.
- Neil's closing reflection: "you can try to knock someone down, you can take all their assets
  away, do all these things, but you can't take the spirit away — and no matter what, they came
  back." (Neil, 2024-05-28)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> It's always the leader's fault at the end of the day.

> I trusted but didn't verify, and I wasn't involved — and that was a big piece of why they didn't
> work out. I do believe they could have worked out had I stayed more locked in.

> My key takeaway for everyone here is: if you want to do this stuff, you're going to do it for a
> very long time — 10, 20, 30 years — you got to stay involved. And the third thing is, you have to
> trust but verify.

> You can try to knock someone down, you can take all their assets away, do all these things, but
> you can't take the spirit away — and no matter what, they came back.

> My mom is smart — she's more entrepreneurial than my dad, she takes way more risk, she's much
> better at business. My dad is much more book-smart... I think if my dad went to college my life
> would have been different — I don't know if I would have done as well, or been an entrepreneur.
