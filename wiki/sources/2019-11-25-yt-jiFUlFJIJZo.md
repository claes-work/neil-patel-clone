# How to Do Marketing In Over Saturated Social Networks (WITHOUT Paid Ads)

## Summary
In this solo talking-head video, Neil Patel gives nine tips for growing on saturated social networks organically, arguing that declining reach (Facebook's organic reach "already a joke," Instagram and LinkedIn dropping) is beaten by out-working competitors on value and engagement. The nine: (1) offer overwhelming value — more free content, respond to every comment, do live "consulting"-style sessions — so the algorithms reward you; (2) scale what works via templates/processes while still constantly testing new content, because tactics decay in three-to-six months; (3) invest in automation for scheduling (Buffer, Hootsuite) to free hours — but never use third-party schedulers on Facebook (use Facebook's own tools); (4) use different media formats per network and find the right length (video crushes on Facebook/LinkedIn but flopped for him on Twitter; quotes work on Twitter/Instagram but not Facebook); (5) cross-post content ideas (IG stories, Facebook Live, AMAs) to source ideas from audience demand; (6) build connections with influential players for unpaid cross-promotion "social rings," but only when you add real value; (7) use video more, as networks push it and now want longer-form (YouTube ~7–10 min vs the 3-min videos that worked years ago) while IGTV/stories want shorter; (8) let users co-create content (Gary Vee's live-call model); (9) directly interact — answer comments, DMs, feature user ideas — because networks prize thoughtful comments above likes and shares. He closes plugging Neil Patel Digital.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said social media reach is decreasing across the board — Facebook's organic reach is "pretty much already a joke," and Instagram and LinkedIn engagement/reach are dropping.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said tip one is to offer overwhelming value — more free content, videos, audio, responding to every comment, participating in groups, doing live sessions that function like live consulting — so networks don't flag you as chasing quick-hit traffic without work, and give more reach to those who drive engagement/comments.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said to scale winning content with templates and processes while constantly testing new formats, because even content that works now won't work well three-to-six months later; reusing the same tactic year after year gets caught by the algorithms and tanks reach.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said to invest in automation for scheduling (Buffer, Hootsuite) to save two-to-four hours a week — but not to schedule Facebook content with third-party tools ("they hate it… you'll get crushed"); Facebook's own scheduling tools are fine.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said to use different media formats and lengths per network, giving his own test results: video works great on Facebook and LinkedIn but not Twitter; quotes work on Twitter and Instagram but not Facebook — and posting the wrong content type for a network hurts your reach there.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said to cross-post content ideas (IG story posts, Facebook Live, group posts, AMAs) to gather ideas — when the majority ask for something, that signals demand and you should make it.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said to actively build connections with influential players for unpaid cross-promotion "social rings," but only when you can add value (providing your two cents also brands you as an expert).
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil predicted video would take off over the next few years and become the main format networks want (competing with TV), noting they favor going live and native uploads over links, and that networks now want longer-form video — YouTube ~7–10 minutes versus the 3-minute videos that used to do well — while IGTV and stories prefer shorter, concise videos.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said to let users co-create content (citing Gary Vee's "Ask Gary Vee" live calls) and to dice interactions into small clips that generate their own engagement.
- As of 2019-11-25, Neil said to directly interact with users — answer comments and private messages, feature their comments/ideas in your content — because social networks prize common engagement (thoughtful, thorough comments) above likes and shares.
