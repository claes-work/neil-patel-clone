# Stop Wasting Money on SEO. Do This Instead. (Marketing School)

## Summary
A co-hosted Marketing School episode in which Neil Patel drives a screen-shared, data-heavy breakdown of paid-ads effectiveness and Eric Siu adds an AI-ads aside, before the two split over business coaches. Despite the SEO-baiting title, Neil's actual thesis is pro-paid-ads-with-a-caveat: using NP Digital ("MP digital" in the captions) benchmark data he argues Google ads convert far better than SEO (3.49% vs under 1%) though SEO is much cheaper, and that when paid ads "don't work" the fault is almost always the advertiser's funnel and customer lifetime value, not the channel. He then ranks social networks by share of website revenue and by ROAS, arguing X/Pinterest/Snap have the best ROAS while Facebook/YouTube win on volume, and that Google still "crushes" social because search captures intent. In the second half Neil argues most business coaches are shams who monetized by running ads and flaunting cars, while Eric (pro-coaching) reframes the premise and defends coaches who have "been there, done that," citing his own coach (Jerry Colonna). Both agree the real test of a coach is proof and relevant track record.

## Key claims
- NP Digital's tools track roughly $2.1B/yr in ad spend; current benchmarks: average Google click-through rate 3.22%, average cost-per-click $2.81, average conversion rate 3.49%. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Organic/SEO traffic converts at less than 1% — much lower than paid — but SEO is far cheaper than paid advertising (you spend time, staff, or an agency fee rather than large media budgets). (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- ~68.3% of users know they are clicking on a paid ad; the ~31–32% who don't realize it still click because they believe the result will answer the query they typed. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- The average advertiser generates about $6.26 in revenue for every $1 spent on Google ads — though that is tracked revenue, not necessarily profit, since other expenses aren't captured. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Google generates roughly $224B/yr in ad revenue; if paid ads didn't work Google could not produce anywhere near that figure — so "it doesn't work" really means "it doesn't work for you." (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- The reason paid ads fail for a given company is almost always the funnel, not the channel: no upsells, no downsells, no repeat-purchase mechanism, and no work on increasing customer lifetime value — LTV is the real key to making paid ads profitable. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Share of a website's revenue by social network (from a Neil tweet dated ~Dec 6 2023, last-30-days data across 1M+ sites): Facebook 32.37%, YouTube 28.19%, LinkedIn 11.47%, Instagram 10.92%, TikTok 8.44%, X 4.41%, Snap 3.18%, Pinterest 1.02% (Reddit and tiny networks ignored as insignificant). (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Return on ad spend (ROAS) by social network: X 6.8, Pinterest 5.2, Snap 4.9, TikTok 4.1, YouTube 3.9, Instagram 3.3, Facebook 3.2, LinkedIn 2.6 — X had the highest ROAS. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Highest ROAS does not mean highest volume: Facebook and YouTube drive the most revenue volume despite lower ROAS, so companies will shift more spend toward high-ROAS platforms (X, Pinterest, Snap) without abandoning the volume channels; the ideal is to leverage all profitable channels (Google, Facebook, Instagram, X). (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Google still "crushes" most social platforms for advertising because search captures intent — a user typing "buy affordable laptop" or "I want a red bike" can be shown an ad directly matching the query. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Neil is against most business coaches: many self-styled coaches "became" coaches by running Facebook ads and flaunting Rolls-Royces, Ferraris, and fancy homes bought from ad revenue — not from actually doing the thing they teach — and he considers most of them shams. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Paying a coach for a specific, proven capability is fine (e.g. M&A from someone with many real deals, sales specifically within your industry, or operations/systems help); paying a general business coach is a waste of money. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- Proof and case studies matter: generic "sales skills" learned from watching videos won't close a $100M/yr marketing deal in a specialized industry. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- On homes vs. cars: Neil and Eric personally do better renting and reinvesting capital into their businesses, but buying a home is not wrong for everyone (the parents' generation often became millionaires buying cheap homes and paying them off); owning five Rolls-Royces while renting and calling a home "a waste of money" is backwards, since cars depreciate. (Neil, 2024-01-11)
- If Eric's former agency coaches were genuinely good they would have helped him avoid mistakes; instead Eric had built a bigger agency than coaches who had only a ~$2M exit yet were advising him on how to grow — which makes no sense. (Neil, 2024-01-11)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> People talk a lot of crap on paid ads, but if it didn't work they wouldn't have ended up generating anywhere near that much revenue in paid ads.

> It's not that it doesn't work — it doesn't work for you. Your competition is still spending money, and the chances are the reason it's not working for you is because of your funnel: you don't have upsells, you don't have downsells, you haven't figured out how to get people to come back and buy from you over time, you haven't figured out how to increase the lifetime value of your customer. That's the real key to make paid ads work.

> Someone's typing in a keyword — "buy affordable laptop," "I want a red bike," whatever the query is — you can show an ad that's directly related to the search query, which is why it's one of the best platforms out there for advertising. As long as there's attention there will be demand, and that's marketing 101.

> When you find people online that are like "I'm a coach" — they're a coach because they're running Facebook ads, and they've made their money by saying "I'm a coach."

> What did you actually really do? Oh, you said you're a coach and you're charging people, and they're going to be successful by paying you because you have flashy cars and homes and stuff that you got from just running ads and telling people you're successful. This is why I don't like most business coaches — I think most of them are shams and it's a bunch of bull.

> If you want to pay a coach for something specific — like "I'm really good at M&A, look at all these deals I've done, and I will help you with just that" — great. But just paying general business coaches is a waste of money.

> Someone told me how they have five Rolls-Royces but they don't own a home, and they're like "buying a home is a waste of money." I'm like, buying five Rolls-Royces is a waste of money — you could have bought a home. Your Rolls-Royces are going to depreciate over time.

## Eric Siu (co-host, context — NOT persona data)
[[wiki/entities/eric-siu]]
- On Google's Performance Max and Meta's Advantage+ (the new AI ad pushes): they will get "a lot better," but there is still a lot of required human intervention and there will always need to be a human in the loop; you can't rely on the AI completely. (Eric, 2024-01-11)
- Frames the whole discussion: there are only two things that matter in business — how you acquire customers and how you serve them — and most people wrongly assume a good product is enough; marketing must be top of mind for at least one of the founders. (Eric, 2024-01-11)
- Pro-coaching. Reframes Neil's premise (which he says is "correct"): coaches who show off Lamborghinis and a flashy lifestyle signal a lack of humility and a shallow belief that smart people will be impressed rather than wanting to learn. (Eric, 2024-01-11)
- His definition of a good coach: someone who guides by asking the right questions to retrain how you think, not someone who tells you what to do (that's a mentor/advisor); the ones that worked for him had "been there, done that." (Eric, 2024-01-11)
- Cites his own coach, "Jerry Kona" (Jerry Colonna): a former VC who worked with Fred Wilson, coaches Twilio's Jeff Lawson and Etsy, is humble and grounded, doesn't need the money, and does it because he enjoys it; notes the coaching business does ~$13–14M/yr. (Eric, 2024-01-11)
- Admits his former agency coaches only had a ~$2M exit; blames himself for not doing due diligence (they came via a referral) and says he quit after three-to-four months once he realized they hadn't "been there, done that." (Eric, 2024-01-11)
- Invokes the "humble, hungry, smart" filter for choosing coaches and the example of legendary coach Bill Campbell. `(attribution: uncertain — the Bill Campbell reference in the exchange is not clearly assigned to one speaker)`
