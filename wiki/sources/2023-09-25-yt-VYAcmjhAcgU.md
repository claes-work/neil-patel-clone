# Should you take every press appearance - Neil Patel

## Summary
Marketing School (co-hosted) episode on whether to accept every press/speaking/podcast
opportunity. Neil says he used to take every press appearance but stopped after having
kids, because most don't drive ROI — branding value only counts if it reaches the right
audience, not just a large one. He and Eric describe vetting incoming opportunities by
asking about audience composition, expected content fit, and *per-episode* reach
(not aggregate downloads), and independently verifying claimed reach numbers (e.g. via
Similarweb or a podcast's ratings/reviews count) rather than taking self-reported figures
at face value. Neil gives an internal example of declining to acquire a $100K+-ish
website with 12,000 visitors because it would distract from higher-priority, unfinished
regional expansion work (Spain, Middle East) and integration of a prior acquisition.

## Key claims (dated, paraphrase unless noted)
- Used to accept every press/speaking opportunity; stopped after having kids because most
  don't drive ROI — branding value requires reaching the *right* audience, not simply a
  large one. (Neil, 2023-09-25) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Vets speaking/podcast/press invitations by asking: who is the audience, what content
  would resonate with them, and what is the reach *per episode* (not aggregate/lifetime
  downloads). (Neil, 2023-09-25) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Recommends independently verifying claimed audience-reach numbers (e.g., checking a
  website's traffic via Similarweb, or a podcast's ratings/review count) rather than
  trusting self-reported reach claims, since inflated numbers are common. (Neil,
  2023-09-25) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Recounts flying 14 hours to speak at a Brazil event without vetting the audience fit,
  ending up on an off-topic panel — cites this as the lesson that led him to set explicit
  criteria (ideal audience profile, desired outcome) before accepting opportunities.
  (Neil, 2023-09-25) — agency-entrepreneurship / biography.
- Says "it's actually better to say no" broadly (not just for press) — internally
  declined a team member's proposal to buy a ~$100K+ website with 12,000 visitors,
  reasoning the company had unfinished priorities (Spain expansion, Middle East
  expansion, integrating a prior acquisition) that were more direct revenue
  opportunities than a new distraction. Self-reported, unverified specifics. (Neil,
  2023-09-25) — agency-entrepreneurship.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> I used to take every single press appearance. I had kids, then I stopped... most just
> don't drive the ROI. They're good for branding, but you need branding with the right
> type of audience.

> It's actually better to say no. When you say no, you tend to have more energy to focus
> on the right stuff, which just gets you much more traffic in the long run.

> This is just a distraction. You know how many things we still have to do that we
> haven't done? ...We already get all these leads there in these regions that we're not
> in right now. Why don't we just focus on that?

> Your attention, your time is perhaps your most valuable resource.
