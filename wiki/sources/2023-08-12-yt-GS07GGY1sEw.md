# How To Become a New York Times Best Seller

## Summary
Solo guest interview, multiple hosts, opening on his 2016 book "Hustle: The Power to Change Your
Life With Money, Meaning, and Momentum." Neil is candid that the book's entire strategic purpose
was becoming a "New York Times" bestselling author to boost his speaking career and credibility —
and states plainly that, by that measure, it "didn't really help much," attributing the shortfall
to two factors: it was co-authored (his co-authors did most of the writing, he did most of the
promotion, diluting single-author credibility) and its subject (general life "hustle") wasn't
related to his actual industry (marketing). He says he has no interest in writing another book,
calling the process "a pain in the butt, headache, not a fun process." Pressed on how he made the
bestseller list, he's transparent that it wasn't driven by broad individual readership but by
structuring bulk corporate pre-orders — describing the mechanics of gaming the "New York Times"
formula (which weights first-week order volume) via a (possibly now-defunct) San Diego firm called
Result Source that specialized in structuring such corporate-order campaigns across many cities/
retailers. He estimates corporate/bulk orders alone moved 50,000-60,000+ copies, and the book hit
#5 on the list for more than one week. He also states he would have made more money self-publishing
than going through a traditional publisher, and that he doesn't know or track his Audible per-sale
royalty because the financial outcome was never the goal — the "New York Times bestselling author"
credential for his bio was. Separately, he does affirm receiving substantial reader feedback that
the book changed people's lives, distinguishing that "end user" outcome from his own admittedly
"selfish" original motive. On paid speaking, he describes fee growth from $5-10K early in his
career to $50-100K per speech, reaching seven-figure annual speaking income within about four
years — but says he later concluded paid speaking is a poor ROI compared to his agency's contract
economics (a single enterprise NP Digital contract can be worth millions, sometimes eight figures,
often retained over five to six years), so he shifted strategy: speak for free at events where his
ideal enterprise decision-makers are present, in exchange for the organizer arranging direct
small-group access to break down case studies with those individuals — a tactic he calls "fishing
with dynamite" that he says generates millions, and in some years tens of millions, in downstream
agency business.
