# What's the Difference Between a 301 and 302 Redirect?

## Summary
In this short technical-SEO explainer, Neil Patel walks through when and how to use 301 versus 302 redirects and what each signals to Google. He frames a 301 as a permanent move — it tells Google a domain or page has permanently relocated and to transfer the existing rankings from the old location to the new one — using the analogy of changing your postal address so all mail forwards to the new home. A 302, by contrast, signals a temporary move, likened to forwarding mail to a summer home before returning, and he illustrates it with a seasonal e-commerce case (shifting a /summer URL to /winter). He notes that Google has said it now treats 301 and 302 redirects the same, but cautions that not all search engines do, so the permanent-vs-temporary distinction still matters. Redirects can be applied to whole domains as well as individual URLs, subdirectories, and subdomains. Patel closes with a cautionary personal story: a botched 301 redirect on his own neilpatel.com/br site cut traffic and took roughly three months to mostly recover, and he recommends non-technical users hire a developer (citing Upwork at around $100–$200) rather than attempt the redirects themselves.

## Key claims
- A 301 redirect signals a permanent move and tells Google to take the existing rankings from the old site and transfer them to the new site (as of 2017-06-21).
- A 301 redirect typically takes anywhere from a few weeks up to a few months to fully take effect (as of 2017-06-21).
- A 302 redirect signals a temporary move, telling Google to temporarily send the traffic and rankings to the new location (as of 2017-06-21).
- Google said recently that it started treating 301 and 302 redirects the same, but not all search engines do this — so use a 301 for permanent moves and a 302 for temporary moves (as of 2017-06-21).
- Redirects can be applied to whole domain names as well as web URLs, web pages, subdirectories, and subdomains (as of 2017-06-21).
- One small mistake in redirects can significantly hurt traffic; Patel botched the 301 redirects on his own neilpatel.com/br site and, even after fixing it, traffic returned to only about 90% of its prior level and took roughly three months to recuperate most of it (as of 2017-06-21).
- Non-technical site owners should hire a developer rather than do redirects themselves; developers on Upwork can make the change for roughly $100–$200 (as of 2017-06-21).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "A 301 redirect tells Google that, 'Hey, we changed names. We have a new domain name. Take all our existing rankings from this site, and transfer it over to this new site.'"

> "Think of it as changing an address. When you move locations, a new apartment, a new home, you're telling the postal service that, 'Hey, I've changed locations,' and now they forward all mail to your new address."

> "A 302 redirect, on the other hand, is telling Google, 'Hey, we temporarily changed locations.'"

> "Now, in the eyes of Google, they said recently that they start looking at 301 redirects and 302 redirects the same, but not all search engines do this. If you do a permanent move, use a 301, and if you're doing a temporary move, use a 302 redirect."

> "I made a mistake with my 301 redirects, and you know what? I eventually fixed it, but my traffic didn't bounce back. It went to 90% of where it was. It took roughly three months to recuperate most of the traffic."

> "The point I'm trying to make is one little glitch or mess up can really hurt your traffic."
