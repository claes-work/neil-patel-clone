# How To Get Rich Using Claude Code (Marketing School)

## Summary

A ~3,700-word co-hosted Marketing School episode that is HEAVILY Eric-dominated — Eric
Siu drives roughly two-thirds of the talk as the AI/"vibe-coding" evangelist, while Neil
plays the measured counterweight. The hook is Eric's claim that the newly-stronger **Opus
4.5** model has made **Claude Code** a "superpower" non-coders can now wield to build
businesses "10 to 100 times faster." Eric narrates his own conversion (made a U-turn from
his Peloton to sit on Claude Code 30–45 min; built a Slack bot combining sales/customer
transcripts + HubSpot + Google Analytics/Search Console in an hour; his CTO and creative
strategist already all-in), and shares an ARR-per-employee chart and viral ex-Google
tweets to argue "we're able to enter the arena now."

Neil's clearly-attributable contribution is the sober middle position: agrees the tools
are real and marketers should use them, but warns that ~70–80% of enterprise AI effort
goes to creating things "that doesn't need to be created" — motion without ROI. He rejects
both the CIO "go go go" and CFO "slow down" extremes, likes the Sergey Brin 10%-time model,
and argues the real under-leveraged ROI is **Claude Code**, not just "getting mentioned in
ChatGPT." His anchor anecdote is an NP Digital exit interview (a long-tenured Latin-America
marketing employee who left for ~double pay and told Neil to cut 30–40% of staff and
"embrace AI"): Neil says the technology isn't there to cut 40% of staff without clients
leaving, and stumped the departing employee by asking why they themselves — using AI daily
— hadn't automated away their own growing team.

Time-bound, opinion/anecdote-driven episode about a fast-moving moment, not a durable
framework. **do NOT star** (no L3-candidate).

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)

- Opus 4.5 has made Claude Code strong enough that non-coders can build what they want;
  businesses will grow "10 to 100 times faster" using it. (Eric, 2026-01-12) — evangelism.
- Eric built, in ~1 hour, a Slack bot that combines sales-call + customer transcripts,
  HubSpot CRM, an HR/analytics data source, Google Analytics, and Google Search Console to
  surface customer challenges/objections and auto-generate briefs from Slack. (Eric, 2026-01-12)
- The bottleneck in marketing departments isn't adoption but discipline: ~70–80% of the AI
  effort at enterprises goes into creating things that have no real value / don't move the
  needle — busywork that wastes ROI. (Neil, 2026-01-12)
- Neil rejects both extremes in the 2026 CIO-vs-CFO AI debate (CIOs pushing "go go go,"
  CFOs saying "slow down, no ROI, just more spend"); the reality is somewhere in between,
  and believers should still implement. (Neil, 2026-01-12)
- Neil endorses the Sergey Brin "10% of your time on whatever you want" model for the
  experimentation slice, with more structure governing the other 90% of AI use. (Neil, 2026-01-12)
- The real under-leveraged AI ROI for marketers is tools like Claude Code, not just chasing
  ChatGPT mentions; ChatGPT is already good at answering queries and won't improve much for
  that use in the next 12 months, so shift focus to using AI to make the marketing itself
  better. (Neil, 2026-01-12) — one of Neil's strongest reusable takes here.
- Hiring is shifting toward "AI-forward" candidates (Neil cites AMD's Lisa Su: AI isn't
  replacing people but changing who gets hired); marketing hiring hasn't slowed, but
  companies now prioritize people who genuinely embrace AI. (Neil, 2026-01-12)
- You can't yet cut 30–40% of a marketing/agency staff and let AI do everything — the
  technology isn't there; doing so would make the majority of clients leave. (Neil, 2026-01-12)
- Some enterprises are blocking Claude Code over security/approval concerns; some strong
  people are leaving stable public companies specifically because those employers move too
  slowly on AI. (Eric, 2026-01-12)
- Eric's AI-fluency prescription: focus on revenue per employee and real efficiencies (not
  "BS"), run weekly AI office hours and quarterly multi-day hackathons (idea credited to the
  Zapier founder). (Eric, 2026-01-12)
- ARR-per-FTE chart (small sample): Cursor ~$6.1M, Lovable ~$3.4M, OpenAI ~$1.5M, [company]
  ~$1.2M vs. incumbents — Atlassian ~$460K, Datadog ~$510K, ServiceNow ~$490K, Salesforce
  ~$540K; incumbents should hope that number ticks up over time, with guardrails so people
  don't "yolo and build whatever." (Eric, 2026-01-12) — see attribution notes for garbles.
