# I Replaced My Marketing With Claude But It Doesn't Look Good (Marketing School)

## Summary

A dense, high-marketing-value episode of the co-hosted Marketing School podcast — a wide-ranging reaction show about AI's impact on marketing, SEO, agencies, and hiring in early 2026. Roughly half the talk is Eric Siu (Single Grain / ClickFlow), so attribution gating matters. The spine: a Jensen Huang (Nvidia) claim that a $500k engineer should be spending $250k on tokens, which both hosts partly endorse but reframe toward **open-source models on local infrastructure to cut cost** (Eric says ~70–80% open source). From there they cover cold-email decay, Neil's shift toward **branding** (in-person events, own conferences, award submissions), AI-transformation change management (Eric's hackathons/"molting" metaphor vs. Neil's "adapt the AI to the person" approach), a rich **2026 SEO/AEO trends list**, why AI-written content doesn't rank long-term, legal-AI limits (Harvey), incrementality/media-mix-modeling/synthetic data, agentic commerce (Stripe ACP on Meta), evals-as-the-new-PRD, and CEO/CFO agents.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)

- Jensen Huang (Nvidia) said he'd be "deeply alarmed" if a $500k engineer wasn't spending $250k on tokens; both hosts read this as a great salesman talking his own book but agree directionally that people should use AI heavily. (Neil + Eric, 2026-04-03) — ai-marketing.
- The cost-smart way to hit that token spend is to run ~70–90% through **open-source models on local infrastructure** and only 10–20% on premium tokens, so you get the efficiency without paying "the Anthropics of the world" full freight. (Neil framed the open-source cut; Eric put it at "at least 70–80% open source on local infrastructure," 2026-04-03) — ai-marketing.
- AI token cost is becoming "a massive cost center"; the parties currently capturing the value are Cursor and Anthropic, not the agencies internally — the near-term job is fine-tuning cost structure, not cutting engineers. (Eric, 2026-04-03) — ai-marketing.
- Cold-email marketing built with AI worked early but is now decaying: an ~11–12-person B2B entrepreneur email group sees positive reply rates around 1% (normal band 1–3%), open rates down ~40% from end of last year to end of Q1, and some members are considering shutting it off. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — email-marketing.
- Inbound influencer/brand-deal outreach volume is a proxy metric for brand health (tracked outside follower count); Neil's team logs ~2–3 "good" inbounds/week plus ~40–50 junk/week and mostly ignores them, but tracks the trendline. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — social-media, agency-entrepreneurship.
- The big 2026 shift for NP Digital is **investing heavily in branding** because technology is no longer a moat: "The question is not can someone replicate it. The question is when will they replicate it." Tactics: in-person speaking, self-hosted intimate conferences (London, Paris, done cheaply in co-working spaces), and award submissions (won Ad Age Performance Marketing Agency of the Year), spending an estimated $400–500k/year on award-related effort. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Speaking-gig selection flipped from "is it well-paid + convenient" to "are our ideal customers in the room and is the topic relevant to what we sell"; they'll now do free/paid ($15–30k) gigs, or pay to speak, when the ICP is present or it drums up business in a city they operate in. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- AI transformation inside the org: Eric finds it painful ("molting," delicate, some people self-select out); Neil's team found it "not painful at all" by **building AI that adapts to the person** — a solutions team that shadows each specialist (on-page, digital PR, content, CRO, paid, email) and productizes their workflow, so output quality is standardized rather than left to individuals producing "slop we can't use." (Eric painful / Neil not-painful, 2026-04-03) — ai-marketing, agency-entrepreneurship.
- Clients who want AI to "do more for less" are usually the ones already shrinking (~1% or negative growth); healthy, growing companies want AI to help them **grow the top line**, not cut cost — and AI hasn't actually changed the old "do more for less" dynamic. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- **AI-written content does not rank well long-term.** Both hosts own AI content tools; you can get short-term traffic but it fades. Use AI for ideation, structure, research, expansion, and content refreshes — but do the actual writing manually and double down on E-E-A-T. Neil believes search engines can detect AI-generated content. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — seo, content-marketing.
- **2026 SEO/AEO trend list (mostly Neil):** (1) go back to sites already linking to you and get them to add explicit brand/product mentions (a link isn't a mention); (2) do SEO/AEO via API so agents can execute (Eric's point; cites Ramp's new CLI); (3) invest in branding — when a brand's Google Trends line rises, rankings for non-branded keywords rise almost always; (4) run an AEO audit/checklist alongside SEO (structured data, 3 key points up top, FAQ, citations) — Neil's "Oracle" agent scores their own site ~80–85 on SEO but ~30 on AEO; (5) write more content manually (E-E-A-T), AI only in the process; (6) publish proprietary data/charts/surveys (linkable, and LinkedIn is increasingly cited by LLMs); (7) repurpose content onto platforms where your ICP isn't (e.g., TikTok) because Google's data deal with TikTok means that content can still feed Google where your ICP is. (Neil for 1/3/4/5/6/7; Eric for 2; 2026-04-03) — seo.
- Publishing large volumes of self-serving listicles ("10 best X") can help GEO/ChatGPT visibility short-term but tends to **hurt your organic search traffic within ~6 months**; NP Digital doesn't recommend publishing that content on your own site. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — seo.
- Legal AI (Harvey) is still too inaccurate for high-stakes work; a partner friend at a 2,000+-lawyer global firm says it often costs more paralegal/lawyer time to correct than doing it from scratch — the firm pays for it mainly so clients see them as current. Use AI supervised; law (like marketing) is not black-and-white and needs human judgment. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — ai-marketing.
- Marketing measurement is getting cheaper for everyone via AI: **media-mix modeling (MMM)** and **incrementality testing** — did the ad actually cause the sale or just take credit — are now accessible to smaller companies, not just enterprises; expect more **synthetic data** use, which also lets companies withhold first-party data from Meta/Google and weakens those platforms' targeting. (Neil, 2026-04-03) — analytics-cro, paid-ads.
- **Evals are becoming the new PRD / the new A/B test.** Eval-driven teams run ~12.8 experiments/day (~384/month) vs. ~3/quarter for classic A/B testing; caveat (Neil): evals only work at high volume, so most non-VC-funded small businesses can't run that playbook and will lean on synthetic data. (Eric citing Aakash Gupta / Ankur Goyal; Neil's caveat; 2026-04-03) — analytics-cro, ai-marketing.
- **Agentic commerce is arriving:** Stripe's Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP) lets businesses sell inside a Meta ad/browsing session (and via ChatGPT), skipping the site checkout (and Shopify's cut); AMP email enables in-email checkout. Keep your website anyway — agents need rich content/documentation to ground on. (Neil + Eric, 2026-04-03) — ai-marketing, paid-ads.
- **CEO/CFO agents** are the emerging high-leverage use: an agent that ingests every metric/thread/escalation and surfaces the 3 things that matter, prepping 1:1s and remembering institutional history; Eric built a CFO agent that surfaced $700k in cost cuts. Big caveat (Neil): AI memory is still unreliable and won't be fully "solved" soon (per an Anthropic employee he met) — it will just get better and err less over time. (Eric building / Neil caveat, 2026-04-03) — ai-marketing.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> [technology] is not a moat anymore. It's accessible to a lot of people. A lot of stuff is open source… The question is not can someone replicate it. The question is when will they replicate it. So we're investing heavily in branding right now.

> even though both Eric and I have our own AI content writing tools, it does not rank well. And you could get short-term traffic, but over time, we see AI content just not do as well… It's great for ide[a]tion. It's great for structure. It's great for research… a lot of the writing I highly recommend doing it manual.

> When we see a Google Trends line of a corporate's brand going up and to the right, we typically see increase in search rankings almost always for non-branded keywords. So… if your brand name becomes more and more popular within your industry, Google starts ranking you higher for non-branded terms.

> just because someone links to you, it doesn't mean they actually mention your product name specifically… hitting up the sites that already link to you to adjust their content to now mention your company name more specifically and go more in depth… is an easy way to not only do better in the SEO perspective, but also do better from a GEO['s] perspective.

> within six or so months of creating a lot of those listicles, assuming you do it in quantity, we see it hurt your organic search traffic… you're trying to do better in one place at the cost of another channel.

> For many years, companies want agencies to do a lot for very little money. But AI hasn't changed that. What we found is the companies who are making profit and are growing are like, how do we do more and do it more efficiently with AI? They don't care about… spending less money.

> we actually haven't found the transformation to be painful at all. We figured out the solution that works for us… So what we're doing is creating the AI that adapts to the person and just say[ing] here's how you use it… so then the quality of the output that we get is at a level where we're happy for our customers.

> Now, we don't look at if it's paid. We look at in the room, is our ideal customers going to be there? And is the talk that they also want me to speak on relevant to the services we pitch and our long-term vision. So if someone's willing to pay a lot of money but their ideal customer is not there, we will turn down the speaking event.

> the way to use this stuff is supervised, at least for right now… it's too high stakes to just let a machine do it… the problem with law is — and with a lot of things, including marketing — it's not black and white.

> was this person actually buying because of the ad, or were they already on the checkout and going to buy anyways and the ad just took credit? … a lot of what you're closing in that current week came from… advertising and marketing that happened months ago or a year ago.
