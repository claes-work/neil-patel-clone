# How to Write Copy That Converts | 5 Things You Need to Know About Writing GREAT Marketing Copy

## Summary
Neil teaches a five-point framework for writing marketing copy that converts visitors into buyers, arguing that persuasion is not about polished prose ("you don't need to be Ernest Hemingway") but about systematically answering reader objections. He recommends surveying readers with tools like SurveyMonkey or Qualaroo (asking "what else would you like to see on this page?") to surface objections, then rewriting copy to address them. He stresses the headline as the highest-leverage element (8 of 10 people read the headline, only 2 of 10 read the rest), claims copy length is irrelevant so long as every objection is answered, and urges writers to segment visitors (ready-to-buy vs. researchers vs. people building a case for their boss) and write copy for each segment — citing CrazyEgg's UX (enter-a-URL-to-start for buyers, a "Learn More" button for researchers) as an example. Finally, he argues most conversions come from follow-up: since at best about 5 of 100 visitors buy, marketers should capture emails (e.g., via the free tool Hello Bar) and write email-sequence copy that drives people back to convert, claiming roughly half of sales come from email follow-up.

## Key claims
As of 2017-08-02, Neil states:
- Copywriting is not about being the smoothest or most literary writer; answering people's objections is what makes copy convert (as of 2017-08-02).
- ★ You can surface reader objections by surveying visitors with SurveyMonkey or Qualaroo, asking "what else would you like to see on this page?", then rewriting copy to address the responses (as of 2017-08-02).
- The headline is critical: about 8 out of 10 people read the headline while only about 2 out of 10 read the rest of the content, so a weak headline means the rest of the copy goes unread (as of 2017-08-02, self-reported figures).
- The headline should address the problem in order to pull readers into the rest of the copy (as of 2017-08-02).
- ★ Copy length does not matter (short vs. long is a false debate); what matters is answering every objection — use short copy if you can address all concerns briefly, longer copy if you cannot (as of 2017-08-02). [Transcript reads "copy link doesn't matter"; "link" is almost certainly a caption garble for "length" given the surrounding short-vs-long discussion.]
- ★ Website visitors fall into different segments (ready to buy now, doing research, gathering info to pitch their boss); you should put yourself in each visitor's shoes and write copy for every segment (as of 2017-08-02).
- CrazyEgg's site illustrates segment-specific copy: buyers can enter a URL and start immediately, while researchers can click a "Learn More" button to expand the feature details (as of 2017-08-02).
- At best about 5 out of 100 visitors buy (that is the high end), so most visitors will not buy on the first visit (as of 2017-08-02, self-reported figure).
- ★ You should collect emails (e.g., with the free tool Hello Bar / hellobar.com) and write email-sequence copy that drives people back to the website to convert — copy should serve email sequences, not just the website (as of 2017-08-02).
- Roughly half of your sales come from email follow-up, so follow-up is where most conversions are won (as of 2017-08-02, self-reported figure).
