# The BEST Way to Grow Your Instagram With Neil Patel

## Summary
A conference-style two-person interview/podcast segment (unnamed interviewer/co-host,
who states elsewhere in the clip he personally has 1.3 million Instagram followers —
**not Neil**) in which Neil argues that **engagement rate, not follower count, drives
Instagram's algorithm**. States giveaway/follow-swap growth tactics backfire: the
follower spike dilutes average engagement, which signals to Instagram that "no one
cares for your content," suppressing future reach. Gives rough engagement-rate
benchmarks: video views around 5% of followers is good (3–4% low end, up to 10% high
end); photo likes around 3% is decent. Recommends going live (Instagram/Facebook) as
the highest-leverage growth tactic, framing it as cross-pollinating two creators'
audiences who "get to know" each other, which Instagram's algorithm favors because the
platform is competing with live TV for attention.

## Key claims
- States Instagram's algorithm ranks primarily on engagement rate, not follower count;
  a sudden follower spike with flat engagement reads as a negative signal and
  suppresses future content distribution (paraphrase, 2021-02-22; consistent with his
  general social-algorithm doctrine elsewhere in the wiki, not previously logged with
  this specific giveaway/follow-swap mechanism).
- States rough engagement benchmarks: ~5% of followers viewing a video is good
  (3–10% range); ~3% liking a photo post is decent (paraphrase, self-reported
  observational figures, not independently verified).
- Recommends going live jointly with another creator as a growth tactic because it
  builds mutual audience familiarity/rapport and because platforms boost live content
  to compete with television (paraphrase).
- ⚠️ Attribution note: the interviewer's personal stats in this clip (1.3M followers,
  a 500K-view personal video, a 150K-view average business post) are **his own data,
  not Neil's**, and must not be attributed to Neil Patel.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> So I think it's a terrible thing to do, and here's why. The way these algorithms

> You don't need the most amount of followers. You need the most engaged followers
