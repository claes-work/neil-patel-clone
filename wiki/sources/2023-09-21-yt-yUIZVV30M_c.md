# Do Offline Ads Work?

## Summary
Marketing School (co-hosted) episode on offline advertising, prompted by a tweet about
B2B airport-ad statistics. Neil's position: offline vs. online is not the real question
— the real question is whether an ad converts, which depends on targeting, creative, and
price, the same variables that determine any ad's success. He tells the eBay/Meg Whitman
billboard anecdote (a company won an eBay acquisition partly via billboards targeting her
commute) as an example of "underpriced attention." He also revisits the "Where's Waldo"
me-too metaphor from other episodes, applying it to AI: if everyone uses the same AI
tools, campaigns start blending together, so creative differentiation matters more, not
less, as AI adoption spreads.

## Key claims (dated, paraphrase unless noted)
- Whether an ad is offline or online is not the determining factor for success — the
  determining factors are audience targeting, creative quality, and price paid for the
  placement. (Neil, 2023-09-21) — paid-ads.
- Tells the story of a company (unnamed) that ran billboard ads targeting eBay CEO Meg
  Whitman's commute and was subsequently acquired by eBay — cited as an example of
  effective, targeted, creative offline advertising. Self-reported anecdote, unverified.
  (Neil, 2023-09-21) — paid-ads.
- Notes offline advertising is increasingly merging with digital (e.g., programmatically
  buyable digital billboards). (Neil, 2023-09-21) — paid-ads.
- B2B/IT-focused offline ads tend to be creatively weak (generic claims like "best
  servers, 99.9999% reliability"); argues IT buyers are still humans who respond to
  emotional/creative advertising, not just technical claims. (Neil, 2023-09-21) —
  paid-ads.
- Reiterates the "Where's Waldo" me-too metaphor (also used elsewhere) for AI-driven
  creative: as more marketers use the same AI tools, campaigns increasingly look alike,
  making genuine creative differentiation more valuable, not less. (Neil, 2023-09-21) —
  ai-marketing / paid-ads.
