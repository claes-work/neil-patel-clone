# Want to Go Viral on LinkedIn? Here's How (Marketing School)

## Summary
A co-hosted Marketing School episode on going viral on LinkedIn. Eric walks through a five-step framework that is NOT original to either host — it comes from an external LinkedIn creator who reportedly got 26 million impressions and grew from 25k to 185k followers in ~11 months after learning to write persuasively. Neil largely defers on the framework mechanics and instead layers on three of his own hard-won learnings: (1) responding to comments and engaging is disproportionately valuable — grounded in his early blog days at Quick Sprout / neilpatel.com, where he personally answered 400–500 comments a day before hiring help (and has since disabled comments); (2) social copy should NOT be "persuasive" in a salesy sense — write conversationally, exactly how you'd talk, so it reads as authentic and people relate; and (3) post ~once a day, but never post junk — low-engagement content actively hurts you with the algorithm and takes time to recover from. Both hosts also nudge against the framework's core premise: earlier in the show they'd said views aren't the point, and both say they'd rather chase targeted marketing/business content that drives dollars than broad "workplace" content that maximizes exposure. Eric reads a "carrot" ABM product/sponsor spot and tells a "backhanded compliment" story from his own YouTube channel (the Seth Godin video) about staying relentless. No landmark Neil framework here.

## Key claims
- Responding to comments and engaging on your content helps "so much" — it is one of the most valuable habits Neil has learned over the years. (Neil, 2024-12-03)
- In his blog days Neil personally responded to every comment — up to 400–500 a day — before it became too much and he hired someone; he did it because people took the time to leave a comment, not for any algorithm ("back then it was the blog, there was no algorithm"). He has since disabled comments on his blog. (Neil, 2024-12-03)
- On social, the win is NOT writing "persuasive" (salesy) copy — Neil writes the way he would actually talk, in a conversational way; it reads as natural, people relate to it and love conversations, versus copy that feels like you're selling at them or talking at them, and the content comes across as more authentic. (Neil, 2024-12-03)
- You should ideally post about once a day, but if you post low-quality "crap" that gets no engagement it hurts you in the future — posting lots of junk sets back your reach and takes time to recover from, so quality matters more than raw volume. (Neil, 2024-12-03)
- Neil would rather target marketing/business content than chase the broadest-audience "workplace" content for views, because targeted content drives more dollars — he audibly agreed ("yep") with Eric's version of this point. (Neil, 2024-12-03 — agreement with Eric's interjection; see Eric section)
- Being relentless / never quitting is the one trait Neil and Eric both credit for their success. (Neil + Eric, 2024-12-03)

## Eric Siu (co-host, context — NOT persona data)
[[wiki/entities/eric-siu]]
- Presented a five-step framework for going viral on LinkedIn, attributed to an external creator (26M impressions; grew 25k → 185k followers in ~11 months after learning to write persuasively): (Eric, 2024-12-03)
  1. Some topics do much better on LinkedIn — motivational, business, and "aha moment" content, and especially posts about LinkedIn itself, culture, HR, leadership, and remote work — because they apply to a broad audience (large TAM); the creator shifted to more workplace content and got 5–10x the exposure of marketing/business content.
  2. Post 5–7 times a week — algorithms reward reliable, consistent posters with reach and starve inconsistent ones.
  3. If you're starting out, engage hard: comment on 10–30 posts a day and immediately respond to comments on your own posts (Eric noted neither he nor Neil has the energy for this).
  4. Copywriting is a great skill — learn to write persuasively.
  5. Ignore nuance and use "bait" comments (Eric: you probably ignore these naturally). Eric's stated biggest takeaway: you can go for a larger-audience TAM and win by engaging.
- Interjected that he'd rather go for marketing/business and more targeted content than chase broad workplace content for views, "because that's going to drive more dollars at the end of the day" — Neil agreed ("yep"). This partially pushes back on the framework's own premise, consistent with the show's earlier point that views aren't the most important thing. (Eric, 2024-12-03)
- On "whatever works for you": people over-think that you must write or run your business a certain way; you should do whatever is natural to you and not cater to anyone's style, because copying others makes you unnatural (referenced Neil in the third person — "Neil likes doing things certain ways… I do things certain way in mine"). (Eric, 2024-12-03)
- Read a product/sponsor spot for "carrot" (carrot ABM): a LinkedIn account-based-marketing tool that lets you target and hyper-personalize ads to up to ~500 enterprise accounts at once — claimed LinkedIn confirmed only they can do this. (Eric, 2024-12-03 — product/sponsor content, non-persona)
- Told a "backhanded compliment" story from his own YouTube channel (a video with Seth Godin that was doing well): a longtime subscriber praised him for never quitting despite years of low views — Eric's lesson: sometimes you just keep going, and his one trait is being relentless. (Eric, 2024-12-03)
- Outro: rate/subscribe, marketingschool.io/agency, and a plug that Neil and Eric were doing a private session with the group the next day. (Eric, 2024-12-03 — outro/sponsor)
