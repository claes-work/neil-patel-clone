# Wall Street rate-cut expectations, Meta ad woes, Burger King's rival tactic, SEO tips, & More

## Summary
Several segments, largely news-reaction commentary. (1) Fed rate-cut outlook: a discussion that
rate cuts are unlikely before the 2025 election given persistent inflation, with a stated view
that historically a rate-cut pivot tends to precede a recession rather than follow one, and that
official recession/unemployment definitions get revised retroactively (raised as a bipartisan
critique, not a partisan one). A Twitter-relayed anecdote (Jesse Pujji) about spending $75M on
Uber driver-acquisition ads in 2014, only to discover via informal rider surveys that most drivers
credited word-of-mouth (friends/family) rather than ads for signing up — used to argue that
marketing attribution is "somewhat unknowable," especially for founders under $25M revenue, and
that brand-recall studies are a better fit than precise attribution modeling in a
cookie-degraded/cross-device tracking environment. (2) A described wave of advertiser complaints
(sourced to an unnamed article and Twitter chatter) that Meta's ad system was producing higher
costs per conversion around this period due to automated-system/attribution issues, with a
theory offered that platforms like Meta and Google are incentivized to over-claim attribution
credit when data is incomplete since they're competing to keep advertisers spending on-platform.
(3) A Burger King marketing case study: Burger King reportedly partnered with a Brazilian delivery
app (Rappi) so that anyone ordering a McDonald's item (e.g., a Big Mac or fries) via the app would
instead be sent a Whopper or fries from a nearby Burger King — a guerrilla campaign exploiting
Burger King's proximity-siting strategy near McDonald's locations, reportedly shut down once
McDonald's/Rappi caught on. Context on Burger King's ownership (3G Capital, the Brazilian
investment firm behind Burger King, Anheuser-Busch InBev, Kraft Heinz, and Tim Hortons/Restaurant
Brands International) is discussed, along with a book recommendation ("The 3G Way"). (4) A segment
on AI/remote "virtual employee" customer-service roles (e.g., Philippines-based agents staffing
hotel/fast-food kiosk screens for far less than US minimum wage) framed as a market response to
rising US minimum wages. (5) A segment on brand-employed social-media personas — John Deere's
"Chief Tractor Officer" role cited as an example of a corporation hiring a person to be its
branded TikTok/social face — with a discussion of the succession/brand-continuity risk if that
person leaves, and speculation about AI-based likeness/persona continuity as a mitigation. (6) A
closing SEO segment: a Twitter-sourced case study (CJ Eats Recipes) is cited as an example of a
content site that not only survived recent Google core/helpful-content updates but grew traffic,
attributed to strong owned-social distribution (1.1M TikTok, 976K Instagram followers) combined
with original photography, authentic UGC/comments, and real product/shop integration — used to
reinforce a stated belief that diversifying traffic sources (audience/brand strength, not just
on-page SEO) is the more durable defense against algorithm volatility.
