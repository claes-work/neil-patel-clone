# Essential Business Tips to Accelerate Your Companies Growth

## Summary
Second half of the same **My First Million** conversation (part 1:
[[2023-04-20-yt-xtYv67lnlzk]]). Debates agency vs. software economics: acknowledges
software gets better public-market multiples (HubSpot vs. Accenture) and higher
revenue-per-employee, but argues that for a private, never-going-public business,
profitability and personal enjoyment matter more than the delivery model; cites
consulting-client LTVs of **6-8 years** as comparable in quality to SaaS retention, and
notes headcount-management "headaches" scale with any large business regardless of
software vs. services. Says the agency is growing **~60%+ year over year** at its current
size and reaffirms he has no interest in selling. ★ **States he never serves as CEO of his
own companies** ("I'm a terrible manager, one of the worst managers ever") and installs an
experienced operating CEO **from day one** of every company he starts; names **Mike
Gullickson** as NP Digital's CEO, previously **president of iProspect** (a Dentsu-owned
agency, ~4,000-5,000 employees) — consistent with [[../entities/mike-gullickson]]. States
he personally put in **~$5M of his own money** to start the agency ("bootstrapped, but not
really bootstrapped"), and that NP Digital is now a **nine-figure revenue** business.
Contrasts his three business types — content/media (fun, but a fresh "bake the cake every
day" grind: newsletters/podcast), software (build once, refine, no need to reinvent), and
services/agency (his least-favorite part: constant client hand-holding/expectations) —
noting each has trade-offs, including an aside about e-commerce supply-chain/warehouse
pain from a company he runs. ★ **States he will not give his children money** beyond
survival/altruistic-purpose support (e.g., funding a child who wanted to volunteer
full-time abroad), framing unconditional wealth transfer as removing "agency"; the family
has a trust that caps support to true-need situations. Says his intended inheritance is
character, mentality, and skills, not cash. Describes the family's philanthropy division
of labor: his **wife selects and vets all causes** and does the hands-on
volunteering/site-visits (framed by Neil as inefficient for him personally — he argues his
time is better monetized so she can donate more, rather than him volunteering directly).
Cites the family's giving rules: avoid high-overhead organizations, favor self-sustaining
programs. Names two specific programs: funding **women's education in India and Africa**
(pay tuition in exchange for the recipient returning to teach in their home village) —
switched from an earlier men's-education version of the same program after observing men
had a much lower completion/return rate; and a **"growing gardens" nutrition program**
supporting AIDS patients' medication adherence (malnourishment reduces medication
efficacy) that also produces surplus food for the wider village. States the family has
donated for **~10-11 years**, with giving accelerating over the last 5-6. On investing:
names **Apple, Amazon, Google, Facebook, Microsoft, HubSpot, Salesforce, Adobe, Atlassian,
Shopify** among fewer than 20 public holdings, deliberately concentrated in tech with no
diversification into other sectors, plus angel investments he cites as a strong-performing
category alongside public equities.
