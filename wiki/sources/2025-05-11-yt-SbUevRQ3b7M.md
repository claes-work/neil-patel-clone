# The New Rules of Marketing: AI, SEO, Sales & Branding (Marketing School)

## Summary

★ Landmark-worthy for later L3 promotion: a long (~17k-word), framework-dense co-hosted episode. Neil's durable frameworks here: (1) his **conference / event-driven sales playbook** — QR-code lead capture on speaking slides (~18% audience scan rate, offer = "download the deck"), a mapped-in-CRM sales cycle where enterprise takes **~1 year 10 months** from stage to signature, plus the full pre-event routine (press interviews → existing clients → speak → meet big-booth spenders → stay for every Q&A); (2) **"the fortune is in the follow-up"** paired with **land-and-expand relationships that follow a buyer from company to company**; (3) **"search everywhere optimization"** extended into the AI-search era — AI Overviews turn impressions into the hot commodity while clicks/zero-click rise, so presence becomes a **branding play**; (4) his **CRO-as-core-skill** identity ("I can improve almost any website's conversions without AB tests"); (5) the **build-it-yourself-and-sell-to-your-own-audience** doctrine — **"distribution is the moat, data is second"** — and a nuanced **"AI for low-stakes, humans for high-stakes"** rule on automating outbound.

A rambling, story-rich Marketing School episode recorded just after Neil returned from Web Summit Rio and just after Eric hosted a private ~185-operator marketing summit in Miami (a YPO event). The two trade conference-monetization playbooks, dissect AI Overviews / AEO / GEO with data Eric brings from Jim Yu (BrightEdge), Kevin Indig, and Bain, argue that black-hat tactics for AI search are as short-sighted as black-hat SEO ever was, and react to a wave of AI-first company manifestos (Shopify, Duolingo, Zapier) and Greg Eisenberg's "new startup playbook." Recurring thesis on both sides: give first, think long-term, build distribution, own your audience, and treat AI as leverage you must learn now — but keep humans on the highest-stakes touches.

## Key claims

**On conferences & event-driven sales (Neil's core playbook)**
- Neil's speaking-to-lead machine: he puts a **QR code on his slides**; on average **~18% of the audience scans it** (consistent across industries and countries worldwide), and the offer that converts best is simply **"download the deck."** (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- The scan captures full qualification data (employee count, revenue) into a CRM, not just an email address, so leads can be scored. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Mapped sales cycle from event leads: **SMBs close in ~3 months at the earliest, ~5 months at the latest** — if an SMB hasn't closed in 5 months Neil considers it dead (still follows up, but treats it as dead). **Enterprise averages ~1 year 10 months** from Neil speaking to the deal actually being signed, because enterprise budgets are allocated/procured before the year starts. (Neil, 2025-05-11 — figures from NP Digital's own CRM tracking; approximate averages)
- Neil's full pre-event routine to maximize revenue: (1) hit up the PR/journalists attending to book interviews and earn press for the agency; (2) meet existing clients who'll be there to deepen relationships and open upsells; (3) speak (QR-code capture); (4) set up meetings with other companies/speakers; (5) find whoever else has a big-money booth — ideally a large publicly-traded sponsor — and do a joint panel/session at their booth (at Web Summit he did this with Vibra, Brazil's ~Exxon-Mobil-scale fuel company, ~8,000 gas stations); (6) **always stay for Q&A and answer every single question** rather than rushing off. Neil frames it as "a pure numbers game." (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Neil's newer format that's working: instead of chaotic booths/private lunches, run **your own small private event the day after** a big conference, co-chaired with a partner who covers expenses — invite ~20–30 people, give a ≤30-minute talk, seat people at tables of 4–5, have each table pick its single biggest problem, then go table-to-table on ~5-minute timers giving solutions, and have attendees teach each other the best answers. Builds deep relationships. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Booths generally don't pay off for NP Digital; Neil bartered booths from organizers and found they "did nothing," and dinners consistently beat booths for him. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Both are speaking at HubSpot's **Inbound (Sept 3–5)**; last year their Marketing School session drew ~700 people plus a private ~200-person table-competition mini-session — Neil values the intimate format for building deeper connection even though it doesn't scale to thousands. (Neil + Eric, 2025-05-11)

**On follow-up & land-and-expand relationships**
- "The fortune is in the follow-up" — a recurring Marketing School mantra both invoke; the point is you keep adding value/content over time without asking for the sale, because most prospects simply aren't ready to buy yet. (Neil + Eric, 2025-05-11)
- Neil's biggest observed growth driver: **a relationship follows the person, not the company.** He tells of a marketer he couldn't win as a client at Deezer, who then moved to Oracle (sent an RFP), then became CMO of a new venture (founder worth ~$20–50B) where NP Digital finally landed a contract — then "land and expand" across the divisions. Keep the relationship alive across every company someone moves to. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Two conditions for this to work: **be helpful and consistent** (which requires being on top of your content), **and be kind.** "If you're kind and consistent over a long period of time, people will remember you." But if you win the relationship and then staff it with your **B-team who screws it up, they'll never follow you again** — you have to be consistently good over a long period. (Neil, 2025-05-11)

**On SEO, AI Overviews & AEO/GEO**
- AI Overviews are reshaping search into a "jaw"-shaped graph: **impressions are skyrocketing while click-through rate drops** — people search more often, so you "have to earn the click more," but impressions become a hot commodity. Neil recasts this as further proof of his **"search everywhere optimization"** thesis: don't rely on one basket, optimize across every channel people search. (Eric brings the BrightEdge/Bain data; the "search everywhere / one basket" framing is Neil, 2025-05-11)
- On zero-click: a Bain study (via Eric) found **~80% of consumers now rely on zero-click results in at least 40% of their searches**, and **~60% of traditional searches end without the user going to another destination.** (Eric, 2025-05-11 — cited third-party figures)
- Neil's read on GEO/AEO visibility data (Kevin Indig's SimilarWeb comparison of Google vs ChatGPT visibility across credit cards, handbags, earbuds): the same sites do NOT simply carry over from organic to LLM citations, and where LLMs cite Google itself as a top source it "doesn't make sense" — Google is mostly scraping the web and re-displaying it, so ChatGPT should be **pulling from the original sources, not from Google.** (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- **Black-hat for AI search is the same mistake as black-hat SEO.** People are already selling "LLM optimization" that games ChatGPT's thumbs-up/down reinforcement signals (a "$15k/month, rank-or-don't-pay for one keyword" pitch Eric surfaces). Neil's stance: it's not new (he gets pitched CTR-manipulation-by-fake-Gmail-IP-addresses schemes yearly and refuses), and even if it works short-term it stops working and can **taint your domain/brand** and make future ranking too hard. **Think long-term; this is a channel that's here to stay.** (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Optimizing/doing black-hat for AEO right now is too early — the space will change rapidly, so Neil prefers to "sit back and wait" and arrive as the fourth or fifth wave rather than the first. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Data caveat Neil raises: even tools with billions of data points (Ubersuggest, Datos, BrightEdge) are a tiny sample vs Google's ~13.6–13.8 billion searches/day, so their trend data can be skewed. (Neil, 2025-05-11 — Google-searches/day figure approximate/uncertain, Neil hedged the number)

**On branding & channel diversification**
- Presence in AI/search results is a **branding play**: if you're not there, your competitors are, and that eats your future revenue. If people continually see you they're far more likely to buy later. Neil cites Apple, Nike, Visa, Mastercard, Range Rover, Louis Vuitton, Hermès as proof branding compounds. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- The best marketers are usually **world-class at exactly one channel** before diversifying (Eric's observation, citing Sean Cannell/YouTube and "Sharan"/email). Even multi-channel operators excel most at one. (Eric, 2025-05-11)
- Neil's own strongest channel: started in **SEO**, moved through Digg/Reddit-style social, and for the last ~5 years his strongest is **CRO** — which he treats as a channel/philosophy because it multiplies every other channel. "I can go to almost any type of website and I usually can improve the conversions without AB tests." If forced to name a traditional channel, he'd say **conferences.** (Neil, 2025-05-11)

**On the future of marketing (one-click / done-for-you / in-platform)**
- The marketing future is trending toward **one-click, done-for-you execution**: tools won't just tell you what to fix (SEO audit, creative, landing pages, offers) — they'll offer to "just do it all for you," one click, then let you evaluate. (Raised in the AI-future exchange; leans Eric — see attribution notes)
- Neil's addition: the future is also **in-platform marketing** — convert people without leaving the platform (Facebook Lead Forms, social shopping on TikTok/Instagram, Google running in-app ad experiments; "if Google doesn't do it, ChatGPT will and Google will have no choice but to copy"). CRO nuance: in-platform leads are **cheaper but lower quality** — landing-page conversions on your own site cost more per lead but convert better downstream; run the numbers on whether the quality gap justifies the cost gap. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Technology's whole arc is making things easier/faster (farming → industrial age → AI as "synthetic intelligence"), so the one-click future is coming regardless. (Neil, 2025-05-11)

**On AI in business & building**
- Neil is seeing **mass-scale inbound** from solo founders and single-store owners who built an AI tool for themselves (AI "digital employees," AI booking agents) and now want to white-label/partner — NP Digital routes these to a team member (Caio) but rarely partners. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Nuanced automation rule: **use AI for low-stakes, keep humans on high-stakes.** Neil would NOT let AI write the outbound email to close a seven-figure enterprise deal today — but would use AI to research faster, then review, craft, and adjust tone. He sees AI eventually involved in most aspects of running a business. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- **AI misreads data at the edges**, so verify before it acts: LinkedIn employee counts are often wrong (an agency may show "300 employees" but be owned by a 100,000-employee holding company / be a subsidiary of a ~$20B-market-cap public company). "You don't want to burn a relationship over AI messing up an email." NP Digital uses AI to send first-touch acquisition/outbound emails at volume ("works like a charm") but does the truly important ones manually. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- On AI-first company manifestos (Neil endorses the spirit): **if it can't fit in a few paragraphs, most people won't read it** — and even then you're lucky to get half your employees to read it. (Neil, 2025-05-11)

**On distribution, VC & the startup playbook**
- **"Distribution is the moat now. Data is the second moat."** If you have the audience/distribution, building the tool is the easy part; most engineers don't want to learn marketing, "but if you can't sell it and you don't have the audience, it doesn't matter." (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Build-vs-partner doctrine: if a tool isn't OpenAI-hard to build (a few hundred K to build today; a couple million a few years ago), and you have the audience, **build it yourself and sell to your own audience to keep the majority of the revenue/profit** rather than partnering. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- **Venture capital is for scale, not creation** — unless the thing is genuinely capex-heavy (a nuclear plant, a land-grab like early Facebook/Instagram/YouTube). "99% of things out there you don't need to raise money for" — you can "vibe code" or "vibe market" them now, be high-agency, and raise later once you've nailed something. A BrightEdge built today wouldn't need the capital it raised in 2013. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- On Greg Eisenberg's "new startup playbook" (part-time contractors + creators + AI agents; $1M from niching down, next $10M from tastefully scaling; agents spinning up ~50 long-tail SEO pages; tiny one-founder empires): Neil largely agrees there's a real shift **but insists on industry edge cases**. He agrees AI generating ~50 long-tail pages is already happening (one-click "we found these opportunities, want us to write them to your guidelines?") — he rejects the strawman of "50,000" pages. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- **Sell outcomes, not software or deliverables.** Neil traces this back to his Kissmetrics/True Ventures sales training ("don't sell software, you're selling outcomes"); agencies put deliverables in contracts mainly as a backing-out/verification mechanism, but the world is moving toward outcome/accountability-based selling. (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Prediction (both, with disagreement on timing): a **one-founder $1B-revenue company** within the next decade — Neil leans "teams of ~10 doing a billion in 5 years" over a solo founder in 10. In the current market, early-stage venture funding still flows (with scale expectations) but later stages have dried up because IPOs and M&A activity are thin. (Neil + Eric, 2025-05-11 — speculative)

**On paid social (TikTok) & the economy**
- TikTok isn't "for broke people" — NP Digital runs a lot of client TikTok ads and finds it works well **depending on what you sell**; physical products (e.g. the Ninja Creami / Ninja Soft Serve) convert really well, whereas Neil doubts mortgage ads would. Advice to a friend second-guessing his ad manager: "if your ad person's performing, listen to him." (Neil, 2025-05-11)
- Economic reality check (framed as: this doesn't mean people are "broke"): Neil cites the **median US bank balance at ~$8,000** (mean ~$62,000, which he notes is skewed by the wealthy), an Instagram reel where most Californians surveyed had under $1,000, and argues no single channel's audience has "tons of money" — the average bank balance just isn't high, amid a widening gap between the super-rich and super-poor. (Neil, 2025-05-11 — figures recalled/looked-up, approximate)
