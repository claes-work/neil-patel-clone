# What Are Backlinks and How Do They Work? ★

> ★ L3-candidate: Foundational explainer of backlinks as Google's top ranking factor, the quality/relevance/authority dimensions Neil weighs, and a concrete link-building tactic — a strong basis for a distilled SEO concept page.

## Summary

In this solo explainer, Neil Patel defines backlinks as one website linking to another and frames them as Google's single most important ranking factor as of 2017. He explains that Google evaluates links not just by quantity but by quality, topical relevance, natural acquisition over time, and the linking site's domain authority. He closes with a recommended tactic — roundup posts — as the quickest way to earn links.

## Key claims

- As of 2017-05-26, Neil states Google uses over 200 ranking factors, and the single factor that most affects rankings is backlinks.
- He cites that Moz interviewed many marketing experts about which factors most affect Google rankings, and they all pointed to backlinks.
- A backlink is defined as one website linking to another; Google treats each link as a "vote," so more links generally mean higher rankings.
- Link quality matters beyond quantity: a link from a high-profile site (his example: Huffington Post) is far more effective than a link from a low-authority site ("Joe the plumber"), partly because higher-authority links are harder to earn.
- Topical relevance matters: a relevant/in-space link outweighs a higher-profile but unrelated one — for a plumbing site, a Home Depot link beats a Huffington Post link because it is on-topic.
- Google watches the pace of link acquisition over time; growth should look natural, and Neil advises against paying for links because Google dislikes it.
- Domain authority is described as a 0–100 authority score, with sites like Google.com, YouTube, and Facebook near 100.
- Domain authority is characterized as a logarithmic scale — moving from 1 to 10 is much easier than moving from 10 to 20.
- The combined recommendation: earn many backlinks, from relevant sites, that have high domain authority, and rankings will climb.
- To check a site's domain authority, Neil points to a paid tool (referenced verbally as "ahrefs.com") and a free alternative, Moz's Open Site Explorer (found by searching "Open Site Explorer," which leads to moz.com).
- Roundup posts are recommended as the fastest link-building tactic: email every expert in your space one question, compile their answers into a polished blog post that names and links to each contributor (including their Twitter/Facebook), then ask each expert to share it.
- The mechanism: experts participate for free press; when they share, the post gets more traffic and eyeballs, raising the probability that others link back and, in turn, rankings climb.
