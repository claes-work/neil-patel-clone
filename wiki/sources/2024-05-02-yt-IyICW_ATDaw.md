# Copying as a marketing strategy: the impact on YouTube shorts, AirChat, & more

## Summary
Three segments. (1) Copying-as-strategy: YouTube Shorts reportedly gets ~70 billion daily views
(the hosts guessed "a couple billion," off by roughly 10x). This opens a defense of copying as a
legitimate, common marketing/product strategy: Neil recalls being criticized in the past for
copying a competitor's tool feature, and directly references his own history — when he built
Crazy Egg, Hotjar later copied several of its features, paralleling Noah Kagan's SumoMe vs.
AppSumo/OptinMonster and other named industry copying pairs. Large companies are cited as serial
copiers (HubSpot's CRM copying Salesforce then undercutting on price; Google Plus copying social
networks; Microsoft Teams copying Slack/Zoom; Facebook copying Snapchat's stories and Foursquare's
check-ins, "crushing" Foursquare). The position taken: obsessing over competitors' features
(not just customers, contra a Jeff Bezos/Patrick Campbell framing debate relayed in the episode)
is necessary to avoid losing market share. (2) AirChat (a Clubhouse-like voice-social app):
skepticism is expressed toward it as a lasting standalone social network — likened to Clubhouse,
predicted to be a feature other platforms eventually absorb/copy rather than a durable platform,
with an anecdote about testing it briefly, concluding it adds friction (reading is described as
faster than listening) and stating a preference for Threads over AirChat due to Meta's algorithmic
reach boost for Threads content at the time. A proprietary stat is cited: crawling "over 5 million"
pieces of Instagram/TikTok/short-form content found subtitles/on-screen text (for silent viewing)
as the number-one common factor across viral short-form videos — ahead of hooks or storytelling.
(3) Communication/attention habits: a described minimalist phone home screen (no messaging/social
notification icons visible), a stated preference for phone calls over texting ("I've trained
people to call me... if I need something from Eric I'll call him"), and a description of going a
full day without answering messages while traveling without major consequence, attributed partly
to having a large team so incoming-message load isn't concentrated on him personally. A leadership
philosophy follows: learning that being personally involved in everything is unnecessary and that
hiring people who are competent/better and delegating fully is the bigger lesson, illustrated by
Dharmesh Shah's (HubSpot) no-direct-reports approach and by his own company history — his
co-founder (Mike Kamo, named) served as CEO for roughly two to three years before a new CEO was
installed; Kamo's operational/sales strength (despite no prior formal CEO title, previously
running his family's car dealership) is credited as his core value-add.
