# A HUGE Trap Just Got Revealed in AI Marketing (Marketing School)

## Summary

A co-hosted Marketing School news-reaction episode (Neil Patel + Eric Siu) with unusually HIGH on-domain marketing density — roughly half the airtime is Eric, so attribution gating matters. The spine and the title's "trap" is **Neil's core argument**: AI is far more "black and white" / verifiable for coding, product and design than for marketing, and marketers keep falling into a **vanity-metric trap** — judging AI content by top-of-funnel engagement uplift (Neil's live anecdote: an India social team getting ~7–8x engagement and 3–4x post volume from AI, while revenue stayed flat) instead of full-funnel revenue. Both hosts hammer the same discipline from different angles: prove the **revenue/ROI** of your AI efforts before celebrating (Eric's counterpart: a $7,200 Anthropic spend that surfaced ~$500k in cuts via a "CFO agent"). Neil then gives a genuinely reusable **AI-CRO** play — using AI to rank countries by checkout/payment drop-off and match the right regional payment processors (humans still do the financial signups and rate negotiation) — plus B2B-vs-B2C notes on Apple Pay/Google Pay. The back half is more news-reaction banter: Stripe provisioning agents as the payment "toll booth," APIs > MCPs and "marketing to agents," SEOs being best-suited to adapt because they've long "marketed to robots," an Anish Acharya (a16z) "end of prioritization / multi-armed bandit" tweet (Eric reading) with Neil's distribution caveat, and a closing MMM / incrementality-testing riff.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)

- AI is much more "black and white" / verifiable for coding, product and design than for marketing; marketing (ad creative, copy) still needs a human in the loop and can't just be let loose. Engineers/product/designers are also the biggest cost centers at tech companies, which is why coding gets the AI mandate first. (Neil framed the marketing-vs-coding split; Eric added "coding is verifiable, like math," 2026-04-07) — ai-marketing.
- **The AI-marketing vanity-metric trap:** a company (India social team) got ~7–8x engagement per post and 3–4x post volume from AI-generated social content, timed to trending news/marketing topics — but revenue stayed roughly flat (that channel drove <1% of revenue before AI; short, self-serve sales cycle, free trial → ~$19/mo). Marketers celebrate top-of-funnel lift and ignore the full funnel. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — ai-marketing, analytics-cro, social-media. ★
- They don't need more time to *measure* whether the AI experiment works (short self-serve sales cycle = tracking is not the bottleneck); they need more time to *adjust their approach* and drive conversions. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — ai-marketing, analytics-cro.
- Timing/relevance is a real driver of AI social performance: cranking content on played-out topics won't perform; picking what's being talked about in the news/marketing world right now is why the India team's engagement spiked. (Neil, describing the team, 2026-04-07) — social-media.
- **AI-CRO beyond the obvious:** instead of "AI, look at my pricing page," use AI to analyze checkout/payment drop-off *by country* — credit-card processors behave differently per market (Adyen strong in Europe; Brazil favors Boleto over PayPal). AI ranked every country by worst credit-card→checkout conversion (Colombia was worst for them) and recommended the best processors per region. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — analytics-cro.
- Division of labor with agents: **agents analyze** drop-off and propose payment-processor options; **humans** do the actual processor signups (too much personal/financial/banking detail to hand an agent) and negotiate better rates than the AI quotes, then test. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — analytics-cro, ai-marketing.
- The math on this: if the best country converts ~5% and you lift every country to at least ~2.5%, that's ~4% more revenue, most of which drops to the bottom line; stack small wins (4% here, 2% there) and you grow the business 10%+. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — analytics-cro.
- Payment options are a conversion lever and count as marketing/CRO: for **B2B**, adding Apple Pay / Google Pay does **not** boost conversions as much as people think; for **B2C** it's **massive**. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — analytics-cro.
- When you take AI work to your boss/CEO, lead with the **revenue impact** you're driving — not the tool or the dashboards. (Neil-leaning wrap; theme shared by both, 2026-04-07) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- **SEOs are best-suited to adapt to the AI/agent world** because they've been "marketing to robots" and dealing with shifting algorithms (Google, Bing bot, Instagram, Facebook, Reddit) for years; in the new world you just keep playing the game of pleasing the bots. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — seo.
- **Distribution caveat to "prioritization is dead":** even if AI makes it cheap to test 50 landing pages/features "Monte Carlo" style, it fails without traffic — you need enough eyeballs to get usage/signal; builders who ignore distribution fall flat, so you must learn to acquire or buy traffic. (Neil, reacting to the a16z tweet, 2026-04-07) — paid-ads, seo, agency-entrepreneurship.
- **MMM + incrementality will reshape 2026:** AI makes media-mix modeling affordable for companies of any size (was enterprise-only, too expensive), giving better pre-launch read on what will work; incrementality testing answers whether the ad *caused* the sale or just *took credit* for a buyer who'd have converted anyway — the real test is turning advertising off and measuring the revenue difference. (Neil, 2026-04-07) — analytics-cro, paid-ads.
- Companies blaming AI for layoffs are largely using it as an "excuse to cut" — they over-hired in the pandemic / low-interest-rate era and higher rates now bite. (Neil said "excuse to cut"; Eric added the over-hiring/rates framing, 2026-04-07) — agency-entrepreneurship, ai-marketing.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> if it doesn't drive revenue, who cares? And I think that's a big part marketers get wrong with AI. They look at only top of funnel metrics, and I'm not saying you should ignore them, but you need to look at the full stack.

> I actually don't think they need more time to figure out if it's working or not. I think they need more time to adjust their approach.

> we had AI analyze which countries have abnormally high drop-off rates from credit card pricing page to credit card, credit card to checkout… it listed in order every single country… that had the worst conversion rate. I think Columbia [Colombia] was number one for us.

> The credit card processing we have humans sign up for that. That is really hard to do with agents cuz it requires a lot of personal and financial details and banking details that we personally don't feel comfortable to give to agents. But the agents are analyzing which ones have the biggest drop-off and then providing solution options. And then we go and call and we negotiate the rates cuz you can get better rates than whatever the agents tell you.

> For B2B, Apple Pay and Google Pay does not boost conversions as much as people think. For B2C, adding Apple Pay and Google Pay [is] massive.

> I don't think it's hard for at least SEOs to adapt because you've been marketing to robots for a very long time… we've been having to deal with this for such a long time. I think SEOs are some of the… best suited people to optimize for bots cuz in this new world, you got to just keep playing the game of pleasing them.

> If you don't know how to acquire traffic or buy traffic, this new way does not work because you need enough eyeballs to test out this Monte Carlo style. Without the eyeballs, you can have 50 features, you just won't have usage on any of them, so you won't know what's working or not.

> a lot of people would have bought your product even if you didn't, even though they clicked on the ad, and should the ad get credit? … You need to actually figure out if you turn off your advertising, what's the difference that you're generating in revenue?
