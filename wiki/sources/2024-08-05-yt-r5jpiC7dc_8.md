# The MIT Neurosurgeon who is now unemployed, and Microsoft & John Deere kill their DEI teams (Marketing School)

## Summary
A short, technical-difficulty-shortened co-hosted Marketing School episode with two segments that both drift off pure marketing. In part one Eric shares a viral 48-minute YouTube video from an unnamed "MIT-educated neurosurgeon now unemployed" (recorded walking in the mountains, ~5.5M→7.4M views, grown from near-zero to ~181–193k subscribers in about two weeks) whose thesis is that root-cause lifestyle factors, not surgery, drive spine-health outcomes. Neil's marketing/business takeaway is the transferable one: most professionals reach for a default response (in medicine, pills and surgery; in business, "hire more people" or "spend more on marketing") instead of diagnosing the underlying cause — he asks why under-resourced competitors get traction when you don't, and grounds it in his own health anecdotes (going bald rather than accept drug side effects; acne mostly resolved via diet not medication; a sty doctor who chases root causes over repeat paid injections). Eric frames the neurosurgeon story through incentives ("show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome," credited to Charlie Munger) and both hosts converge that the video succeeded because of authenticity — Neil generalizes that unedited, iPhone-shot, "too-real-to-be-polished" content often beats highly produced content in marketing. Part two reacts to Microsoft and John Deere shuttering their DEI teams (and Google cutting DEI spending); Eric drives the corporate/political commentary (Microsoft's leader calling it "no longer business critical," mission-first, screening candidates on their DEI stance) while Neil offers a measured merit-based-hiring principle: hire and promote on character and ability, treat diversity as an outcome of seeking the best people (who bring different viewpoints), not as the selection criterion. Attribution here matters: the incentives framing, the DEI-rollback commentary, and both ad reads (Single Grain, the Agency Owners Association) are Eric's; Neil's persona-relevant material is the root-cause-diagnosis parallel, the authenticity point, and the merit-hiring principle.

## Key claims
- Root-cause vs. default response — the core transferable lesson: most professionals apply a rote default response instead of diagnosing the underlying cause. In medicine that default is pills and surgery; in business it's "you've got a problem, so hire more people" or "spend more on marketing." Neil's better questions: why isn't it actually working? do you need to spend more, or to get more creative? and how do competitors with one-tenth of the money get traction when you don't? People (and businesses) should figure out the underlying cause rather than reach for the default. (Neil, 2024-08-05)
- Supporting personal health anecdotes for the same root-cause thinking: Neil chose to go bald rather than keep taking a hair-loss medication once he learned of bad long-term side effects; his teenage acne resolved ~80% through non-medication/diet changes (another ~10–15% via lighter medication, the rest with age), because that doctor asked what he was eating and doing rather than pushing Accutane; and his sty doctor, despite making "$300–400" per injection visit, pushes compresses, diet and cleansing changes to find the root cause instead of just re-charging him — good practitioners chase the underlying cause. (Neil, 2024-08-05)
- Authenticity wins in marketing: the neurosurgeon video succeeded because it's authentic, and authenticity is what performs "extremely well in marketing." This is also why unedited, iPhone-shot social content can outperform highly produced, "too professional"-looking content — it's real and more relatable. (Neil, 2024-08-05)
- Merit-based hiring principle: hire and promote on the content of someone's character and whether they can do the job — immutable traits (race, gender, sexual preference) are irrelevant to the decision. Neil says NP Digital has leaders who "would fall under DEI" (e.g., a person-of-color leader in Utah he rates as a "badass," and a gay collaborator he currently builds with) who were hired, raised and promoted purely on performance, not on identity. The stated goal is the best people, not the demographic box. (Neil, 2024-08-05) `(political-adjacent — kept to the hiring-philosophy takeaway; see Attribution notes)`
- Diversity as an outcome, not a criterion: the hirers Neil knows aren't refusing candidates by gender, ethnicity or race — they want the best people for the job and hope to end up with a mix, because different backgrounds provide different viewpoints. He notes Google cut DEI spending without eliminating the department. (Neil, 2024-08-05) `(political-adjacent — dated)`

## Notable verbatim quotes
> How is it that some competitors who have one tenth of money are doing well and getting traction but you're not? ... I think just in general, in professions, people need to start figuring out the underlying cause or the issue versus just going to the default response.

> The reason this video did well is authenticity. What does extremely well in marketing is authenticity. It's also why on social media you see the videos filmed with these kind of phones... people are just taking out their iPhone and recording, versus something that's highly edited. [Unedited content] can do extremely well because it's authentic, it's real, it's more relatable versus something that's highly edited and looks just too professional.

> I never looked at someone being like "you're this color of your skin, you should get a raise for it." I don't look at the color of the skin. I'm like, "you're just amazing at what you do and you should be promoted."

> We just care if you're good people and you're good at what you do — and you can find people in all categories for that. You just have to look. Can you get the job done? Do you have good character?
