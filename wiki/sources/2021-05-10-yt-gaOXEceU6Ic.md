# Welcome to Growth Hacking Unlocked! Free Course with Neil Patel

## Summary
Launches a new free training course, "Growth Hacking Unlocked" (two weeks, six lessons,
worksheets at neilpatel.com/training), Lesson 1. Neil frames the course's method as
learning growth tactics not just from his own agency work (NP Digital) but from case
studies of other founders/companies (Microsoft named as the Lesson 1 subject). Repeats
his standard self-introduction (client roster incl. Microsoft, Amazon, NBC, Airbnb,
Facebook; Obama top-100-entrepreneurs-under-30 self-citation) with an explicit self-
deprecating framing — "I don't think I know everything... I'm not the smartest person out
there" — arguing his tactics are worth following only because they're evidenced by
sustained work with major brands, not because of personal genius. States his core belief
that learning from OTHER founders' mistakes and journeys (not just your own) is what
separates great entrepreneurs/marketers from mediocre ones. Delivers a detailed Bill
Gates/Microsoft case study as the lesson's content: Gates dropped out of Harvard in 1974
after founding Microsoft with childhood friend Paul Allen; the 1976 MITS contract to build
a BASIC operating system for early microcomputers as the inflection point; an "idea man"
anecdote (Gates checked into a hotel with legal pads/pencils, emerged five days later with
thousands of bytes of assembly code, handed it off, and left for Harvard) illustrating
early hyper-focused execution; the 1980s IBM OS deal (IBM was 6% of the S&P at the time)
as the company-making break; the 1990 Windows launch (alongside Apple, first GUI) as the
product that captured the OS market; the 1986 IPO, subsequent stock splits, and the
creation of 3 billionaires (Gates, Allen, Ballmer) plus an estimated 12,000 employee
millionaires. Frames a "copy-the-leader" lineage: Google modeled itself partly after
Microsoft (mobile OS, later Chromebooks), Facebook modeled after Google, and companies
like Snapchat/WhatsApp modeled after Facebook (which itself now owns WhatsApp and
Instagram). Cites a Gates quote on the primacy of talent ("Take away our 20 best
employees, and... Microsoft would become an unimportant company") and closes on the
"relentless pressure even at the top" lesson — a 1995 Gates quote on NBC that past success
guarantees nothing if you fall behind technically.
