# How To Leverage The Top Retailers In The World - Module 1 - Part 2 - eCommerce Unlocked

## Summary
eCommerce Unlocked lesson on choosing sales channels, opening with a brief e-commerce
history timeline (1979 Aldrich "teleshopping," 1991-peak French Minitel, 1994 SSL via
Netscape, 1995 Amazon bookstore launch and eBay launch, 1998 PayPal, 2001 Amazon mobile
commerce, 2005 Amazon Prime and Etsy launch). Frames channel selection as
product/brand-fit driven rather than "picking popular sites": Amazon (55% of Americans
start product searches there, per cited research; huge market cap), eBay (auction/
buy-it-now, no fees under 50 listings/month, best for vintage/collectibles — Neil's own
example: rebuying the same replacement sunglasses there), Etsy (handmade/custom goods,
listing + transaction fees, multi-billion-dollar market cap), Newegg (technology
products specifically; "terrible channel" for e.g. clothing). Closes urging sellers to
weigh audience location, preferred payment processor (Apple Pay/PayPal/Google Wallet),
and to start with an owned website while recognizing marketplaces bring pre-existing
traffic that owned sites must build themselves.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-06-25, Neil cited that 55% of Americans begin product searches on Amazon
  (attributed to unnamed "research," not independently verified here).
- As of 2020-06-25, Neil said in 2018 holiday season, 40% of all e-commerce purchases
  were made via smartphone (unsourced datapoint), used to support a "smartphones now
  outpace desktop for e-commerce" claim.
- As of 2020-06-25, Neil's stated channel-fit heuristic: match sales channel to brand
  positioning — vintage/collectible → eBay, handmade/custom → Etsy, technology →
  Newegg; picking a channel wrong for the product (e.g. clothing on Newegg) is "a
  terrible channel" choice regardless of the channel's overall size.
- As of 2020-06-25, Neil said most sellers should start with their own website but
  must recognize the tradeoff: owned sites require doing their own traffic generation,
  while third-party marketplaces (Amazon, Walmart) already have "the eyeballs" —
  echoes the existing wiki's product-vs-platform distribution tension.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "You don't just have to sell products on your own websites... you could take some of these other stores that are really popular, like Amazon or Walmart, and start selling products on these sites as well."

> "Newegg works really well if you're selling technology, but if you're selling clothing, Newegg would be a terrible channel."

> "For example, I wear the same sunglasses. And every time my baby breaks one of my sunglasses, I go to eBay and buy the same old sunglass all over again... it's one of the only places where I can get it."
