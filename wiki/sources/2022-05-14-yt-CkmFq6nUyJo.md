# Is Having a Digital Marketing Certification Really Important?

## Summary
Neil argues that formal digital-marketing certifications are not necessary and, on
their own, don't teach implementation or produce results (as of 2022-05-14). He frames
the first question as one of purpose: are you trying to get a job, run your own
business, start an agency, or freelance? Certifications may help for certain job
listings that explicitly require them, but for entrepreneurship/freelancing he says
learning "in the trenches" is superior: pick one channel, learn fundamentals, and test
tactics for at least six months. He recounts his own path — doing SEO for his own sites
first, reaching six-figure income as a teenager, then later losing that money, before
eventually applying the same self-taught approach to client work. He recommends: follow
strong marketing blogs (naming Backlinko and his own neilpatel.com), participate in
Facebook groups/Reddit/Quora, attend conferences (since much discussed in person isn't
covered online), read marketing books, study/dissect competitors' marketing, use tools
like Ubersuggest for free competitive intelligence and Facebook Ad Library to see
competitors' running ads, and find a mentor. He concludes that online courses/
certifications aren't inherently bad and can offer a "kickstart," but they only go so
far — real skill comes from experimenting and implementing on your own site/business.

## Key claims
- Digital-marketing certifications alone don't teach implementation or guarantee
  results, since marketing is fundamentally results-based (as of 2022-05-14).
- For entrepreneurship or freelancing (as opposed to jobs that explicitly require a
  certification), Neil recommends picking one channel, learning its fundamentals, and
  testing tactics for at least six months as the best way to learn (as of 2022-05-14).
- Neil says he reached six-figure income doing SEO as a teenager, working on his own
  sites first before taking on other people's, and later lost that money entirely
  before eventually reapplying the same self-taught approach professionally
  (self-reported, as of 2022-05-14).
- Recommended ongoing-education sources: marketing blogs (Backlinko, neilpatel.com),
  Facebook groups/Reddit/Quora communities, in-person conferences, marketing books,
  studying competitors' marketing directly, Ubersuggest for competitive intelligence,
  Facebook Ad Library for competitor ad research, and finding a mentor
  (as of 2022-05-14).
- Online courses and certifications are not bad and can provide a useful "kickstart,"
  but they only go so far — durable skill comes from experimenting and implementing on
  your own website/business (as of 2022-05-14).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Marketing is based on results. All the theoretical knowledge on the Earth won't save you if you can't deliver results for the products that you're working on.

> Learning in the trenches, for me, is the best way to learn. Pick one channel, learn the fundamentals, start testing and try out those tactics for at least six months.

> I was making six figures as a teenager, I'm not tryna brag... because a few years later, I lost all the money I made.

> Taking online classes and getting certifications isn't bad but it only gets you so far. It can get you a kickstart, but you still need to go out there, experiment and try it on your own to do really well.
