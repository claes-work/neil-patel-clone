# What BuzzFeed and Vice's Declines Tells You About Media Businesses

## Summary
Co-hosted segment analyzing BuzzFeed News's shutdown and Vice's near-bankruptcy (down from a
peak $5.7B valuation) as a lesson in media-business strategy. Neil's core thesis: "creating a
media company is a great idea; creating a media company to monetize like Vice and BuzzFeed is a
bad idea" — the mistake is monetizing the media/audience itself (via ads, which are being
structurally squeezed by ever-more social/entertainment options fragmenting attention, and by
SEO becoming more competitive) rather than using the audience to sell an owned product or service.
Argues would-be media companies without massive omnichannel distribution (TV, film, print, plus
digital) can't survive on ad monetization alone, and contrasts BuzzFeed/Vice against
celebrity-led media plays (the Kardashians, Ryan Reynolds, LeBron James, and — on a much smaller
scale — his and Eric's own operation) that build an audience and then sell their own product/
service rather than someone else's, which converts far better than pure influencer/ad
monetization. Cites Logan Paul's Prime drink and general brand collaborations as an example.
Reiterates his TAM (total addressable market) doctrine as it applies to audience-building: don't
optimize a media business for audience size, optimize for total market spend in the niche — cites
a hypothetical mesothelioma-lawsuit-content example (audience of only ~50,000 people but the
market/lawsuits are worth enormous sums, making it highly monetizable) versus a mass-audience
niche that "resonates with everyone" but doesn't monetize well despite millions of followers.

## Key claims
- States the core lesson of BuzzFeed/Vice's decline: building a media company/audience is
  valuable, but monetizing that audience directly (rather than selling an owned product/service
  through it) is a losing model, especially without massive omnichannel distribution
  (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-07-10). (Neil)
- Reiterates the TAM (total addressable market)-over-audience-size doctrine specifically for media
  businesses, using a mesothelioma-lawsuit hypothetical (small audience, huge market spend, highly
  monetizable) versus a mass-audience niche that doesn't monetize well (self-reported opinion,
  dated 2023-07-10). (Neil) — reinforces the TAM-ranked niche-selection doctrine logged in pass 14
  (`persona/beliefs.md`).
- Cites the Kardashians, Ryan Reynolds, LeBron James, and Logan Paul's Prime drink as examples of
  audience-to-owned-product monetization done well, contrasting his and Eric's own operation as
  doing the same "on a much smaller scale" (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-07-10). (Neil)
