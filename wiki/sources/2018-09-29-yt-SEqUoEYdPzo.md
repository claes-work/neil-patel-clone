# WordPress Mistakes to AVOID for Startup Websites

## Summary

Solo tactical. Neil's counter-intuitive take for people launching a new site (most on
WordPress): **the #1 mistake is obsessing over design, templates, and plugins before you
have any traffic.** None of that matters at the start — "all it's going to do is stroke
your ego." A beautiful design nobody sees is worthless; and if people do come and complain,
the design isn't working anyway. Instead of polishing UX/design/plugins with zero
visitors, **just get the site out there and get traction**: create content and share it on
Twitter/Facebook; if you have no following, ask friends to share; if friends won't, **go
comment on related industry blogs** providing value (the name/email/URL comment fields let
people click your name back to your site). Only **once it's working and you know you love
it** should you circle back to design and plugins. The through-line is his recurring
ship-first, traction-before-polish philosophy.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-09-29, the top startup-website mistake is **prioritizing design, templates,
  and plugins over shipping and getting traffic** — polish only strokes the ego early on.
- As of 2018-09-29, **traction comes before aesthetics**: a pretty site nobody visits is
  worthless; user complaints, not your taste, tell you if the design works.
- As of 2018-09-29, early distribution tactics: **create content, share on Twitter/Facebook,
  ask friends to share, and comment on related blogs** (the URL field links back to you).
- As of 2018-09-29, **only after the site is working** should you invest in design and
  plugin decisions.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I want this pretty design, I want it customized, I want all these templates, I want all these plugins… and I hate to say it but none of that really matters."

> "just start your website, get it out there, get traffic."

> "if I told you hey your design is pretty, it looks amazing — what is it going to do? All it's going to do is stroke your ego and make you feel good."

> "just get something out there, get some traction. Once you know that you love what you're doing and it's starting to work, then worry about your design."
