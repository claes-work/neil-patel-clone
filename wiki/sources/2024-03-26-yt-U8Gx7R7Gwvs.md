# Netflix posts to YouTube to steal back the traffic, Building startups via Telegram, and more

## Summary
Several segments. (1) A discussion of Netflix uploading a full Oscar-nominated film (Nimona) to YouTube, getting 2M views in 4 days — framed not as "stealing traffic" but as smart marketing: giving away free samples (like Netflix's per-show superfan social accounts, or the hosts' own habit of watching movie clips on YouTube that lead to a full Netflix watch) to convert attention into subscriptions; broader point that "you have to go where the attention is," illustrated with declining movie-theater ticket sales (WSJ: -15% despite more movies/theaters) as audiences prefer the convenience of streaming platforms, and that publicly-traded incumbents (e.g. AMC) struggle to pivot because they're structurally defending a legacy model. (2) A personal exchange: Neil recently bought (or tried to buy, per his wife's pushback on their "tiny condo") a 100-inch TV; a related anecdote about "Nimona"-style long-form-clip marketing leading to a full watch. (3) A segment on AI-driven automation/distribution experiments: an AI "gift-giving" Zapier workflow (detects birthdays via Google Calendar/Contacts, calls a local flower shop via an AI voice agent, sends a handwritten-style card) is criticized as an unnecessary added cost; Neil states a firm preference for genuinely handwritten notes over any automated/robot-assisted version, citing better response/feedback from customers and clients, and describes writing physical postcards/letters to his daughter three to four times a week when traveling (his son, by contrast, is described as "transactional" — wanting treats rather than notes). (4) A closing segment on whether "community acquisitions" work as an M&A strategy, referencing a webinar with Founder Path's Nathan Latka: cites Outreach's acquisition of Sales Hacker and Stripe's acquisition of Indie Hackers as examples that reportedly haven't retained much post-acquisition engagement (contrasted with HubSpot's acquisition of The Hustle, whose post-acquisition traction is unclear but framed as a rounding error for a ~$30B company); general conclusion that a community "loses its luster" when transplanted to a new owner, similar to outsourcing someone's personal writing voice.
