# Why You Shouldn't Use Exact Match Domains (Don't Make This SEO Mistake)

## Summary
Neil argues against buying generic "exact match domain" (EMD) names (e.g.,
hotels.com-style domains matching the target keyword exactly), using his own
neilpatel.com Search Console data as the counter-example: he gets ~81% CTR when
people search "Ubersuggest" specifically because of brand loyalty, not because of a
matching domain string. His core mechanism claim: if a domain matches a generic
keyword but the site DOESN'T earn strong branded CTR on searches for that keyword
(example: nurse.com ranking #3, not #1, on "nurse"), Google reads the low CTR as a
signal of weak relevance/brand loyalty, which can suppress rankings — the opposite of
the EMD's intended advantage. Sites like hotels.com work only because of massive,
separately-built brand equity (Expedia-backed budget), which is not replicable for
most site owners.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-12-07, advises against buying exact-match-keyword domain names for SEO
  purposes; frames hotels.com as an anomaly explained by brand investment
  (Expedia Group ownership), not by the domain string itself (paraphrase).
- Core mechanism (paraphrase): if searchers Google your brand/domain-matching keyword
  and skip your listing for another result, that low click-through rate signals to
  Google that the site isn't relevant/liked, which can suppress rankings broadly —
  illustrated with nurse.com ranking #3, not #1, for "nurse."
- Self-reported example: neilpatel.com gets ~4.43M Google clicks over a trailing
  28-day window; ~81% CTR specifically on the branded query "Ubersuggest" (self-
  reported, not independently verified).
- General stance: "It's fine to have your keyword in the domain, but you don't want
  your domain name to be a... generic keyword, because it can hurt your overall
  rankings" — recommends brand-first domain naming over exact-match generic naming
  (paraphrase + quote).
- Connects strong branded search performance to broader-keyword ranking lift: cites
  his own rankings for "SEO" (~#4–5) and "online marketing" (~#2) as evidence that
  strong brand CTR helps a site rank for many non-branded terms too, using Nike as the
  generalized analogy (strong brand → ranks well for category terms like "shoes").
