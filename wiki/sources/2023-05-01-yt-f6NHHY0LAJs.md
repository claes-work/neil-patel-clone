# Tips for Beginner Angel Investors

## Summary
Family-office-focused interview (the same conversation as
[[2023-05-06-yt-XTLHQGdcxsY]] — both share the "family office" framing, the host's questions
about structure, and closely related content; this video's cold open and this one's own body
both use the same "friend's jet" anecdote, and the host references the same "Dany Roizman"
family-office-manager framing used across the pairing) in which Neil describes how his
personal-wealth management "team" evolved organically ("Hey, I need help") rather than as a
deliberate family-office launch — a finance-industry contact now handles legal, accounting,
trusts (a grantor trust and a dynasty trust), and investment tracking, in a cost-shared
**"multi-family office"** arrangement with other wealthy individuals. Describes a division of
labor with his wife: she runs philanthropic giving on instinct/emotion (self-deprecatingly
nicknamed the **"tear factor"** — the more a pitch moves her, the more she donates) while he
wants outcome data on programs. Gives a detailed **day-trading anecdote from the March 2023
regional-bank crisis**: after the government signaled it would backstop deposits and JPMorgan
announced a same-weekend deal for First Republic, he bought roughly **$900K of First Republic
stock around $19/share** the following Monday (having tried and failed to fill an order for
$3M), held overnight, and sold the next day for **roughly $1.4M profit**, framing it as a
short-term bet on market overreaction/panic rather than a fundamentals call. Says his own
trading and stock-picking (buying and holding companies he understands — Apple, HubSpot,
Shopify, Google, Amazon) has outperformed his hired money managers, whose mandate he
characterizes as capital preservation ("they won't make you rich, they help you stay rich").
Describes a self-professed minimalist streak inconsistent with typical UHNW spending: drives a
Honda Odyssey as the family car (his wife has "a nicer car"/a Bentley), uses a Maybach with a
driver for work commute ~5-6 times a week purely for productivity (laptop table, connectivity)
rather than status, and says he actively avoids owning a private jet, preferring to fly on
friends' jets for free and let them absorb the maintenance headaches ("that is the best thing
you can do as a rich person"). States the family's giving philosophy in concrete terms: a
blind trust for his children pays only for catastrophic needs (cancer treatment, homelessness)
or values-aligned choices (e.g., unpaid volunteer medical work abroad), not lifestyle spending
("if you have a job, you can figure it out on your own"), and that he would rather donate the
cost difference between a private flight and JetBlue economy ("lay-flat," ~$1-2K) to a friend's
inner-city-kids charity than fly private for convenience. Credits an older Seattle-based mentor
named **John** with formative advice in his early 20s: don't try to figure out investing alone
— "ride the coattails" of successful people you know by co-investing alongside them, learning
from their process while avoiding costly mistakes.
