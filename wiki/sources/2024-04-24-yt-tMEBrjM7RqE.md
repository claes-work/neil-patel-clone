# Long-form content boosts SEO?, The return of SEO A/B testing, $7.6B revenue from the eclipse, & more

## Summary
Four segments. (1) Whether long-form content still matters for SEO: Neil's stance is that it's intent-dependent — a query like "how to tie a tie" is best served by a short video or animated GIF, not long text, while research-style queries can still reward long-form (Wikipedia cited as an example of long-form content that performs well specifically because it is continually updated). A case study (via David G. Quaid on Twitter) is discussed where a company split a single long PDF into 60 short, single-paragraph sub-pages, doubling both blog traffic and traffic to conversion-driving "solutions" pages, with the company later acquired by Rapid7 for $335M. (2) SEO A/B testing is confirmed still active and productive, via a Search Pilot (spun out from Distilled/BrainLabs) roundup of case studies: adding dynamic pricing to title tags (+10% organic traffic), replacing templated with unique content (+14%), highlighting freshness in title tags (+11%), shortening title tags (+11%), moving content out of tabs (+14%), and removing product carousels from category pages (+29%). This leads into a description of Neil's system for sharing research/data with his team: rather than sending a bare link, he always attaches an explicit expectation level (FYI/no response needed, highly recommended, or "pleading" — repeated/emphasized for the highest-priority items), a practice he says he adopted a couple of months prior to the recording after hearing Dharmesh Shah (HubSpot) describe a similar system on Lenny's Podcast; his team reviews and reports on tested experiments during weekly marketing stand-up calls. (3) A discussion of the April 2024 total solar eclipse as a marketing/trend-jacking case study: the Perryman Group estimated ~$7.6B in total economic impact (~$1.6B direct travel spend plus ~$6B in secondary consumption), used to illustrate a "trend-jack whatever's trending" content-marketing principle, with historical examples cited (Oreo's "you can still dunk in the dark" Super Bowl blackout tweet; Goldman Sachs keeping its office lit during Hurricane Sandy as an inadvertent PR/brand-strength signal). (4) Sports sponsorship ROI skepticism: reacting to an unsolicited NBA sponsorship pitch (San Antonio Spurs offering jersey/branding placement at a reported $36M/year, referencing a market rate around $112M/year for top-tier placements), Neil states that based on NP Digital's own brand-recall studies for clients who have purchased jersey, stadium-naming, and league sponsorships, the realistic incremental revenue generated is typically only about 2-3% of the sponsorship spend — reinforced by the Crypto.com Arena naming-rights deal (~$700M) as an example of a very large spend with reportedly negligible measured direct-response return. The takeaway offered is that marketers seeking direct-response growth should generally look elsewhere than large sports sponsorships.

## Key claims
- **Long-form content's SEO value is query-intent-dependent, not universal: short-intent queries (e.g., "how to tie a tie") are better served by short video/GIF content, while research-style queries can still reward long-form — with continual updating (the Wikipedia model) as a key differentiator of what still works.** `(opinion, dated 2024-04-24; attribution: Neil, first-person "I do... it depends on the keyword")`
- **Neil's system for sharing research/data links with his team explicitly tags each link with an expectation level — FYI (no response needed), highly recommended, or "pleading" (repeated/emphasized) — adopted after hearing HubSpot's Dharmesh Shah describe a similar system on Lenny's Podcast; the team reports back on tested experiments during weekly marketing stand-up calls.** `(self-reported operational practice, dated 2024-04-24; attribution: Neil, first-person)`
- **Based on NP Digital's own brand-recall studies for clients who purchased major sports sponsorships (jersey/stadium/league placements), the realistic incremental revenue generated is typically only about 2-3% of the sponsorship spend — used to argue that large sports sponsorships are generally a poor direct-response marketing investment (the Crypto.com Arena ~$700M naming-rights deal is cited as an example of large spend with reportedly negligible measured return).** `(self-reported / opinion grounded in agency data, dated 2024-04-24; attribution: Neil, first-person)`
- **"Trend-jacking" (tying marketing/content to whatever is currently trending, e.g. the April 2024 solar eclipse) is presented as a durable, generalizable content tactic, with Oreo's Super Bowl-blackout tweet and Goldman Sachs' lit-office-during-Hurricane-Sandy moment cited as historical reference examples.** `(opinion, dated 2024-04-24; attribution: uncertain — undiarized)`
