# Revolutionizing Marketing with AI: The Future is Here

## Summary
Part 1 of a two-part "Marketing Against The Grain" debate (with [[2023-05-04-yt-lA55yrabOSQ]]
recorded as the same taped conversation — its cold open reuses a line from later in this
episode's argument, and its opening question picks up directly from this episode's closing
theme) on whether ChatGPT/AI disrupts search and search advertising. Neil argues AI will
change **how** people search (more chat-native) but will not gut the ad economics, because
the majority of ad dollars sit on **transactional keywords** (bottom-of-funnel purchase
intent), not informational queries an AI chatbot could just answer directly — a distinction
he frames as navigational / informational / transactional query types. He is skeptical that
ChatGPT output quality (misinformation risk from scraping an already-imperfect web) will beat
brand-mediated trust for the foreseeable future, citing an paraphrased, admittedly
inexact Eric Schmidt line about brands filtering signal from the "cesspool" of information.
He frames **OpenAI as functionally Microsoft** ("mainly funded by Microsoft and controlled"),
positioning the real contest as Bing vs. Bard rather than OpenAI vs. Google, and argues
first-mover status doesn't guarantee a winner (citing — inaccurately dated by the host in a
later riff, uncorrected here — that Google was not the first search engine to dominate).
He expects Google/search engines to keep monetizing regardless of format shift, citing Danny
Sullivan (Google) having said clicks-to-websites have risen every year despite features like
the Knowledge Graph, and predicts new monetization layers (subscription/verification models
like Twitter's, or embedded referral/affiliate hooks — e.g. "want a lawyer to review this
AI-drafted contract?"). On the chat-as-super-app threat to booking/price-comparison
middlemen (Expedia, KAYAK), Neil calls it "progression": if a chatbot can execute a booking
end-to-end against user-specified constraints (price range, seat type, dates), the ad/bidding
model shifts from per-click keyword auctions toward bidding on the actual transaction/price,
which he says favors platforms that "own" the transaction layer (Google Pay/Apple Pay-style
checkout) over both traditional publishers and single-purpose booking sites. He closes on a
brand-loyalty argument: consumers gravitate to convenience and price, and an entrenched
brand (his examples: Zapier despite cheaper competitors, Nike vs. similar-quality shoes)
survives commoditization pressure from AI tools as long as it keeps delighting the "98%" of
users, not edge cases.

## Key claims
- States that most search-and-advertising revenue is concentrated in transactional
  (bottom-funnel, purchase-intent) keywords, not informational queries, and that AI/chat
  interfaces mainly threaten the informational layer — self-reported, drawing on NP Digital
  managing "billions of dollars" of client ad spend, dated 2023-04-27.
- Frames OpenAI as effectively controlled/funded by Microsoft, and casts the real platform
  contest as Bing vs. Bard rather than OpenAI vs. Google (self-reported opinion, dated
  2023-04-27).
- Cites a McKinsey study (unnamed specifics) as evidence that the primary enterprise value
  of AI is in business-process efficiency and analytics, not content generation, which he
  says Fortune 1000 clients are "less concerned about" since content is "already cheap"
  (self-reported, dated 2023-04-27).
- Predicts chat interfaces will shift transactional advertising from per-click/per-impression
  bidding toward bidding on the underlying transaction/price itself, disintermediating
  price-arbitrage middlemen like Expedia/KAYAK, while favoring platforms that own the
  checkout layer (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-04-27).
- Argues consumer loyalty is driven by convenience, price, and accumulated brand trust more
  than by feature completeness — citing Zapier and Nike as examples of brands retaining
  users despite cheaper/comparable alternatives (self-reported opinion, dated 2023-04-27).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> I don't look at OpenAI as OpenAI. I look at OpenAI as Microsoft... so I look at OpenAI as
> Bing versus Bard.

> If your inputs are off, your outputs are going to be off... for a portion of the queries
> and the responses... you're going to get misinformation as well, inaccurate, wrong,
> whatever it may be.

> The real revenue generation from Google and these search engines when people are typing is
> for a lot of the transactional keywords.

> As a marketer, we don't really care if someone clicks on our website, all we care about is,
> are we generating the revenue?... This is now turning into like affiliate marketing.

> Convenience and price are really hard to beat.
