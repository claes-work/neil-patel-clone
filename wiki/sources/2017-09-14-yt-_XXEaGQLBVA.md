# What's The Difference Between Do Follow and No Follow Links

## Summary
Neil explains the practical distinction between do-follow and no-follow links for SEO. A do-follow link tells search engines you are intentionally passing "link juice" to help a site rank higher, while a no-follow link signals that you don't want to help the linked site rank. Neil advises using no-follow whenever money is involved in the link (advertising, affiliate links) or when the target site is sketchy, and notes that user-generated content like blog comments is no-followed by default (WordPress does this to prevent spammy comment link-building). He recommends do-follow links when citing reputable sources, even competitors. He cautions that link type alone doesn't determine rankings — many other factors matter — though more do-follow links should, in theory, help.

## Key claims
As of 2017-09-14, Neil states:
- There are two types of links: do-follow and no-follow (as of 2017-09-14).
- A do-follow link tells search engines you are linking intentionally to pass link juice and help that site rank higher (as of 2017-09-14).
- A no-follow link tells search engines you don't want to help that site rank higher (as of 2017-09-14).
- No-follow is used when you were paid, for affiliate links, or when something about the target site is sketchy (as of 2017-09-14).
- No-follow is mainly used for advertising and blog comments; user-generated content like blog comments is usually no-followed (as of 2017-09-14).
- WordPress no-follows comments by default to stop marketers from writing spammy comments to gain links (as of 2017-09-14).
- Search engines want you to no-follow links that can be easily manipulated (as of 2017-09-14).
- If you promote an affiliate offer and don't no-follow it, that site can unfairly rank higher because of its many affiliates (as of 2017-09-14).
- When someone is paying you or you make money by linking out (affiliate link or advertisement), use a no-follow (as of 2017-09-14).
- When linking to a reputable site or citing a source — even a competitor — use do-follow (as of 2017-09-14).
- More do-follow links doesn't guarantee higher rankings, and more no-follow links doesn't mean lower rankings; many other factors determine rankings (as of 2017-09-14).
- More do-follow links is supposed to, in theory, help with rankings (as of 2017-09-14).
