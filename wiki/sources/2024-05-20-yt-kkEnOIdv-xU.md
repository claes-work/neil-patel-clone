# OpenAI developing a search engine, A company wants to buy Neil's company, and more (Marketing School)

## Summary
A co-hosted episode anchored by two Neil-heavy threads: (1) reaction to the (then-rumored) OpenAI
search engine and why search itself isn't going away, just changing; and (2) a detailed, first-person
Neil story about a heavily-funded Indian social-media conglomerate that tried to acquire a
majority stake in NP Digital in spring 2024 — including the exact deal structure offered and why
Neil walked away. Along the way Neil explains private-equity "platform" roll-up economics using
NP Digital as the worked example, and shares his own M&A financing details (a company he bought
at 8x that is now near a 2.6x payback, 100% financed, first via CapChase then refinanced through
CIBC at 3.25% + SOFR). Strong persona material: tactical M&A/financing knowledge plus an explicit,
quotable statement of Neil's post-money-milestone motivation ("it has everything to do with
purpose and fulfillment").

## Key claims
- Search isn't dying even as OpenAI builds a search product — as long as humans are curious they
  will keep searching; search engines will change, not disappear. (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- Private-equity "platform" roll-ups: a PE firm buys a large "platform" company (worked example:
  NP Digital, hypothetically ~$50M revenue/$10M profit) and tucks in smaller complementary
  agencies (e.g. a $5M-revenue CRO shop) to cross-sell services across both customer bases,
  expanding combined revenue/profit and earning a better exit multiple — "that's why private
  equity says 1 + 1 = 3." (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- Common ways to fund an acquisition without founder personal cash: seller financing (pay a
  fraction down, rest over 3–5 years), SBA-style debt, friends/family capital, or revenue/invoice
  factoring (paying ~10–15% interest to draw an advance against monthly revenue). (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- Neil's own acquisition ("Adex" — a non-growing but valuable software asset): bought at 8x, now at
  roughly a 2.6x payback multiple; financed 100% (first via CapChase, later refinanced through
  CIBC) at ~3.25% + SOFR (~8.55% effective at the time of recording). (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- Neil's deal style is deliberately "clean," not "creative": pay a straightforward multiple upfront
  plus one deferred tranche rather than structuring elaborate earn-outs — "good companies don't
  want to do creative deals, they want clean deals, and the bigger the company, the cleaner the
  deal typically." (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- **Deal walked away from (2024, dated to on-air):** a company backed by "trillion-dollar" public
  investors (a large Indian social/media conglomerate, name withheld) approached NP Digital wanting
  to buy 51%. The structure offered: 2x EBITDA cash upfront + 3x EBITDA paid over 3 years in
  "rollover equity" tied to the company's own future profits (effectively ~5x total, but funded
  largely by NP Digital's own future earnings) plus 75% of remaining equity/stock. Neil concluded
  the buyer didn't actually have the cash on hand (raising >$1B doesn't mean $1B sits on the
  balance sheet) and declined. (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- Why Neil declined despite the size of the offer: "it has nothing to do with the money anymore, it
  has everything to do with more so purpose and fulfillment in life" — selling would mean starting
  over and having to rediscover purpose; he'd rather keep building toward a long-term vision.
  (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- The deal-structure idea (2x upfront + rollover profit share) is, in Neil's view, still useful
  applied *by* an acquirer tucking in smaller struggling agencies — the target keeps their own
  distributions while ceding a percentage of the combined holding company, letting cross-selling
  synergies compound over ~5 years. (Neil, 2024-05-20)
- The "Batman and Robin" description of the Neil/Eric partnership — sometimes each plays either
  role depending on the deal — originated with **Hiten Shah**. (Neil, 2024-05-20)
