# How to Do SEO For A Tiny Site With No Backlinks

## Summary
Neil Patel argues that a tiny website with few pages and no backlinks can still rank and pull over 100,000 visitors per month by competing on quality rather than quantity. He points to Backlinko (Brian Dean's blog) as proof that a small site with little content and no heavy manual outreach can dominate rankings. His step-by-step method: use Ubersuggest to analyze competitors' top pages and keywords, then create a handful of extremely thorough "definitive guides" that are five-to-ten times better than what ranks now; invest at least 50% of your time promoting each guide via email outreach to the people already linking to and tweeting competitor articles; nurture a community by responding to every comment (which drives brand queries and loyalty); build a repeatable network of content promoters you can return to for each new guide; and continuously mine Ubersuggest's backlinks report for competitors' fresh links as the highest-probability outreach targets. His thesis is that with over a billion blogs, Google does not need more content — it needs a small number of amazing, high-quality pages, so small sites should focus on quality over quantity.

## Key claims
- Sites that have both backlinks and content do well in SEO; sites with neither tend not to (as of 2019-08-17).
- A tiny site with only 20-30 pages can still get hundreds of thousands of visitors per month, even if less than a million-page site (as of 2019-08-17).
- Brian Dean's Backlinko ranks well and gets hundreds of thousands of monthly visitors despite few pages and little manual outreach/link building (as of 2019-08-17).
- Step 1 — content: use Ubersuggest, enter each competitor's URL to see their traffic, top pages, and the keywords those pages rank for (as of 2019-08-17).
- Check whether you've already covered competitors' top topics; adjust existing content or create new content if not (as of 2019-08-17).
- Create in-depth "guides" (definitive/advanced/beginner) so extensive readers feel the rest of the space is useless — aim to be 5-10x better and more thorough than ranking competitors (as of 2019-08-17).
- Ranking is not just about putting keywords in; thoroughness and better user metrics (time on site, low bounce-back) drive outranking over time (as of 2019-08-17).
- Add custom images/graphics using free tools like Canva, or pay via Fiverr (as of 2019-08-17).
- Integrate the most popular relevant competitor keywords (surfaced by Ubersuggest) into your guide (as of 2019-08-17).
- Step 2 — promotion: invest at least 50% of your time promoting each guide; Neil's usual advice is 80%, but detailed guides take so much work that 50% is the realistic floor (as of 2019-08-17).
- Use Ubersuggest's backlinks report on competitor URLs to find everyone linking to their pages, then email 50-100-200 of them per guide to earn links (as of 2019-08-17).
- Use search.twitter.com with competitor URLs to find everyone who tweeted their articles and ask them to share yours; most won't, but a large portion will (as of 2019-08-17).
- Email everyone you linked out to within your guide and ask them to share it — another easy source of social shares (as of 2019-08-17).
- Step 3 — nurture a following: respond to every comment on your site, Facebook, and YouTube to build loyalty, which over time drives social shares, backlinks, higher rankings, and more brand queries (as of 2019-08-17).
- Google looks for brand queries; having more branded searches than competitors signals people love your brand and should rank you higher (as of 2019-08-17).
- Step 4 — build a network of content promoters: after publishing several articles, identify the consistent minority who share/link, and return to that promoter list for every new guide (as of 2019-08-17).
- You don't need a guide every week — once a month, or even every two to three months, is enough if quality is high enough to maintain traffic (as of 2019-08-17).
- Final step — use Ubersuggest's backlinks report to track competitors' new and lost links; people who linked recently are far more likely to link to you than those who linked five years ago (as of 2019-08-17).
- With over a billion blogs (roughly one per seven people), Google doesn't need more content or big sites — it needs amazing, high-quality sites; focus on quality over quantity (as of 2019-08-17).
