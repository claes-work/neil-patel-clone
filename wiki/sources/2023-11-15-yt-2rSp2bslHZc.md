# Will This Destroy Ahrefs' Marketing? (Ep. 2604)

## Summary
Short daily episode reacting to a viral X/Twitter thread in which an Ahrefs customer (Jason Mills) posts a screenshot showing Ahrefs is sunsetting support for "legacy" plans (action required by Nov 20, 2023) — effectively pushing ~15% of heaviest-usage legacy subscribers to upgrade/pay more or leave, with users publicly threatening to switch to Semrush. Eric drives the segment (he shares his screen and reads the thread), framing the thesis that customer service IS a big piece of marketing and that mishandling loyal customers invites public blowback; he cites Peter Drucker and recommends grandfathering. Neil's distinct angle (line-marked "anything else Neil") is a business-economics one: because Ahrefs is highly profitable (~$100M/yr revenue on ~50 employees), there's a point where a company is better off leaving money on the table to keep customers happy, since brand damage is hard to recover from. On competitive positioning, the passage tied to SEO-tool ownership ("we have a few ourselves," i.e. Ubersuggest) frames SEO tools as relatively commoditized/low-differentiation vs. content businesses like Netflix — so when you raise prices with many similar alternatives available, you lose customers. Both hosts stress they still like Ahrefs and are friends with its team (Tim Soulo).

## Key claims
- Customer service is actually your marketing, or a big piece of it — mishandling a support/pricing change (like Ahrefs ending legacy-plan support) can become a major, public marketing problem. (Eric, 2023-11-15)
- Penalizing your heaviest/most-loyal users (Ahrefs stopping renewals for ~15% of legacy subscribers consuming the most data) breeds resentment — a loyal customer feels punished for actually using the product. (attribution: uncertain — likely Eric during screen narration, 2023-11-15)
- Standard SaaS best practice for preserving customer goodwill is to grandfather existing customers in rather than force upgrades. (attribution: uncertain — likely Eric, 2023-11-15)
- There's a counter-argument the thread surfaced: blocking users who scrape against your wishes is reasonable (attributed in-thread to Google's John Mueller [John "Mu"/"moo" — sic, caption garble]). (Eric relaying, 2023-11-15)
- If you hike pricing on customers who aren't heavily dependent on you, and there are many very similar alternatives with little differentiation, you will lose customers to churn. (attribution: uncertain — likely Neil, 2023-11-15)
- Netflix example of pricing power: Netflix recently raised prices; even though annoyed, the speaker pays because the content (original shows) is genuinely differentiated with no real substitute. SEO tools are the opposite — Ahrefs, Semrush, "and a few ourselves" [Ubersuggest] deliver largely similar functionality, so there's low switching cost and less pricing power. (attribution: uncertain — likely Neil given SEO-tool ownership reference, 2023-11-15)
- Peter Drucker framing: the purpose of a business is to create/generate a customer — and you also want to keep the customers you generate. (Eric, 2023-11-15)
- The lesson: think about grandfathering going forward, or you'll get negative blowback like this. (Eric, 2023-11-15)
- It's not just pricing — anything that disrupts loyal customers is a comparable risk. (Neil, 2023-11-15)
- Ahrefs is almost certainly highly profitable: Neil infers ~$100M/year revenue ("someone said online... close to 100 million a year") on ~50 employees. (Neil — self-noted as inferred from online statements, 2023-11-15)
- Above a certain profitability threshold you're better off not maximizing revenue and instead keeping customers happy, because once something tarnishes your brand it's very hard to recover. (Neil, 2023-11-15)
- Ahrefs takes risks (e.g., building a search engine [Yep], publicly going after a competitor on Twitter); risk can yield rewards but sometimes you pay the price — in this case they're paying it, and the search engine may pull focus from the core business. (attribution: uncertain — likely Eric closing the episode, 2023-11-15)
