# 7 Marketing Lessons Learned From a Google Employee

## Summary
Neil relays seven marketing lessons an (unnamed) Google employee shared with him, framing them as insider knowledge that ordinary marketers lack. His core argument is that growth comes from underexploited levers: (1) expand internationally into lower-competition languages (German, French, Portuguese, Hindi) where content is scarce; (2) but don't just translate — transcribe and adapt content per market with country-specific keyword research (he plugs Ubersuggest's per-country keyword data), warning that bad translated content with poor engagement metrics can drag down rankings sitewide; (3) exploit region-specific payment-plan mechanics (e.g. Brazilian credit-card companies guarantee all installments and pay merchants upfront for ~6%), making installment offers far more lucrative abroad than in the US; (4) go omnichannel to lower CPAs across all channels (citing his Expedia work); (5) treat "old is new" — update old content aggressively (he writes one post a week but updates ~90/month with a three-person team) because Google favors fresh content; (6) build a brand, since brand queries are a Google quality signal and the "rule of seven" interactions drives repeat visits (via email, push notifications/Subscribers.com, and messenger bots like MobileMonkey/ManyChat); and (7) keep "feet on the ground" with offline/grassroots marketing, noting even Google runs pop-up shops, cross-country buses, and that direct mail still works.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said he historically created content only for the English market until a Google employee advised him to produce content in German, French, Portuguese, and Hindi — regions that lack content and are far easier to rank in than English.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said expanding globally does not guarantee traffic: content must be transcribed and adapted (not merely translated), with keyword research done per country/language.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said bad translated content can hurt rankings across the whole site — poor click-through and bounce metrics on a translated version can drag the English site's rankings lower.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said Ubersuggest provides keyword data for every country in different languages because search demand differs by region.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said payment plans are far more lucrative outside the US: in some regions (Brazil cited) credit-card companies charge the merchant ~6% on a 12-pay but guarantee all payments and pay the merchant upfront, absorbing customer default risk.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said reducing upfront cost via payment plans should drive many more sales.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said going omnichannel lowers CPAs across the board, citing his work with Expedia where using multiple channels made advertising more affordable even for an already-established brand.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said "old is new": he writes ~1 blog post a week but updates ~90 old posts a month with a three-person team, because Google favors fresh content over old, outdated content.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said updating old content (better UX, expanded content, removing dead links) signals Google to rank it higher; neglecting it lets rankings dwindle.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said branding is a ranking factor — the more brand queries a site gets monthly, the better it ranks — and cited Eric Schmidt's view that brands are how you tell good sites from bad.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said the "rule of seven" applies: after ~7 brand interactions people are much likelier to return; grow brand queries via email blasts, push notifications (Subscribers.com), and Facebook Messenger bots (MobileMonkey, ManyChat), which he says have very high open and click rates.
- As of 2019-10-24, Neil said offline/grassroots marketing still works — even Google, a digital company, runs pop-up shops and a cross-country bus helping small businesses build websites; direct-mail postcards remain effective.
