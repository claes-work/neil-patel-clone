# The Best Marketing Strategy Produces the Worst ROI

## Summary
Neil argues that optimizing marketing purely for measurable direct ROI caps how big a
business can get (as of 2022-05-28). His illustration: people pick brands they're
already familiar with for athletic shoes, credit cards, or cars (Nike, Visa/
Mastercard/Amex, Tesla/BMW/Mercedes/Toyota) — a brand-recall decision, not a search-
or-ad-driven one. He cites **Tesla's marketing budget as $0**, attributing its brand
strength to product quality alone, while noting most people (including himself) can't
replicate that. His prescription is the recurring **rule of seven** (someone needs
~seven touches with a brand before they're likely to evangelize it), operationalized
via multi-channel repetition: repost the same content across all major social
platforms, use email as a channel to keep the brand in front of people (linking to his
"list is an ATM" framing), use SEO/blog content for search-based touches, use SMS
follow-up (higher open rates than email), and use push notifications. Advises adding
channels slowly rather than all at once to avoid burnout, and republishing content
across networks to maximize reach per piece.

## Key claims
- Neil frames Tesla's marketing budget as $0, attributing brand strength purely to
  product quality, and explicitly says this approach isn't replicable by most
  businesses, including his own (as of 2022-05-28).
- Restates the rule of seven — ~7 brand touches before someone is likely to evangelize
  a brand — as the organizing principle for a channel-stacking strategy (as of
  2022-05-28; consistent with prior rule-of-seven references, e.g.
  [[2022-05-21-yt-pwGr4Pl3Wxo]]).
- Recommends adding marketing channels one at a time rather than all at once, to avoid
  burnout, and republishing the same content across every applicable social platform
  (as of 2022-05-28).
