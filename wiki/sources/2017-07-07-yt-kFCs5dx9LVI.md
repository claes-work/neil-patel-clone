# How to Make A Passive Income Online With A Mobile Responsive Website - Responsive Web Design Guide

## Summary
Despite the "passive income" title, this is a five-part tutorial on improving mobile conversion rates. Neil opens on the premise that mobile search has overtaken desktop, then argues for a single responsive site (not a separate mobile-only site) as the foundation, layers on Google's AMP framework for speed, and gives three conversion tactics tuned for small screens: minimize what you show, use delayed exit pop-ups (to dodge Google's mobile interstitial penalty), and make phone numbers tap-to-call.

## Key claims
As of 2017-07-07, Neil states:
- More people now search Google on mobile devices than on desktop or laptop (as of 2017-07-07).
- Build a responsive design (one site that reflows across screen sizes), not a separate mobile-only site — a mobile-only site doubles your update work because desktop and mobile versions must be maintained separately (as of 2017-07-07).
- Tablet users are usually grouped with mobile users (as of 2017-07-07).
- Leverage Google's AMP framework to make pages extremely minimal and fast on mobile; this matters even for users on 4G/LTE because signal/wifi is poor in some places (as of 2017-07-07).
- Faster AMP pages improve user signals/experience, and Google will "eventually push your webpages higher"; Neil predicts AMP users are "much more likely to rank higher" in the future because of better user signals (as of 2017-07-07 — a prediction; contrast with Google's later deprecation of AMP as a ranking factor).
- Minimize what mobile users see: on small screens too many elements overwhelm visitors and lower conversions, so show the most important elements first and push the rest to the bottom (he contrasts his content-heavy desktop blog with a stripped mobile view) (as of 2017-07-07).
- Use delayed exit pop-ups on mobile (e.g. after 20–40 seconds, via the free tool Hello Bar); showing them after engagement rather than on entry avoids Google's mobile pop-up penalty announced in 2016 (as of 2017-07-07).
- Use Tap-To-Call HTML so phone numbers are tappable; making the number an image (to keep it "pretty") is the wrong choice (as of 2017-07-07).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Mobile devices are so popular that more people are searching Google.com with the mobile device than they are with the desktop computer or a laptop."

> "The last thing you want to do is just create a mobile specific website. ... It's too much work. You want to have one website for both mobile and desktop users, and even tablet users."

> "With mobile devices, if you show them too much stuff it overwhelms them, on especially a small screen, and your conversions will go down."

> "I know they stated in 2016 that they're going roll out a penalty for people [who] use exit pop-ups on mobile devices, but if you show it after 20 or 30 seconds of them browsing your site on a mobile device, you won't get penalized."

> "Most people like making their phone numbers images, so that way it stays all pretty. That's the wrong thing you want to do. You want to use Tap To Call HTML."
