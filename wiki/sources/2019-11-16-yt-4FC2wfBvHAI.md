# The Mom's & Pop's Guide to Massive Organic Traffic Through SEO and Content Marketing

## Summary
A tactical local-SEO playbook for small "mom and pop" businesses that want organic Google traffic without an expensive SEO agency. Neil walks through three layers: (1) fully leveraging Google My Business — claiming the profile, systematically attracting reviews, responding to every review (positive and negative), and using under-utilized features like short videos (30 seconds max) and photos of the location, staff, and products; (2) structuring the website for local search — a home page with services above the fold, business detail paragraphs, a map, a video explainer, and opening hours; creating cross-linked location pages for surrounding cities, one service page per service, a reviews page, and applying Schema Markup (reviews, local business, geo-coordinates, opening hours, plus vertical-specific markup for restaurants/lawyers); and (3) local content marketing — posting one high-quality piece per month that references local landmarks, people, events, neighborhoods, and current community events to signal local relevance to Google. He closes by promoting his agency, Neil Patel Digital.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said small businesses should assume customers search for them online rather than in the Yellow Pages, so being found through Google matters even when SEO feels too expensive.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said the first step is to claim your Google My Business profile so people know the listing is genuinely yours.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said reviews are decisive on Google My Business — without them you won't do well — and businesses should actively ask clients for reviews, using a printed handout that both reminds and teaches customers how to leave one.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said you should always respond to both positive and negative reviews, acknowledging negatives politely and explaining how you'll fix the problem, because "fighting fire with fire" backfires.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said to add videos to Google My Business (a virtual tour, video intro, or staff video) kept to 30 seconds max, noting few businesses leverage video so it's a differentiator.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said to add photos of the real location (inside and outside), smiling staff, popular products/items/food, and customers enjoying themselves — because most businesses only claim their profile and ask for reviews without using half these features.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said the home page should state a basic idea of your services clearly above the fold, followed by two to four paragraphs covering what you do, years in business, what sets you apart, and areas served.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said the home page should include location info, a map, a video explainer (the same video can be reused from Google My Business and A/B tested), and — importantly — opening hours, which businesses often forget and whose absence irritates prospective customers.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said to create location pages for surrounding cities you serve (he cites Hollywood, Beverly Hills, Century City, Pacific Palisades, and Santa Monica around Los Angeles), tailoring each page with details like travel time and the quickest route, and to cross-link those pages so they rank higher.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said to create one dedicated service page per service (10 services = 10 pages) so each can rank for its specific term and drive more traffic.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said to build a reviews page using proper Schema Markup so star ratings appear in Google results, helping the listing stand out and get more clicks.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said to also apply local business Schema Markup, vertical-specific markup (restaurants and lawyers have their own), geo-coordinates markup, and opening-hours markup.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said to write in-depth content for static service pages covering differentiation, team qualifications, years in business, tools/technologies used, use cases, and testimonials, so the pages rank better.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said content marketing for local sites is easier than most people think — posting just once a month a high-quality piece that mentions local landmarks, people, events, neighborhoods, and institutions signals to Google that you're a locally relevant business.
- As of 2019-11-16, Neil said covering current events happening in your community through content marketing — which few competitors do — helps you rank higher for local terms including your service-related terms.
