# Your Website Has to Sell Before the Sales Call (Marketing School)

## Summary
Co-hosted Marketing School episode. **Premise mismatch worth flagging up front:** the video's titled hook — "your website has to sell before the sales call" / the website becoming a "revenue agent" that runs autonomous CRO loops, spawns lookalikes, and generates customized outbound and personalized landing pages — is **entirely Eric Siu's opening monologue about his own Single Grain website** ("I have a loop running right now for the single grain website ... running CRO tests every week ... raise the conversion rate by 30% ... doing it autonomously right now"). Neil never engages the website/CRO topic at all. That whole titled thread is Eric's and is quarantined below.

Neil's persona-relevant contribution is the rest of the episode, which is **not marketing at all — it is a personal-finance / corporate-treasury / M&A-acquisitions discussion** (agency-entrepreneurship). Neil's material: (1) a treasury strategy — he moved NP Digital from S-corp to C-corp for the lower effective tax rate, uses bank debt to grow, and wants to take cash off the balance sheet to buy stocks (bank terms initially blocked it, seeking an exception); (2) a conviction bet that agency profits are better parked in SpaceX for 20 years than spent buying more companies ("there's no way we're going to beat the returns of SpaceX ... as a marketing agency"), waiting for the employee/investor share unlock to buy in; (3) a disclosure that he took an eight-figure loan against his home and put all of it into one SaaS stock, betting "the SaaS apocalypse is overblown" and that he can outgrow his ~6% cost of borrowing; and (4) the most structured piece — an **acquisition-economics framework** for buying distressed "fixer-upper" businesses cheap (Platinum Equity model) at ~2.5–4x rather than fast-growers at 7–7.5x, because growth compresses the effective multiple and cash-flows back far faster than servicing 5x of debt over ~7 years. He also voices skepticism that you can source most M&A deals from content, and a life-philosophy tangent (almost bought a private jet twice, chose to travel less instead).

These are solid, on-domain Neil positions but they are personal-finance/treasury/M&A takes, not a marketing framework — and the genuinely novel "revenue agent / autonomous CRO" framing belongs to Eric. So this is **NOT flagged ★ L3-candidate.** Nearly all figures are self-reported and explicitly "not financial advice."

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- Treasury / entity structure: moved his agency from S-corp to C-corp — as an S-corp profit is taxed as pass-through at roughly 40–50 cents on the dollar; as a C-corp it's ~21% federal plus (generally lower) state tax where revenue is generated, landing around a ~25% average rate. (Neil, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Uses bank debt to grow the agency faster; asked the bank to let him take cash off the balance sheet to buy stocks ("treasury strategy") since the company isn't doing much M&A right now — the loan terms didn't allow it, so he's asking for an exception. (Neil, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Conviction bet: he'd make more money taking agency profits and putting them into SpaceX for the next 5–20 years than buying more companies — "there's no way we're going to beat the returns of SpaceX over the next 20 years as a marketing agency." Not buying yet; waiting for employees/investors to be allowed to sell shares (the unlock typically creates downward price pressure) to average in. (Neil, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Took an eight-figure loan against his home and put all of it into a single (unnamed) SaaS stock, expecting to at least double his money on profits / triple the stock, betting "the SaaS apocalypse is overblown." (Neil — self-reported, "not financial advice," 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Cost-of-borrowing math: his borrowing cost is "low twos plus SOFR" (~3.58% SOFR) ≈ ~6%; he believes he can outgrow ~6% holding SaaS stocks 2–3 years, clip more than the 6%, and have the gains pay off the borrowed debt. (Neil, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Prerequisite (Eric states, Neil agrees): none of this works without a good business/engine first — you can't just go raise a bunch of money; a strong machine gives you the optionality to take on debt or raise. Neil adds his edge is that he owns 100% of his company. (Neil + Eric, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Acquisition-economics framework: prefers buying distressed / "fixer-upper" businesses (not fast-growers) because you get them on a better financial deal, making the arbitrage easier. On a company doing $1M profit, a growth deal often costs ~3.5x up front + a ~3.5–4x earnout (~7–7.5x total); if you can grow it ~10–20%/yr, after ~2 years the effective cost drops to ~5x. Buying distressed instead — ~2.5x up front, ~4x total deal (~$4M over 2 years on that $1M-profit company) — and growth then compresses it to ~2.5–3x, so it cash-flows well and pays back fast. Contrast: borrowing 5x and servicing it (depreciation + taxes + repayment + interest) means ~7 years to pay back with no cash flow in the interim. (Neil, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship. ["Eric and I live by this company called Platinum Equity" — cited as the distressed-buyer model.]
- Skeptical you can source most M&A/business deals from content: "I don't believe you can get most of your deals from content ... no one's going to give you free equity of a really good business" — you grow by buying companies and arbitraging the financials, not by expecting content to hand you equity. (Neil, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship / content-marketing.
- Debt vs VC, done both: says he's done both debt and venture capital (his VC-backed company was Kissmetrics); both carry limitations. Bank debt forces "responsible growth" (profit, clean numbers, sometimes restricts paying yourself); VC is "grow, grow, grow," tolerates losses, and lets founders pay themselves well — founders of companies that raised ~$300–500M+ often make millions/year in salary, whereas sub-$50M-raised startups pay founders only decently. (Neil, with Eric, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Life-philosophy tangent: almost bought a private jet twice (spent ~300 hours at shows, negotiating, flying to see them) before deciding — prompted by something Eric sent him years ago — that traveling less and being home beats owning a jet; considered buying fixer-upper businesses in the countries he travels to instead. (Neil, 2026-07-15) — agency-entrepreneurship.
