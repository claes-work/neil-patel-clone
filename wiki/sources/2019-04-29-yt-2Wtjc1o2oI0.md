# A Simple Hack to Finding the Right Keywords to Rank #1 on Google (The Best FREE SEO Tool)

## Summary

Neil argues that **more traffic ≠ more sales** if you rank for the wrong keywords, a mistake
he made himself before learning to target **commercial-intent** keywords. His hack uses
**Ubersuggest** to reverse-engineer competitors' revenue keywords: (1) enter a
money-making competitor's URL (find them by searching your space for advertisers with many
employees); (2) view their **traffic overview** by region and open **Top Pages**;
(3) under Top Pages, click **View All** on estimated visits to see the keywords driving that
traffic, and select ones with a **high cost-per-click** (ideally $1+; five-cent keywords are
weak) and **SEO difficulty under 50** — high CPC signals the keyword drives revenue.
Then Google the chosen keyword, read the top-10 ranking pages, and **write a more thorough,
hole-poking version** (thorough beats mere length). Finally, close the loop with outreach:
whenever you link out, email that site ("I linked to you; feel free to share mine") to earn
traction and rankings. He cites **NeilPatel.com at 3M+ visits/month** and points to the
**neilpatel.com/backlinks** tool for competitor link data.

## Key claims

- As of 2019-04-29, Neil said growing traffic without the **right keywords** won't grow
  sales — he learned this "the hard way" scaling his sites into the hundreds of thousands of
  visitors; NeilPatel.com now gets **3M+ visits/month**.
- As of 2019-04-29, Neil's keyword filter: from a competitor's **Ubersuggest Top Pages →
  View All**, pick keywords with **CPC ≥ ~$1** and **SEO difficulty < 50**, because high CPC
  indicates the keyword drives revenue.
- As of 2019-04-29, Neil said to find money-making competitors by searching your space for
  firms spending on ads, with many employees.
- As of 2019-04-29, Neil said to read the current top-10 pages and write a **more thorough**
  version — "it's not just all about length, it's about being thorough" — then earn shares
  and links via outreach whenever you link out to others.
