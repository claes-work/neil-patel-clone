# Yeezy's $0 Super Bowl Ad, Super Bowl vs. MrBeast Ads, 2024 Election Deepfakes, and More

## Summary
Reacting to a viral tweet arguing Super Bowl ad spend is driven by "religious" industry
habit rather than data (MrBeast gets more views weekly than the Super Bowl's 123M
viewers), Neil largely agrees with the critique on ROI grounds but defends the Super Bowl
as a legitimate branding/industry-schmoozing event rather than a performance-marketing
buy. He breaks down the real cost structure: ~$7M for a 30-second spot, another ~$7M+ in
"upfront" commitments the network extracts to guarantee future Olympic-year spots, and
production budgets that are typically under $1M (the real expense is celebrity fees) —
netting a ~$21M all-in spend for one ad. His verdict: for pure ROI, Facebook/TikTok/Google
ads and CTV outperform and are more trackable; the Super Bowl's value is branding, executive
schmoozing, and reach (100M+ eyeballs) that's hard to replicate elsewhere. He cites Kanye
West's $7M Yeezy ad (which drove an estimated $19M revenue on ~250,000 purchases, at
prices low enough that Kanye had to refund a price-drop difference to earlier buyers) as
an example of a founder with enough built brand equity to skip traditional production
polish. This connects to Neil's data point that over 70% of high-performing social videos
in NP Digital's dataset had no "fancy production," reinforcing his standing UGC-over-
polish belief. The episode closes on 2024 election deepfake risk (a Big Tech pact to
combat AI deepfakes, which Neil is skeptical will actually work, citing the Taylor Swift
deepfake as a precedent) and a first-person security-fraud anecdote: NP Digital lost
"100-200 grand" a few years ago to a business-email-compromise scam (a fraudster gained
access to a TV-ad vendor's email and redirected a payment), recovered via cyber insurance
(~90% reimbursed, with the remainder as deductible).

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- Super Bowl ad cost breakdown: ~$7M for a 30-second spot; networks typically require an
  additional ~$7M+ in upfront ad-spend commitments (tying buyers into future high-profile
  slots, e.g. Olympic years); production for most commercials costs under $1M (the real
  expense is celebrity fees); all-in spend for a single Super Bowl ad campaign can reach
  ~$21M. Neil's verdict: poor ROI relative to Facebook, TikTok, Google, and CTV ads (which
  are also more trackable), but genuinely strong for brand-building and reaching 100M+
  eyeballs in one placement — plus real value as an industry-networking/executive event.
  (Neil, 2024-02-21) — paid-ads. ★ (a clear, quantified ROI-vs-branding framework —
  synthesis candidate for the paid-ads topic page.)
- Kanye West's ~$7M Yeezy Super Bowl ad reportedly drove ~$19M revenue on ~250,000
  purchases (unclear if profit), at pricing low enough that he had to publicly offer
  refunds to buyers who'd paid the earlier higher price; Neil frames this as only possible
  because of years of pre-built brand equity — not a repeatable move for most brands.
  (Neil, 2024-02-21, citing public reporting — self-reported revenue figures not
  independently verified) — paid-ads / wealth.
- **UGC-over-polish stat**: in NP Digital's data analysis, more than 70% of high-performing
  social videos had no "fancy production" — reinforcing that TikTok/Instagram/YouTube-
  Shorts-style unpolished, phone-shot content typically outperforms heavily produced
  content, including for categories like the Super Bowl. (Neil, 2024-02-21) — social-media
  / content-marketing. ★ (echoes/extends the content-quality-vs-production material in
  earlier February 2024 episodes; consolidation candidate.)
- Skepticism that the Big Tech (Microsoft/OpenAI/Google, etc.) election-deepfake pact will
  meaningfully prevent AI deepfakes from influencing the 2024 US election — cites the
  Taylor Swift deepfake incident as evidence existing safeguards already failed to prevent
  a high-profile case. (Neil, 2024-02-21) — ai-marketing.
- Self-reported first-person security-fraud anecdote: a few years prior, NP Digital was
  running paid TV ad placements (CNBC, Bloomberg, and other financial-network airtime)
  through a contractor, and a scammer who'd gained access to that contractor's email
  redirected a payment to a fraudulent account; the loss was roughly $100-200K. NP Digital
  had cyber insurance, which reimbursed ~90% of the loss (the remaining ~10% was the
  deductible); an investigation was also commissioned. Neil recommends any company
  handling vendor payments carry cyber insurance and, as headcount grows, hire a dedicated
  security lead (or outside security consultant while small). (Neil, 2024-02-21,
  self-reported) — agency-entrepreneurship. ★ (a concrete operational/risk-management
  anecdote — good candidate for the agency-entrepreneurship topic page.)
