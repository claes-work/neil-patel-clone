# Is Trump Media (DJT) a scam or a genius marketing move?

## Summary
Four segments, mostly Neil-driven. (1) Tesla's Q1 2024 deliveries declined for the first time in ~4 years, prompting up to $7,000 in Model Y discounts; an aside notes Neil's NP Digital co-founder Mike Kamo owns two Tesla Model X's (one "Plaid" tier). (2) Reacting to Barry Diller calling Trump Media (DJT) "a scam" and its buyers "dopes": despite DJT trading far below its post-IPO peak ($79 to ~$46) on ~$4.1M of 2023 revenue against a multi-billion market cap, the conclusion is it's "too dangerous to short" given a large, loyal, Trump-supporting shareholder base that treats the stock as an identity statement — framed as a stark demonstration of personal-brand power, with a comparison to Gary Vaynerchuk's "reach all 8 billion people" broad-audience strategy (explicitly framed as not a fit for Neil, who values privacy). Neil recounts declining an attempted "follow-me-around" videographer at a Collision conference in Toronto, and a similar earlier arrangement with a hired videographer ("Noah") that he shut down after a few weeks partly over safety concerns (a near-accident while the videographer was filming in traffic). (3) A tangent on Alex Hormozi/Cody Sanchez-style creators out-viewing far larger accounts (~130M monthly views cited, including shorts) leads into Neil's account of shifting NP Digital's growth strategy over time: after observing competitors with fewer followers/traffic outperforming NP Digital financially via M&A roll-ups, Neil says he now spends more time on M&A calls/outreach than on content creation, backed by a dedicated M&A team including a hire who ran M&A for a major ad agency (Deloitte-adjacent, "Denue," in North America). (4) Reacting to an Andrew Wilkinson tweet ("private equity sucks at marketing"), Neil's public reply ("but they may not need marketing... returns on large sums have been great, especially with the use of debt") opens into an explanation of the private-equity agency roll-up playbook: acquire several single-service agencies (SEO, PPC, CRO/email/social), cross-sell each one's client base the others' services, raising average revenue per customer and reducing churn/account-management overhead — with Mountain Gate/Elite SEM (rolled into "Tenuity," later sold at a high multiple) cited as a real example, plus "multiple arbitrage" (buying several $1-3M-profit companies at ~5x, combining them into a $12-13M-profit entity that commands a much higher multiple on exit, often financed partly with debt). Neil also recounts advising Gary Vaynerchuk (re: his stated interest in buying the NY Jets) that VaynerX's strategy of acquiring diverse businesses and adding marketing value is essentially applying the same private-equity-plus-marketing logic.

Neil discloses the AnswerThePublic acquisition economics: he paid roughly $8.6M for the company, based on a seller-claimed run rate of ~$130K/month in revenue described as "almost all profit" aside from server costs — which NP Digital's own post-acquisition analysis found closer to ~$800K in real annual profit (implying roughly a 10x-profit multiple paid). After taking over operations and making changes within about two and a half months, Neil states the business is now valued at "less than 3x" (his phrasing: "2 point something") its current EBITDA, i.e. it worked out favorably relative to the entry multiple.
