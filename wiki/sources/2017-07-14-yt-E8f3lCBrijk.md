# Why Domain Rating is More Important Than PageRank - Google Search Console Guide

## Summary
An SEO-metrics explainer arguing that Domain Authority is a more useful working metric than Google PageRank. Neil's core reasoning is cadence: PageRank updates too infrequently (months to a year) to guide iterative SEO work, whereas third-party Domain Authority scores update frequently and serve as a leading indicator that predicts rankings/traffic gains before they show up. He recommends tracking DA (0–100) via Open Site Explorer (free, Moz) or Ahrefs (paid, which he prefers for more frequent updates), and pursuing links from sites with higher DA than yours.

## Key claims
As of 2017-07-14, Neil states:
- Google PageRank is a 0–10 score, and its algorithm was created by Google co-founder Larry Page (as of 2017-07-14).
- PageRank's problem is it doesn't update frequently — historically Google used it heavily (0 = not authoritative, 10 = super authoritative; Google itself a 10, a typical site a 4–6), but as an SEO you can't wait 6 months to a year to see whether your changes worked, so it's a poor indicator (as of 2017-07-14).
- The true key metric is whether rankings and traffic are rising, but those also take a long time (as of 2017-07-14).
- Self-example: neilpatel.com ranks page one for "online marketing" and "SEO"; those took 6 months to ~1.5 years, and the leading indicator that told him he was on track was that his Domain Authority kept increasing (as of 2017-07-14).
- Two main tools for Domain Authority: Open Site Explorer (free but usage-limited, then paywalled; owned by Moz) and Ahrefs (paid) — he personally prefers Ahrefs because it updates more frequently than Moz (as of 2017-07-14). [Caption note: the transcript garbles "Ahrefs" as "atrs"/"hrs" and "PageRank" as "PID rank."]
- Domain Authority runs 0–100; higher is better; in link building, pursue links from sites with higher Domain Authority than your own to raise rankings and traffic faster (as of 2017-07-14).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "The problem with page rank is ... it doesn't really get updated frequently. So as an SEO when you're doing changes to your website ... are you really going to wait 6 months to [a] year for Google to update that [page]rank number to figure out if you're doing better or worse? Probably not."

> "It took 6 months, a year, and even a year and a half to rank for some of these key terms, but the leading indicator that showed me that I was doing well is that my domain [authority] continually started to increase."

> "When you're doing your link building efforts try to go after links from sites with domain authorities that are larger than yours ... your ranking and traffic are going to increase at a much quicker pace."
