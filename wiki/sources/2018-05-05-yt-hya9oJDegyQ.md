# How to Survive When A Major Client FIRES You

## Summary

An interview-style clip (an unnamed interviewer asks about the risk of one or two clients making up 75–80% of a business). Neil accepts the risk is real and largely uncontrollable, illustrating with Twilio: Uber was one of its biggest customers, and when Uber announced it would build texting in-house, Twilio's (publicly traded) stock tanked hard. He gives a B2C parallel — a friend he calls "Dr. Fresh" whose site GuruNanda (essential oils/fragrances) derives most revenue from Walmart — but notes a great product/service makes cancellation less likely. His playbook for client concentration: (1) go after your big client's competitors (if you have Walmart, pitch Target using your Walmart results as proof); (2) write auto-renewal clauses tied to hitting performance levels; (3) diversify by adding smaller clients even at lower margins so some income remains if the whale leaves; (4) run lean and keep savings ("cut the fat") so you can weather a loss. He stresses taking care of your team above yourself — he says he'd move back in with his parents to keep paying his team and nearly did, years ago during his lawsuits — and citing Richard Branson (met at a conference in Brazil): a business is built by its team, not the lone entrepreneur. He closes with a personal example: around age 16–18 he had one client paying $25,000/month that was his largest profit source, eventually lost it, and recuperated over time — framing whale loss as a "when, not if."
