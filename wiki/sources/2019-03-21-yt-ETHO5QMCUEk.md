# The Three Unseen Laws of Entrepreneurship | Neil Patel

## Summary

★ L3-candidate — a named Neil framework plus a candid Kissmetrics post-mortem. Three "unseen laws":
(1) **You make money by solving problems, not by wanting to be rich** — Neil says he only started
succeeding once he shifted from wanting wealth to solving people's problems better than competitors.
(2) **It's harder to make money than to save it** — be frugal; the cash you waste on lifestyle
(BMW, Netflix, extra apps) could be reinvested; even giants (GE, Apple) rise and fall, so keep a
war chest for rainy days and to double down. (3) **Speed over perfection** — execution beats a
perfect product. The persona/bio nugget: he says Kissmetrics raised **$17M+**, put in a CEO who
couldn't execute, and a competitor who *copied* their product out-executed them to **$100M+/yr in
revenue** — "they didn't create a better product ... they out-executed us."

## Key claims

- As of 2019-03-21, Neil rejected "lack of capital" as the main reason entrepreneurs fail, saying the
  web lets you succeed without much money. [belief]
- As of 2019-03-21, Neil's first law: you don't make money by wanting to be rich, you make money by
  **solving problems** better than competitors (more affordable, easier, better results). He said he
  did poorly when he only wanted to be rich, and started succeeding when he solved people's problems.
  [belief; self-reported bio]
- As of 2019-03-21, Neil's second law: "it's harder to make money than it is to save it" — spend on
  the business, not lifestyle (called out BMW/Mercedes, Netflix, Amazon Prime, extra apps); keep cash
  because "what goes up, comes down" (cited GE's decline and Apple's dip + Microsoft's late-1990s
  bailout). He credits his mom for the save-money lesson. [belief — frugality; bio]
- As of 2019-03-21, Neil's third law: **speed over perfection** — "move as fast as you can, fix the
  mess at the end"; traction unlocks options (investment, loans), and a faster competitor will beat
  you. [belief — execution/speed]
- As of 2019-03-21, Neil said his last startup **Kissmetrics** raised "17 plus million dollars," later
  installed a CEO who "couldn't execute" and "kept spinning his wheels," while a competitor that
  copied their product out-executed them to over **$100 million/year in revenue**. [self-reported bio
  — Kissmetrics fundraise + failure narrative]
