# How to Commission and Structure a (High-Performing) Sales Team

## Summary

Q&A Thursday (Neil with interviewer Adam Lodolce from Viewership). Question from repeat
asker Jason Valasek: what compensation structure works for commission salespeople vs
task-oriented people, and how to blend in 1099 contractors? Neil's core advice:
**pay salespeople a base plus commission, not commission-only** — the best reps get a base,
and base+commission produces "much higher quality sales reps." He notes you *can* run
commission-only in the US (he cites Utah as a hotbed of great commission-only reps —
"if you can sell religion you can sell anything," Mormons who'll go door-to-door for free
because they believe in the cause — which is why Adobe, Salesforce and others staff sales
teams there; he's clear that's a saying he learned, not his own). Look for reps who are
experienced and hungry "but not too experienced." His key metric: across the **entire**
sales department — reps, VP of sales, sales director, plus fulfillment/others who help
close — **sales should cost no more than ~10% of the revenue you close** (he clarifies:
~10% of the *first year's* deal, not lifetime value). Venture-funded companies flex higher
(20–30%), but bootstrappers need a self-sustaining model. If your sales cost runs higher,
it eats margins you need for product, support, engineering and profit; if it's *lower* than
10%, "increase it — treat them right." He warns against over-spending via the public
example of **Domo** (recent IPO, stock "got hammered," a once-billion-plus valuation now in
the hundreds of millions) — "their expenses are too high." Threaded throughout is the
team-first refrain: pay people well because "without the people on your team you will not
have your business" — again citing Elon/Tesla and Richard Branson ("it's all about your
team ... you're not going to build anything big without an amazing team," from speaking
together at a conference). On contractors/tasks: he doesn't set fixed rates; he hires on
**Upwork** but insists on quality over cheapness — check completed jobs, success ratio,
reviews, and pre-screen — balancing quality and price so it's "worth it." When a contractor
gets so expensive that a full-time hire would be cheaper, **move them in-house.** Adam adds
two points (context): the *psychology of money* — past a certain pay level, more money
doesn't add motivation; people want to feel part of something — and echoes the passion
theme, quoting Steve Jobs (via Neil) that you hire hustlers/passionate people, not the best
résumés, because they stay for the long haul.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-09-20, **pay salespeople base + commission, not commission-only** — it yields
  higher-quality reps; hire experienced, hungry reps who aren't "too experienced."
- As of 2018-09-20, commission-only can work in the US (cites Utah/Mormon reps — "if you
  can sell religion you can sell anything" — used by Adobe, Salesforce), but base is better.
- As of 2018-09-20, the **whole sales department should cost ≤~10% of revenue closed**
  (specifically ~10% of the first year's deal); VC-funded firms flex to 20–30%,
  bootstrappers should not. Below 10% → pay more; above → fix it to protect margins.
- As of 2018-09-20, over-spending kills valuations — cites **Domo** (post-IPO stock
  hammered, billion-plus valuation down to hundreds of millions) as "expenses too high."
- As of 2018-09-20, **pay your team well** — "without the people on your team you will not
  have your business" (Elon/Tesla, Richard Branson team-first anecdote again).
- As of 2018-09-20, hire contractors on **Upwork by quality, not price** (completed jobs,
  success ratio, reviews, pre-screening); **move a contractor in-house** once full-time
  would be cheaper.
