# Yellow fever (Why Whole Foods had to sell), $700M crypto deal, and more

## Summary
Three segments. (1) A discussion of Whole Foods' ill-conceived "Yellow Fever" Asian-inspired restaurant brand (approved internally pre-Amazon-acquisition), which Amazon exec Tony Hsieh was reportedly assigned to shut down after the acquisition — used as a case study (alongside a Jeremy Lin "Linsanity"-era ESPN headline controversy, "chink in the armor") in how offensive/racially-charged brand naming can seriously damage a business, contrasted with Manscaped as an example of risqué-but-not-offensive wordplay branding done well. (2) A segment on the Crypto.com Arena naming-rights deal (~$700M over ~7 years, for the former Staples Center), used to illustrate a broader observation: very large, cash-flush companies (Nvidia, Google, Facebook, Apple-scale) will spend heavily on brand/attention plays like stadium naming deals because an incremental dollar of ad spend still returns a dollar-plus of profit for them, even if the ROI logic looks wasteful/vain from the outside — "that's probably around 5% of the conversations" where founders eventually understand this reasoning; the other 95% chase splashy spend without understanding the math. (3) A "pick my brain" segment recapping the most common questions/advice given on unpaid founder calls, posted as an X/Twitter thread: (a) Performance Max underperforms in B2B/long-sales-cycle contexts unless only the highest-quality leads are fed back into the algorithm as "conversions"; (b) micro-influencer marketing is an underused, high-performing paid channel; (c) more traffic doesn't equal more revenue 86% of the time — focus on the bottom line; (d) AI-generated content without a human in the loop risks hurting E-E-A-T and long-run rankings; (e) "content isn't king, high-quality content is" — given ~4 billion pieces of content published daily, simply publishing content doesn't guarantee an audience; (f) being on only a few social platforms is a mistake — the average person uses 6.6 social networks, and cross-posting the same content format across all of them is not "spreading yourself thin"; (g) outspending competitors on ad budget alone does not guarantee winning (cited: losing to a better-funded competitor is not just about capital) and building a brand takes ~10 years regardless of ad spend. Closes with a note that programmatic SEO practitioners are getting "torched"/de-indexed en masse right now, and that the antidote is scaled, quality-controlled content with a human in the loop, not pure automation.

## Key claims
- **Whole Foods' internally-approved "Yellow Fever" Asian-themed restaurant brand is cited as a contributing factor in the company's eventual sale/troubles**, alongside a Jeremy Lin-era ESPN headline controversy, as examples of racially-insensitive branding causing real business damage — contrasted with Manscaped's risqué-but-inoffensive wordplay as an example of branding done right. `(third-party case study/opinion, dated 2024-03-29; attribution: uncertain — segment led by Eric, who self-identifies as Chinese)`

- **Performance Max underperforms for B2B advertisers with long sales cycles unless only genuinely high-quality leads are marked as "conversions"** fed back into the algorithm — a specific, actionable paid-ads tactic given repeatedly on unpaid advisory calls. `(opinion, dated 2024-03-29; attribution: uncertain — undiarized, but consistent with NP Digital's paid-ads practice)`

- **"86% of the time, more traffic doesn't mean more revenue — focus on the bottom line"** and **"content isn't king, high-quality content is"** (given an estimated 4B+ pieces of content published daily) are both restated as recurring pieces of advice given on advisory calls. `(opinion, dated 2024-03-29; attribution: uncertain — undiarized, but consistent with long-established Neil positions on traffic-vs-revenue and content-quality elsewhere in the wiki)`
