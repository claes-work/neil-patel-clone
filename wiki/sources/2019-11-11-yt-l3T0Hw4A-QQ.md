# 9 SEO Metrics You Need to Measure When Launching a New Website

## Summary
In this solo talking-head video, Neil Patel lays out nine metrics he considers most important to track when a brand-new website launches, arguing that focusing on the right measurements (rather than getting lost in everything Google Analytics offers) is what makes or breaks early success. The nine are: (1) load time, (2) dwell time / average session duration, (3) average time on page, (4) percentage of returning visitors, (5) referral traffic, (6) organic traffic, (7) bounce rate, (8) email opt-ins, and (9) pages per session. Recurring themes across the metrics are that speed and internal cross-linking improve most engagement metrics, that retention (returning visitors, email capture) is the cheapest way to grow versus a "leaky bucket," and that traffic only matters if it converts. He recommends tools including Google Analytics (behavior flow report), Google Search Console, and his own Ubersuggest, and repeatedly plugs his agency Neil Patel Digital.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said knowing exactly what to measure when launching a new website "will make or break your success," and that with all the metrics Analytics platforms offer it is easy to get lost, so he keeps it simple.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said load time is the first metric to monitor; it has a huge effect on SEO and user experience and affects both search rankings and conversion rates.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil cited (paraphrasing an article about Walmart) that for every one second of load-time improvement, they saw roughly a 1-2% increase in conversions.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said the second metric is dwell time (or at least average session duration in Google Analytics); if people like your content they spend more time on it, and a simple way to improve it is cross-linking your content together (e.g., linking an SEO article to a related link-building article).
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said to track average time on page per individual page: identify what your best-performing pages have in common and what your worst pages have in common, to learn what to do more of and less of.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said to track percentage of returning visitors; when a site is brand new most returning visitors are you and your friends, but as the brand grows the returning-visitor count climbs. In the early days you "live and die" by returning-visitor numbers.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said the easiest way to grow is to retain people already on your site (fix the "leaky bucket"), and you get people back via collecting emails, push notifications, and messenger chats such as MobileMonkey or ManyChat.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said referral traffic matters because organic won't be great early on; relevant referring sites can drive sales, more referral traffic usually means more backlinks (and thus rising organic traffic over time), and he focuses effort on the sites that drive conversions, not just traffic — "traffic that doesn't convert into sales is useless."
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said to track total organic traffic (not just individual keywords), watch rankings climb (e.g., from the 100s to 50 to top 10), and use Google Search Console or Ubersuggest to track rankings and search traffic daily.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said for organic traffic a bounce rate around 50% or lower is good and anything above that needs site improvements; bounce rate reflects experience (slow load and lack of interlinking raise it), not just content, and tells you what users think of your site.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said email opt-ins/subscribers will be one of your best traffic sources — not the largest volume, but the source of paying users and customers — and that emails are effective for both Fortune 1000 companies and small/medium businesses.
- As of 2019-11-11, Neil said pages per session of two to five is good while one-point-something is low, and recommended the behavior flow report in Google Analytics (which he called "one of the best reports that I love") to see where users navigate.
