# This is What Will Happen with Instagram, Facebook and Tiktok Whether You Like it Or Not

## Summary
Opens with a "everyone copies everyone" thesis in social platforms: Snapchat's success
prompted Instagram to copy its features (cites Instagram's estimated $100B+ valuation
per "EarthWeb"); TikTok's success prompted YouTube Shorts (cites "over 30 billion"
monthly views per "Statista"); BeReal's anti-curation format ("notifies other users
when to take a picture") will likewise get copied by others eventually. Generalizes
this into a business strategy: if a competitor innovates and you don't respond, "you
are going to lose market share and business." Prescribes a three-step response: (1)
study what's driving a competitor's growth using tools like Ubersuggest or
SimilarWeb ("similar web") for traffic-source analysis, plus audience surveys (names
SurveyMonkey) at scale (30-100+ respondents) to find patterns in what people like
about the competitor; (2) hire better talent by identifying standout employees at
outperforming competitors via LinkedIn (look for people with steady promotions and
tenure), then recruit them — recommends asking a target "if they know anyone else...
as you're open for a similar role" rather than pitching them directly, which he says
converts better than a direct "do you want a job?" approach; and hunt for similar
talent even outside direct competitors; (3) run continuous experiments (at least
monthly, ideally weekly) to leapfrog rather than merely match competitors, framing
failure as a normal part of eventually finding what works. Clarifies "copying" means
adapting the underlying idea/strategy to fit your own audience, not pixel-for-pixel
replication — illustrated with Apple iCloud vs. Dropbox. Closes with an NP Digital
plug.

## Key claims
- Cites Instagram's valuation as "estimated... over $100 billion" per "EarthWeb"
  (third-party stat, not self-reported).
- Cites YouTube Shorts at "over 30 billion... monthly views" per "Statista" (third-
  party stat, not self-reported).
- Recommends a specific LinkedIn recruiting tactic: ask a target candidate if they
  know someone else suited to an open role (indirect approach) rather than a direct
  job pitch, claiming the indirect approach converts better (self-reported tactical
  advice, dated 2022-10-31, no data cited to back the claim).
- Recommends running "at least one experiment a month, and ideally, one a week"
  (tactical prescription, self-reported).
- Frames Apple iCloud as a copy of Dropbox's cloud-storage success that "started
  eating into their market cap" (his own competitive-strategy framing, not a cited
  claim).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> If something is working for your competitors and you aren't doing it, you are going to lose market share and business.

> When you hit someone up directly and you say, "Hey, do you want a job?" Doesn't work as well. ... ask them if they know anyone else who is like them as you're open for a similar role.

> When I say copy, it doesn't mean it has to be pixel for pixel copycat of what they're doing. It's idea. It's a strategy. You need to figure out how it can tie into your business, how it can fit with your customers.
