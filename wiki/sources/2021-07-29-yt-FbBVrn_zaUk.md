# Social Media Marketing Tips For Every Brand (And What You Should Avoid at All Costs)

## Summary
Same host ("Dan") and likely the same interview session as
[[2021-07-24-yt-SiPvYXsiA_s]] — this video explicitly references "that example Dan
helped me with, with who is Neil Patel," confirming Dan as the credited co-strategist of
the "Who is Neil Patel?" campaign (attribution: uncertain full name). Core advice:
**own your own website first** — social platforms are rented land subject to algorithm
changes, so the goal is always to convert social reach into an owned email/SMS list via
the **rule of seven** (already documented). New, concrete belief content: an explicit
**politics/religion-avoidance stance**, illustrated with a named case study — a realtor
friend in Nevada who serves high-net-worth/billionaire clients lost an estimated
"$100,000+" in commissions after her political affiliation became known from her posts
(self-reported anecdote about a third party, not Neil's own experience, but stated as
his reasoning for avoiding political content himself). Earned-media strategy, also new
and specific: **prioritize small/local publications over "Forbes"/"Inc."/"Huffington
Post"** — the big outlets are hardest to get, and due to their sheer content volume,
articles are rarely seen unless featured on the homepage; smaller sites showcase
contributions more prominently and are more likely to actually be read. Niche-community
advice (Reddit/forums): only viable with a dedicated marketing team; recommends niching
to one subreddit/topic rather than being a generalist, citing Jason Lemkin (SaaStr) as
an example of an expert who only answers startup-related Quora questions. Facebook
mechanics: platform suppresses off-platform links because it wants users to stay and
click ads; recommends **Facebook Live commerce** (QVC-style), citing a haircare seller
generating "five figures" per livestream session, advises increasing livestream
frequency. Instagram surface breakdown (feed / IGTV / Reels / Stories), each mapped to a
purpose (IGTV competes with YouTube, Reels competes with TikTok, Stories drive
day-in-the-life engagement + "swipe up" selling).
