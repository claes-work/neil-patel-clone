# How to Run SEO Experiments | 3 SEO Tests You Can Try Today to Boost Your Rankings

## Summary
Neil teaches SEO as an experimental discipline: because Google's algorithm continually changes and no SEO knows everything, you learn by testing. The beauty of SEO is speed — Google picks up an on-site change within a few days to a week, so you can quickly see impact. His rules: (1) change only **one major SEO thing at a time**, or you won't know what helped or hurt; (2) give each change time — about one to two weeks — after using Search Console's fetch/crawl to get it indexed right away, then watch whether rankings move up, stay flat (at least not hurting), or drop; (3) expect many experiments to go wrong. He tells a cautionary story: a region-based language redirect on his blog accidentally also redirected Googlebot (Google's US servers got bounced from the Portuguese blog back to the US blog), and his traffic dropped — so monitor Google Analytics search-traffic daily and revert bad changes immediately, re-submitting the URL to recover. After a failed test, wait a week or two to recover a clean baseline before running the next test.

## Key claims
As of 2017-07-29, Neil states:
- SEO must be run as experiments because algorithms continually change and no SEO — including him — knows everything; he learns through experimentation (as of 2017-07-29).
- On-site SEO changes are picked up by Google within a few days to a week, so you can quickly see whether a change helps or hurts (as of 2017-07-29).
- Rule 1: only make **one major SEO change at a time**; making 5–10 at once means you can't tell what helped or hurt (as of 2017-07-29).
- Rule 2: after a change, use Search Console to fetch/crawl/index the URL immediately, then wait one to two weeks and watch rankings — up = keep doing more, flat = at least not hurting, down = revert (as of 2017-07-29).
- Rule 3: many experiments will fail. Example: a per-region language redirect accidentally also redirected Googlebot — Google's US servers crawling his Portuguese blog got redirected back to the US blog, and his traffic dropped (as of 2017-07-29). *(Ties to the internationalization/hreflang tactic he described 2017-07-20.)*
- Monitor Google Analytics search-traffic report daily during experiments; if it drops, revert immediately and re-submit the URL to Search Console — recovery usually takes a week or two (as of 2017-07-29).
- After a failed test, wait a week or two to recover a solid baseline before running the next test, so results stay attributable (as of 2017-07-29).

## Notable verbatim quotes
