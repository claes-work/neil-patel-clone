# How I Started 4 Multimillion Dollar Businesses By the Time I Was 28 ★

> ★ L3-candidate: Origin-story video that bundles Neil's core entrepreneurship philosophy (focus, extreme work ethic, one-product focus, fast adaptation, listening to peers, learning from failure) with self-reported biographical anchors — a strong basis for a distilled persona/entrepreneurship page.

## Summary

In this solo video, Neil Patel recounts the shared habits behind building four multimillion-dollar companies before age 28, framed as lessons rather than a chronology. He argues that success starts with picking a single opportunity and focusing on it, then out-working competitors (80–90 hours a week), building internet businesses that sell 24/7 rather than retail, concentrating on one product or service until growth flatlines, adapting quickly when things go wrong, listening to peers and gathering multiple opinions before deciding, and treating failure as an unavoidable teacher. He repeatedly downplays innate talent, attributing his results to relentless execution and learning from mistakes.

## Key claims

- As of 2017-11-12, Neil says he is 32, built his first multimillion-dollar company before he was 21, and built four of them before he was 28 — quick, he adds, though "not as quick as Mark Zuckerberg."
- He says focus comes first: having many ideas does not mean you should pursue each one; pick one, get it running and doing well, then take on three or four more.
- He attributes an analogy to Richard Branson — heard while headlining a conference in Brazil he calls "Vitex," speaking just before Branson — that business ideas are like buses and opportunities: many keep going by, and the question is which one to jump on.
- He argues running a successful business takes more than 40 hours a week; if competitors work 60–70 hours, he works 80–90 to beat them, saying that is how badly he wants to win.
- He describes an extreme work-focused lifestyle as of 2017: no chill life, not watching TV shows all day, no TV in his house, and says he does not even have a home — "all I'm doing 24/7 is working."
- He claims business does not sleep: while you sleep, competitors are awake, so you should build an online business/website that promotes and sells continually — a 24/7 "salesman."
- He says none of his businesses are retail stores because he hates retail and loves the internet; a website is a storefront that can sell over and over, even overnight and across countries.
- He advises focusing on one product or service within the chosen business — not two or three — and only expanding into more revenue items once growth flatlines.
- He credits this one-product advice to Brian Lee (whom he identifies as founder of LegalZoom, ShoeDazzle, and The Honest Company with Jessica Alba), heard at a group dinner: have laser focus on one product or service, then expand when the business can no longer grow.
- He advises adapting fast, describing himself as a quick executor who keeps testing and adjusts quickly when things go wrong, warning that stopping means getting crushed by the next hungry 14- or 16-year-old founder.
- He says to listen to your peers, learned "the hard way" — citing advice from his mom and dad when younger — while noting peers (and parents) are not always right; the goal is to keep an open mind and weigh recurring feedback.
- He recommends gathering multiple opinions when unsure whether advice is right, arguing that more feedback improves decisions and raises the odds of succeeding.
- As a bonus point, he frames failure as inevitable for entrepreneurs — the question is when, not if — and urges learning from mistakes and not repeating them so you eventually learn what not to do.
- He downplays his own intelligence, saying he is "not that smart" and attended a "mediocre college" he names as Cal State Fullerton, attributing his success to learning from mistakes rather than being smarter than others.
