# McDonald's & Taco Bell show the real economy's state, and The genius of Bryan Johnson's marketing

## Summary
Eric opens with 2019-vs-mid-2024 fast-food price comparisons (Big Mac +87%, 10 McNuggets +68%,
cheeseburger +215%, McDonald's average +141% across five items; Taco Bell average +57%) as evidence
of persistent inflation and consumer strain, name-checking Taco Bell as "Neil's favorite restaurant"
and the place the two first met in person. Neil recounts a recent Brazil trip where a rushed
bathroom stop forced him into a McDonald's outside Florianópolis; the outlet's broken digital kiosk
and ~90-minute wait for a small order taught him that in Brazil, restaurant star ratings often
reflect service speed rather than food quality (a location with famously slow service was rated ~1
star despite average food). Neil then describes a $4,600 dental bill for his first-ever cavity,
using it to explain why he keeps an Amex "black card" — not lifestyle signaling but a genuine
travel-points arbitrage (~50% off first-class international tickets, e.g. Emirates ~$20K down to
~$10K by paying in points) — and describes the dentist office's luxury draws (floor-to-ceiling LA
view, a massaging chair, ceiling-mounted TV) as a case of experiential/word-of-mouth marketing that
keeps the practice booked out for weeks despite high prices. The episode closes with Neil previewing
an interview he just recorded with Bryan Johnson ("the Don't Die guy," founder of Braintree/Venmo,
now running the "Don't Die"/Blueprint longevity brand): Johnson's polarizing public-health stunts
(blood-plasma exchanges with his son and father, penile electromagnetic/sound-wave therapy and
Botox treatments framed around erection frequency as a health biomarker), his public data-driven
attacks on AG1/Athletic Greens' unsubstantiated claims, his open-research publishing on food
microplastics/heavy metals, and his stated belief ("making money is the most expensive thing you
can do") that Neil interprets as a preference for long-term compounding over short-term monetization
— building "a movement," even "a religion," rather than chasing an early exit.

## Key claims
- **Fast-food inflation (2019→mid-2024) as a proxy for broader economic strain:** McDonald's
  average +141% across five items (Big Mac $3.99→$5.29/[$7.49 cited variant] ~87%, 10 McNuggets
  $4.49→~$7.58 ~68%, cheeseburger $1→$3.15 ~215%); Taco Bell average +57% (Nachos BellGrande
  $3.29→$5.89 ~79%). (Eric Siu, figures presented on-air; cited as context, 2024-06-03)
- **Restaurant star ratings in Brazil often reflect service speed, not food quality** — learned
  firsthand when a slow-service McDonald's near Florianópolis (~90-minute wait for a small order,
  broken digital ordering kiosk) carried a ~1-star rating despite otherwise-average food, while
  faster-service locations rated ~4 stars. (Neil, 2024-06-03; personal anecdote, self-reported)
- **His Amex "black card" is a deliberate points-arbitrage tool, not a status symbol:** describes
  ~50% off international first-class fares by paying with points (e.g., an Emirates ticket priced
  at ~$20,000 cash effectively ~$10,000 in points); says the family previously held four black
  cards and has drawn down millions of points, now down to ~5-6 million remaining, used mainly by
  family members rather than himself. (Neil, 2024-06-03; self-reported)
- **A $4,600 first-ever dental bill** (two teeth requiring drilling between them) is framed as an
  example of experiential marketing: the practice's LA-skyline view, massaging treatment chairs,
  and ceiling-mounted TVs generate word-of-mouth strong enough to keep the practice booked out for
  weeks despite the price point. (Neil, 2024-06-03; personal anecdote)
- **Interviewed Bryan Johnson (Braintree/Venmo founder, now running the "Don't Die"/Blueprint
  longevity brand) for an upcoming episode.** Reports Johnson's stated quote, "making money is the
  most expensive thing you can do," interpreting it as a preference for deferred, long-term
  compounding (building "a movement"/"religion") over short-term monetization; notes Johnson's
  public, data-driven criticism of Athletic Greens (AG1) for making unsubstantiated ingredient
  claims, and Johnson's open research on food microplastics and heavy-metal content as a content-
  marketing tactic that builds authority while also "vilifying" a competitor. (Neil, 2024-06-03;
  Johnson's claims are third-party/self-reported by Johnson, relayed by Neil, not independently
  verified)
