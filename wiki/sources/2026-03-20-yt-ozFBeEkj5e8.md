# Why AI Engineers Are Beating Marketers (Marketing School)

## Summary

A dense, wide-ranging ~2,600-line episode of the co-hosted Marketing School podcast — HIGH marketing density, roughly an even Neil/Eric split, with several durable Neil frameworks worth promoting. ★ L3-candidate.

The spine (from the title): at the biggest Silicon Valley companies, "marketing" is increasingly run by **engineers and product people, not marketers**. It's triggered by a viral X claim that Anthropic's entire growth team was one non-technical person for 10 months (running paid search/social, app stores, email, SEO for a company valued in the hundreds of billions behind Claude). Neil is skeptical — the "team of one" narrative ignores external contractors (Super Bowl ads, four commercials) and internal engineers doing marketing work. Neil anchors this in his own experience: NP Digital has worked ONLY with engineers (never marketers) at a $100B+ private SV company for ~3 years, and years ago at Airbnb he worked with co-founder/CTO Nathan (Blecharczyk) on SEO/content/CRO and "not once" talked to marketing.

From there the two cover: **replace processes, not people** (Bret Taylor / Sierra framing, via Eric); Neil's **"train the AI, not the people"** thesis (organizations can't force 500 employees to a minimum AI-output quality bar — instead train the AI to adapt to each person's level → a Bain/McKinsey-style consulting opportunity); ad-spend mix by company size (startups → paid search #1; mid-size → content/SEO rises to #2; enterprise → **brand advertising #1**); **AI search is far more winner-take-all** than Google (position #1 ≈ 100% of revenue, #2 ≈ 32%, #3 ≈ 8%, #4 ≈ 1%); **ChatGPT ads** economics (cheaper CPA than Meta and Google, lower lead quality than Google) and the ChatGPT–Criteo partnership; **relationships/trust** as the enterprise revenue engine (Google E-E-A-T); why Neil still builds charts manually (AI errors + reputational risk); Eric's **"Single Brain" / forward-deployed-marketer** product and its AI-generated pitch deck (critiqued live); **APIs + docs as marketing** so agents discover you (Mintlify); **marketing to agents** as the next frontier; Lovable-vs-Cursor unit economics; and a long, personal close on brand-as-moat, work-hours/family (the "31% IRR vs. your daughters" post Eric sent Neil), and resiliency.

Key durable Neil positions: **brand is the moat in an AI world** ("very hard to replicate… doubling down"), **train the AI not the people**, **AI search winner-take-all economics**, **marketers are best-positioned to market to agents**, and **resiliency + learning from failure** as the core of their success. Because this is a co-hosted show with no caption speaker labels, attribution was inferred from anchors (below) and quarantined accordingly.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase unless quoted)

- Great products grow largely on their own; a good product is far easier to grow "naturally than having a crap product and amazing marketers." (Neil, 2026-03-20) — ai-marketing.
- The viral "Anthropic's growth team is one person" story is misleading: big companies contract marketing externally (Super Bowl ads, "four different commercials") and have engineers/product people doing marketing work internally. "There's no way it's just one person." (Neil, 2026-03-20)
- NP Digital has worked with a privately-held $100B+ Silicon Valley company for ~3 years and works ONLY with engineers there — never marketers; at Silicon Valley companies you deal with technical/product people, the reverse of Fortune 500. (Neil, 2026-03-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Years ago, while running Kissmetrics, Neil consulted for Airbnb via co-founder/CTO "Nathan" (Blecharczyk), fixing SEO/content/duplicate-page/CRO issues over ~1–2 years and "not once" spoke to anyone in marketing. (Neil, 2026-03-20)
- The right AI reframe is replacing **workflows/processes, not people** (attributed to Bret Taylor, CEO of Sierra); resistance comes from people tying identity to doing the task themselves. (Eric, 2026-03-20)
- **Train the AI, not the people:** rather than train 500 employees to hit a minimum AI-output quality bar (which fails — different skill levels, uneven buy-in), train the AI to detect who it's dealing with and adapt so it produces a minimum quality output regardless of user. This is a mass-scale consulting play — "another version of Bain or McKinsey or Accenture," but focused on training AI to deal with people. (Neil, 2026-03-20) — ai-marketing.
- Ad-spend mix by company size (NP Digital data): **startups** — paid search #1, organic social #2, content/SEO #3; **mid-size** (nine-plus figures) — paid search #1, content/SEO #2, paid social #3; **enterprise** — **brand advertising #1**, paid search #2, paid social #3. Big old brands get most customers from brand name, not paid. (Neil, 2026-03-20) — paid-ads, seo.
- Brand campaigns need humans in the loop; the Coca-Cola AI-generated polar-bear ads drew backlash ("you took the soul out of the ad") — a branding screw-up can tarnish reputation. (Neil, 2026-03-20)
- **AI search is far more winner-take-all than traditional search.** If position #1 = 100% of revenue, position #2 ≈ 32%, #3 ≈ 8%, #4 ≈ 1% in ChatGPT/Gemini/Perplexity answer lists. (Neil, 2026-03-20) — seo, ai-marketing.
- **ChatGPT ads** (NP Digital chart, Neil-presented, Eric-narrated): vs Meta, +256% lead quality and 46% cheaper CPA; vs Google, ~49% lower lead quality but 38% cheaper CPA. Google keeps the highest lead quality but the most expensive clicks; ChatGPT ads are currently the "Wild Wild West." Lesson: "whenever a new ad channel comes out, you want to hit it really hard before the cost start to go up." (Neil, 2026-03-20) — paid-ads.
- ChatGPT announced an ads partnership with **Criteo**, easing access to ChatGPT ads; both were surprised (Criteo seen as an old/spammy-ish affiliate network) — "a lot of this stuff is relationship-based." (Eric announces; Neil/Eric react, 2026-03-20)
- **APIs + technical docs as marketing:** as agents crawl the web, good API/documentation coverage lets agents discover your product first; Mintlify is winning by focusing only on documentation. (Eric, 2026-03-20) — ai-marketing.
- **Relationships/trust drive enterprise revenue:** Neil says NP Digital enterprise sales over the last ~3 months were "almost all relationships," and ~90%+ of enterprise revenue is becoming relationship-driven (even RFPs come through relationships). Relationships create trust; trust is what marketers overlook (ties to Google's E-E-A-T — the last "E"/T being trust). Single Grain (Eric, smaller) is ~20% relationships. (Neil + Eric, 2026-03-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Neil still produces charts **manually**: AI messes up the design style guide ~15–20% of the time and the data ~25–30% of the time; the reputational hit and error-fixing cost outweigh the ~$3,000/month design spend. He expects AI to get it 100% right in 6–12 months. (Neil, 2026-03-20)
- Eric's **"Single Brain"** = forward-deployed AI-agent teams for revenue operations ("2–3x your revenue output without growing head count"), modeled on Palantir/Anthropic forward-deployed engineers embedded in client stacks; NP Digital already embeds full-time marketers in some client offices (5+ years). (Eric concept; Neil confirms NP Digital embed model, 2026-03-20)
- **AI either makes you a learning machine or a lazy machine** — smart users ask more questions and expand; lazy users throw work "over the fence" and get slop. (Eric, 2026-03-20)
- **Marketing to agents** is the next frontier (eBay/G2/social-network "for agents"); marketers are among the best-positioned to do it because "they're already marketing to bots. Algorithms. And bots are trained off of humans." (Eric raises; Neil's take, 2026-03-20) — ai-marketing.
- **Brand is the moat in an AI world:** with less differentiation between corporations, brand is "still priceless… very hard to replicate, very hard to build," so Neil is doubling down on brand while others chase AI tactics. (Neil, 2026-03-20) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- The core of their success is **resiliency + learning from failure**, not raw intelligence; "most people just give up too early." Consistency (per the McDonald's-CEO content example) compounds. (Neil + Eric, 2026-03-20)
- Lovable vs Cursor economics (Eric-presented): Lovable ~$400M ARR / ~45 employees but most revenue passes through to model providers (Anthropic/OpenAI), margins reportedly worse after agentic modes, high churn (Neil heard ~$20M/week new vs ~$15M churning at one point — net ~$5M); Cursor ~$2B ARR with ~60% from enterprise. Both would "sell, not hold." (Eric + Neil, 2026-03-20)

## Notable verbatim quotes (Neil only)

> good products are much easier to grow naturally than having a crap product and amazing marketers.

> Is it really one growth person? Or are there a lot of other people dipping their toes in marketing? … Sure, they do that, but there's no way it's just one person.

> The solution isn't to train everyone on AI and get a minimum. The solution is to train the AI to deal with different personality and levels, so that way it can figure out who they're dealing with … so they can still produce a minimum quality output.

> instead of training the employees, train the AI to adapt to the humans, and do this at mass scale for everything, from marketing to engineering … It's pretty much another version of Bain or McKinsey or Accenture, but specifically focus on just training AI to deal with people wherever they are.

> AI is way more of a winner take all than traditional search.

> every time with marketing, whenever a new ad channel comes out, you want to hit it really hard before the cost start to go up.

> I don't want the reputational hit of just AI messing up, but one day it will get it right 100% of the time.

> I think that trust part is something that people overlook a lot in marketing … they forget to really emphasize on building trust.

> With all these changes happening in AI, I think the one thing that is still priceless and is the moat in business and in marketing is your brand. And I think that is very hard to replicate, it's very hard to build, and I'm doubling down on building our brand … especially in a world of AI, where there's less differentiation between corporations.

> I actually think it's a really easy transition. I think marketers are some of the best people set up to market to agents.

> if you have like half a brain and you're just resilient and you keep going, I think most people just give up too early.

> it's resiliency combined with learning from our failures … we try to avoid making the same mistakes over and over again.
