# How Often Should Content Be Updated - Neil Patel

## Summary
Neil explains NP Digital's/neilpatel.com's shift from a high-volume new-content
strategy (10+ blog posts/day at peak) to a low-volume-new + high-volume-refresh
strategy (roughly one new post/week, but 3-7 existing posts updated per weekday).
He frames content freshness (using Wikipedia as the exemplar) as a ranking factor
independent of raw content quality, and describes using Google Search Console
month-over-month and year-over-year traffic comparisons per page to decide what to
refresh. Eric adds a Forbes case study showing the same industry-wide shift toward
updating over net-new publishing.

## Key claims (dated, paraphrase unless noted)
- States that content freshness is why Wikipedia ranks well — not because it's the
  definitive resource on a topic, but because it is continually updated; extends
  this to a general preference searchers have for recent results over old ones.
  (Neil, 2023-10-30) — seo.
- Describes shifting neilpatel.com's own strategy from 10+ new blog posts/day at
  peak down to ~1/week (4-5/month), while a dedicated team updates 3-7 existing
  posts per weekday — a deliberate reallocation because updating existing content
  produced a bigger traffic/ranking uptick and higher ROI than net-new content; new
  content kept gaining traffic while old content quietly lost it. (Neil, 2023-10-30)
  — seo / content-marketing.
- Ledger of how he decides what to update: compares each page's traffic
  month-over-month (last 3 months) AND year-over-year in Google Search Console;
  anything declining in *either* window gets prioritized for an update, regardless
  of overall year-over-year growth. (Neil, 2023-10-30) — seo / analytics-cro.
- Explicitly reframes the common "just keep writing new content" mentality as
  wrong for a mature site — argues most sites should shift more resources to
  updating existing content instead. (Neil, 2023-10-30) — seo / content-marketing.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> If you ever do a search on Google, would you rather see a five-year-old result, or
> a one-year-old result, or a result that's from two weeks ago?

> We used to blog... 10 plus blog posts a day, right?... It's now shifted to I'll
> write a blog post once a week... but on the flip side, my team will update three
> to like six or seven blog posts a day.

> When we're focusing on just new content what we're finding is the new content
> will get traffic, but the old content was starting to lose traffic.

> I don't know why people have that mentality of like, oh, I just got to keep
> writing new content. I'm like, "Eh, I don't think you really need to continue
> writing new content."
