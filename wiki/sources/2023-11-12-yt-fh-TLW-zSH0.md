# Your Marketing Strategy is Doomed Without This (Ep. 2601)

## Summary
Short daily Marketing School episode on the one thing that will crush your marketing: the absence of a durable culture of experimentation. Eric Siu opens the argument (experimentation as an open-minded, failure-tolerant, "strong views loosely held" mindset; wins are nice but failures teach you), and Neil Patel agrees but adds his own caveat and the episode's most concrete contribution: a past winning test is not an "end-all be-all" because consumer behavior changes, so you must keep re-running experiments. Neil illustrates with a Kissmetrics story where a "login with Google" change first lifted conversions ~90%+ and later tanked them as privacy attitudes shifted. Neil's second angle is organizational: founders must drive the experimentation culture themselves because it gets harder to push down the hierarchy, and as companies scale (Neil cites ~19–20 countries) legacy issues, layers, and politics slow implementation even for founders.

## Key claims
- The one thing that will crush your marketing is failing to build/keep a culture of experimentation — being open-minded, open to failure, holding "strong views that are loosely held," and treating failures as learning. (Eric, 2023-11-12)
- Many people build a testing culture for a while, then let it lapse, and their marketing falls apart. (Eric, 2023-11-12)
- Neil agrees experimentation is essential but adds a caveat: internally your culture can't treat a test that once won as a permanent end-all-be-all — you may need to revert it, retest it, or try new/better variations because people change over time. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
- Kissmetrics case: simplifying registration to "sign up with Google," then changing verbiage to "login with Google" and removing the manual signup fields, produced a ~90%+ lift in conversions initially, carrying through the funnel. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
- Over time that same change decreased signups/conversions because consumers grew more conscious of privacy/data and not everyone wanted to authenticate through Google — but no one re-ran the experiment; they assumed the past winner still worked. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
- The eventual best-converting model was letting people either manually enter their info (name, email, phone, website URL) OR log in with Google — but reaching that required retesting. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
- Founders drive the vision and the change; if you're a founder who wants the most impact, it's in your interest to understand marketing so you can drive the culture of experimentation — it gets harder to push the further down the hierarchy it goes. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
- As an organization gets older/bigger, founders can still push change but not as fast as they'd like, because of legacy issues — policies, code, systems, procedures, tech stack, politics, and a large customer base. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
- Neil's company is in ~19–20 countries; new local leaders in markets like France, Germany, and the Netherlands run sales differently from how the company did it in the US, Canada, UK, Australia, Brazil, and India. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
- Neil's co-founder started getting on calls with every leader — not to say they're wrong, but to integrate local strategies with the company's and experiment toward a better combined approach. (Neil, 2023-11-12)
