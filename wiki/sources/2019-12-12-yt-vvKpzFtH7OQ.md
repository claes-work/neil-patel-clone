# How to Optimize For Google's E-A-T Algorithm

## Summary
Solo explainer on Google's E-A-T (expertise, authority, trustworthiness) and the Medic update.
Neil frames the update as Google refusing to rank people without topical authority regardless
of links or domain authority, using himself as the example: as a marketer he should not rank
for cancer cures, and — since he is 34 and "nowhere near" retirement — he should not rank for
401(k) and retirement advice either. He explicitly endorses the policy as the right thing to
do for users. Affected categories: happiness, health, financial stability, and anything
touching people's safety — news, government, law, financial advice, shopping information,
medical advice. Three tips: (1) establish expertise — put a truthful author bio on everything
explaining why you should be listened to (Google does not want a freelance journalist writing
financial advice or an Upwork generalist writing about brain tumors), plus guest posts in your
niche, podcast appearances (noting Google can now crawl podcasts), and external reviews and
testimonials on your site; (2) work with trusted sources — link out to back up claims, which
is why he attributes the 49% no-click stat to Jumpshot rather than asserting it bare; (3)
design content to help people, not to please Google/Facebook/Instagram — the platforms optimize
for their end users, so aligning with the user may cost short-run rankings and social reach but
wins long-run because the user signals are real. Closes plugging Neil Patel Digital.

## Key claims
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil said Google's Medic update stops people without authority on a topic from ranking well, and that no amount of links or domain authority overrides missing topical expertise.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil said he is a marketer, not a doctor, so Google should not rank him for medical content — and that Google also wants to exclude phonies claiming medical knowledge they lack.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil said he is 34 years old and has no clue about retirement because he is nowhere near that age, so he should not rank for 401(k)/retirement advice.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil said Google is doing this in the best interest of its users and called it the right thing to do.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil said E-A-T stands for expertise, authority and trustworthiness, and that building it lets you rank for more things.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil listed the page types most exposed to Medic/E-A-T as those touching happiness, health, financial stability or people's safety — news, government and law sites, financial advice, shopping information, medical advice, and general information about people.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil's tip 1 was to establish expertise by putting a bio everywhere you publish, stating truthfully why you are an expert on that topic — he said he would never claim to be a doctor because he is not.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil recommended guest posting on niche-relevant sites, appearing on other people's podcasts (noting Google can now crawl podcasts), and displaying external reviews and testimonials, all as authority signals.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil's tip 2 was to work with trusted sources and link out to back up claims — citing his own habit of attributing the "49% of Google searches result in no clicks" stat to Jumpshot, which derived it from analyzing billions of clicks.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil's tip 3 was to design content with the specific purpose of helping people rather than optimizing for Google, Facebook or Instagram — because those platforms optimize for their end users, so user-first content may underperform short-term but wins long-term via genuine user signals.
- As of 2019-12-12, Neil said the test of a page is whether it has a beneficial purpose and whether readers take away something that helps them rather than a gamble (like investing money where they shouldn't).
