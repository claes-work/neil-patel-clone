# What big brands sell, Mcdonald's Netherlands billboards that smell like french fries, & more

## Summary
Five segments. (1) Big brands (McDonald's, Nike) are observed to market "experiences and feelings" rather than products on Instagram — illustrated by McDonald's running unbranded (no logo, brand-color-only) billboards in the Netherlands that are scented like french fries. (2) A Q1 2024 ad-industry earnings recap: NBC Universal generated a reported $1.2B in Olympic ad sales; Publicis and Omnicom show recovery while WPP and Dentsu lag. Neil describes his own Q1 as "decent, not as good as we wanted," but with the US market up significantly year-over-year; NP Digital operates in 19 countries, with India specifically growing 40-50%+ YoY with "no dip" — NP Digital has grown to nearly 200 India-based employees serving India-market clients directly (not outsourcing), including Amazon India, Tata (which owns Jaguar/Range Rover), PwC, ESPN India, Black & Decker, Apollo Health (a major Indian hospital chain), and Zee TV (Sony) — described as paying enterprise-level rates, not discount pricing. (3) A tangent on ESPN's cord-cutting pressure (reportedly ~$10 of every cable-bundle subscriber's monthly fee flows to ESPN, a growing "ESPN tax"; Disney owns ~80%, Hearst ~20%) and its pivot to a multi-network streaming bundle, contrasted with Elon Musk's ~75% staff cuts at X/Twitter alongside claims of faster shipping velocity; and Instagram's ad-revenue growth (reported to be catching up to or surpassing Facebook and already exceeding YouTube by some measures) as a "these things take a decade to prove out" M&A patience lesson, following the well-known "Zuckerberg overpaid for Instagram" narrative. (4) A detailed private-equity anecdote: Neil recounts that when he and his co-founder (Mike Kamo) were previously approached by private-equity firms about selling NP Digital and ultimately chose not to sell, a dinner conversation in Las Vegas with Michael Delaney (owner of PE firm Court Square Capital, which went on to acquire Power Digital) stuck with him — Delaney reminisced that in earlier eras PE firms could buy and hold companies indefinitely, whereas modern PE has LP-driven exit timelines forcing sales; Neil took this as validation that his own "keep growing and holding" approach (rather than selling) can be the better model. A second cited lesson, relayed via his business partner listening to a podcast with Ben Horowitz, was that top-tier investors (Andreessen Horowitz) will intentionally overpay for high-conviction deals rather than get a "good price" on mediocre ones — paralleled to a bullish stance on Nvidia being justified even at an "overvalued" multiple. (5) Neil recounts a recent meeting with a wealth adviser who pushed for portfolio diversification; Neil pushed back, stating he prefers concentrating his effort/capital in his own business (citing that a "good" wealth adviser targets ~10%/year returns, which he says his business exceeds) — invoking a version of the Warren Buffett "diversification is for idiots" framing. Neil adds a candid hypothetical regret: if he could redo his career, he says he would have sold 100% of NP Digital during a high-valuation period and then bought it back later at a discount, rather than holding onto it the whole way through — though he immediately affirms he's "really happy" he kept the business as things stand.
