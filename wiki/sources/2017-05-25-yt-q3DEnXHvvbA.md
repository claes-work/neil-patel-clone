# How to Promote Your Content When You Have No Followers

## Summary
Neil Patel explains how to promote blog content when you have no social following, framing it as the same strategy he uses whenever he launches a new blog in a new space under a new author name (for coworkers or companies he invested in). The core tactic is to generously link out to relevant external sources from within your posts — even competitors — whenever it genuinely benefits the reader, on the order of 10 to 20 outbound links per 2,000-word post. Linking out builds reader trust and drives free traffic to the sites you cite. The second step converts those links into promotion: after linking to someone, email them a short, flattering note letting them know you featured their work, and add a light postscript asking them to share the post. Because people feel flattered and value the free traffic, many will share it on Twitter, LinkedIn, and Facebook, earning you additional traffic. Neil says this is exactly what he did with his own blogs before he had a large reader base.

## Key claims
- When you write a blog post you should link out to relevant sources — even to competitors — whenever the linked content benefits your readers (as of 2017-05-25).
- Linking out puts other people first, which shows trust; as trust builds, readers keep coming back (as of 2017-05-25).
- Neil links out to at least 10 to 20 sources in a typical 2,000-word blog post, using other websites' stats to back up his points (as of 2017-05-25).
- You should not link for the sake of it or "shove" links in — only link out when it genuinely benefits readers (as of 2017-05-25).
- Linking out benefits both parties: readers learn more, and the linked sites appreciate the free traffic you send them (as of 2017-05-25).
- After linking to someone, email them a short note telling them you linked to their work and inviting them to check it out, with a P.S. asking them to share the post (as of 2017-05-25).
- Flattered recipients whose content is strong will often share your post on Twitter, LinkedIn, and Facebook, driving you more traffic (as of 2017-05-25).
- This outbound-link-plus-outreach method is how Neil promoted all his content and grew each new blog when starting off, and it is the same strategy he teaches coworkers and invested companies who launch blogs under new author names (as of 2017-05-25).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> When you're writing a blog post, what you need to do is link out to relevant sources. Even if it's a competitor, that's okay. Don't worry about it.

> It really is that simple, because by doing that you're putting them first. It shows trust. As you build more trust, people will love you and they'll keep coming back.

> In general, every time I have a 2,000 word blog post, I link out to at least 10 to 20 sources.

> Now you don't want to shove them in there, you don't want to just link up for the sake of it, only link out when it benefits other people.

> I'll email John saying, "Hey John, I love your work, so much so that I even linked out to you in my latest blog post. Feel free to check it out. Cheers, Neil. P.S. If you share this post, it wouldn't just make my day, it would make my year."

> That's how I promote all of my content when I'm starting off. That's what I did when I started my blogs.
