# NVIDIA's Jenson Huang's unorthodox leadership style, and How AI agents will change marketing

## Summary
Reacts to a Patrick Collison/Stripe Sessions interview with Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang,
cross-referencing it against Neil's own recent podcast interview with Eight Sleep CEO
Matteo Franceschetti ("Mato"): both leaders reportedly have no standing 1:1 meetings
(Huang has 60 direct reports and no management layer between himself and them; Mato uses
open office hours plus a memo-required system for idea pitches — the same detail Neil
first related in the 2024-05-11 AOA coaching-call preview). Other Huang leadership
takeaways discussed: feedback given in group settings rather than privately (contrasted
with Warren Buffett's "criticize privately, praise publicly" doctrine), and a philosophy
of "torturing people into greatness" alongside taking care of them well — which Neil
says he agrees with, framing NP Digital's demanding-but-generous management style
similarly. Neil states NP Digital's core values include "own it" (his personal favorite)
and "be improv[e] and obsessed," and relates a story (unnamed company, "IBM" floated
uncertainly) about not accepting a subordinate's resignation after a ~$250K mistake,
instead treating it as a paid lesson. The conversation turns to AI agents: reacting to a
Aaron Levie (Box) tweet arguing AI agents unlock SaaS growth uncorrelated to headcount,
Neil predicts that in 5-10 years far less headcount will be needed and only "the top 5%"
of people will remain valuable, citing a *21 Lessons for the 21st Century* (Yuval Noah
Harari) passage about a coming "useless class" and stating he told his team he won't
accept anyone at Single Grain choosing to become "useless." He tempers this with an
account of a contact (Max, at Dentsu, a ~$9-10B-revenue global ad agency) who spent ~2.5
years trying to automate roles out of the organization and found strong internal
resistance even with leadership buy-in — used as evidence that large/enterprise
organizations adopt AI far slower than technology alone would suggest (citing NP
Digital's own enterprise clients still not being "fully on the cloud" years after cloud
adoption became technically trivial). The two hosts explicitly disagree on how AI's
scale compares to the cloud/internet: Eric argues AI is being called "bigger than the
internet" by top investors and that mega-cap tech is deploying capital at a pace beyond
what cloud saw; Neil agrees AI will be larger long-term but insists enterprise-adoption
timelines will still lag years behind startups. Closes with adoption-curve data
(smartphones, electricity, AI user counts) and a personal anecdote about a phone call
with a former Reliance Jio executive on India's smartphone-driven AI-adoption speed.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> I told my team... you know there's this book called 21 Questions for the 21st
> Century... there's going to be a class of useless humans. I was like, guys, I don't
> know about you, I don't want to be useless, and I will not accept anybody at Single
> Grain wanting to be useless.

> Crap just moves slow no matter what people say — like technology may be there but
> they still move slow. I think startups will embrace it really quickly... but not
> necessarily the way that big corporations do business.

> My main core value [is] "own it"... if you did great, great, if you did bad, own it —
> what did you learn from it? Let's move on and give yourself a pat on the back for
> learning and let's try to be better next time.
