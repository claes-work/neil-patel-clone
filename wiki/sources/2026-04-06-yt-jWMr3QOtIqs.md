# Meta Just Crossed A Dangerous Line With AI (Marketing School)

## Summary
Co-hosted Marketing School episode, and the attribution split is heavily weighted toward Eric this time: roughly the first half and most of the AI thread is Eric Siu narrating his own operations (his Anthropic bill hitting ~$7,500, his "Alfred" agents "invading Slack," buying Nvidia infrastructure, using an open-source Claude-family agent tool rendered "Open Claw" to auto-generate episode topics, and his "Mr. Sue" agent auto-negotiating influencer deals). Eric also drives the two headline news reactions — Nvidia's re-accelerating growth and the alarmist "Meta dropped an AI (rendered 'Tribe V2') that knows what will make you feel something before you feel it" segment that gives the video its clickbait title. That Meta segment is Eric's dramatized news reaction and is largely unverifiable; it is quarantined below.

Neil's persona-relevant contribution is concentrated in the marketing-substance middle-to-back: (1) a "negative marketing works" observation (doctors' PFAS/microplastics scare content drives him straight to Amazon to buy bamboo toilet paper — "Reel," Amazon Basics), (2) a premium-positioning parable from a gas-station stop with his kids ("if you're going to eat chocolate candy, eat the good stuff"), (3) his answer to "highest-paying customer-acquisition IC job" (paid or SEO, not organic social) plus the counter to Kevin O'Leary that in real enterprise data the highest-paid non-C-level role is whoever manages the money, because budgets flow to Google and Meta, and (4) an influencer-marketing stance: disclosure kills performance, influencer doesn't scale like paid/SEO/GEO, and the model that works is right-audience niche fit (his Legion Athletics example) not follower count — with a personal sponsorship rule that he'd rather a partner drive him business/leads than pay him a fee. These are solid, on-domain, reusable Neil positions but they restate known Neil views rather than introduce a new framework, so this is best read as **mostly news-reaction banter with a few embedded Neil marketing takes** — NOT flagged ★ L3-candidate.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- Negative/scare marketing reliably drives purchases: fear content about PFAS/microplastics in paper products sends him straight to Amazon to buy "clean" alternatives (bamboo toilet paper), so the scientists posting it are inadvertently growing the alternative brands. (Neil, 2026-04-06) — content-marketing.
- Premium/quality positioning: some indulgences are unavoidable, so market and choose the good version — "if you're going to eat chocolate candy, eat the good stuff," don't bother with the cheap version. (Neil, 2026-04-06) — content-marketing.
- Asked the highest-paying customer-acquisition individual-contributor role, Neil picks whoever runs paid or SEO — not organic social. (Neil, 2026-04-06) — paid-ads / social-media.
- Counter to Kevin O'Leary's "social-media customer acquisition is the highest-paid job": in the enterprise data NP Digital sees, organic social is NOT the highest-paid IC; for large corporations budgets flow to Google and Meta (visible in their stock prices), and people get paid the most to manage where the money goes (excluding C-level like CMO). (Neil, 2026-04-06) — paid-ads / agency-entrepreneurship.
- Their read isn't just their own businesses — same pattern shows up across many enterprise clients and offline data across sectors. (Neil + Eric, 2026-04-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Influencer marketing underperforms paid because it must be labeled as paid: the moment the paid-disclosure appears at the start of a piece, drop-off is severe; influencer can be profitable but doesn't scale the way paid or organic SEO/GEO does. (attributed Neil — see notes, 2026-04-06) — social-media / paid-ads.
- The influencer model that works is right-audience fit, not reach: e.g. Legion Athletics pays die-hard niche lifters (smaller followings, lower fees) and it scales profitably — good product + right influencers beats chasing follower/view counts. (attributed Neil — see notes, 2026-04-06) — social-media.
- Personal sponsorship rule: he doesn't chase influencer/sponsor income — he'd rather a partner drive him more business/leads than pay a fee, so he mostly ignores paid influencer offers. (attributed Neil — see notes, 2026-04-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Deals still need a human: even with AI agents, the parties reaching out are usually agencies acting on a platform's behalf (TikTok, etc.), not the platform; getting set up/paid as a vendor directly (e.g. LinkedIn) is painful, so you can't fully offload negotiation to an agent yet. (attributed Neil — see notes, 2026-04-06) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- AI-agent spend is exploding and becoming real (not "pie in the sky"): individuals, not just companies, now run affordable agents to work more efficiently — a durable demand catalyst behind Nvidia's re-accelerating growth. (Eric, 2026-04-06) — ai-marketing.
- Kevin O'Leary claims social-media customer-acquisition talent went from ~$48k to ~$250k salaries, with contractors clearing ~$500k by turning content into ~59-second social ads that acquire ~200 customers/week. (Eric relaying O'Leary, 2026-04-06) — social-media.
- Meta released an AI ("Tribe V2") trained on ~1,000 hours of brain scans from ~700 people in MRIs that predicts what content will trigger your dopamine before you feel it, which Meta could hand to advertisers. (Eric — unverified news reaction, 2026-04-06) — ai-marketing.
- Eric's agent ("Mr. Sue") auto-negotiates inbound influencer offers, refusing anything under $25k and countering on different angles, sending him end-of-day summaries for approval; Sam from Writesonic runs a similar Open-Claw + Telegram approve/reject setup. (Eric, 2026-04-06) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
