# Jaguar's Controversial Marketing Campaign (Marketing School)

## Summary

A short co-hosted Marketing School episode reacting to Jaguar's late-2024 rebrand and campaign — the dropped Jaguar logo/wordmark and a much-mocked commercial that had "nothing to do with cars" and instead centered on gender identity/diversity. Eric carries the play-by-play (the ad looked like fashion not cars; the brand's X account looked "hacked" as it replied to feedback; Elon Musk tweeted "I thought you sell cars?"; comparisons to the Bud Light/Budweiser controversy; Volvo and Porsche running product-first ads the same day; the Collison brothers of Stripe and DHH of Basecamp calling it a fiasco). Neil delivers the core marketing argument: keep political/social stances out of your marketing, because taking a stance alienates your core customer base (Jaguar's being ~middle-aged men), and a publicly traded company owes its shareholders profit "whether people like it or not." Both stress they have no issue with anyone's personal beliefs or identity (friends and team members who've transitioned) — the objection is companies imposing politics through their brand. Neil predicts the responsible Jaguar people get fired within a year. The "you should have hired an ad agency" beat leads into Eric's two Agency Owners Association sponsor reads (marketingschool.io/agency). The recurring positive counter-example is Volvo: stick to your core mission (safety) and your buyer market — the "tank" brand recognition.

## Key claims
- Taking a political/social stance in your marketing takes a "stance in a certain direction" that alienates your main/original customer base; if you choose to do that you have to be okay with revenue loss — and if that's genuinely where the business wants to go, fine. (Neil, 2024-11-27)
- A publicly traded company's job is to do what's best for shareholders — make them money "whether people like it or not"; injecting politics that alienates core customers works against that duty. (Neil, 2024-11-27)
- Neither Neil nor Eric objects to anyone's personal beliefs or identity — they support people doing whatever makes them happy (they have friends who have transitioned and team members) — the problem is companies imposing those politics on everyone else through their marketing. (Neil, 2024-11-27)
- Prediction: within a year, some of the Jaguar people behind this will be fired/terminated. (Neil, 2024-11-27)
- Jaguar dropped its Jaguar logo and changed its lettering/wordmark, and ran a commercial that had nothing to do with cars — it looked more like fashion and was focused on gender identity/diversity; most viewers (including the hosts) couldn't tell what company or product it was for. (Eric, 2024-11-27)
- The campaign misread its target audience — reported online as roughly middle-aged men — and repeats the Bud Light/Budweiser mistake ("another Budweiser campaign all over again... they didn't learn"). (Eric, 2024-11-27)
- Positive counter-examples: Volvo and Porsche released ads around the same time that simply showed the product; the lesson is "build a good product, don't talk about other [stuff]." (Eric, 2024-11-27)
- Brand lesson via Volvo: stick to your core mission and buyer market — Volvo's whole pitch is safety (a recent ad dramatizes a Volvo stopping "on a dime" to save a pregnant driver's family; "the safest Volvo they ever built"), which builds durable brand recognition (the "tank" association). (attribution uncertain — likely Eric; kept out of Neil's quote bank)
- Business framing offered against the controversy: a business is people having a problem and you solving it — remove opinions and politics and just help people solve their problem, and stick with your core mission. (attribution uncertain — likely Neil or Eric; kept out of Neil's quote bank)
- Jaguar should have hired an ad agency — a rebuttal to the "you can do everything on your own with AI and technology" idea; an agency likely would have prevented this mistake. (Eric, 2024-11-27 — leads into sponsor read)
