# The ONLY Video You Need To Understand SEO

## Summary
Neil Patel opens a seven-week SEO training series with a foundational introduction to what SEO is and why it matters, framing it as the lesson that precedes keyword research. He begins by busting five common myths ("Fact or Fiction") — that hard industries can't rank, that only big companies win, that more pages is always better, that you must manually submit your site, and that you must spend money to win — arguing each is false or misleading. He then organizes the lesson around three keys: why SEO is important (it's a "24-hour salesman" that keeps delivering traffic without ongoing ad spend), choosing your battleground (Google's dominance across desktop and mobile, and the fact that ranking position determines whether you're seen at all), and unlocking the SEO factors that win online. He stresses there is no single silver bullet — rankings come from many small factors adding up — and walks through page speed, mobile user experience, topical/in-depth content, URL length, content length, backlinks, brand and user signals, and site security (SSL/HTTPS). Throughout he points viewers to his own tools and resources: Ubersuggest's Site Audit Report and Dashboard for tracking, plus downloadable guides at neilpatel.com/training, and recommends following Google's Search Liaison and Danny Sullivan for algorithm updates.

## Key claims
- 93% of online experiences start with a search engine, and search engines (Google, YouTube, Kayak, etc.) are used constantly all around us (as of 2020-05-04).
- Myth "my industry is too hard to rank in" is false — any industry can rank on Google, though some take longer than others (as of 2020-05-04).
- Myth "only big companies rank well" is false — small mom-and-pop businesses regularly rank and change their lives (as of 2020-05-04).
- Myth "more pages is better" is false; as an example, About.com cut more than half its content, split it into many minisites, and started getting more traffic (as of 2020-05-04).
- Myth "you must manually submit your site" is false — Google detects sites automatically, though creating a Google Search Console account and submitting a sitemap is still worthwhile (as of 2020-05-04).
- Myth "you must spend money to win online" is false, and that affordability is why Neil got into SEO in the first place (as of 2020-05-04).
- SEO is the process of optimizing a site for a given search word or term; Neil frames it as a "24-hour salesman" that gets found across any time zone or country (as of 2020-05-04).
- Unlike paid ads, where traffic dies when you stop paying, SEO continues delivering traffic even if you do nothing for a week (as of 2020-05-04).
- Neil's top five reasons to love SEO: it's simple, it increases traffic, it generates positive ROI, it builds brand awareness, and it builds trust — plus your competition is already doing it (as of 2020-05-04).
- People tend to trust content that ranks in Google ("it ranks in Google, it must be true"), which builds trust — but this is not a license to publish deceptive information (as of 2020-05-04).
- Google's estimated desktop search share is 87% (roughly 8.7 out of 10); on mobile it is even higher at roughly 9.5 out of 10, driven partly by Android device usage; there are an estimated 4.5 billion active internet users (as of 2020-05-04).
- Position is everything: over 31.7% of people click the number-one result (per Advanced Web Ranking), and if you're not in the top 10 you won't be seen at all (as of 2020-05-04).
- Google offers some SEO guidance (guidelines to stand out and optimize pages for users, not search engines) but it's generic and doesn't reveal much (as of 2020-05-04).
- Ubersuggest's Site Audit Report breaks down the factors Google looks at — health, issues, redirects, warnings, recommendations — and you should fix them across all pages, since Google wants to rank fully optimized sites, not just a page or two (as of 2020-05-04).
- Useful Google information sources: the Search Liaison Twitter account and Danny Sullivan (both at Google) for algorithm updates, and the Think with Google website for data (as of 2020-05-04).
- There is no silver bullet in SEO — rankings come from many small factors that add up (as of 2020-05-04).
- Page speed matters: every one-second delay means roughly a 7% reduction in conversions (per HubSpot), and Ubersuggest's Site Audit Report flags speed issues (as of 2020-05-04).
- Mobile user experience is critical as mobile usage keeps rising — so much so that Google uses a mobile-first index (as of 2020-05-04).
- Topical, in-depth content ranks better: covering a topic deeply across related subtopics (e.g., a cats page also covering kittens, cat food, healthy weight) beats going broad and shallow across random topics (as of 2020-05-04).
- URL length affects rankings (per Ahrefs): the longer the URL, the lower the rankings, so keep URLs short and to the point (as of 2020-05-04).
- Content length generally helps — longer content earns more social shares and performs better — but only without fluff; fluff-filled long articles won't do well (as of 2020-05-04).
- Backlinks are a major factor and (per an Ahrefs chart) have more impact on rankings than on-page SEO; getting other sites to link to you is very important (as of 2020-05-04).
- Brand and user signals matter and are overlooked by most SEOs; Google's former CEO said brands are how you sort out the low-quality web, and former Head of Web Spam Matt Cutts described a classifier separating authoritative sites (IRS, Wikipedia, New York Times) from low-quality ones (as of 2020-05-04).
- Site security matters: use an SSL certificate to get HTTPS, since sites without SSL are more likely to trigger a browser warning (as of 2020-05-04).
- Getting-started actions: create a project in the Ubersuggest Dashboard to track rankings over time, and download the Google Search Packs, Local Landing Page, and Local Directory Checklist from neilpatel.com/training to set the right foundation before keyword research (as of 2020-05-04).
