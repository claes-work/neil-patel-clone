# How Nerds Candy Went From $50M To $500M (Marketing School)

## Summary

A short, casual co-hosted Marketing School episode built around a Wall Street Journal story on Nerds candy. The hosts riff on how a decades-old, stale 1980s brand was revitalized after a 2018 acquisition: the launch of Nerds Gummy Clusters (2020) turned a ~$40–50M brand into a line where one new product now does ~$500M in sales and ~90% of Nerds' overall revenue, rivaling Starburst, Sour Patch Kids, and Skittles. The hosts draw a parallel to the Stanley Cup reusable water bottle, which grew from ~$74M (2019) to ~$750M (2023) after pivoting from targeting construction workers to leaning into a pastel/mom audience surfaced by a blogger-influencer campaign and TikTok virality — framed as "same product line but better/different marketing" plus a new target audience. They also note U.S. sugar-candy sales are up ~74% since 2020. The marketing lesson: sometimes one new product (or a reposition/revitalization by new ownership) can 10x a company, and sticking with a long-lived brand asset pays off. One sponsor ad read for the Agency Owners Association is Eric's. No standalone Neil framework or heuristic is introduced; this is a low-stakes banter episode.

## Key claims
- Nerds was a ~$50M-in-sales brand about six years ago; this year Nerds Gummy Clusters alone has generated more than $500M. (host — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Per the WSJ piece: the brand was acquired in 2018 when the entire Nerds line was ~$40M in sales; the privately held candy conglomerate needed a new product to revitalize the stale 1980s brand and released a bean-shaped gummy coated in irregular Technicolor pebbles in 2020. (host reading WSJ — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- The following year (2021) Nerds Gummy Clusters were responsible for nearly $100M of sales; this year sales crossed $500M even before Halloween, and this one new product now generates ~90% of Nerds' overall revenue, rivaling Starburst, Sour Patch Kids, and even Skittles. (host reading WSJ — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Speculation that the (likely private-equity) owner will sell the company again, gearing up for another exit. (host — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Lesson: sometimes you just need to figure out one new product and it changes the entire company. (host — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Stanley Cup parallel: reusable water bottles became one of the most sought-after beverage containers largely thanks to TikTok; Stanley's revenue jumped from ~$74M (2019) to ~$750M (2023). (host reading article — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Stanley "blew up" because it pivoted: originally targeting construction workers, a blogger/influencer asked for the cups in a pastel color for a campaign, it took off with moms, and Stanley leaned into that far more profitable audience — "same product line but better/different marketing," just changing colors and targeting women. (host — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Aside: the speaker prefers/buys Yeti products, and Yeti is estimated to do over $100M a year too. (host — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Nerds Gummy Clusters' creators shrank the size of the hard-shell Nerd candy to better coat the gummy middle. (host reading WSJ — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- U.S. sugar-candy sales, which you'd expect to be down, are actually up ~74% since 2020. (host — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
- Closing takeaway: because Nerds has been around so long, you can 10x it just by sticking with it; sometimes a company needs someone to come in and revitalize it, which is what happened here. (host — attribution uncertain, 2024-11-15)
