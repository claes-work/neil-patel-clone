# Future-Proof Your Content Strategy in the Age of AI

## Summary
AI-focused interview (April 2023, early-ChatGPT era). States his **three-part
"marketing formula"**: (1) go after a large **TAM** (total addressable market) —
niche and broad markets take similar effort to market, so pick the bigger one;
(2) identify something to give away that disrupts the category (his example:
giving away payroll software for free to sell health insurance, since payroll
customers are a proxy for insurance buyers); (3) go **omnichannel**, since no
single platform sustains growth alone. Repeats and extends his "first inning of
AI" analogy: expects each successive "inning" of AI maturity to be shorter than
the last as the technology compounds (compares to the acceleration of private
spaceflight/Starlink after NASA's slower early progress); frames "inning two" as
AI becoming ambient/integrated into daily workflows and analytics (not just
prompt-response novelty), rather than dramatically more powerful chatbots.
Cites a self-reported survey of "over 8,000 companies" finding heavy 2023 AI
investment (same 8,000-company figure recorded in
[[2023-04-06-yt-Ji5urXQRoLo]] this batch) and references a McKinsey report
finding most AI spend goes to internal systems/process automation, not content
creation. On content quality: reiterates that AI-generated content is only
useful once heavily human-edited for E-E-A-T (experience, expertise,
authority, trust); demonstrates with a "how to dunk a basketball if you're
short" search returning 13M+ results against ~20 monthly US searches, to argue
that raw content volume (AI-assisted or not) can't beat the already-saturated
long tail — the fix is fewer, better pieces plus heavy promotion (his rule of
thumb: "spend 20% of time creating content, 80% promoting it"). Discusses the
Ryan Reynolds ChatGPT-scripted-ad example as a real but heavily-produced/
scripted case, not representative of typical AI use. On jobs: argues 2023
layoffs (cites Facebook's ~5,000-10,000-person layoff announcement around this
date) are driven by the broader economy and missed earnings, not AI
displacement; states AI is currently creating more marketing/engineering/
product-role demand than it eliminates, though he expects that to shift over
"three-plus years"; identifies manual/repetitive labor (e.g. burger-flipping)
as most vulnerable, and creative/coaching roles as relatively AI-resistant as
long as practitioners keep innovating rather than relying on AI's regurgitated
output. Recommends Copyscape to check AI output for plagiarism before
publishing.
