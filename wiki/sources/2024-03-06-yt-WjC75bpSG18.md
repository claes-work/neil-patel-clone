# How to run your company like Amazon and Why Google and Microsoft are laying off people

## Summary
Built around the book *Working Backwards* (by a former Bezos chief-of-staff and a 15-year Amazon veteran who helped launch Prime/Kindle), this episode centers on two Amazon practices Neil says he has personally **adopted at his own company**: (1) replacing PowerPoint pitches with written six-to-seven-page narrative memos that force clear thinking before a decision meeting, complete with Amazon's meeting-time-allocation formula (a third of the meeting for silent reading, the rest for debate); Neil describes writing his own ~2-hour, 6–7-page memo on the "missing middle 60%" funnel problem surfaced at his recent founders retreat (see [[wiki/sources/2024-02-23-yt-XsWZmxY33bc]]), which sparked concrete follow-through. (2) A detailed account of NP Digital's own "Extreme reference checking" hiring process — collecting references from two former managers, two direct reports, and two peers for senior hires, largely discounting glowing references (which are the majority) but treating rare negative or "back-door" references as highly informative, including an anecdote where a back-door check surfaced a serious red flag (rumored sexual-offender history) on a candidate HR otherwise loved, leading to a pass. Also recaps Amazon's "Bar Raiser" hiring-oversight role and its removal of formal reference checks in favor of back-channel ones, a Seattle-residency-era personal observation about Amazon's talent-acquisition lengths (relocating an Indian family and replicating their apartment layout in the US), and closes on an Azure-vs-AWS growth-rate comparison and speculation on 2024 Google/Microsoft layoffs.

## Key claims
- **Adopted narrative-memo culture from *Working Backwards* at his own company.** Instead of PowerPoint pitches, ideas must be written as narrative memos (often six to seven pages, sometimes taking a week or two to draft) before a decision meeting; Neil says this "forces clear thinking" and cuts down on scattered ideas, and that he personally writes memos too ("leading by example"). He wrote a ~2-hour, 6–7-page memo on the middle-60%-of-funnel problem after his founders retreat, which "sparked a lot of side conversations and ideas" and turned into a concrete action list his team is now executing. (Neil, 2024-03-06)
- **Amazon's meeting-time-allocation rule, cited approvingly:** a six-page narrative gets 60 minutes (20 minutes silent reading, 40 minutes deciding/debating); a three-page memo gets 30 minutes (10 minutes reading, 20 debating) — the read-during-the-meeting structure ensures accountability since no one can claim they didn't have time to read it beforehand. (Neil, 2024-03-06)
- **NP Digital's "Extreme reference checking" process.** For senior hires (e.g., VP level), NP Digital collects references from two former managers, two direct reports, and two peers, and documents/rates all of it. Positive references are the large majority and are largely discounted as unreliable signal; negative or back-door references are rare but "quite telling" and have prevented bad hires. One anecdote: a back-door reference check on a candidate the HR team loved surfaced they were rumored to be a sex offender, leading NP Digital to pass despite HR's enthusiasm. (Neil, 2024-03-06)
- **Personal Seattle-era observation of Amazon's talent-acquisition practices.** Neil says he "used to live in Seattle" and, during that period, observed Amazon go to unusual lengths to recruit strong "systems and processes" operators globally regardless of pedigree — including one case (recounted from the book/his own knowledge) where Amazon relocated an Indian family to the US and had a crew photograph and replicate their apartment's layout before the family arrived, prior to Amazon even entering the Indian market. (Neil, 2024-03-06) `(biographical note: corroborates a "Seattle residency" period already flagged as a texture point elsewhere in the wiki per synthesis pass 15 notes — cross-reference at synthesis)`
- **Endorses Amazon's "Bar Raiser" hiring-oversight role** (a senior employee, uninvolved in the hiring decision itself, who checks that interviewers are rigorously testing candidates against Amazon's ~14 leadership values via structured historical-experience questions) as a genuinely useful structural hiring safeguard, and recommends the book outright ("no affiliate commission needed"). (Neil, 2024-03-06)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> [Writing memos] is a pain in the ass, yes, but at the same time it's really — it feels a lot clearer in terms of where we're going.

> We're not just throwing ideas all over the place — we're forcing clear thinking.

> The way we look at it is: if someone's going to give you reference checks, almost everyone's going to say positive things — you can pretty much ignore those. The ones that are negative, if you get those, it is quite a bit telling, and it saved you potentially making a bad decision.

> We do this thing called Extreme reference checking where... if you're a senior, like a VP or something, we want two people that were your managers, two people that are your direct reports, and two people that were your peers.
