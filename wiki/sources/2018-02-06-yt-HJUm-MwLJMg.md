# How to Make a Landing Page That Converts — 5 Tips

## Summary

In this solo explainer, Neil Patel gives five tips for building a high-converting landing page. He argues that a lead-capture page (name and email) can convert over 40%, while product-sales pages convert lower (roughly 3–10% depending on price point and traffic targeting). His five levers are: start from proven templates (Instapage, ClickFunnels, Leadpages), craft an amazing unique value proposition, keep the page simple and free of fluff, show social proof, and add a short explainer video. The title carries a "(2023)" re-title, but the video was published 2018-02-06 and claims are dated accordingly.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-02-06, Neil claims a landing page collecting leads (name and email) can convert over 40%, whereas selling a $100 product will not reach a 40% conversion rate.
- As of 2018-02-06, he states product-sales landing pages typically convert at roughly 3, 4, or 5 percent, and sometimes up to around 10 percent, depending on price point and how targeted the traffic is.
- Tip 1 — Use landing-page tools like Instapage, ClickFunnels, and Leadpages: Neil says they break down which landing pages in their system convert best using data from their large customer base (thousands to hundreds of thousands of customers), so you should start with the highest-converting template rather than guessing on looks.
- Tip 2 — Have an amazing unique value proposition: a weak offer ("give your name and email and I'll send you a free PDF or weekly updates") converts poorly. Neil says his own opt-ins give free courses and guides he has spent thirty thousand dollars creating, and that going above and beyond is why his offers convert better than most.
- Neil frames the value proposition as needing to solve a real, deep problem the visitor faces; merely scratching the surface makes people much less likely to opt in or buy.
- Neil states that across everything he has tested on landing pages, copy and value proposition are among the biggest factors affecting conversion rate.
- Tip 3 — Keep it simple ("keep it simple silly"): Neil says he named one of his companies KISSmetrics on this principle. Remove unnecessary form fields (e.g., cut 10 fields to the 2 you actually need) and cut repetitive/fluffy copy. Simple pages tend to convert better than long, rambling ones.
- Neil clarifies he is not advocating short landing pages per se — a longer page is fine if it is needed to answer objections (the concerns people have before opting in or buying) — but it must get to the point and remove fluff.
- Tip 4 — Show social proof: this can take many forms — testimonials, case studies, results other people are experiencing, or videos. He cites his friend Sam Ovens' [transcript garble: "Sam Evans"] site consulting.com as having over 3,000 video testimonials as an example of heavy social proof, and says the best type of social proof depends on the landing page and business.
- Tip 5 — Create a video: not everyone likes to read; a video should explain everything the visitor gets when they opt in, buy, or sign up. Neil advises the video be short — under three minutes, ideally under one minute — answer objections, and describe things in a logical order rather than bouncing around.
- Neil notes a tradeoff: adding video can sometimes decrease conversions, but the quality of customer generated tends to be better and they tend to spend more over their lifetime value, so video should be tested.
