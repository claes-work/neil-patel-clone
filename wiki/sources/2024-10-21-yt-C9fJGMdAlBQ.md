## Alex Hormozi (GUEST — quarantined context, NOT persona data)
[[wiki/entities/alex-hormozi]]
- acquisition.com runs the opposite of the Berkshire "delegation to the point of abdication" model — they are hands-on **growth partners**, because their deal flow wants help scaling rather than a buy-cheap-and-hold arbitrage. (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Deal-box criteria: like the founders, big enough vision, product good enough long-term (they won't redo product-market fit), and a floor of ~$3M top-line / at least $1M EBITDA, in consumer services or business services (brick-and-mortar chains or national services), where an "easy 5x" is visible. (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- "We want you to be very specifically incompetent" (his head of biz-dev) — the best acquisitions are businesses broken in exactly the ways acquisition.com is excellent (e.g. horrible sales process but high LTV and sticky retention). (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Example: bought a 32-location teeth-whitening chain, rolled out one improved sales process and **5x'd LTV in a single rollout** ("we just created $50 million Enterprise Value"). (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Funnel/throughput: ~3,000 companies apply per month; automated screening removes ~90%; they target ~2 LOIs/month and about half close (~12/year). "All of our deal flow comes from content." (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- On the hold-co fad: buying businesses is "in vogue" partly due to a retirement-driven supply spike and people "who never made their money buying businesses selling courses on how to buy businesses." "The best people at buying businesses buy businesses and hold them" (paraphrasing Naval: the best investors don't sell courses on investing). Even a no-money-down deal leaves you unable to run the business — "I could sell... I could build a skyscraper" but still have to actually build it. (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Focus thesis: the person going all-in on one thing makes the most money; "the wealthiest 50 people in the world all went all in on one thing." His nine-companies era was "horrible... so stressful" because he was rainmaker for all of them with no leadership cadence; he only learned to run multiple things once he sold Gym Launch and got "above the business" (in / on / above the business distinction — above = zero direct reports on the org chart). (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Content strategy "single throughput line": make content **only you can make** — "one of zero" stories (e.g. how he bought and 5x'd a teeth-whitening chain) rather than commodity breakdowns anyone could do. (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- "Too good to fail": if a piece over-delivers, people click the next one without reading the thumb/headline; better to ship **fewer good pieces than more mediocre ones**. (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Hooks matter enormously and this is not a contradiction of "too good to fail": a great hook also gets mediocre content shared. Story — a friend's Larry-King book infomercial tanked; he re-recorded only the first 30 seconds with a new hook → **$100M in the next 12 months**. His own video went from ~5,000 to ~900,000 views after cutting a 3-second "so I had this time when" preamble. "You have 5 seconds but it's more like two." (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Platform view: social has undergone "the tiktokification of social media" — subscribers/followers are now a vanity metric; the only thing that matters is **click, retain, reward**; a brand-new account can do 10M on its first upload — "a great equalizer" that makes content king. (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Story framework (from studying storytelling ~6 years, mostly via fiction + articles rather than books): character → unmet desire → obstacle → guide with a solution → tries the new way → it works → internal achievement (who they became) + external achievement. Teaching = connecting things people understand to things they don't (why metaphors/analogies work). (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Twitter/X workflow: no ghostwriters, no cadence, no engagement groups — "I tweet [expletive] as I think of it." Wakes up with 3-4 tweets and posts before getting out of bed; tweets are often him "talking to myself" to get over a moment of discomfort. Twitter + Hemingway (the app) are the two things that most improved his writing. (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Brand discipline: won't chase the algorithm ("algo whores"), won't make off-brand content, won't bash people ("criticize generally, praise specifically" — attributes the maxim to Warren Buffett); publishes only when it's a "win-win-win" (Alex's brand / the audience / the subject of the content). Advice to new creators: "just talk about stuff you know about." (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Media is run as a loss leader — "we lose money on media"; spend roughly doubled from ~$120K/mo to ~$240K/mo; AdSense doesn't cover it, but "the deal flow is good." (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Majority vs. minority: now "100%" moving toward majority stakes because implementation is faster ("fewer emotions to manage on the founder side"); recounts doubling a company's profit in 30 days via a research-backed price increase after six founder calls — as a majority holder "it would have just been an email." Keeps "plain English" fifth-grade-readable contracts and has "never taken a vote in any company ever." (Alex, 2024-10-21)
- Marriage to Lila: "fell in business love" first (pitched her a job the day they met, asked daily for 30 days); they consciously flipped priorities from business-first to marriage-first in Dec 2019 ("I think our premise was wrong") and it's been better since; the relationship is "founded in respect" and pure acceptance ("she has never tried to change me"). Credits Neil Patel with the insight that his wife Michelle "just let me be me." (Alex, 2024-10-21 — the Neil reference is Alex reporting Neil's past private remark, NOT a Neil claim in this episode)
