# How We're Raising Money for SingleBrain (The Clean Story for VCs) (Marketing School)

## Summary
Co-hosted Marketing School episode. The framing (a fundraise for **SingleBrain**, which is Eric Siu's company) implied this would be almost entirely Eric-context, but the actual attribution split is the reverse: this episode is unusually **Neil-heavy on persona-relevant business judgment**. Eric is the one seeking advice about his own company, and Neil plays the senior fundraising/finance advisor for most of the runtime, delivering several coherent, reusable frameworks. Eric's confirmations ("there's two parts that Neil had here"; "Neil's number two is actually a great idea"; "what Neil's also talking about too is leverage is an amplifier") repeatedly attribute the substantive advice to Neil in the third person, which makes the split unusually clean for a `>>`-only, unnamed-speaker transcript.

Neil's contributions cluster into (1) a **clean-story fundraising playbook** — spin the new/"sexy" AI company (SingleBrain) out of the legacy agency (Single Grain) so the deal is clean, raise on vision without legacy economics muddying the growth rate, and investors "like deals with no hair"; (2) a **raise-less-keep-optionality** stance — take a smaller clean round (~$10M, maybe only lose ~20%, even on a SAFE) then a bigger round 6-12 months later, keep the cash-flowing agency separate so a failure doesn't wipe you out; (3) a **debt/leverage framework** — Charlie Munger's "ladies, liquor, and leverage" (really it's just leverage), size debt in "turns" of profit (half a turn to one turn max, personally always <1x), SBA loans are the best debt, deploy wisely; (4) an **AI-implementation M&A read** — sellers want 20-25x profit but real growth is 30-40%/yr not "triple," per-unit prices will compress as competitors and optionality arrive while aggregate spend still grows, and NP Digital is already hunting acquisitions; and (5) **life-stage / passion-project philosophy** — he'll put money but not time into non-passion businesses, and at this stage prefers compounding capital (SpaceX, stock market) over starting another company.

Eric's own material (SingleBrain's pilot→base-fee→outcome-based pricing ladder, his ~$15-25M raise ambition, his agent "Mr. Siu" doing inbound outreach, and the news/market banter) is quarantined below. Because Neil actually delivers a crisp, quantified debt-leverage framework plus a clean-story spinoff fundraising playbook as teaching (not just restated opinion), this is a **borderline ★ L3-candidate** — flagged for a synthesizer to judge, not asserted.

## Key claims (dated, speaker-tagged, paraphrase)
- Clean-story fundraising: raise money for the new AI company (SingleBrain) on its own, not bundled with the legacy agency — investors want clean, simple deals "with no hair," and you can raise on vision without existing economics; bundling in legacy agency revenue "muddies the water." (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Bundling the legacy agency actually hurts the raise: combined, VCs see the AI narrative sitting on "too much legacy revenue," so the implied growth rate from the new thing isn't appealing enough to justify a $15-25M check — a clean new-co is a much easier sell. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Raise less, keep optionality: don't take the full $15-20M; a clean ~$10M round on great terms (maybe only ~20% dilution, or even a SAFE) beats it — then push hard and do a bigger round at $30-40M valuation 6-12 months later. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Keep the agency separate so risk is contained: if you raise a big round against the combined entity and it goes wrong, investors can take everything and you get nothing for 10+ years of work; keep the cash-flowing agency separate (hire someone to run it) so you always still own a growing business. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Risk/failure framing: every great entrepreneur (Musk, Zuckerberg, Gates) has had failures — "no one has a 100% batting average; if you do, you're not really taking enough swings." (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Narrative sells the raise: with a clean single-brain story, "hey, this is where I want to spend my time," investors love it — an easy clean pitch; even if you get $7M instead of $10M, that chunk lets you grow fast because you don't have to be profitable. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Debt is the fastest way to bankruptcy if abused: one of the quickest ways to go bankrupt is taking on too much debt; be conservative if you ever leverage — quoting Charlie Munger ("sits in my home"), the "three ways men go broke: ladies, liquor, and leverage" — really it's only leverage that matters (the first two were for fun). It's not "don't take leverage" but avoid too much and bad terms. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- SBA loans are the best debt: favorable payment terms vs. banks/traditional debt, ~10-year payback; the tradeoff is a lower dollar cap (~$5-7M for a tech business), so a bank gives more dollars but on worse terms. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Entrepreneurs with zero debt are often leaving growth on the table: many do $7M profit with no debt and defer obvious growth moves "for a year or two" to avoid burning cash flow — a modest SBA loan (~$3-3.5M over 10 years) can fund those moves now. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Debt sizing in "turns" of profit: start at half a turn to one turn — on $10M profit, half a turn = $5M, one turn = $10M of debt; deploy it wisely (draw it as a line or SBA loan, don't burn it fast). On $1M profit, a $500K SBA loan over 10 years is only ~$50K/yr — very manageable. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Personal leverage rule: always operate at less than 1x; never go over — even 2x (which many call conservative) is too much for him, in case of another COVID-type shock. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Low leverage for expansion is worth it: cites friend "Sean" leveraging (at less than one times profit) to get into big-box retail (Walmart/Costco) — low-leverage, high-return scenarios like this are worth doing. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- AI-implementation M&A is overpriced vs. reality: acquisition targets want 20-25x profit claiming they'll "triple every year," but observed growth is closer to 30-40%/yr; NP Digital is already looking to acquire implementation companies but passing on those multiples. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
- Price compression is coming even as spend grows: as ~10 competitors enter and optionality/efficiency improve, buyers will pay less per project/hour for the same AI-implementation work — but in aggregate companies will spend more in total on it. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — ai-marketing.
- Outcome-based pricing for AI services is a slow roll: Neil agrees it's coming but says it takes years of patience before it's fully implemented (agreeing with Eric's pilot→base→outcomes model). (Neil, 2026-07-16) — ai-marketing.
- Life-stage / passion-project philosophy: he was always willing to put in the *work* only for his own passion businesses (100%/50%-owned, truly loved); for non-passion ventures (e.g. a "place for mom" competitor, a poker site, insurance lead-gen) he'd put in money/resources but not time — and nowadays won't put in the time for any new business. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Capital compounding over company-building: at this career/life stage it's easier and more fun to compound profit into SpaceX or the stock market than to grind out a new company; he's fine making less money as a result. (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- Conference time-use: he doesn't attend sessions — he'd rather send a team member to take notes and implement; his conference value is networking, deals, and meals (which doesn't feel like "work" to him). (Neil, 2026-07-16) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- SingleBrain fundraise intent: after several successful SingleBrain pilots (clients saying "we need this for everything"), the momentum says "raise now"; a well-known NY firm (backers of OpenAI, Anthropic, Shopify, Databricks) wanted to meet last year and he deferred — this year he'd raise ~$15-25M across the combined entity while still owning ~100%. (Eric, 2026-07-16, self-reported) — agency-entrepreneurship.
- SingleBrain pricing ladder: managed "revenue agents" for clients start as a ~$25K 45-day pilot (CTO + Eric on the call), then a monthly base fee (~$5-20K), then outcome/performance-based pricing (e.g. cut an Anthropic bill 90% for 5% of savings; $50 vs. $500 per creative) — pilot → base → outcomes because it's too early to map outcomes upfront. (Eric, 2026-07-16, self-reported) — ai-marketing / agency-entrepreneurship.
