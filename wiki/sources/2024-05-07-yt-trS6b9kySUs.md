# MrBeast made me cry and How Prime saved Amazon

## Summary
Three segments. (1) A reaction to MrBeast's first ~40-minute long-form video (a departure from his
usual fast-cut short format), a "1 to 100" elimination game with a $250,000 prize: the narrating
host describes tearing up watching it, citing the emotional stakes built through participants'
personal stories (elderly participants, a participant raising money for a sick parent) and a
notable late-game twist where a $250K buyout offer causes two participants to simultaneously
self-eliminate for nothing. Runner-up payouts (~$10K each, speculated, not confirmed) and
MrBeast's newly announced Amazon Prime game-show deal are discussed as evidence he is expanding
into longer-form, higher-production content. (2) The Amazon Prime origin story, drawn from the
book *Working Backwards* (a Jeff Bezos/Amazon insider account): Amazon Prime originated around
2002-2003 when Amazon's year-over-year growth rate was quietly decelerating as competitors caught
up; Bezos reportedly pushed to launch a new retention offering (free two-day shipping) on an
aggressive deadline ("by my next shareholder update"), despite the bet not being expected to prove
out for five to seven years — framed as an example of Amazon's "say yes by default" culture
(contrasted with companies like Nokia that missed major shifts) requiring significant risk
tolerance. (3) A stock-timing/investing discussion: reflection on Microsoft's historically
depressed valuation despite strong underlying financials (attributed to a period where "Wall
Street didn't believe in the vision," before the cloud/AI-driven re-rating) and a story about
watching Meta's stock crash to a ~$90 share price / ~14 P/E in 2022 after the Apple iOS
privacy-tracking changes hit its ad revenue — with an admission of trying and failing to time a
buy near the bottom (waiting for expected further ad-revenue damage from a rumored Google cookie
change that kept getting delayed, so the anticipated further dip never came and the opportunity
was missed). This leads into a stated personal stance on real estate: a described exchange with
his mother over the "cap rate" math on a hypothetical $2M house appreciating to $10M over 20 years
(reportedly ~5% annualized) framed as an underwhelming return versus alternatives like a CD,
concluding "it's better to focus on what you actually understand" than to buy into conventional
real-estate wisdom by default; the segment closes with a mention of currently living in a "tiny
home," described as temporary.
