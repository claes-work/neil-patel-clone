# How one influencer tanked a stock by 80%, The fall of a $5.7B company, and more

## Summary
Several segments. (1) Neil presents two influencer-driven stock-collapse case studies: Oprah Winfrey's departure from the WeightWatchers board and public acknowledgment of using GLP-1 weight-loss drugs (contradicting the company's diet-and-points model), which coincided with the stock falling ~44% over the prior month and ~75% year-to-date; and Marques Brownlee (MKBHD) publicly panning the Fisker electric car as "the worst car I've ever driven" after Fisker declined to loan him a review unit, which Neil frames as a decisive negative signal ahead of Fisker's stock collapse (down ~54% in 5 days, ~97% over 6 months at the time of recording). (2) A Vice Media segment — narrated by Eric on a shared screen — tracing Vice's valuation history from ~$1.4B (2013, Rupert Murdoch's 21st Century Fox) to a peak $5.7B (2017, TPG investment) down to a $350M bankruptcy-court-approved sale to Fortress Investment Group in 2023, used to argue ad-dependent media and newsletter-subscription business models are structurally weak compared to platform-scale or productized businesses. (3) A first-person exchange in which Eric asks what Neil is doing with Ubersuggest/AnswerThePublic's proprietary and public data; Neil describes running private-instance AI analysis on NP Digital's SEO/paid/social data to surface trends and run experiments, explicitly states a policy of not selling that data and not building an "education" business around it, preferring instead to build automated products that get customers results directly. (4) A joint SEO case study (Eric-led) on SEMrush's and Backlinko's traffic growth (SEMrush: 24M monthly visits, 1.1M ranked keywords, DR 91; Backlinko: ~778K monthly visits), attributing the growth partly to systematic old-content updates ("a Wikipedia strategy"), programmatic SEO, and brand/press-driven ranking advantages, while noting the traffic growth doesn't map 1:1 to revenue growth for either.

## Key claims
- **Oprah Winfrey's WeightWatchers board exit + public GLP-1 drug use coincided with a severe stock decline** (down ~44% over the trailing month, ~75% year-to-date, worse over six months, per data presented on the episode), framed as a case study in the downside power of influencer/celebrity association. (Neil, 2024-03-20) `(third-party financial data, dated)`
- **Marques Brownlee's negative Fisker review (after Fisker declined to provide a demo unit) is framed as a leading negative signal for the stock's subsequent collapse** (~54% down over 5 days, ~97% over 6 months per data cited), with the broader point that refusing access to a top-tier reviewer ahead of launch is itself a red flag. (Neil, 2024-03-20) `(opinion, dated)`
- **NP Digital runs private-instance AI analysis over its own (and Ubersuggest/AnswerThePublic-adjacent) SEO/paid/social data to surface trends and drive experimentation**, and explicitly declines to sell that data or build a data-education/training business around it, preferring to build automated products that deliver results directly to customers "in the most convenient, automated way as possible." (Neil, 2024-03-20) `(self-reported)`
- **Belief: staying ahead in a fast-changing market (AI, platform bans, algorithm shifts) comes from experimentation and execution, not from reading/following news.** States that reading about what ChatGPT/OpenAI are doing "is not going to keep you ahead," and credits ongoing experimentation (shared with Eric) as the real differentiator; states neither host believes AI is a passing fad. (Neil, 2024-03-20) `(opinion, dated)`
- **Ad-dependent media and paid-newsletter-subscription business models are structurally weak**, per the Vice Media bankruptcy case study, "unless you're a massive platform like TikTok, Google, [or] Facebook" — argued you'll generally make more money selling directly to an audience than monetizing through subscriptions or site ad revenue. `(attribution: uncertain — undiarized, joint framing)`

## Notable verbatim quotes
> We're taking so much of our data — taking private instances and having AI compute[/]analyze [it to] help us find out trends — and then we're running experiments based on the data that we're seeing, [for] both SEO, paid, and social.

> We don't [want to] give away the data... we wouldn't sell our data... we don't want to end up running the business of "you take the data and then you educate people based off of it" — we want to end up going with "you take the data, you build something that helps people get the results in the most convenient, automated way as possible," and then you help people just achieve their end goal, versus education and training.
