# The difference between $1M, $100M, and $1B+ ad agencies, and Why MrBeast keeps getting views today

## Summary
Three segments. (1) A framework, based on a Neil tweet, describing how ad agencies change in character across revenue tiers: **$1M agencies** serve mostly small/mom-and-pop clients, can't be selective about which deals to take, have high client churn, and offer a limited service menu due to constrained resources. **$10M agencies** (typically 4-5 years old) begin developing a mini-brand and grow meaningfully through referrals (NP Digital cites ~10% of revenue from referrals at this stage, historically higher), serve larger "midsize" clients (hundreds to low-thousands of employees), retain clients 2-3 years, and can broaden services (lowering churn as more services are cross-sold). **$100M+ agencies** generate the majority of new revenue from client referrals, employee referrals (employees' former clients following them), and RFPs (NP Digital reports ~66% of 2024-YTD revenue from these three sources combined, calling this "backwards" from his original expectation that paid ads/SEO would dominate); serve true enterprise clients (tens of thousands of employees, global, multi-region, multi-service, 4-5+ year retention); and often grow partly via acquisition (NP Digital cites a closed acquisition in November 2023, another expected to close within a month, another around June, and a fourth in an earlier LOI stage). **$1B+ agencies** (WPP, Omnicom, Publicis, Havas, Dentsu, Interpublic) generate most revenue from RFPs targeting large enterprise accounts (minimum ~$10M/year in fees), have low organic growth (per a cited Q4 2023 chart, all under ~8%, several under 2%, Dentsu negative ~6%), and grow primarily via acquisition — with a specific critique that "organic growth" reported by holding companies is often inflated, because revenue growth from an already-acquired agency gets counted as the parent's organic growth going forward, even though the underlying growth originated from the acquisition itself. Frames "switching costs" (deep multi-service, multi-region embeddedness) as the actual moat for agencies at scale, contrary to the common belief that agencies have no moat. (2) A second Neil-tweet-based framework on how marketing philosophy differs by company revenue tier: **sub-$1M companies** need marketing spend to be profitable immediately (limited cash reserves); **$10M companies** have enough cash flow to spend a dollar to break even up front and profit on the back end; **$100M+ companies** typically know customer LTV well enough to spend aggressively (even at a short-term loss) to acquire customers, expecting profitability over a multi-year relationship — cited examples: Amazon, Snowflake. (3) A segment on why MrBeast is getting more views despite (per his own tweet) slowing down video output, cutting fast-paced editing/transitions, and leaning into longer-form storytelling with more "personality" and "breathing room" — the hosts connect this to their own podcast's short-form/reel performance, with Neil citing specific Instagram numbers for storytelling-format clips (~122K and ~100K+ views on lightly-edited, story-driven posts) as evidence that storytelling outperforms high-production "overstimulation," a pattern also observed across Eric's own Instagram Reels. Closes with a segment on influencer-income data (cited from Carrot, a fintech/credit-card company for creators; surveyed ~5,920 of ~15,000 invited influencers): overall average influencer monthly income ~$323; 100K-999K followers ~$1,727/month; 1M+ followers ~$6,193/month; full-time creators with 1M+ followers average ~$314K/year total income, with YouTube (~$90K/year platform-only) and Twitch (~$54K/year total, "shockingly" high per-follower) the strongest-monetizing platforms and TikTok the weakest (~$473/year per the cited data) — used to argue that money is rarely full-time creators' primary motivation ("80%+ of influencers care mostly about money" is called out as a "dirty secret" of the industry that leads to poor brand alignment, but the underlying income data shows most influencers, even those with substantial followings, aren't making much money from it).

## Key claims
- **A four-tier agency-revenue framework ($1M / $10M / $100M+ / $1B+) describing how client mix, service breadth, churn, growth channels, and moat structure systematically change with scale**, based on a tweet Neil authored. Central data points from NP Digital's own experience: ~66% of 2024-YTD revenue from client referrals, employee referrals, and RFPs combined (vs. an original expectation that paid ads/SEO would dominate revenue generation); ~10% of revenue currently from referrals (down from historically higher). (Neil, 2024-04-02) `(self-reported, dated)`
- **NP Digital's active M&A pipeline as of 2024-04-02: one acquisition closed November 2023 (announced February 2024), one expected to close within about a month, one expected around June (already at LOI stage), and a fourth still being negotiated toward an LOI.** (Neil, 2024-04-02) `(self-reported, dated)`
- **Argues that publicly reported "organic growth" at billion-dollar holding companies (WPP, Omnicom, Publicis, Havas, Dentsu, Interpublic) is systematically inflated**, because future revenue growth from an already-acquired agency gets attributed to the parent's organic-growth figures going forward, even though the underlying growth originated from the acquisition, not the parent's own organic execution. (Neil, 2024-04-02) `(opinion, dated)`
- **Frames agency "moat" as switching costs, not brand: the more services and regions a client is embedded in with an agency, the harder/more painful it is for them to switch away**, contrary to a common belief that agencies structurally lack a moat. (Neil, 2024-04-02) `(opinion, dated)`
- **A second tweet-based framework: marketing spend philosophy shifts by company revenue tier** — sub-$1M companies need immediate profitability from ad spend (cash-constrained); ~$10M companies can spend to break even upfront and profit on the back end; $100M+ companies (citing Amazon, Snowflake) know customer LTV well enough to spend aggressively at an upfront loss, expecting profitability over a multi-year customer relationship. (Neil, 2024-04-02) `(opinion, dated)`
- **Cites his own Instagram storytelling-format posts (~122K and ~100K+ views, lightly edited) as evidence that authentic, less-produced storytelling content outperforms highly-produced, fast-cut content** — consistent with, and used to explain, MrBeast's own stated pivot toward slower, longer-form, personality-driven videos. (Neil, 2024-04-02) `(self-reported, dated)`

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Basically here's his story: majority of my revenue would come from paid ads and SEO — it's really backwards for us. We looked at year-to-date so far in 2024... it was 66% or so were either RFPs, client referrals, or employee referrals.

> I always thought that majority of my revenue would come from paid ads and SEO, it's really backwards for us.

> Most people think agencies don't have a moat — I would say the one thing agencies do have, especially as you get bigger, is switching costs... the more services and the more locations or regions you're embedded in, the less likely they are to churn, because there's relationships there.

> A $100 million company, they usually know the lifetime value of their customer, and they're willing to spend something like more money — whatever percentage on the front end — to get that customer, [even] lose money, like an Amazon... but they know the customer will be with them for so many years, they'll make that money back on year two, three, four, five.
