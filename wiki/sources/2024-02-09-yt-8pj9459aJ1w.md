A cross-podcast panel on the future of marketing agencies in an AI/automation world. The guests set a grim tone (Ralph: media buying is "over," an "Arbitrage" model with no future; Kasim: he's "on the center of the planet the meteor is hitting"). Neil's contribution is more measured and pricing/valuation-focused: he argues the percentage-of-media model *can* still work but only if the fee reflects real strategic value (creatives, landing pages, email, data, LTV work), not button-clicking; that "the winner is going to be the platforms, not the people," so agencies that just sit on top of Google/Facebook automation have no moat; and that agency valuation multiples were artificially inflated by COVID and are now compressed toward reality (why pay 7×+ profit for an agency whose clients don't even stay 7 years?). Eric reframes toward outcome-based pricing (charge per meeting booked, per solved ticket, per programmatic page) and programmatic/AI-assisted SEO. The panel converges on: agencies must go **deep not broad**, add real value beyond media buying, and the big fee/partnership model works with well-funded enterprise clients but is hard for under-funded SMBs who "don't know their numbers."

## Key claims
- The percentage-of-media agency model can still work, but only if the fee maps to real delivered value — you're not charging to "manage the media," you're charging to build creatives, optimize landing pages, write email sequences, analyze data, and continually increase customer LTV. At $10M/month (~$120M/yr) in media you can't charge 18%, 10%, or even 5%. (Neil, 2024-02-09)
- The right frame is cost-and-margin, not a fixed percentage: most agencies operate at 10–20% margins, and a 10–20% margin is fine *if* you deliver strategy the client couldn't achieve on their own; but charging a percentage of media while netting ~70% won't last — clients aren't getting the value and it's a disservice by the agency owner. (Neil, 2024-02-09)
- Agencies cannot survive on "clicking a few buttons and letting the AI do its thing" — the durable future is creative, strategic, hard-problem work. Google and Facebook will automate as much as possible, so **the winner will be the platforms, not the people** (the agencies/tools sitting on top). (Neil, 2024-02-09)
- Adaptation lags reassure agencies against overnight extinction: "the cloud was going to change the internet" — it did, yet many multi-billion-dollar companies *still* aren't on the cloud. People and companies will always have problems that need human solving. (Neil, 2024-02-09)
- Buying agencies to automate them doesn't create the value people assume: a customer who can go to ChatGPT and generate AI content for free won't keep paying an agency that is merely reselling someone else's software. (Neil, 2024-02-09)
- Agency valuations were artificially inflated (COVID era). Demands of 10×/15×/20× profit are unjustifiable when even Berkshire Hathaway buys billion-dollar companies at ~7× profit — and 7× is itself questionable because the average agency client doesn't stay 7 years, so the agency isn't "worth" 7 years of profit. EBITDA multiples today are more compressed and more realistic. (Neil, 2024-02-09)
- The high (15–20×) agency multiples are not coming back; the people who paid them are underwater and have lost money. `(attribution: likely Neil — asked "before I answer," then answered "no… all those people are underwater"; turn boundary slightly unclear between Neil and a guest)`
- The deep-value / big-fee agency model works best with **enterprise** clients: agencies will do the deep work for a fee when the account is large (e.g. $10M/yr), because the fee itself is large enough to justify it. Enterprise contracts take 6+ months through legal and procurement and become genuine multi-year partnerships (clients stay 3–6 years). (Neil, 2024-02-09)
- For **SMBs** the same deep-value model is hard: SMBs usually can't fund it, so the agency must either take a performance cut or the client must be well-funded — but SMBs "are SMB for a reason" and typically don't know their own numbers/LTV, which makes performance-based deals fall on deaf ears. `(attribution: likely Neil, but this SMB-numbers point sits in a fast Ralph/Kasim exchange near the transcript's end — partly guest material)`
- Big corporations have procurement divisions that already know market rates and roughly what your margins are; if your margin looks too high they'll negotiate it down. Large, highly profitable companies (making ~$10B/yr) often just want a fixed cost they can plan around because they have the cash — an outcome/per-lead/percentage-of-revenue model doesn't fit their playbook. `(attribution: likely Neil — spoken as a counterpoint right after Eric's outcome-pricing pitch; boundary with Eric not perfectly clean)`

## Co-hosts & guests (context — NOT persona data)
[[wiki/entities/eric-siu]] · guests **Ralph Burns** (Tier 11 / Perpetual Traffic) and **Kasim Aslam** (ex-owner of a Google Ads agency, sold ~2022) — external panelists, no entity page. None of the material below trains the Neil persona.

**Eric Siu** (co-host):
- Positions himself and Neil as "the SEO guys" versus Ralph/Kasim ("their bread and butter is paid"), and says he leans toward the guests' view: the old "charge 15–20% for the same thing" model is going away because technology is deflationary and will eliminate a lot of jobs. (Eric, 2024-02-09)
- The value shift is toward outcome/AI-native delivery: programmatic SEO, AI-assisted SEO, "AI meetings booked," charging per outcome — cites Intercom charging ~$1 per solved ticket and Zapier charging per zap; you can charge per meeting booked or per programmatic page published. (Eric, 2024-02-09)
- Cites **Centerfield** as a pay-for-performance agency working with telecoms (AT&T, Verizon) that "gift-wraps" customers on a CPA/cost-per-lead basis and even bought its own call center — ~$800M revenue — as a rare example of the performance model at scale. (Eric, 2024-02-09)

**Ralph Burns** (guest, Tier 11 / Perpetual Traffic host):
- Relays Perry Marshall's analogy: today's digital-marketing agencies are like travel agencies just before the internet — travel agencies died but travel exploded; agencies won't all die, but the pure "media buy" is over. (Ralph, 2024-02-09)
- If you're not doing post-click data analysis / first-party data work — something with more story than "Arbitrage" — you can't survive an AI-driven, machine-learning ad world. Survival requires **depth, not breadth**. (Ralph, 2024-02-09)
- Has lost more clients in the last four months than in the prior two years combined; a peer's high-end agency lost 67% of clients in Q4. Clients now need a (fractional) CMO, not just a media buyer. Sold his agency in October 2023 at ~35% net margins, now on a two-year earnout. (Ralph, 2024-02-09)
- The real value shift requires the agency to become a **partner with equity / profit share** — he won't build a client's whole "assembly line for customers" for a monthly fee only to be dismissed and made replaceable. (Ralph, 2024-02-09)

**Kasim Aslam** (guest, sold his Google Ads agency):
- The media buy specifically is "over"; he's "on the center of the planet that the meteor is hitting." Google's Smart Shopping / Performance Max already left campaigns unchanged for 6–8 months — clients were effectively "paying us to watch it." (Kasim, 2024-02-09)
- Agreeing with Neil's platforms-win point: anyone building automation that lives *on top of* the platforms is committing "an absurdity" — you can't out-build a trillion-dollar company spending hundreds of billions on its own AI; get out of the platform's way and add value where they're *not* automating. (Kasim, 2024-02-09)
- Makes it tangible with an HVAC example: an agency must learn a client's true LTV (e.g. an HVAC customer worth ~$1,500) and then it becomes a factoring problem — "how much of the LTV do I deserve?" Requires knowing the client's business inside out. (Kasim, 2024-02-09)
