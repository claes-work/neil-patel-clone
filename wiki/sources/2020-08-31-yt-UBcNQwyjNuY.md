# How to Create The Perfect Blog Layout

## Summary
A page-by-page walkthrough of Neil's own blog-layout template as a CRO checklist, using
neilpatel.com as the live example. Recommends against a single-column, sidebar-free
text page (fewer conversions/leads); instead: a sticky top bar (built with his own
Hello Bar tool) for lead capture/discounts/social pushes; a sidebar with rotating —
deliberately *non-identical* — calls-to-action (newsletter signup, product, service)
"because if you keep showing them the same thing over and over again... it won't always
convert"; an About section; a search bar; links to the site's best-performing posts
(claims these internal links raise the linked posts' own rankings "within six months");
a sticky CTA and sticky social-share icons that scroll with the reader; an accessibility
widget (AudioEye) for users with disabilities; image-led post openings with breadcrumbs
and embedded video where relevant; a closing conclusion section (reasoning that many
readers scroll to the bottom, read the conclusion, then scroll back up); and a comment
section. On the main blog index, recommends a clickable "I want to learn about..."
category dropdown in place of a plain category list, framing it as one of the most-
clicked elements on the page, plus a static (non-rotating) monetization CTA.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-08-31, Neil's sidebar-CTA doctrine: vary CTA copy/placement across the
  page rather than repeating identical CTAs, on the theory that repetition alone
  suppresses conversion.
- As of 2020-08-31, Neil claimed internally linking to a site's best-performing posts
  causes those posts to "start ranking higher" within roughly six months (self-reported,
  unaudited).
- As of 2020-08-31, Neil includes an AudioEye accessibility widget on his blog for
  visitors with disabilities/hearing impairment — framed as both an accessibility and a
  UX practice.
- As of 2020-08-31, Neil's stated rationale for a category dropdown over a plain
  category list: "no one really clicks on categories, but the moment you put it like
  this [as an 'I want to learn about...' dropdown]" engagement rises (self-reported,
  no figure given).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "If you keep showing them the same thing over and over again, with the same exact messaging, it won't always convert, hence you need to rotate it up."

> "In many cases, people scroll to the bottom, read the conclusion, then they scroll back up."
