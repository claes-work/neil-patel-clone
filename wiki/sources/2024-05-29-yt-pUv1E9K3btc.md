# Why Marketing Is A Joke To Private Equity (Marketing School)

## Summary
A tactically dense, almost entirely Neil-led episode on how NP Digital actually runs M&A day to
day, closing with Neil's stated belief about marketing's place relative to raw financial/business
power. Distinct from other sources in this batch for two reasons: the captions on this file are
clean and properly punctuated (unlike the rest of the batch), and the content is close to a solo
Neil monologue with Eric mostly prompting follow-up questions — making attribution unusually
confident throughout.

## Key claims
- Attention-to-detail parable: a YPO peer who runs a ~$30M/year events-logistics company still
  personally walks his 40,000–50,000 sq ft warehouse every day, 20 years in, and still catches
  small things — Neil's takeaway is that no amount of equity given to a hire will make them care
  about the details the way the owner does. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- Operational rule for integrating simultaneous acquisitions: assign different regional leaders to
  different deals so no single person is absorbing two acquisitions at once (worked example: NP
  Digital's Southeast Asia deal led by "Dan," and the July close split between "Hobby" [LatAm] and
  "Louise" [Spain]). (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- M&A target-selection framework: avoid both extremes — hot, fast-growing "sexy" companies are
  overpriced and competitive; large "hairy" companies are still competitive with private equity.
  NP Digital instead targets small, still-growing companies that are too small to interest private
  equity or big holding companies individually; a single small acquisition doesn't move the needle,
  but aggregating 10–20 of them does, at the cost of much more operational grinding than buying one
  large company. Expects to keep targeting small-to-mid-size deals for the next 10–20 years.
  (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- Cites **Accenture**'s M&A model as a reference point: during COVID, Accenture (200B+ revenue,
  ~600,000 employees) sometimes closed more than one acquisition a week, buying tiny companies to
  fill geography/skill-set gaps and then cross-selling its existing client base into them; Accenture
  pays a large cash chunk upfront with modest earn-outs, plus heavy knowledge transfer. NP Digital
  sometimes competes with (and loses to) Accenture on the same small targets. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- Time allocation: Neil is now spending roughly **50–60% of his week on M&A**, up from ~5% a year
  earlier. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- NP Digital's M&A function is run day-to-day by **Carlos Cesta** (previously headed Americas M&A
  for Dentsu Aegis — cited by Neil as roughly a $9–11B/year revenue competitor — and before that
  tucked in an estimated $0.5B–$1.5B of companies at Presidio, a cloud-services company). Carlos's
  team sources deals: ~250 companies contacted per week (5 days/week, ~50/day), ~30% response rate,
  ~18% of respondents open to a conversation. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- Neil's personal involvement starts once a founder is interested: multiple in-person meetings
  ("get to know them... eating meals, breaking bread... 5–6 hours, not just a 1-hour thing"),
  asking about deals they've *lost* and why, what makes their team unique, and — deliberately —
  "why do you want to stay here? Why not just take the money and leave?" NP Digital does not rush
  into a deal on financials alone; it wants confidence the founding team wants to stay. (Neil,
  2024-05-29)
- Explicit dating analogy for M&A process pacing, echoing a personality-test comparison with Eric:
  Neil scores as highly deliberative and detail-oriented on a joint personality test; his approach
  is to "date slowly," meeting many founders and passing on good businesses that simply aren't a
  fit rather than rushing. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- Recommends Brad Jacobs' *How to Make a Few Billion Dollars* again (see also
  [[2024-05-28-yt-eHgmjJ6Kemw]]) — notes Jacobs did only 18 deals out of ~2,000 evaluated (a 0.9%
  hit rate), and used an ex-CIA associate's facial-pattern-analysis technique as an extra
  due-diligence signal; one deal Jacobs walked from on a "gut feeling" flag later turned out to
  involve financial fraud. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- Risk-management framing: a $10M deal that fails isn't a wash, it's a full loss — so spending
  extra diligence money (e.g. "an extra $10K") to cut risk on a much larger deal is obviously worth
  it. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- **Belief: marketing is subordinate to whoever controls the capital.** Cites a real (unnamed)
  example: a company went public, was bought out by a top-10 private-equity shop for billions when
  its stock underperformed, had leverage added, and was resold to a Fortune-100-scale buyer —
  netting the PE firm roughly 6x on actual cash invested (excluding the debt-financed portion) —
  despite the PE firm doing little visible marketing. His conclusion: large PE firms "don't need
  marketing to work because their playbook works" (financial engineering, relationships, capital
  access); marketing is a real lever but sits *under* control of capital in the hierarchy of
  business power. (Neil, 2024-05-29)
- Division of labor within his own marketing practice: Neil delegates paid advertising to
  specialists on his team (self-rates as not as strong at paid as some of his team), keeps
  copywriting himself (dislikes when others avoid writing copy, prefers to rewrite it personally),
  and generally advises focusing on your own strengths rather than trying to be excellent at
  everything. (Neil, 2024-05-29)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Even if you give someone 10-20 points of your company, they're not going to care nearly as much
> as you will about this stuff.

> The deals we've focused on are small deals that don't really have too much competition, 'cause
> they're too small for private equity or the holding companies... buying one of them doesn't
> really move the needle, but buying 10-20 of them really moves the needle.

> It really is like dating. And the big thing that we really do is we don't rush into deals. We
> also don't try to close them really fast.

> Marketing certainly helps, but for any marketer to think that what you bring is going to be much
> more different than a private equity shop — no, you're not going to beat the level of
> relationships, the financial engineering, and all the cool things that they know how to do. It's
> hard to beat that. Just keep in mind, marketing is under that stuff.

> Focus on your strengths, and whatever your strengths are, that's your real edge. Just continue to
> get better at them versus diversifying and trying to learn everything.
