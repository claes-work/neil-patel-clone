# Growth Hacking Strategies | Tips to Get More Traffic, Customers and Traction

★ **L3-candidate** — a long-form (~50 min) conference keynote + Q&A (Pioneers-style festival, Vienna) that is unusually dense with reusable growth frameworks AND biographical detail. Flag for the next synthesis pass; do not inline-promote here.

## Summary

A conference keynote in Vienna (audience uses the "Pioneers" app; likely the Pioneers Festival) followed by an extended live Q&A. Neil opens with his "bad weather = more work = more money" startup quip, then frames growth hacking around three prerequisites (know exactly who your customer is; know how to reach them; don't just chase the most popular, most crowded channels) illustrated by his KISSmetrics failure ("everyone with a website is our customer" was a disaster) and Dropbox's unit-economics problem on AdWords that pushed it to the invite-a-friend distribution hack. He then teaches **five distribution strategies**: (1) **integrations** with other platforms that share your customer base (win-win, customer-validated, with strong partner pages + Facebook targeting of the partner's fans); (2) **emails / viral onboarding** (social-signup to lift conversions ~30%, invite flows measured against average contact-list size, first-week engagement thresholds); (3) **embeds** (YouTube/SlideShare — give a real reason to embed, make it one-click, add relevant CTAs, use relevant not keyword-rich anchor text); (4) **"powered by" badges** (must be relevant and clearly describe what they power; test the wording); and (5) **free stuff** (HubSpot's grader, New Relic benchmarking, ebooks — mapped to the customer's buying decision, repurposed cheaply, optimized for revenue). The Q&A ranges over B2B growth hacks (invite-to-unlock resets monthly), long sales cycles (optimize for leads via a guest-post laddering play), two-sided marketplaces (grow one side, artificially seed the other, à la Airbnb + Craigslist), and underpriced ad channels (Facebook mobile, YouTube pre-roll at ~$100K/month). Several Q&A tactics are ethically dubious and Neil flags them as such (Mechanical Turk fake App Store upvotes = "the shady answer"; scraping Yelp for cold-email lists; an Instagram "free socks to models" play).

## Key claims

- (~2014, pub 2018-12-04) Neil said growth must be taken seriously as a percentage metric, citing early Facebook's internal goal of growing new-users-per-day by a faster *percentage* than the prior day, not just a flat number.
- (~2014) Neil said the three prerequisites before growth-hacking are: know exactly who your customer is; know how to reach them (which channels they use); and don't only chase the most popular channels, which get crowded fast.
- (~2014) Neil said KISSmetrics failed early because he and his co-founder marketed to "anyone with an online website"; the real target was companies doing roughly €7–8M/year in e-commerce, SaaS, or subscription. [Attribution: Neil = subject.]
- (~2014) Neil said Dropbox first tried Google AdWords but its ~$99/year product cost ~$233–$388 to acquire a customer there, so it pivoted to a distribution hack (invite friends / tweet / connect iPhone for more free space) that worked.
- (~2014) Neil told a self-deprecating story of being "frugal" (attributing it to being Indian/Asian) and tweeting Dropbox's referral to 100K+ followers to avoid the $99 fee — only to fill his free space and pay anyway; the point: the frictionless one-click hack worked on him.
- **Strategy 1 — Integrations:** (~2014) Neil said to integrate with non-competitor platforms that share your customers (Shopify, Salesforce, GitHub, MailChimp, Google Analytics, Evernote, Dropbox all cited with large user bases); the integration must make BOTH products better, be validated by asking your customers what tools they use, and be supported by a strong partner page, Facebook ads targeting the partner's fans, and getting the partner to co-promote (they benefit from stickier users).
- (~2014) Neil said an integration must fit: KISSmetrics integrating with Google Analytics made sense (complementary data) but integrating with Evernote did not (wrong use-case/audience).
- **Strategy 2 — Emails / onboarding:** (~2014) Neil said email is the most universal channel; viral products (Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, Podio, Yammer, Box) grow by capturing emails and enticing invites (e.g. Box gives more space per colleague invited).
- (~2014) Neil cited Facebook's early insight that a user who reaches ~10–30 friends within the first 7 days is very likely to stay forever, which is why onboarding pushes hard on email import + invites.
- (~2014) Neil said social sign-up (Sign up with Facebook/Twitter) can raise conversion rate by ~30%+ by removing the name/email/password step, while still tapping the user's contacts.
- (~2014) Neil said to measure invite flow against average contact-list size — if users have ~200 contacts but invite only 10, the onboarding is under-optimized (aim for 40–50).
- **Strategy 3 — Embeds:** (~2014) Neil said embeds (YouTube, SlideShare) work only if you give a genuine reason to embed (info/humor/emotion), make embedding one-click (copy-paste widgets per platform), and track conversion — YouTube's embedded "watch next" recommendations are what drove return traffic. He recounted naively wanting people to embed Crazy Egg's traffic data on their own sites, which no one would do because there was no benefit to them.
- (~2014) Neil said to use relevant (not keyword-rich) anchor text in embeds; keyword-stuffing embed links for rankings eventually gets you penalized, but relevant embeds lift search traffic over time.
- **Strategy 4 — "Powered by" badges:** (~2014) Neil said badges must be relevant and clearly state what they power (survey/support/feedback "powered by X"); vague "powered by KISSmetrics" underperformed descriptive wording like "analytics by [company]," and you should test wording and track views/clicks/conversions/LTV. He recounted uselessly wanting a "powered by Neil Patel" badge on his early consultancy clients' sites (e.g. TechCrunch) — no one would do it because it was irrelevant to them.
- **Strategy 5 — Free stuff:** (~2014) Neil cited HubSpot's free marketing grader (near-billion-dollar valuation at the time), New Relic's benchmarking, and ebooks/PDFs; free tools must map to the customer's buying decision (HubSpot's grader shows what's wrong to drive signups), be repurposed cheaply as a minimum version first, educate prospects to build goodwill, and be measured/optimized for revenue.
- (~2014) Neil's closing thesis: growth hacking isn't just about more users, it's about providing more value so users stay and refer; and there is "no excuse" not to make money — rare zero-revenue billion-dollar cases (Pinterest, Snapchat) are not a model to copy.
- **Q&A — B2B:** (~2014) Neil called invite-to-unlock-that-resets-monthly (HubSpot's Signals/Sidekick, Wealthfront) the best recent B2B growth hack — one acquired user becomes two, and the unlock resets every 30 days to force repeat inviting.
- **Q&A — long sales cycles:** (~2014) Neil said to optimize for leads (not the sale) and generate them via a guest-post laddering play: warm up a big publication's guest author with 2–3 genuine appreciative emails, offer to write an article for them (with a backlink you don't mention up front), get introduced to the editor, then publish articles that drive to a gated checklist/lead magnet (built cheaply on Fiverr) capturing name/email/phone for sales follow-up. He said this is exactly how he ended up blogging on Forbes, Entrepreneur, Inc, and Fast Company — and jokingly told the room not to run the tactic on him.
- **Q&A — two-sided markets:** (~2014) Neil said you can't growth-hack both sides at once — grow one side and artificially seed the other, citing Airbnb pulling listings from Craigslist and buying AdWords traffic to those listings.
- **Q&A — underpriced channels:** (~2014) Neil said Facebook mobile ads were among the cheapest channels and that he tried to spend ~$100K/month on YouTube pre-roll ads (outsourcing video creation to Demo Duck), recouping more than his cost within the first 30 days — calling YouTube advertising his #1 ROI channel, even for B2B, because few advertisers make the pre-roll videos.
