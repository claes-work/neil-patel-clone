# ConvertKit open metrics - good or bad idea?, How people use Google and what they click on, & more

## Summary
Three segments. (1) Reacting to ConvertKit founder Nathan Barry's public revenue-metrics dashboard (~$38.8M ARR at the time, built on Baremetrics-style tooling by Cal.com's team): stated position is that publicly sharing company metrics isn't helpful past a certain point — it doesn't drive meaningful input (better to share selectively with specific advisors) and it paints "a bigger target on your back" as perceived wealth grows. Contrasted with a personal blogging strategy from early career: sharing a "from aha to oh, [X]" growth-journey narrative (credited as adapted from Groove HQ) worked well as a lead magnet from 0→100K monthly visitors, worked less well from 100K→1M, and was discontinued entirely past 1M visitors because the numbers became unrelatable — the stated lesson is that transparency builds community fastest at smaller/relatable numbers, and should stop once the community is established. A stock-trader analogy (Tim Sykes) is used to reinforce that "starting over" narratives at small dollar amounts are more relatable/higher-converting than showing the big cumulative numbers. (2) A first-party Google click-behavior study (framed as the hosts' own research, collection took multiple weeks for statistical validity): only ~11% of searchers scroll to the bottom of page 1; of all sessions, 58% clicked an organic listing, 8% a paid result, 34% no click; search pages with an above-the-fold video got a 61% click rate; local map-pack results got 52% of clicks; 7% of searchers modified their query; 90% used a Google Autocomplete suggestion at some point, with 19% clicking directly on one; on product searches, 14% clicked a Google Shopping result — 3.3x more likely than an organic Amazon result when both were present; 35% of organic clickers used the back button (didn't find what they wanted); average search session lasted 59 seconds; 54% of listing-clickers clicked exactly one result and were satisfied. Takeaway: prioritize video content and rich-snippet-eligible formats (maps, video) since human click behavior consistently favors visually distinct results; for ecommerce, treat Google Shopping as essential alongside marketplaces. (3) A recap of a Gary Vaynerchuk interview (for the "Leveling Up" podcast): asked how he'd market a software company in a crowded market, Gary's answer was ~40 paid-ad creatives/day across formats with meaningful spend (targeting +25-50% growth), not "just make more content"; separately, Gary's stated ambition is to buy the NY Jets via VaynerX → private equity (positioned as bringing marketing expertise PE firms like Carlyle/KKR allegedly lack); a broader claim that PE firms like Vista/KKR under-invest in marketing because they get higher ROI from cross-selling/M&A than marketing spend, illustrated by an anonymized example of a PE portfolio company sold to a Fortune-50 acquirer for ~6x return in under six years.
