# Facebook Video Monetization (How Profitable is it REALLY?)

## Summary

Q&A Thursday (Neil with interviewer Adam Lodolce from Viewership). Question from "Sankeet":
discuss Facebook video monetization — Facebook paying creators to run ads on their videos,
borrowing YouTube's model ("AdSense for videos"). Neil runs ads on his own YouTube channel
(blocking competitors from advertising on it) but calls it "a very small percentage of the
overall revenue" — it just covers editing and extras. His hard number: YouTube pays roughly
**$2,000–$2,500 per million views**, though it varies by audience (a golfing channel that
attracts Rolex advertisers earns far more per view than, say, a relationship-advice
channel). He says flatly he **dislikes AdSense-type monetization** (Google AdSense, YouTube,
Facebook video ads) because it earns far less than monetizing yourself. Using Adam as the
worked example: Adam's YouTube channel gets 1M+ views/month, but selling his own info
products/ebooks off that same traffic made **$10,000–$20,000/month** vs the ~$2,000–$2,500
from ads. Neil's principle: **drive everyone to your main income source and double down** —
don't let a viewer click an ad to a competitor's site when they could buy your product. His
rule of thumb: **you should make at least ~10x more selling your own stuff than from
YouTube/Facebook ads** (in his own niche it'd be closer to 1,000x, so he'd "never turn on
ads"). He reframes ad economics: when someone advertises on your video they pay the network
(which takes a big cut), so your share is small — and the advertiser must be "making a
killing" to recoup costs, so study who advertises on you and what they sell, then build your
own competing product/service. Caveat: there are **two types of people — creators and
business people.** Pure creators who are great at video but not business may be right to
take YouTube/Facebook monetization; business-minded people who can build even a basic
product should sell their own. The only case where pure ad monetization makes sense is
internet-celebrity "day in the life" channels (they cite Logan and Jake Paul) — but that
model is bimodal: you either get extremely popular and make money, or stay middling and
make very little, and it's better to build a topic-specific channel (dating, marketing)
that's easier to monetize consistently. (Adam adds the Pauls often run *their own* ads to
sell merch and get *paid* to endorse products — "the 0.0001%.")

## Key claims

- As of 2018-09-13, YouTube ad revenue is roughly **$2,000–$2,500 per million views**,
  varying widely by audience/advertiser demand (golf→Rolex earns far more than generic
  niches).
- As of 2018-09-13, Neil runs ads on his own YouTube channel (blocking competitors) but it
  is "a very small percentage" of revenue — it just pays for editing.
- As of 2018-09-13, **selling your own products beats ad monetization** — Adam made
  $10k–$20k/month selling info products off 1M+ monthly views vs ~$2k–$2.5k from ads.
- As of 2018-09-13, **make at least ~10x more from your own products than from YouTube/
  Facebook ads**; drive all traffic to your main income source and double down rather than
  leaking viewers to advertisers' sites.
- As of 2018-09-13, study **who advertises on your videos and what they sell** (they must
  profit heavily to recoup ad costs) and build your own competing product/service.
- As of 2018-09-13, there are **"creators" and "business people"** — pure creators may
  rightly take ad monetization; business-minded people should sell their own products.
- As of 2018-09-13, pure ad/influencer monetization only really pays for internet
  celebrities (cites Logan/Jake Paul); it's bimodal, so a topic-specific channel monetizes
  more consistently than a "day in the life" one.
