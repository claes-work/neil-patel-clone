# How to Use Testimonials to Increase Conversions (Gain Social Proof)

## Summary
Neil teaches how to use social proof — primarily testimonials and case studies — to lift conversion rates, and stresses that placement matters more than volume. He argues that generic "Loved by X, Y, Z" or "Featured on Huffington Post / New York Times / Wall Street Journal" badges splattered across a site do little; social proof only works when integrated on the right pages (consulting/lead pages, product pages, checkout pages) and woven into sales copy at the point of decision. He cites Amazon product-page reviews (five-out-of-five vs. two/three stars drives purchase intent) and Basecamp's checkout-page testimonials as models, and points to his own NeilPatel.com testimonials page, which pairs testimonials with case studies and specifics of work done. He closes with the anatomy of a credible testimonial: an image, the person's full name, a linked business/website, and a specific, results-oriented statement rather than vague praise — offering a model quote citing 30% business growth after a year.

## Key claims
As of 2017-08-11, Neil states:
- Social proof for conversions is typically testimonials or case studies, and applies to both B2B and B2C — the more, the better (as of 2017-08-11).
- Generic social-proof badges ("Loved by X, Y, Z," "Featured on Huffington Post / NYT / WSJ") splattered across a website do not really boost conversions (as of 2017-08-11).
- ★ Social proof only lifts conversions when integrated on the right type of page and within the sales copy at the point of decision, not scattered site-wide (as of 2017-08-11).
- On a consulting/lead-gen page, showing testimonials and case studies from other clients builds trust and makes visitors more likely to convert (as of 2017-08-11).
- Amazon product-page reviews function as testimonials/social proof; five-out-of-five-star reviews make buyers much more likely to purchase than two- or three-star ratings (as of 2017-08-11).
- Checkout/sign-up pages are a valid place for testimonials; Basecamp (a project-management product) places testimonials on its payment/sign-up flow (as of 2017-08-11).
- Neil's own NeilPatel.com testimonials page integrates case studies alongside testimonials and details the exact work done for each business, because prospects want to see results, not just praise (self-reported, as of 2017-08-11).
- ★ A credible testimonial requires four elements: an image (or people doubt it's real), the person's full name, and their linked business/website if they have one (as of 2017-08-11).
- A testimonial must be specific and meaningful — vague praise like "Great product, Neil is awesome, amazing service" adds little value; a strong one names timeline and measurable results (e.g., unsure at first, results after three months, 30% business growth after a year) (as of 2017-08-11).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Social proof is typically testimonials or case studies. Whether it's B to B or B to C, you can always have testimonials. The more, the better."
