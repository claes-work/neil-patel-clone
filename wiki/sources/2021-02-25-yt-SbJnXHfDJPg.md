# 7 Landing Page Hacks That'll Double Your Sales - Part 1

## Summary
This is a live conference talk (Part 1) in which Neil Patel critiques real landing pages familiar to an audience of affiliate marketers and offer owners, then layers in his own conversion-optimization tactics on top of basic fixes. Framing the talk around the idea that better-converting landing pages mean more money for affiliates, he first walks through "basic" fixes any page should make — stripping navigation options down to a single call to action, simplifying hard-to-read copy, cutting excess footer links, and placing calls to action throughout long pages rather than only at the top and bottom. He then presents his higher-value tactics, grounding his authority in the fact that his analytics companies track over 3 billion visits a month: engage visitors first with quizzes and sliders before asking for the lead (citing a 108% lift on Nutrition Secrets and a 52.11% lift on Hello Bar), use exit pop-ups with a GIF background (a 42% email-collection lift), apply GeoIP targeting in copy and product selection (12.49–27.57% sales lifts across five pages), add checkout bumps priced at or below 50% of the main product (~15% more revenue), make prospects "work" through qualifying hoops to boost conversion (up to 58.86% lifts, citing Instant Checkmate and Mike Chang's Six Pack Shortcuts), and treat headline and copy tweaks as the highest-leverage change since roughly eight of ten people read only the headline. He closes with fear-based copy for a VPN offer (39.14% lift by showing the visitor their own IP-derived details) and a bonus "ask for a favor" email tactic (6.41% sales lift). Throughout, Neil offers to give free advice to anyone who implements the tactics and fails to see a conversion increase, positioning it as proof of his confidence.

## Key claims
- Reducing a landing page's navigation to a single relevant call-to-action button (e.g., "Apply") instead of many options generates more clicks and sends users to money-making pages rather than places like FAQ pages (as of 2021-02-25).
- Long landing pages should place calls to action throughout, not only at the very top and very bottom (as of 2021-02-25).
- Engaging visitors first — e.g., quizzing them or having them interact with a slider before asking for the lead — outperforms asking for name and email upfront; on his Nutrition Secrets site, quizzing before collecting the lead increased conversions by 108% (as of 2021-02-25).
- On Hello Bar, asking users to enter their URL and build email-collection models before prompting them to sign up produced a 52.11% increase in signups (as of 2021-02-25).
- Engagement works because of a sunk-cost/reciprocity effect — after investing several minutes answering questions, people feel compelled to complete the form; Neil analogizes it to dating rather than proposing marriage on first contact (as of 2021-02-25).
- Getting visitors to engage first also results in fewer ad accounts being "burned," saving money for affiliates who buy accounts (as of 2021-02-25).
- Exit pop-ups should be used to collect leads from the ~97% of visitors who don't buy; using an animated GIF as the exit pop-up background — while keeping the copy the same — increased his email-collection rate by 42% (as of 2021-02-25).
- On mobile, a wealth-building site's opt-in using a dollar-sign slider that must be dragged fully to the end before the button activates accounted for 16% of that page's leads (as of 2021-02-25).
- Blog-style ("advertorial") landing pages should place calls to action inside the blog content where the reader's eyes are, not just in sidebars, yielding roughly three to four times more clicks; "articles you may like" sections should be removed so readers aren't diverted from buying (as of 2021-02-25).
- GeoIP targeting in copy (naming the visitor's city/location) converts better; tested on five landing pages, it increased sales by anywhere from 12.49% to 27.57% (as of 2021-02-25).
- Products should be selected by visitor IP — e.g., Babbel should have shown Neil, browsing from Brazil, the most commonly desired language to learn (English) rather than defaulting to it as an assumed native language (as of 2021-02-25).
- Checkout bumps increase revenue by roughly 15% without decreasing conversions; they should be priced at 50% or less of the main product, have a benefit explainable in one sentence, be heavily discounted, and emphasize speed/automation because people are lazy (as of 2021-02-25).
- Making people "work" by going through qualifying questions and roadblocks increases conversions; Instant Checkmate's delays and warnings drive purchases, and tests on Mike Chang's Six Pack Shortcuts–style sites showed one page dropping 34.63% while the other four rose up to 58.86% (as of 2021-02-25).
- Copy tweaks have the biggest impact on conversions; roughly eight of ten people read the headline but only two of ten read the rest of the landing page, so the headline matters most (as of 2021-02-25).
- Headline rules: numbers and negative words increase click-through and reads; keep headlines under 65 characters for SEO; make headlines match the content to avoid post-click fall-off; odd numbers outperform even; six-word headlines convert extremely well; avoid words with multiple meanings; and use power words and adjectives (as of 2021-02-25).
- General copy rules: evoke curiosity, write in a conversational tone using "you" and "I," avoid being too salesy, keep copy clear and easy to read on mobile, make mobile pages AMP-friendly for faster load, and answer objections (using tools like Qualaroo to surface them) — answering one objection lifted conversions by 14% on one of his sites (as of 2021-02-25).
- Fear-based copy can drive conversions; on a VPN offer, showing visitors their own IP-derived details (location, device, browsing habits) and framing it as "just scratching the surface" increased conversions by 39.14% (as of 2021-02-25).
- Asking customers/visitors for a favor — dripping free information and requesting shares in exchange for a free sample — generated an extra 6.41% increase in sales even though traffic itself didn't rise much; Neil uses the subject line "I need a favor, can you help?" (as of 2021-02-25).
- Neil's analytics companies track over 3 billion visits per month, which he cites as the basis for his conversion data (as of 2021-02-25).
