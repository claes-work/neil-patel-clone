# How I Collected 714,000 Emails Through A Simple Quiz

## Summary
Solo tactical video. Shows his ConvertKit list at 714,000 collected emails (~3,000/day
on weekdays, ~1,800-2,000/day on weekends), sourced almost entirely through one
channel: an exit-intent quiz on neilpatel.com, built with the third-party tool
LeadQuizzes (discloses he doesn't own the company but has used it for years). Walks
through his own quiz (9 questions, personalized "seven-week action plan" as the
reward) and states several design rules learned from testing: keep quizzes under 10
questions (ideally 6-7 — both fewer and more hurt completion/opt-in rates); explain
*why* each question is being asked to build trust; avoid a "zero" option on
effort/commitment questions so respondents don't self-select out of getting value;
allow a back button on all steps except the very last one (a back button on the final
step measurably increases abandonment); and gate the results behind an
email/name/phone capture with explicit consent checkboxes and bot filtering. Reports
an 80%+ completion-to-opt-in rate at the final step.

## Key claims
- States quiz length has a tested sweet spot around 6-7 questions: fewer (~3) reduces
  opt-in rate even though completion looks high, and more (~10) also reduces
  submissions relative to the 6-7 range (paraphrase, self-reported A/B-style finding).
- States removing the back button specifically on the last step (pre-email-capture)
  measurably reduces abandonment vs. keeping it, because users who go back are less
  likely to complete (paraphrase, self-reported test finding).
- States over 80% of visitors who reach the final quiz step submit their contact info,
  which he describes as a high completion/conversion rate for a lead-gen mechanism
  (paraphrase, self-reported, unaudited).
- Deliberately avoids a "zero hours/zero budget" answer option on commitment-style
  questions, reasoning that a respondent who commits to zero effort won't get results
  from any plan he gives them, so the option is removed by design (paraphrase, product/
  funnel-design philosophy).
