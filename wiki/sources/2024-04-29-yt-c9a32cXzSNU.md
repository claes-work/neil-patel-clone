# India's market valuation and Marketing lessons from Africa's leader

## Summary
Two segments. (1) India IPO/valuation arbitrage: a mutual friend's family took a manufacturing
company public in India at a ~70x P/E multiple — a level Neil calls "ridiculous" for
manufacturing — prompting a wider discussion of Indian public-market valuations (a data-center
company cited at ~160x earnings vs. ~3x achievable buying privately in the US). Neil relays that
he was approached about taking a company public in India at what would be a 3-4x higher
valuation than in the US, but declined: he has no interest in being a publicly traded company,
and notes private-equity firms (e.g., General Atlantic, Warburg Pincus, named by the people he
spoke with) have already been running this valuation arbitrage for years — buying cheap in the
US, listing/selling at much higher India multiples, a largely relationship/sales-driven play
rather than a marketing one. This leads into a stated belief: Neil says he never built NP Digital
to make money — it is a "love project" tied to his identity ("I feel like I'm tied to it like a
child of mine... I would never give up my kid for a billion dollars") — contrasted with explicit
advice to people whose business is *not* tied to their identity to "go take the cash" if a
life-changing offer comes along. Two dated family anecdotes follow: his daughter Emma, on video,
first says she'll always live with him, then later says she'll live next door "in Beverly Hills"
when she grows up; his son William's only communicated demand is "give me milk." (2) Marketing
lessons from Russ Cook ("Hardest Geezer"), the first person to run the length of the African
continent: his run generated tens of millions of cumulative video-view impressions across
segments, and the meal-replacement brand Huel is credited (self-observed, not confirmed
sponsorship-contract detail) as smart for sponsoring/appearing throughout his journey, drawing a
comparison to Nike-style athlete sponsorship. The broader marketing point: doing something
extreme and "remarkable" (citing David Goggins as a non-marketer whose polarizing,
extreme-endurance content performs well from a marketing standpoint for the same reason)
naturally attracts outsized attention/eyeballs.
