# Neil Patel on His First Job Creating Content - Selling Strategies

> Note: title mismatch — this is a sit-down biographical interview recorded at the GPeC
> conference in Bucharest, Romania (interviewer: "Kartlin"), not a content-creation
> tutorial.

## Summary
Origin-story interview: states he was born in London, started his first business (a
job board) at 16 because "there's not too many jobs" for a 16-year-old and "the only
other option [besides a job] is you go make your own job" — not because he set out to
be an entrepreneur. The job board failed to attract visitors; he paid marketing
agencies who "ripped him off," so he learned marketing himself, discovered he enjoyed
and was good at driving traffic more than the underlying job-board business, and
pivoted into marketing services. Also recounts an earlier/parallel failed clothing-
import business, where Facebook ads drove some sales but thin margins and poor
inventory management lost money — again reinforcing that promotion, not the product,
was his strength. States he is not CEO of any of his companies (including KISSmetrics),
delegating day-to-day operations because he "doesn't enjoy managing people," citing his
agency co-founder as the operator. States his strengths are narrow ("traffic
acquisition, anything marketing... good at making deals... good at sales although I
don't like doing it") and everything else is a relative weakness, framing a "be great
at one thing, outsource/hire for the rest" philosophy, and cites Malcolm Gladwell's
10,000-hour framework as backing. On starting a business: says figuring out how to
start is "the easiest part," and if someone can't do that on their own (without asking
others how) they are "probably not cut out to be an entrepreneur" — framed as harsh
but consistent advice. Describes a sales technique of charging for a diagnostic/SWOT
analysis before proposing a paid fix, learned from how corporate buyers (MBA-trained)
expect to be sold to; cites this as reducing price resistance because the client
already trusts the diagnosis. Describes a "micro-commitments" psychology tactic
(small asks escalating to large asks, using a dating-to-marriage analogy) as a
generalizable persuasion principle. States he does not need to motivate himself
because he loves the work ("I love what I'm doing... you won't see it as work, you'll
see it as fun"), illustrated by working the same day he landed from a 10-hour
time-zone flight.
