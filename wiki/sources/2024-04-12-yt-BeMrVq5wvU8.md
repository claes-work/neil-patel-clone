# ChatGPT makes links more prominent, NYT is now a gaming company, Erewhon marketing lessons, and more

## Summary
Five segments. (1) Noting that ChatGPT's paid tier was beginning to surface source links more often (compared to Perplexity, which already did this well); framed as important for marketers/researchers to verify AI-generated claims, and a belief that being a cited, trusted source in an increasingly AI-mediated information environment will carry outsized traffic/authority benefits (drawing an analogy to Google News citation "avalanche" effects). (2) Reacting to data (Matthew Ball) showing the New York Times' fastest-growing engagement segment by time-spent is now Games (ahead of Cooking, News, and The Athletic) — framed as a retention play common across media companies (Netflix also cited) since more in-app time supports more ad/affiliate monetization; a personal aside notes Netflix games don't work without Wi-Fi (found out mid-flight) and are perceived as still lower-quality than standalone games (Diablo IV, Monopoly are named as personal favorites). (3) A marketing case study on Erewhon (LA specialty grocer): its Instagram grew from ~94K to ~372K followers in 4 years, driven substantially by celebrity smoothie collaborations (e.g. the Hailey Bieber smoothie); its strategic positioning is explicitly the inverse of "Whole Paycheck" criticism aimed at Whole Foods — deliberately targeting affluent, quality-insensitive-to-price customers rather than trying to broaden appeal. (4) A speaker-fee rate-card reaction (source: a viral post by Jeremiah Owyang): reviewing tiers from $0 (no brand) through $7,500-30,000+ (established full-time speakers); the hosts note they have personally charged above the top-listed tier despite not being full-time speakers, and add that the most in-demand/highest-paid speakers (Hillary Clinton cited at ~$500K/talk) typically don't rely on speaking income and are monetizing reputation/access instead — the broader point made is that speaking fees are rarely the real business model; the real model is what speaking is a funnel into (a newsletter/high-ticket offer parallel is drawn). (5) A closing reaction to reports that Google was considering acquiring HubSpot: skepticism is expressed (an antitrust-heavy regulatory climate is cited, along with the recently-blocked/cancelled Adobe-Figma deal and its reported cancellation fee) and a structural argument that owning one marketing-cloud/CRM platform (vs. Salesforce Marketing Cloud, Adobe) would create channel conflict by discouraging other platforms from referring advertisers to Google, versus the status quo of neutral partnerships with all of them.
