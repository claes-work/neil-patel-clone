# Growth Hacks: How to Scale in Record Time

## Summary
★ Landmark-quality persona source — a long, financially-detailed interview
covering an origin-story variant, verified-adjacent acquisition economics, and
explicit stated values on selling the business. Self-reports age 37, started
"the first real [business]... which was a ad agency" at 16, retells the
Speech-101-community-college → power-supply-manufacturer-client story
(consistent with the Elpac Electronics account recorded elsewhere: $5k/month
scaling to ~$25M in client revenue via SEO/paid). From there, names three more
early agency clients by name — **Countrywide** (later part of Bank of America),
**Blue Cross**, and **ING Direct** — obtained via a referral chain, at which
point he was earning "20 grand a month" as a teenager. ★ **A new variant of the
Crazy Egg origin story**: he says a subsequent large account, **General Motors**
(~$3M/month ad spend under management), led him to advise the client that a
few million dollars/month of spend was wasted and could be cut without hurting
revenue — advice that got him fired from the contract, because the budget owner
needed to spend the full allocation to protect next quarter's budget headcount.
He frames this as the direct trigger for building **Crazy Egg**: software to
prove which spend actually drives ROI, sparing clients (and their agencies) this
perverse budget incentive. This causal narrative (GM-firing → Crazy Egg) is a
different framing from the previously-recorded "hated being a consultant" /
pre-AWS cloud-computing-failure account of that era — recorded as an additional,
not necessarily conflicting, telling (see [[../entities/crazy-egg]]). Says he
"got back into the ad agency world five years ago" (NP Digital) because
established competitor agencies with 50,000+ employees still worked manually
without software automation. ★ **AnswerThePublic acquisition economics
(self-reported, previously "terms undisclosed" per registry-verified press)**:
purchase price **~$8.6M total — $6M upfront plus $2.6M in payments over six
quarters**, with "three more quarters" of payments remaining as of this
interview; at acquisition the tool was doing roughly $100-130K/month in
EBITDA/revenue, which he expects to grow to $300-400K/month EBITDA (~$4M/year),
framing the payback as ~2 years plus a large multiple-arbitrage benefit when
folded into NP Digital, plus lead-generation value from its 600-700K monthly
uniques (including large enterprise visitors NP Digital doesn't yet have
contracts with). ★ **States NP Digital's 2023 revenue is "well into the nine
figures," growing "a little bit more than 40%" year-over-year, versus a
~70% growth rate he estimates the company would have hit without the 2022-23
economic downturn**; expects sub-20%, "more than 10%" growth the following
year. Discusses M&A financing mechanics in detail: his acquisitions are financed
around 3% + prevailing Fed-rate spreads via a relationship with **CIBC** (a
Canadian bank), versus much higher-rate financing (~11-13%) for larger/riskier
deals; explains private-equity "roll-up" multiple arbitrage (1+1=3 via cross-
selling, e.g. a hypothetical NP Digital + Vayner Media combination) as distinct
from a simple sum-of-parts. ★ **States NP Digital has received "quite a few"
acquisition offers over the prior two years (private equity and IPO-adjacent
bank conversations, including Goldman Sachs) and has declined all of them**,
citing a Brazilian-entrepreneur friend's marriage analogy ("like your daughter's
getting married") and his own framing: "the business is like a baby... I don't
want to give my kids away." Compares NP Digital (performance marketing) to
**Vayner Media** (creative/content shop) as complementary, non-competing
categories. On celebrity/influencer-brand economics: argues **Fenty's success is
attributable to its LVMH partnership's operational infrastructure**, contrasting
it with **Kylie Cosmetics** (built solo) and citing (via his exposure to CAA
deal-flow) many 50-100M-follower influencers whose branded product companies
generate surprisingly little revenue/profit due to weak operations/systems —
explicitly rejecting "riches are in the niches" for hyper-specific
influencer products (cites an unnamed "vodka-infused whipped cream" example) in
favor of pursuing a large total addressable market (TAM).
