# 3 Ways to Write Content That Will Go Viral | Get More Facebook Shares!

## Summary
Neil Patel argues that social sharing is driven more by the headline than the content itself — citing his own studies where posts got thousands of likes/retweets while receiving less traffic than shares, meaning people shared without reading. He restates his "8 of 10 read the headline, only 2 of 10 read on" rule and gives three headline tactics for more social shares: (1) keep headlines to about five-to-seven words (too long overwhelms, too short is unclear); (2) use adjectives like "effortlessly" ("10 effortlessly ways to double my search traffic" beats "10 ways to double your search traffic") because everyone wants the easy route; and (3) create mystery/curiosity ("The 7 benefits of green tea — number 6 will shock you"), but only when the payoff is genuinely good — don't trick people, or they lose trust and stop sharing.

## Key claims
As of 2017-11-21, Neil states:
- From his own studies, high like/retweet counts often come with less traffic than shares — meaning people share content ahead of time without reading it (as of 2017-11-21).
- Generating social shares is not really about the content, it's about the headline; content still shouldn't be crap, but shares are driven by the headline (as of 2017-11-21).
- His tested rule: 8 out of 10 people read your headline but only 2 out of 10 read the rest (as of 2017-11-21).
- Tactic 1 — keep headlines around five-to-seven words; too long is overwhelming and unshared, too short is unclear and unshared (as of 2017-11-21).
- Tactic 2 — use adjectives (e.g., "effortlessly") because everyone wants an easy route; "10 effortlessly ways to double my search traffic" outperforms "10 ways to double your search traffic" (as of 2017-11-21).
- Tactic 3 — create mystery/curiosity (e.g., "number 6 will shock you"), but only with genuinely amazing tips behind it; tricking people with a plain payoff destroys trust and future shares (as of 2017-11-21).
