# NY Times' Billion Dollar Lawsuit Against ChatGPT (Marketing School)

## Summary
A co-hosted Marketing School episode in three parts. Eric Siu drives the opening segment — screen-sharing the NYT v. OpenAI/Microsoft complaint (verbatim GPT-4 output beside NYT text) — while Neil Patel supplies the marketing doctrine: he hopes the Times wins because traditional search engines link back and send publishers traffic (ad revenue, leads), whereas ChatGPT reproduces content verbatim, strips the links, and gives no credit, which destroys the publisher business model. In the second segment Neil delivers the episode's most actionable tactic: the fastest way to get traffic from AI right now is to have ChatGPT generate "[Competitor] vs [You] — which one is right for you" comparison articles for every competitor; low-competition terms whose traffic adds up with conversion rates "through the roof" (17,000+ visitors and climbing over ~6 months). Eric elaborates (bottom-of-funnel, programmatic SEO) and adds the ethics caveat; Neil adds monetizing competitor-bound signups with affiliate links. The third segment covers solopreneur strategies: for services, target freshly VC-funded companies via Crunchbase, audit them, and email both the CEO and the funding investor for a warm intro; for products, use a tripwire/loss-leader (Orabrush, Gillette, his Remington guns-and-ammo pitch). Neil recounts landing a ~$30k/month contract at 25 by answering SEO questions on Quora.

## Key claims
- On the NYT v. OpenAI lawsuit, Neil hopes the Times wins: the key difference from ordinary search is that when Google "scrapes" your content it links back and sends you traffic (so a publisher still earns ad revenue, and an agency like NP Digital still gets leads/customers), whereas ChatGPT reproduces the content verbatim, removes the links, gives no credit, and is now a more popular destination than the NYT itself — which destroys the content business model. (Neil, 2024-01-15)
- The problem is not merely having/training on the data: using data to produce a genuinely *different* output is one thing, but scraping it and outputting it verbatim as your own (without credit) is another. (Neil, 2024-01-15)
- Apple as a marketing case study: Apple deliberately is not a first mover — it waits to see what everyone else does, then ships a much better-experience version (late to watches, yet its watch beats "$500,000 watches"; the Vision Pro). Being late but executing right is itself part of what makes its marketing so effective. Neil pre-registered for the Vision Pro and says he'll buy it regardless of price ("two grand, three grand, five grand, ten grand") because it "looks really cool." (Neil, 2024-01-15)
- Fastest way to get traffic from AI right now: have ChatGPT generate comparison articles of your company vs. each competitor (works for a service or a product). The title format that works is "[Competitor] vs [You] — which one is right for you," placing the competitor's name first. Per-article traffic is small, but the terms aren't competitive, the traffic adds up, and the conversion rates are "through the roof" because it's bottom-of-funnel intent. Neil has driven 17,000+ visitors from it (that figure was one small subset; significantly more across multiple subsets) over roughly six months, and the same model works for affiliate revenue. (Neil, 2024-01-15)
- Monetization add-on for comparison pages: a portion of readers will sign up to the competitor anyway, so use the competitor's affiliate link and monetize those visitors too. (Neil, 2024-01-15)
- Solopreneur services tactic: find companies that just raised venture money (e.g. via Crunchbase). Freshly funded companies must deploy the capital and grow fast or the VC math fails, so they're flush and must spend soon. Audit a target, document everything they're doing wrong and how to fix it step by step, then email both the CEO and the investor who funded them — the investor often makes a warm intro. Rather than implementing it themselves, they usually ask you to do it for them because they have the cash. (Neil, 2024-01-15)
- Neil's own solopreneur origin: at 25 he landed a ~$30,000/month contract by answering a lot of SEO questions on Quora (no article links, just a link to his profile); someone reached out via his profile and it eventually became the contract. Content is a longer-term play that "increases your surface area of luck"; "hand-to-hand combat" (direct outreach) is the shorter-term play. (Neil, 2024-01-15)
  - `(note: caption garble — the figure is stated once as "$330,000 a month" and twice as "$30,000 a month"; the consistent value is $30k/month.)`
- Solopreneur product tactic: use a tripwire / loss-leader. Give away a cheap or free front-end product (customer just pays shipping), then upsell aggressively. Examples: Orabrush ("Ora brush"/"AA brush" in captions) gave a free tongue cleaner and upsold the toothbrush/toothpaste/floss; Gillette gave away the razor handle and made its money on recurring blade sales; and his own pitch to Remington — give the guns away free with an ammo purchase (the guns otherwise sit in a warehouse making $0) and profit from recurring ammo. (Neil, 2024-01-15)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> When people complain about Google scraping your content, in most cases they're linking back to your website and you're getting traffic for it — you're making ad revenue if you're the New York Times, or for us we're getting leads or customers. But this, they're straight up just jacking it.

> It's one thing to have the data and then use the data to create a different output; it's another thing to just scrape it and just say it's your own.

> The one thing I like about Apple as a company is they tend not to be the first mover... they were late to watches, but yet their watches are better than these $500,000 watches that people buy. They're later to come to most things that are out there, but when they do it, they do it right — and I think that's what also makes their marketing super effective.

> These terms aren't that competitive to rank for, so the traffic adds up, and the conversion rates are through the roof... you typically want to place the competitor name first versus your name, and then say "which one is right for you."

> There's always going to be a portion of people that are going to sign up to your competitors, so you should just use the affiliate link and monetize that as well.

> When people raise money, the thing they need to do is deploy the money and grow faster, or else it just doesn't work out... I'll email the CEO and the investor that funded them, and a lot of times I'll get an intro from the investor to the CEO. In most cases they're not going to say "team, implement this" — they're going to be like "can we just pay you and you just fix it?" because they have the cash.

> When you're creating content, you're basically increasing your surface area of luck.

> You've got all these guns that are sitting there that you don't make any money from, sitting in a warehouse — why don't you give away the guns for free if people buy some ammo, and then they'll keep buying more ammo?

## Eric Siu (co-host, context — NOT persona data)
[[wiki/entities/eric-siu]]
- Drove the whole opening segment via screen-share: the NYT alleges OpenAI pulled 100M+ tokens by crawling for ChatGPT outputs; ChatGPT reproduces NYT articles nearly verbatim while stripping the affiliate/subscription links the Times monetizes (ads, subscriptions, ~10–15% affiliate cuts). One passage differed by a single word ("premiered" in GPT-4 vs. "debuted" in the NYT). (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- Cited Jason Calacanis calling this OpenAI's "Napster moment"; predicts the NYT wins and OpenAI settles for hundreds of millions, setting precedent and reinforcing the importance of owning your own data to train LLMs rather than crawling others' without permission. (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- Apple is quietly signing licensing deals with content publishers to get ahead of the crawling-permission problem. (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- Switched from being "a PC guy" to full Apple because "their products are just better... and they just work." (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- Cited a tweet from lawyer Cecilia Zanetti: the NYT is the single biggest proprietary dataset in Common Crawl used to train GPT (ranked #4 overall, after patents.google.com and Wikipedia). (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- Elaborated the comparison-article tactic: it's bottom/middle-of-funnel content (e.g. "HubSpot vs Salesforce review," "Salesforce alternatives"), low-hanging-fruit low-competition keywords, done AI-assisted or as programmatic SEO; Single Grain runs it around services/agency pages. (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- Ethics caveat on AI-generated comparison articles: review and modify the AI output, showcase your advantages honestly (don't claim more features or lower price if untrue), and add clear CTAs; cites a friend's acquired blog whose acquirer unethically stripped all competitor mentions and got called out on X. (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- Recommends Instagram "broadcast channels": he started one and they're growing fast; they let you poll your audience for topics/feedback and gather data without blasting your email list, and partners can collaborate on one. (Eric, 2024-01-15)
- His own Quora tactic: found popular Quora questions and pasted answers from his existing blog posts with links back to his site; Quora repeatedly banned him for it, then relented. Notes Quora is ~the 60th most popular website globally and its CEO (Adam D'Angelo) sits on OpenAI's board. (Eric, 2024-01-15)
