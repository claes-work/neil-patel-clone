# The Reddit SEO Strategy That Actually Works (Marketing School)

## Summary
★ Standout, evidence-backed Neil finding worth flagging: Reddit is very good at detecting AI-written replies, and using AI to write your Reddit responses is the single biggest mistake companies make. Neil ran a small controlled experiment — he and ~10 buddies posted AI-generated content 100 times (10 posts each) on non-main Reddit accounts — and Reddit detected and removed the AI-written content **over 70% of the time** (Neil places it under 80%). His practical takeaway: don't be promotional, focus on genuinely helping the community, and have a human manually write your replies.

A short, tactical co-hosted Marketing School segment on how to actually rank/play on Reddit. Eric prompts ("what are you seeing on the Reddit side… how to play the game?"), noting two things companies do — buying high-authority Reddit accounts to insert themselves into conversations, and Reddit reputation management — and that Reddit aggressively removes AI-created replies. Neil's answer is a relationship-first playbook: participate natively in the community and network with influential, long-tenured Reddit contributors; buying accounts is shortsighted because companies buy them only to promote their own business and don't know how to maintain them. Neil grounds this in his own Digg track record (highest submit-to-homepage-hit ratio when Digg was popular, earned by working with and getting to know other users) and his broader relationship philosophy (meet people, build friendships, revenue is a long-run side effect, not the goal). Eric agrees you need someone native to Reddit who won't spam ("they catch it very quickly"). They close on a disagreement about sourcing Reddit talent: Eric argues these people are hard to hire and live in the "nooks and crannies of the internet" (Reddit itself, Discord) rather than LinkedIn; Neil counters that NP Digital finds most of them through relationships and by going to marketing events (even HubSpot's conference) and talking about Reddit. Neil + Eric only; no guest.

## Key claims
- The real way to play Reddit is to participate in the community and network with influential, long-tenured Reddit contributors — not to buy authority accounts and promote your own business. (Neil, 2025-09-25)
- Buying Reddit accounts is shortsighted and tends to fail: many companies buy high-authority accounts but don't know how to maintain them, and treat them purely as a channel to promote their business, which causes problems. (Neil, 2025-09-25)
- Neil's Digg track record: when Digg was popular he did well by working with other users and getting to know them, and he had the highest ratio of submits to homepage hits. (Neil, 2025-09-25)
- Relationship framing: like meeting up with enterprise companies just to build the relationship (not to talk business), it generates revenue in the long run — but that's not always the goal; many of these people are simply friends he's known for years. (Neil, 2025-09-25)
- Biggest mistake companies make on Reddit is using AI to create their responses; instead, avoid being too promotional, focus on helping the community, and have someone manually write the replies — you're much more likely to succeed. (Neil, 2025-09-25)
- Reddit has a high hit ratio for detecting AI content. In Neil's own experiment (10 people posting AI content 10 times each, 100 total, on non-main accounts), over 70% — less than 80% — of the AI-written (ChatGPT) content was detected and removed. (Neil, 2025-09-25 — approximate; Neil says he'd need to confirm the exact figure, "over 70… less than 80")
- NP Digital sources Reddit contributors mostly through relationships or by going to events and talking about Reddit (finding a lot of active Reddit users even at large marketing events such as HubSpot's), not through LinkedIn. (Neil, 2025-09-25)
