# Impactful Marketing: Why You Should Incorporate Giving Back into Your Strategy

## Summary
Long biographical interview, the most detailed personal/family account in the corpus to
date. **Career origin** (self-reported, ages as stated 2023-05-25, Neil then 37): at 16, in
a required Speech 101 night-college class (taken while still in high school), he gave a
speech on how Google's algorithm works and how to rank — a classmate's employer (a power
supply manufacturer) hired him afterward, first hourly ($100-200/hr) then at **$5,000/month**,
and he says he drove that one client **$20-25M in revenue in the first year**. The client's
owner then resold his services (via the owner's son's ad agency) to Blue Cross, ING Direct,
and Countrywide (a mortgage company later absorbed by Bank of America in the 2008 crisis) at
$5K/client — reaching **$20K/month** while still 16 and in high school; he says he started
his own ad agency the same year (still 16), with an office and employees, before finishing
high school. Says his parents pressured him to finish college regardless ("no good Indian
girl's going to marry you without a degree," later softened to just "get married") — he did
not drop out. **2008 crisis**: the ad agency lost significant revenue as clients (including
the subprime-mortgage-adjacent Countrywide relationship) collapsed; a software company
started a year or two earlier — **Crazy Egg** — kept growing through the downturn, so he
shifted focus to software. States his **sister runs Crazy Egg today** (name given as
**"Amy"** in this transcript — flagged as a spelling variant against "Amee" recorded
elsewhere in the wiki/`persona/biography.md`, not reconciled); Crazy Egg was never taken
public. **NP Digital**: his newest company, ad agency, ~5 years old at time of speaking,
ranked **21st-fastest-growing company in the US** (Inc.), fully bootstrapped/self-funded,
grew from zero to "a solid, real solid nine figures" in revenue in under 5 years; says he
has turned down "way more than five, maybe 7, 8, 9" buyout offers across his companies
(including NP Digital), and has never sold a company he built, citing emotional attachment
("they're like babies") over pure financial upside — contrasted explicitly with an IPO route
he says he's had the option to take with NP Digital but rejects due to loss of control and
public-market stress. **Personal lifestyle**: says he drives a Honda Odyssey by choice (calls
it more convenient with young kids) despite having a driver when working, and states he was
never drawn to big houses or luxury cars ("never dreamed about having a Ferrari") — his
stated drug-of-choice is building a bigger business/more revenue/more employees as a
milestone metric. **Marriage/family** (self-reported, first detailed account of this kind in
the corpus): married roughly four years before this interview (i.e. ~2019); wife is
explicitly **not Indian** ("a mutt... some Korean, some Chinese, Mongolian, some American,
some European"); two kids, described as conceived/born during COVID, whom he says "look
white"; kids currently ages **three and one**, in private preschool (no free public option
at that age), with an open decision between Vegas and Southern California public schools
later — he says he attended public school in Orange County, CA and it was "decent." Frames
his wife's personal drive as education/anti-poverty philanthropy and cites an explicit
**inner-city-vs.-privilege thesis** he and his wife hold: kids from Compton/East LA, given
the same tools/education as Beverly Hills kids, would "outshine" them because of greater
hunger/hustle born from having fewer options — he frames this as a belief, not proven, and
notes his own family owns a home in Beverly Hills, disclaiming any intended insult. Closes on
**Google's planned 2024 third-party-cookie deprecation**: frames it as privacy-driven
(citing GDPR and tightening global regulation) rather than data-exhausted, predicts Google's
own revenue will be insulated (it doesn't need third-party cookies given its first-party
data moat: browser, search history, YouTube, email, and Android's global device-marketshare
lead over iOS) while the change will primarily hurt **Facebook's** ad revenue — drawing a
direct parallel to Apple's 2021 ATT privacy change, which he says hurt Facebook's revenue,
not Apple's.
