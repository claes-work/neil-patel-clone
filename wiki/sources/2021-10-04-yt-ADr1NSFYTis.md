# EVERYTHING You've Been Taught About Content Promotion is WRONG

## Summary
Argues that content promotion fails when the intent is self-serving; the fix is
reframing every promotion ask around the *other* audience's benefit. Examples: turning
a stat-heavy post into a data-visualization image and sending it to the publication
that already linked to the original stat source (they benefit, so they're more likely
to link); refreshing a "how to get more Facebook reach in [year]" outreach angle for
sites that linked to an older, now-outdated version, since the new algorithm makes the
old citation stale and the publisher benefits from updating it. Rejects generic
cold-outreach templates in favor of personal voice. Revisits his own "Who is Neil
Patel" self-promotion campaign with new color: a bodybuilder influencer who could
bench-press ~300 lbs put "Neil Patel" on his pecs and flexed them to make the name
"dance" on Instagram — driving broad brand-name recognition independent of any single
piece of content. Also describes his own social-hook formula (lead with a
pain-point-driven hook, e.g. "the way you're promoting content is all wrong," before
directing to the site) as outperforming a plain "check out my article" post, and
recommends targeting smaller/niche brands over big ones when there's an audience
mismatch.
