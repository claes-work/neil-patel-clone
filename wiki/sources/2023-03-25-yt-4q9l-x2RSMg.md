# Common Marketing Mistakes to Avoid: Tips for New Marketers

## Summary
Origin-story-driven podcast interview. Self-reports his age as 37 at recording
(consistent with a 1985/86 birth year) and retells the entrepreneurial start at
"around 15 and a half," explicitly framing his original motive as wanting money
(not solving a problem) — "not the right reason, but that's what I did" — via a
failed job board. States that failure forced him to learn marketing; within six
to seven months the job board hit ~100,000 visitors/month but still made no
money, which nearly pushed him to abandon entrepreneurship for college and a
job. Repeats (consistent with existing bio) the community-college "Speech 101"
detail and the resulting client contract, here framed generically ($5k/month
scaling to $20k/month by referrals) rather than naming Elpac Electronics. On
reflection/regret: names *lack of focus* — starting "way too many businesses" in
parallel — as his biggest strategic mistake, only returning to agency focus with
NP Digital five years prior (i.e., ~2018 self-reported, in tension with the
2016-12-29/2017 NP Digital founding already recorded elsewhere — likely a loose
approximation, not a hard contradiction). On good decisions: credits saving
money (attributes the "easier to save than to make it" line to his mother,
consistent with existing record) and, from the KISSmetrics experience,
internalizing investor advice that "your only job as a founder is to hire really
amazing people... who have done it before." Recommends Ray Dalio's *Principles*
as his top non-own-book read. Describes NP Digital's client base (all sizes,
franchises to Fortune 100) and names an **SMB-focused division, "NP Digital
Accelerate"** (consistent with the previously recorded "NPD/NP Accelerate"
naming inconsistency). On the 2022-23 downturn: says growth has slowed but
remains positive; recommends hiring good people regardless of market conditions
and being "cautious," "patient," and maintaining a downside cushion rather than
over-hiring/over-acquiring. States downturns favor digital marketing spend
because profitable channels get defended even when stock prices fall, though
some CFOs still cut reflexively (cites an anonymous large-client anecdote of
being told to pause profitable spend, then restarting it a month later after
revenue dropped).
