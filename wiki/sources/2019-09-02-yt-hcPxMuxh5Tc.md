# The Anatomy of a High Converting Landing Page

## Summary
Neil Patel lays out a step-by-step formula for high-converting landing pages, arguing that conversion follows a repeatable structure and that deviating from it causes conversions to tank. The seven components he prescribes are: (1) a clearly stated value proposition in a bold, short headline (ideally around six to eight words); (2) a single visual focus such as a headshot, product shot, or video; (3) an outline of features and, more importantly, benefits tied to the customer's actual problems; (4) a single, focused call to action placed only after the visitor has the full story; (5) testimonials and other visual proof; (6) a guarantee or risk-reducer such as a free trial or money-back guarantee; and (7) storytelling woven through the page to carry the visitor through an emotional arc. He stresses that optimizing for total completions (visitor-to-purchase/lead) matters more than maximizing clicks to a checkout or lead form, and closes by pointing viewers to his agency, Neil Patel Digital, for conversion help.

## Key claims
- A high-converting landing page follows a repeatable formula, and following it step by step produces better results while deviating causes conversions to tank (as of 2019-09-02).
- The first component is clearly stating the value proposition in the header, because the headline determines whether a visitor reads the rest of the page (as of 2019-09-02).
- Roughly eight out of ten people will read the headline, but only two out of ten will read the rest of the copy on the page (as of 2019-09-02).
- Headlines should be bold, short, and to the point — a statement — ideally around six words and no more than seven or eight words; run-on headlines lose people (as of 2019-09-02).
- The second component is a single visual focus (headshot, product shot, or video); text-only pages convert less well, and higher production quality improves results (as of 2019-09-02).
- The third component is outlining features and benefits, with emphasis on benefits, because people buy for the benefit (typically the solution to their problem, e.g. saving time or money), not the feature (as of 2019-09-02).
- Benefits should be derived from talking to ideal customers about their actual problems, and quantified where possible (e.g. "on average, this saves three hours") rather than stated generically (as of 2019-09-02).
- The fourth component is a single, focused call to action placed after the visitor has the full story (headline, visual, benefits, solutions, objections handled); additional CTAs can come later down the page (as of 2019-09-02).
- Maximizing conversion is about the highest number of completions, not the most clicks to the checkout or lead-form page — fewer clicks with higher completion rates can be better (as of 2019-09-02).
- The fifth component is including testimonials and other visual proof/reviews, because reviews let prospects judge whether the product is a fit (as of 2019-09-02).
- The sixth component is giving a guarantee (e.g. free trial with no credit card, or a 30- or 60-day money-back guarantee) to lower perceived risk and make people comfortable (as of 2019-09-02).
- Failing to refund unhappy customers tarnishes the brand in the long run, so a guarantee both reduces risk and protects reputation (as of 2019-09-02).
- The seventh component is integrating storytelling to take people through an emotional arc, described as a thousand-plus-year-old marketing technique that keeps working regardless of channels or algorithm updates (as of 2019-09-02).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> I can look at a landing page right away and know that it's not going to convert. On the flip side, I can also look at a landing page and know if it's going to convert exceptionally well.

> The attention span of a human is less than a goldfish.

> Eight out of 10 people will read your headline, but only two of the 10 will read the rest of the copy on the page.

> People don't buy something because of features, they buy because of the benefit.

> It's not about getting the most amount of people to your checkout or lead form pages. It's about getting the highest number of completions.

> It's that thousand-plus year old marketing technique that we all tend to forget. But it's worked for so many years, and it'll still keep working, no matter what channels are out there, no matter what algorithm updates happen.
