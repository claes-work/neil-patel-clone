# How to Script & Storyboard Your YouTube Videos - Module 2 - Lesson 1 - YouTube Unlocked

## Summary
Lesson 4 of "YouTube Unlocked" (Module 2, Lesson 1; follows
[[wiki/sources/2020-10-10-yt-lazS3Gwv0vI]]), covering content outlining, scripting,
storyboarding, and video-level SEO/publishing mechanics. Neil walks a worksheet-driven
outlining method (list ten strengths/topics, weigh them against what competitors'
audiences are sharing/commenting on, and validate with Ubersuggest/Google
Trends/BuzzSumo search-volume data), then a video-vetting heuristic identical in
spirit to Lesson 2's: a new, low-subscriber channel's video getting strong views
signals real demand. He details the YouTube-SEO checklist for uploading: put focus
keywords early in the title (not the end), keep descriptions to 200-400 words with
the exact title repeated in the first line, cap tags around 5-10 secondary keywords
including some phrased as competitor-channel names, upload manual transcriptions
(claiming this outranks YouTube's auto-generated captions), and design attention-
grabbing thumbnails. He also states the core algorithmic thesis running through the
course: YouTube "rewards retention," so scripts/storyboards should be built to
maximize watch time and hook viewers within the first minute.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-10-12, Neil states manually uploading a transcription (SRT file, via a
  service like Rev) helps a video rank better than relying on YouTube's own
  auto-generated captions (self-reported, "we've seen, if you don't upload them,
  you don't rank as high in the long run").
- As of 2020-10-12, Neil's title-optimization rule: the focus keyword must appear in
  the first part of the title, not the end, because YouTube weights the first part
  more heavily.
- As of 2020-10-12, Neil recommends uploading new videos as "unlisted" first so they
  can be fine-tuned before going public.
- As of 2020-10-12, Neil's core algorithm framing for the course: "YouTube rewards
  retention" — the central design constraint for scripting and storyboarding.
- As of 2020-10-12, Neil recommends using competitor/other-channel names as tags,
  arguing this captures traffic from adjacent-channel searches.
