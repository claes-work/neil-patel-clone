# 5 Dumbest Mistakes Entrepreneurs Make With Their SEO

## Summary
Solo mistakes-list video aimed at founders — people he says are good at solving problems
and shipping ideas but who repeat SEO errors costing "hundreds… thousands… sometimes even
millions of dollars," including after they have built big brands. (1) **Chasing the wrong
keywords, and competitive keywords too early.** Two failure modes: the term is too
competitive to rank for, or it ranks but never converts. His fix is competitor-derived:
Ubersuggest → competitor URL → *Top Pages* → *view all* under estimated visits → the
keywords they're actually winning on, then run those through keyword ideas for
comparisons, prepositions and variations. The selection rule is a three-way filter —
**high CPC** (someone paying a lot to advertise on it proves it converts), **low SD/SEO
difficulty** (20s–40s good, above 50 gets hard), and **high volume**. (2) **No analytics,
no regular audits.** Not just visit counts: goal tracking, conversion tracking, which
pages and content drive sales, and the fact that a visitor from California may not be
worth the same as one from New York or Texas. He extends this into an unusually blunt
epistemic rule about advice — including his own: implement what you read online, but if it
doesn't convert into revenue, **stop doing it**. Pair with regular site audits (Ubersuggest
free). (3) **Overlooking page titles and meta descriptions.** If the #2 listing out-clicks
the #1, that tells Google #2 is more relevant "no matter if the number one has more
backlinks or better on-page SEO" — user metrics decide. So write persuasive copy, study
the paid ads (whose top slots are earned by clicks as much as by bid, because clicks are
what make Google money), keep meta descriptions around **160 characters**, be punchy, and
put keywords near the beginning of title and description. (4) **Anchor-text abuse.** Not
every inbound or internal link for "online marketing" should read "online marketing"; he
narrates his own rotation ("if you want to rank higher in Google, follow these tips" vs.
"check out these SEO tips"), mixing brand, random and topical anchors — and frames it as
naturalness for the reader rather than manipulation. Over-rich anchor profiles produce the
classic pattern: rankings climb, then "boom," they dip and keep dipping. (5) **Writing for
algorithms instead of the audience.** He reads **E-A-T** and **BERT** (natural-language
processing) as Google adapting *to* what humans want, so the winning move is the reverse
of the spammy competitor's: **write for people first, even if Google doesn't like it**,
then optimize afterwards — right keywords, flow, internal links, on-page — and accept a
less-optimized article if that is the price. Not word count, not keyword stuffing.

## Key claims
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil said entrepreneurs — including those who have built big successful brands — keep making the same SEO mistakes, at a cost of hundreds to millions of dollars.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil said new businesses commonly target keywords that are either too competitive to rank for or that won't convert even if they do rank.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil's keyword-sourcing method was competitor-derived: Ubersuggest → competitor URL → **Top Pages** → *view all* under estimated visits → their converting keywords, then keyword ideas for comparisons, prepositions and related terms.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil's keyword-selection filter was **high CPC + low SEO difficulty + high volume** — the CPC being the proxy for commercial intent ("if someone is paying a lot of money to advertise on it, you can bet that it converts highly into sales").
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil put the SEO-difficulty threshold at **above 50 gets hard**, with 20s–40s the good range.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil said too many companies track only visits, and pushed for **goal tracking and conversion tracking** plus knowing which pages and content drive sales, and that visitor value varies by region (California vs. New York vs. Texas).
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil gave a revenue-first rule for evaluating marketing advice generally: implement it, but "**if that advice isn't converting into revenue, who cares?**… stop doing that."
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil said **user click behaviour outranks backlinks and on-page SEO**: if the #2 listing gets the clicks, Google reads it as more relevant than #1.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil said the top paid-ad slots are not purely the highest bidders — they also earn a lot of clicks, because clicks are what make Google money — so paid ads are worth studying as CTR copy models.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil's meta-description guidance was **~160 characters**, punchy, with keywords placed near the beginning of both the title tag and the description.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil said neither inbound nor **internal** anchor text should repeat one target phrase; he rotates brand-oriented, random and topical anchors so the text reads naturally.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil framed anchor rotation as user-first, not manipulation-avoidance: "I'm trying to do this because I'm doing what's best for the user."
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil described the over-optimized-anchor failure pattern as rankings climbing, then dipping and continuing to dip "because your anchor tag's just too rich."
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil read **E-A-T** (authority, expertise, trust) and **BERT** (natural language processing) as evidence Google is adapting its algorithm to what humans want, not asking you to write for the algorithm.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil rejected the split-content approach ("some content for Google and some for people") outright: "**Write content for people first, even if Google doesn't like it**," then optimize — accepting a less-optimized article as the cost.
- As of 2020-01-25, Neil said it is "not about your word count" and not about keyword stuffing.
