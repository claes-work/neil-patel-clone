# What a TikTok Ban means for advertisers and marketers, Google gets boosted after big update, & more

## Summary
Several segments. (1) A continuation of the prior episode's TikTok-ban discussion, largely undiarized, guessing at TikTok US's valuation (estimates ranging $16B–$100B+ cited from various analysts) and predicting a forced-divestiture sale rather than a shutdown; interrupted mid-recording by one of Neil's children coming in to report a sibling dispute ("her brother slapped her head... but I love them both to death"), a rare candid, in-the-moment family moment. (2) Prompted directly by Eric ("Neil, let's talk about politics"), Neil reacts to Bernie Sanders' proposed 4-day workweek bill, arguing it's a headline-generating political move and that if it materializes as reduced output (not just compressed hours) it forces trade-offs — either payroll cuts, proportional pay cuts, or hiring to cover the lost 20% — citing economist Thomas Sowell's "there are no solutions, only trade-offs" (he owns a copy of Sowell's "Social Justice Fallacies") and floating an unprompted idea that politicians should face both term limits and age limits, citing Mark Zuckerberg's 2018 Senate hearing (where senators visibly didn't understand how Facebook's ad business worked) as evidence of a generational technology-literacy gap in government. (3) Reacting to a tweet showing an in-store Advil "Pain Equity" campaign (endcap copy citing a study that "3 in 4 Black people suffering from pain believe there is a bias in how pain is treated"), Neil states, self-identifying explicitly as Indian, that he finds the campaign exploitative of race for profit regardless of whether the underlying bias claim has merit, calls it an example of companies "taking advantage of woke ideology" to generate attention/sales, and says he would have fired whoever approved the campaign. (4) A closing segment (Eric narrating on shared screen) showing Google's own hotel/travel search results gaining ~40–50% organic traffic following a recent core update, which Neil/the hosts frame as an unsurprising, incentive-driven example of platforms favoring their own properties, drawing a direct comparison to Apple's 30% App Store fee.
