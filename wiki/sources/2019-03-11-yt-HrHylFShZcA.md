# The Real Reason Your Blog Doesn't Rank (How to Write a Blog Post that Ranks) | SEO Tutorial

## Summary

Neil's core content-SEO thesis: blogging is no longer a quantity game (there are now ~1 billion
blogs, roughly one per seven people), so cranking out volume no longer works — you win on quality
and thoroughness. He gives a repeatable Ubersuggest-driven workflow: reverse-engineer a
competitor's top pages and keywords, target "money keywords" (high volume, SEO difficulty ideally
under 50, high CPC of $1–3+), read the top-10 ranking articles for the term, then write a *more
thorough* (not merely longer) article that covers every facet of the topic. Closes with a
link-outreach tip: email every site you sourced/linked to so they may share it. Note the title's
"2023 Update" is a re-upload label; the video published 2019-03-11.

## Key claims

- As of 2019-03-11, Neil said blogging shifted from a quantity game to a quality game: when he
  started there were <30–40 million blogs; now there are over a billion (~one per seven people), so
  cranking out volume no longer ranks. [belief — content quality over quantity]
- As of 2019-03-11, Neil's ranking workflow: (1) enter a competitor URL in Ubersuggest; (2) open the
  overview for your target region (customers usually come from a specific region, not everywhere);
  (3) open Top Pages → View All to see the keywords driving their traffic.
- As of 2019-03-11, Neil defined "money keywords" as high-volume terms with SEO/search difficulty
  ideally under 50 and a high cost-per-click (over $1–2–3 is better) — high CPC signals the traffic
  converts into customers.
- As of 2019-03-11, Neil advised reading all top-10 Google results for a keyword to understand what
  Google rewards, then writing "a much better article" — emphasizing thoroughness over length ("not
  double, triple ... not 10 times the length"), using an exhaustive pizza-recipe analogy.
- As of 2019-03-11, Neil's promotion step: email every site you linked to/sourced, telling them you
  linked out, to increase the chance they share the article on social. [content-marketing tactic]
