# How Many Keywords Should A Blog Post Contain? | The Right Way to Use Keywords in your Blog

## Summary
Tactical Ubersuggest-demo video answering "how many keywords per blog post" with a
depth-over-count argument. Workflow: enter a seed keyword into Ubersuggest's Keyword
Ideas report, review the Suggestions/Related/Questions/Comparison/Preposition keyword
buckets (demoed with "marketing" — 542 suggestions, 1.1 million related), and pull in
every keyword that's genuinely relevant to the specific sub-topic being written about
(e.g., "social media marketing" keywords, not generic "marketing" or "marketing
agency" keywords, which he treats as off-topic for that piece). States keywords should
be added only where they flow naturally — never force-inserted for count's sake — and
that Google's own NLP will surface a page for related-but-unmentioned variations once
the topic is covered thoroughly. Demonstrates via Ubersuggest's Top Pages report on
HubSpot's blog: a single "best time to post on Instagram" page (3.2M monthly visits
per Ubersuggest's estimate) and a "small business ideas" page each rank for hundreds of
long-tail keyword variations neither page explicitly targeted. Concludes the real rule
is topical narrowness + exhaustive depth on one specific sub-topic, not a keyword count.

## Key claims
- Cites Ubersuggest's estimate that HubSpot's "best time to post on Instagram" blog
  page draws ~3.2 million monthly visits and ranks for hundreds of unrequested
  long-tail variants of that phrase (paraphrase, tool-estimated data, not independently
  re-verified).
- States there is no target keyword count — "as many as you can, that's relevant to
  that post" — explicitly rejecting the premise of the video's own title as the wrong
  question to ask (paraphrase, previously-unlogged framing of the topical-depth
  doctrine).
- States a specific narrowness rule: an article should cover ONE sub-topic
  exhaustively ("poke holes in your article and cover every single aspect of that
  topic") rather than ten topics shallowly, using "social media marketing" (not generic
  "marketing") as the right scope example (paraphrase).
- States Google's own semantic matching will surface a page for closely-related
  keyword variations that were never explicitly written into the copy, provided the
  core topic is covered thoroughly — used as the justification for not manually
  stuffing keyword variants (paraphrase, self-reported operating belief).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> How many keywords should you have in your posts? As many as you can, that's relevant
> to that post.

> You want to try to poke holes in your article and cover every single aspect of that
> topic. You don't need to cover 10 topics in that article.
