# Discounting is for Dummies — Here's Why And What to Do Instead | Sales Training

## Summary

★ L3-candidate — a tight restatement of Neil's pricing-discipline doctrine (see the
closely-related [[2019-03-07-yt-uLgUg5QZ19o]] "The Power of No"): discounting devalues you,
trains "coupon buyers," and actually *loses* deals versus holding firm. Neil argues that if you
want to be the premier solution you shouldn't discount at all; only discount if you deliberately
want to be the cheap option. He supports it with a self-reported A/B test from a past company and
gives a 3-step alternative: say "no" and explain the price, offer to get on the phone with the
buyer's boss, and sell benefits/outcomes rather than features. Includes an NP Digital plug on how
he sells (traffic → sales → revenue → profit, not deliverables).

## Key claims

- As of 2019-03-09, Neil argued that offering discounts as a sales rep makes you *less* money and
  *fewer* sales (lower commissions) — you shouldn't do it. [belief — pricing discipline]
- As of 2019-03-09, Neil said coupon/discount buyers are "conditioned" to buy only when there is a
  discount and won't buy otherwise; discounting devalues your product and signals you are the cheap
  solution rather than the premier one.
- As of 2019-03-09, Neil described a self-reported test at a past company (~30+ reps, split in two):
  one group had a hard floor (minimum price ~$3,600/yr for the software, ranging up to $10K, $50K,
  even $100K+/yr based on usage) and could not discount; the other had "wiggle room." The
  discounting group closed **fewer** deals and made **less** revenue. After that he stopped
  offering discounts altogether. [self-reported]
- As of 2019-03-09, Neil analogized sales to dating: saying yes to everything kills attraction,
  while saying "no" makes the prospect chase — so telling a customer "no" on a discount makes them
  *more* likely to sign up.
- As of 2019-03-09, Neil's 3 steps: (1) use the word "No" and explain what the price buys (offer a
  smaller-scope/less-features contract if they want lower); (2) offer to call the buyer's
  boss/decision-maker to explain the value; (3) sell benefits and the end goal, not features —
  "stop selling your product or service, start selling the end goal."
- As of 2019-03-09, Neil said that for his agency Neil Patel Digital he never pitches deliverables
  ("x backlinks, x articles") but rather projected traffic → sales → revenue → profit. [self-reported]

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "If you want to devalue your product or service and be the cheap solution, by all means, discount away. But if you want to be the premier solution you shouldn't discount."

> "The group that offered discounts closed less deals and made less revenue than the group that said 'Hey, we're not giving a discount.'"

> "The moment they tell you 'No' you're like 'Whoa, I can't have what I want!' ... And the moment you tell your potential customers, when they ask for a discount 'No', they're more likely to sign up."

> "In sales, stop selling features, stop selling your product or service. Start selling the end goal."

> "I don't tell people on the phone ... 'Hey, you're going to get x backlinks x articles or content.' We talk about 'Here's how much more traffic you're going to get ... here's how many more sales ... here's how much revenue ... here's how much more money you're going to make.'"
