# Why You Should (Always) Dress For Success

## Summary

Solo, personal/mindset. Neil recounts a **belief reversal**: he used to think dress didn't affect
business success — that people judge you on how smart you are and how much you can help them. He
admits he personally still dresses casually (Lululemon workout clothes at home; a polo only because
he's on camera) and notes that in some communities (e.g. **San Francisco / tech**) casual dress is
fine. But he ran a **self-experiment** while living in **Las Vegas**: he **spent "over six figures
on clothes"** to see what would happen, and found that **dressing nicer made companies trust him
more, want to work with him more, and pay him more money** — despite his knowledge being unchanged.
His counsel: **appearances matter to a lot of people**, so unless you actively choose not to
conform, **put your best foot forward** to be seen positively and earn more. He cites his **mom's
advice** ("stop wearing those t-shirts with holes") when he speaks on stage — he joked that a paid
speaker can wear what he wants, and she countered "that doesn't mean you should," which he now says
was "100% correct." He references a **blog post he published on how dressing nicely affects your
income** that drew many comments from readers who tried it and also earned more. Caveat/self-aware
note: he says "these days i don't really dress as nice because i just don't care and if i don't make
as much money from it so be it."

## Key claims

- As of 2018-10-20, Neil **reversed his earlier belief** that dress doesn't affect business outcomes;
  it does — though it's **context-dependent** (matters less in SF/tech communities).
- As of 2018-10-20, in a **Las Vegas self-experiment** he **spent "over six figures on clothes"** and
  found dressing nicer led people to trust him, want to work with him, and **pay him more** — with his
  actual expertise unchanged.
- As of 2018-10-20, **people judge you on appearance whether you like it or not**, so (if you're
  willing) **dress up to be perceived positively** and increase income.
- As of 2018-10-20, he cites his **mom's advice** against holey t-shirts on stage and a **blog post**
  claiming dressing nicely raises income, corroborated by reader comments (self-reported).
- As of 2018-10-20, Neil personally **prioritizes comfort** (Lululemon at home) and says he no longer
  dresses as nicely, accepting he may leave money on the table.
