# Counterintuitive YouTube ads strategy, Google's 7-11-4 rule, AI for content multiplication & more (Marketing School × Perpetual Traffic)

## Summary
★ **Premise correction:** despite the two-host framing, this is a FOUR-person crossover between *Marketing School* (Neil Patel + Eric Siu) and the *Perpetual Traffic* podcast (Ralph Burns of Tier 11 + Kasim Aslam of Solutions 8), structured as a "wicked smart" round-robin where each host shares a tactic and then a recent learning. Roughly a quarter of the talk is Neil; the rest is split across Eric, Ralph, and Kasim, all of whom are quarantined below as context. Neil's two substantive contributions are (1) a **platform-native / in-app selling** paid-ads tactic — build ads that deliver the full pitch inside the native platform (e.g. multi-card Instagram Stories carrying all the copy a landing page would) so users only leave to buy, which he reports is producing "massive ROAS" and much lower cost than driving cold clicks to a website — and (2) a **leadership-and-hiring framework** for scaling an agency: hire people who earned repeated promotions at competitors, and once you scale (NP Digital nearing ~1,000 employees) accept you won't get all rockstars — instead install strong leaders at the top who build systems that lift C players to B and B to A. He also riffs on attribution chaos (his own AirPods purchase completed at an airport kiosk after months of untracked online exposure). Prefixed ★ because both Neil frameworks are clean, quotable, and belong in `agency-entrepreneurship` (leadership/hiring) and `paid-ads`/`social-media` (in-app selling) topics + `persona/beliefs` on a later synthesis pass.

## Key claims
- **In-app / platform-native selling is producing "massive ROAS."** Most ads — brand-awareness or performance — try to drive people off-platform to a website to convert; keeping people on the platform is "way cheaper." Example: instead of one Instagram Story ad, run multiple Stories that deliver the full pitch and all the copy a user would otherwise get on the landing page, keeping them inside the native platform they're comfortable in; some platforms now let them buy in-app, so the website is visited only to complete the purchase. Neil reports this converts well and is much more cost-effective. (Neil, 2024-02-22)
- The in-app approach makes the unit economics work again versus the classic squeeze (spend $10, make a $30 sale, ~$10 profit, then front-end losses hoping to recoup on LTV) — Neil credits that break-even framing to Kasim's example, then argues native-platform ads "make the numbers more favorable to the advertisers again." (Neil, 2024-02-22)
- **Modern buying is non-linear and hard to attribute.** Neil's first AirPods purchase: saw them online, read articles, saw them on Apple's site, didn't want to spend; later saw people wearing them, then at an airport kiosk swiped a card and bought on the spot — none of which is trackable back to the months of prior online exposure. "That's how buying works these days." (Neil, 2024-02-22)
- **Leadership is everything; hire from competitors by promotion track record.** The hardest lesson Neil learned as an entrepreneur: whatever problem you're solving, someone in your space has likely already solved it. VCs told him early "your only job is to hire amazing talent," which took years to click. His model: recruit people who worked for competitors and earned *continual* promotions — repeated promotions (especially across several competitors) signal the company genuinely valued them; if they're also a cultural fit they "can do wonders." He credits this for NP Digital's growth over the last ~6 years. (Neil, 2024-02-22)
- **At scale you don't need all rockstars — you need leaders who build systems.** From friends who sold companies into 50k–100k-employee orgs (MasterCard, Accenture), Neil learned that a large organization is never staffed with all A players. What you need is amazing leaders at the top who train, instill values, and create systems/processes that lift C players into B players and B players into A players. Leaders should be people who've already solved the problem in your industry (fewer mistakes) *and* can build the systems to bring D players up to C, C to B, B to A. (Neil, 2024-02-22)
- NP Digital is approaching ~1,000 employees and Neil expects to hit that within ~3–4 months (self-reported, 2024-02-22). `(caption garble: he first says "500,000 plus employees," then immediately corrects to "close to hitting a thousand" — the ~1,000 figure is the intended one.)` (Neil, 2024-02-22)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Don't think of your ads as just an ad to drive people back to your website. How can you have an ad that engages them and gives them [everything they need] on the platform that they're on, so they don't have to go to your website other than to make the purchase? We find that to convert really well and be much more cost-effective.

> I literally swiped a credit card and bought AirPods right then and there. How are they going to track that I bought that at the kiosk at the airport, because I saw it all previously online? That's how buying works these days — and it's funny, some of this stuff is really hard to track.

> You don't need an amazing team of 100,000 people to build a big organization. What you need is amazing leaders at the top that can train and instill values and create systems and processes that bring up your C players and turn them into B players, and turn your B players into A players. You're never going to have an organization filled with all amazing people.

> Your leaders should be people who know how to solve problems and have already done it in your industry before, because there's less chance that they'll make mistakes. But they also need to be able to create systems and processes to bring up the D players and make them C players, and make the C players B players.

> One of the hardest lessons I learned as an entrepreneur is leadership really is everything in an organization.

## Other voices (co-hosts & guests — context, NOT persona data)

### Eric Siu (Marketing School co-host) — [[wiki/entities/eric-siu]]
- Sees social media as "a slot machine now — the more you put out, the more chances you have to hit the triple sevens." (Eric, 2024-02-22)
- AI-assisted content for the Marketing School podcast: 90-minute episodes are run through Riverside's AI transcript, trending topics turned into blog posts with AI, then a human in the loop adds context, links, images, and cross-links to recent publications; costs fell, output sped up, and it's now driving up to ~500–600 clicks/day and ramping. They mine the XML sitemaps of marketing-news sites for trending topics so writers don't have to ideate every time — building top-of-funnel with AI while using programmatic SEO for bottom-of-funnel. (Eric, 2024-02-22)
- TripAdvisor example for programmatic SEO: ~127M organic visits/month, ranking for ~20M keywords ("fun things to do in Rome," "things to eat in Japan," etc.); combine AI top-of-funnel content + programmatic bottom-of-funnel and "99% of people are not doing this." (Eric, 2024-02-22)
- Recent learning — reading *Working Backwards* (by two ex-Amazon leaders): two-pizza teams, single-threaded leadership, and the six-page narrative that replaces PowerPoints ("PowerPoints are for lazy people" — they let the presenter be lazy and shift the onus to the audience). (Eric, 2024-02-22)
- On A-player reality (building on Neil's point): even if you're really good, maybe 20% of your team are A players, usually 10–15%; nothing wrong with C/D players — D players are often new grads still learning — but you need leaders to bring them up rather than treating them as "another cog in the wheel." (Eric, 2024-02-22)

### Kasim Aslam (guest — Solutions 8 / Perpetual Traffic; no entity page)
- Counterintuitive YouTube-ads tactic (credited to Demetri Smirnoff at the "Driven"/War Room mastermind): YouTube ad watch times are far higher than other platforms (a Meta ad might get ~15–20s; a YouTube ad can run minutes; average time-on-site for YouTube ~20 min vs sub-60s across all sites). If watch time hits 60–80%, don't cut the ad — let people keep watching. Also run ads *to other YouTube videos* rather than straight to the funnel/offer — use high-value content to pull viewers into more high-value content ("give value first"). (Kasim, 2024-02-22)
- Google's **7-11-4 rule** (a real Google framing, from a Google multivariate study of purchasing behavior): a customer needs roughly **7 hours** of content, across **11** touchpoints/moments, in **4** locations before they're ready to engage with you. (Kasim, 2024-02-22)
- His agency spends $150k–$200k/month on YouTube ads; one of their highest-performing ads is an hour-long video. `(attribution: uncertain — the transcript describes it as "Ja Morant explaining media efficiency ratio," which the hosts joke about; whether the NBA player is literally the presenter or this is a caption/running-joke garble is unclear. "Media efficiency ratio" (MER) is a real metric.)` (Kasim, 2024-02-22)
- Recent learning — the **Lindy effect** (via Perry Marshall): the longer something has already existed, the longer it's likely to keep existing. (Kasim, 2024-02-22)

### Ralph Burns (guest — Tier 11 / Perpetual Traffic; no entity page)
- Treats AI as a force multiplier for existing staff more than a replacement (though he's replaced some content writers). Content-multiplication stack: **Opus.pro** clips long-form video into short-form segments (a human then refines); **contentatscale.ai** for blog syndication. One video editor "pumps out ~20 videos a day" — "the more we put out, the more leads, visibility, and opportunity to scale." Suggests Googling "AI tools for content multiplication." (Ralph, 2024-02-22)
- Buyer journeys are chaotic and non-linear — people don't go video → landing page → retargeting → buy in a tidy 3-touch window; expect them to bounce around. Back-to-basics David Ogilvy: call out your avatar's pain points and desires in the ad, then pull them through a non-linear sequence (he uses YouTube video ad sequences of 7–9 videos). (Ralph, 2024-02-22)
- Recent learning — *Make Your Brand Legendary* by Scott Wozniak: four components — operational excellence, memorable moments customers talk about, deep customer insights ("flash surveys"), all wrapped by a leadership team. Chick-fil-A example: per-franchise revenue ~2x the McDonald's average, driven by a customer-experience engine (the "My pleasure" script; measuring smiles-per-hour rather than average order value). (Ralph, 2024-02-22)
