# How Much Should You Invest In Marketing? (PROVEN Strategy)

_Q&A Thursdays; **Adam Lodolce** (viewership.com) hosts/asks and adds his own commentary,
Neil answers. Only **Neil-attributed** material trains the persona; Adam's remarks are marked
as the co-host's. Viewer question (Segar Sangam): how to decide a marketing budget for a startup
when it's hard to convince the boss to invest._

## Summary

Neil's answer: **a bootstrapped/low-cash startup should NOT set big quarterly marketing budgets**
— instead run **many tiny experiments** ($50–$100 each) until something gets **traction**. At the
experiment stage the effort **doesn't need to be profitable yet**; it just needs to show traffic/
sales you can see a path to fine-tuning into profit. **Double down on the ones with potential,
shelf the rest** (revisit in 6–12 months). Once a channel is profitable, budget stops being a
guess: "if you're spending a dollar making two dollars… your boss is going to be like how much
can you spend" — and profitable, tracked economics make it easy to **raise VC money** to scale.
He notes credit-card float helps (expenses hit ~30 days later while revenue often comes in first).
Rule: **let the budget be determined by how much revenue a channel can profitably generate**, not
by a pre-planned quarterly number; push hard while profitable, slow down the moment it slows.
Venture-funded companies are the exception (they can run quarterly budgets and invest ahead of
growth). Second half turns to **branding vs. trackable performance**: Neil endorses the **"rule of
seven"** (a person needs ~7 exposures to a message for it to land) and defends **billboards/TV/
press** as valuable even though they're **hard to track** — demonstrated with a quick brand-recall
quiz (credit card → Amex, shoes → Aldo, workout gear → Nike, car → Tesla; none Googled). Branding
is easier for large corporations with big budgets (cites **GEICO** spending "over 100 million a
year on tv ads"); harder but still real for startups.

## Key claims

- As of 2018-10-18, a **bootstrapped startup should run small $50–$100 experiments**, not large
  budgets; find traction first, then **double down on winners** and shelf losers for 6–12 months.
- As of 2018-10-18, an experiment **need not be profitable immediately** — it needs traction and a
  visible path to profitability via conversion/fine-tuning.
- As of 2018-10-18, **budget should be set by a channel's profitable revenue capacity**, not a
  pre-set quarterly figure: if $1 spent reliably returns $2, spend as much as it can absorb.
- As of 2018-10-18, **profitable, tracked unit economics make VC funding easy** ("vcs will gladly
  write a check if you've already figured out the economics"); credit-card float (~30-day terms)
  eases cash timing.
- As of 2018-10-18, Neil endorses the **rule of seven** and argues **branding (billboards/TV/press)
  is valuable but hard to track**; easier at corporate scale (GEICO example, self-reported figure).
- **(Adam Lodolce, co-host — not persona data):** watch **how fast you recoup ad spend** (30 days
  is great, 7 days is ideal); uses a tool "Wicked Reports" for long-term per-click value.
