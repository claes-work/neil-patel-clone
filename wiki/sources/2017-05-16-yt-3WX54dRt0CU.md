# 2 Ways to Grow Your Email List Fast

## Summary
Neil Patel argues that email is one of the most valuable marketing assets and channels, and lays out two tactics he uses to grow an email list quickly. The first is exit popups: he uses the free tool Hello Bar (garbled in captions as "Hellobar.com") to build them, and concedes that while people find popups tacky, they work — which is why they are everywhere. His highest-converting exit popup on NeilPatel.com uses an animated GIF background that previews the offer (in his case, a GIF showing him ranking #1 in Google for "online marketing"); the GIF should preview exactly what the visitor will receive (an e-book, a newsletter, etc.) so they feel comfortable opting in. The second tactic is content upgrades: a bonus resource tied to a specific blog post — for example, a downloadable cheat sheet ("chit sheet" in captions) or checklist that packages the post's key points — placed within the first few paragraphs of the post. He states both tactics can be built without design or technical skills using Hello Bar, and that he includes a cheat sheet on every blog post he writes. The video's title self-reports 700,000 emails captured via Hello Bar; that figure appears only in the title, not the transcript body. Advice reflects 2017 tooling and popup-heavy tactics.

## Key claims
- Email is one of the most valuable marketing assets, and for many companies (Neil cites Amazon and Overstock) email is the most valuable marketing channel (as of 2017-05-16).
- Two main tactics grow an email list fast: exit popups and content upgrades (as of 2017-05-16).
- Exit popups feel tacky and are widely disliked, but they work — their ubiquity is offered as evidence they convert (as of 2017-05-16).
- An exit popup with an animated GIF background is Neil's best-converting opt-in, out-performing all his other opt-ins on volume; his own example previews him ranking #1 in Google for "online marketing" (self-reported, as of 2017-05-16).
- The GIF should preview the actual offer (e.g., the e-book or newsletter) because showing people what they'll get before subscribing makes them more comfortable and lifts opt-ins (as of 2017-05-16).
- A content upgrade is a post-specific bonus (e.g., a cheat sheet or checklist repackaging a post's key points) offered to readers who want more on that topic (as of 2017-05-16).
- Content upgrades should be placed within the first few paragraphs of the blog post, and the click prompts a name-and-email capture (as of 2017-05-16).
- Both exit popups and content upgrades can be created with the free tool Hello Bar without any design or technical skill (as of 2017-05-16).
- Neil includes a cheat sheet/checklist on every blog post he writes (self-reported, as of 2017-05-16).
- Self-reported (title only): these methods captured roughly 700,000 emails via Hello Bar (as of 2017-05-16).
