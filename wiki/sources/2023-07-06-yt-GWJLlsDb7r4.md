# Mistakes to Avoid When Launching Your Product

## Summary
Solo interview with a founder-focused podcast host (references building founderos.com, traveling
extensively, based partly in Medellín). Covers newsletter/list growth, mentorship habits, wealth/
reward philosophy, and a speed-over-perfection operating principle. On newsletter growth: states
the single most effective lever for scaling an email list is giving away free tools people opt in
to use repeatedly (credits this as the mechanism behind NP Digital's own list exceeding one
million subscribers, growing roughly 286,000/month at the time), ranking free opt-in eBooks/guides
and paid-traffic-to-blog as distant seconds; recommends non-technical founders build lean tools
cheaply by white-labeling scripts from codecanyon.net (typically $10-30) rather than building
custom software. On mentorship: says he doesn't deliberately "surround himself" with mentors
(logistically hard while traveling ~35 weeks/year across different time zones/languages);
instead credits conferences (where he's met people, including this host, at "two conferences now")
and unstructured self-directed learning from other people's YouTube videos as his main channels of
informal mentorship. On rewards/wealth philosophy: states no single purchase has meaningfully
changed his life; having a driver has helped productivity (works from the car) more than any
one-off "reward" purchase; reiterates the home-buying-as-mistake belief with specific texture —
homes carry recurring maintenance problems (cites his own experience with leaky roofs even in new
builds) and he'd rather redirect the equivalent of a mortgage/20%-down payment into growing a
business, betting on himself, than into real estate. On the emotional-return-to-purchases arc:
says his mindset shifted once he noticed buying more watches (mentions owning Patek Philippe
watches he rarely wears) produced only brief happiness before wanting "what's next"; now largely
indifferent to cars (mentions a Maybach bought and later resold during COVID, then a newer Maybach
model bought out of habit rather than as an intentional reward) and states he and his wife are
actively debating selling their Beverly Hills house, having barely used it in a year and a half,
preferring to redirect that capital to donation or other uses; contrasts this with continued
attachment to a family Honda Odyssey (a hand-me-down from his father) as a comfort object. On
speed: states he optimizes explicitly for shipping fast/imperfect over polish, revising toward
quality only after something is already producing results, and separately names not generating
revenue quickly enough as one of his biggest pet peeves in other founders — advises simply trying
to sell before building out more infrastructure. On travel: frames international travel not as a
tradeoff against building a business but as something he was already doing for work, and argues a
major (self-reported, not sourced further) insight from years of travel is that unglamorous "basic
necessity" businesses (his examples: rice, dirt-hauling/construction waste, HVAC) are frequently
far more lucrative than they look, contrasting this against a California/Silicon-Valley-raised
assumption that tech is where "everything is."
