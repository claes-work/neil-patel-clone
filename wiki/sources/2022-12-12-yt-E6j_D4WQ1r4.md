# How To Future Proof Your SEO Against Google's Next Update

## Summary
Argues the only durable defense against Google core updates is optimizing for user
experience rather than any specific ranking-factor trick, since every update's goal is
"make the algorithm more reflective of human behavior." Cites Search Engine Land: since
2016 there have been 16 core updates, roughly three per year since 2018, and roughly
4,500 total algorithm changes per year (most imperceptible). Concrete UX levers: (1)
page speed — cites HTTP Archive that images make up 21% of average page weight;
recommends image-size optimization and a CDN (names Cloudflare) to serve content from
geographically closer servers; (2) match content length/format to actual search
intent — explicitly criticizes blanket "5,000-word article" SEO advice, using "how to
tie a tie" as an example where an animated gif or short video beats a long article; (3)
use Crazy Egg heat maps to see where users click/scroll and where they drop off; (4)
competitive research via Google Search Console — pull your ranking keywords, then
manually Google them and study the top ~10 results for content/product/UX gaps to
close. Notes that even doing everything right, a given update can still cost traffic
short-term, framing SEO as a long-run optimization, not a one-update game. Closes with
an NP Digital plug.

## Key claims
- Cites Search Engine Land: 16 Google core updates since 2016, roughly three per year
  since 2018, and roughly 4,500 total algorithm changes per year (as cited, dated
  2022-12-12).
- Cites HTTP Archive: images account for 21% of an average webpage's total weight (as
  cited, dated 2022-12-12).
- States explicitly he considers blanket "you need 5,000-word articles to rank"
  advice wrong, arguing content length should match search intent (self-reported
  stance, dated 2022-12-12) — consistent with the word-count stance in the 2022-11-03
  "Quick Read Test" source.
- Recommends Crazy Egg heat maps and manual Search-Console-driven competitive
  research (checking the top ~10 ranking results per keyword) as his own SEO workflow
  (self-reported, dated 2022-12-12).

## Notable verbatim quotes
> Every Google update has essentially the same overall goal, make the algorithm more reflective of human behavior. So think about what is best for people, not what's best for Google.

> I hate it when SEOs are like, you got to create 5,000 word articles to rank while in Google... People want the answer as quick as possible. That's good user experience.

> You're not optimizing for the short run, you're optimizing for the long run.
