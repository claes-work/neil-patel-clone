# Will Google's AI Kill Your SEO Traffic? (Ep. 2620)

## Summary
A co-hosted Marketing School episode on Google's SGE (Search Generative Experience) and its effect on organic SEO traffic, driven largely by Eric Siu, who introduces and defines SGE and walks a Michael King (Search Engine Land) analysis on screen — the redistribution of the search demand curve, lower click-through rates for top positions, "multi-dimensional" searchers, and the long tail getting longer. Neil Patel, who says he only skimmed the linked article, supplies the persona-relevant reactions: SGE returns the kind of answer you'd previously get from a blog post (not just definitions), Google will stay cautious and neutral on YMYL ("your money, your life") topics like financial and health advice, and (less certainly) that thorough, continually updated content is how you stay visible. Neil's stance here is measured rather than alarmist — he stresses SGE won't appear on every query and Google still needs ad revenue — which reads as a more moderate framing than the "SEO industry extinction" tenor of the nearby Ep. 2616 from days earlier (no wiki page yet); position appears broadly consistent (AI reshapes but does not erase SEO), just calmer in emphasis. Roughly the back half of the episode is off-topic driving banter (biographical color: Neil is a self-admitted bad driver with a full-time driver).

## Key claims
- SGE returns the kind of answers you'd previously get from reading a blog post, not just dictionary-style definitions — it handles deeper, multi-step questions (e.g. "what interior-design style goes with a traditional house") and offers suggestions on what to look for. (Neil, 2023-12-01)
- Google will be deliberately cautious and neutral on YMYL ("your money, your life") topics — financial advice, health advice — inside SGE, because the last thing it wants is to give wrong advice or sway people one way or another. (Neil, 2023-12-01)
- To stay visible under SGE, keep content super thorough and continually updated; Google prefers more up-to-date sources, fresher sites tend to rank better (Wikipedia ranks well partly because it stays current), and Google won't showcase decade-old data when people want what's relevant today. (Neil, attribution: uncertain — flow is continuous with Eric's screen walkthrough but the framing matches Neil's signature freshness/Wikipedia talking point, 2023-12-01)
- Neil is a self-admitted very bad driver and keeps a full-time driver specifically so he doesn't risk injuring himself or others. (Neil, biographical color, 2023-12-01)
- SGE = Search Generative Experience: Google surfaces an AI-generated answer at the top, then ads, then local/other boxes depending on the query. (Eric, 2023-12-01)
- Per Michael King's analysis, SGE redistributes the search demand curve and lowers click-through rates for top positions — the post-SGE CTR for the #1–3 positions drops sharply (vs. the ~30–40% a #1 result could earn ~10 years ago); impact varies because SGE isn't present on every query. (Eric relaying Michael King, 2023-12-01)
- Searchers become "multi-dimensional," so the onus is on creators to make content fulfill multiple stages/intents to stay in the consideration set — argues for a pillar piece that covers the cluster and cross-links, so SGE can pull your result into its box. (Eric relaying Michael King + own framing, 2023-12-01)
- Google won't trigger SGE for everything because it still needs ad revenue — "one of the best money printers in the world." (Eric, 2023-12-01)
- Article stat: users spend an average of ~14.6 seconds; content beyond that window doesn't immediately threaten organic traffic because users scroll down to the standard results rather than wait. (Eric relaying Michael King, 2023-12-01)
- A trend they're seeing: searches are getting more long-tail (e.g. "best auto insurance for my Mercedes G-Wagon with 22,000 miles") and the long tail will get even longer, with the bulk of search volume shifting into it. (Eric, 2023-12-01)
- No solid data yet on how user behavior changes in an SGE environment; suggested SimilarWeb for traffic data (explicitly unsponsored). (Eric, 2023-12-01)
